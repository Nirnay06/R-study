① Developmental Projects & Tribal Displacement

▼ MIND MAP — Developmental Projects
Michel Cernea — People-centric R&R 50 million displaced Dam Projects (Narmada, Polavaram) Mining Projects (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh) 8.6% population → 40-50% displaced R&R Policy 2007

Introduction

Development of infrastructure is necessary for national growth. However, projects on forest lands result in displacement of tribals living at construction sites, with a huge impact on their lives. Development and displacement have become uncomfortable companions in post-independence India.

Michel Cernea
  • Involuntary displacement should be minimised and, if possible, avoided.
  • If unavoidable, displacement must be carried out in a way that protects the livelihood of tribes.
  • Rehabilitation and resettlement should be "people-centric" in approach.
Nalin Negi & Sujata Ganguly (2011, University of Bielefeld, Germany)
  • Around 50 million people displaced due to development projects in India.
  • 40–50% of the displaced are tribals — a severe disproportionate impact.
  • Tribal population = only 8.6% of total India — yet bear the majority of displacement burden.

Examples of Dam-Induced Displacement

DAM PROJECT | Andhra Pradesh
Case Study 1 — Polavaram Dam

Resulted in estimated displacement of 3 lakh people, the majority of whom were tribals. A stark illustration of how dam projects disproportionately affect tribal communities.

DAM PROJECT | Narmada River
Case Study 2 — Sardar Sarovar Dam (Narmada Eviction)
  • On 12 December 1979, despite widespread protests, the Indian government decided to raise the height of the Sardar Sarovar Dam.
  • Plan: 30 major, 135 medium, and 3,000 small dams on the Narmada and its tributaries.
  • Purpose: Provide water to 40 million people + irrigation + electricity generation.
  • Impact: 200,000 people displaced, majority Adivasis — one of India's largest displacement events.

Examples of Mining-Induced Displacement

MINING PROJECT | Jharkhand
Case Study 3 — Pakri Barwadih Coal Mining Project (Hazaribagh District)
  • Operated by NTPC (National Thermal Power Corporation) under the North Karanpura Coalfield.
  • Land acquisition began 2016; involves 3,319.42 hectares (8,202 acres) — including 1,950.5 ha farmland, 643.9 ha forest land, 101.22 ha settlements.
  • 19 villages affected — Barkagaon, Sonbarsa, Pakri Barwadih, Deoria Khurd.
  • Compensation: Rs 9.85–10.85 lakh per adult; re-employed only as contract guards or labourers on land they once owned.
  • Promised jobs for women ended when factories shut down.
  • In Jharkhand overall, mining companies acquired over 87,000 hectares — larger than the city of Bengaluru.
  • Skilled jobs went to migrants from other states; locals left unemployed.
  • Jharkhand's 2008 Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy promised locals would benefit first — in reality, tribals lost both land and livelihood.
MINING PROJECT | Chhattisgarh
Case Study 4 — Chaal Coal Mine, Raigarh District (Kawar Tribe)
  • Chaal open-cast coal mine, Raigarh district, operated by South Eastern Coalfields Limited (SECL) since 2003.
  • Displaced ~400 Adivasi (Kawar tribe) families from Lath village, leaving only 56 families behind.
  • Farmlands and rice fields destroyed by blasting and excavation → loss of livelihood + forced migration.
  • Mine waste discharged into nearby rivers → severe water contamination (cut off clean water and fish supply).
  • Promised jobs and compensation remained only on paper.
  • Hindalco Industries nearby demolished Adivasi homes for mine expansion without consent or compensation.
  • Together, these cases demonstrate land alienation, livelihood loss, and erosion of tribal dignity and culture.

Impacts of Displacement

Livelihood Impacts

  • Loss of access to forest lands and forest produce.
  • Loss of natural habitat lived in for generations.
  • Forced to move to fringes with no access to forest or forest produce.

Economic Impacts

  • Loss of access to Minor Forest Produce (MFP).
  • Transformation into landless peasants.
  • Severe poverty among tribals.
  • Shift to industrial labour from traditional occupations.
  • Inadequate and delayed monetary compensation.

Socio-Cultural Impacts

  • Reduction of collectiveness to individualistic attitude — loss of community values.
  • Agriculturalists forced into industrial labour — loss of traditional livelihoods.
  • Religious transformation — ritualistic cultures fade.
  • Changing marriage rules — shift from endogamy to exogamy due to displacement.

National Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy, 2007

Key Objectives:
  1. To minimise displacement and promote non-displacing or least-displacing alternatives.
  2. To ensure adequate rehabilitation package with active participation of affected families.
  3. To ensure special care for protecting rights of weaker sections — SCs and STs — with sensitivity.
  4. To provide a better standard of living and sustainable income to affected families.
  5. To integrate rehabilitation concerns into development planning and implementation process.
  6. To facilitate harmonious relationships through mutual cooperation.

Key Conclusion: Displacement has always been a companion of development. The process should be as humane and voluntary as possible. Cost of rehabilitation should be included in project cost.

② Forest Policies — Colonial Era to Post-Independence

▼ MIND MAP — Forest Policy Evolution
1865 — Formal state control 1878 — Reserved/Protected/Village forests 1894 — Eminent Domain 1927 — IFA, Section 64 1952 — Concessions not rights 1980 — Protect tribal rights 1982 — Roy Burman Committee 1988 — PARADIGM SHIFT; JFM 2006 — FRA: Historical Justice

Tribal-Forest Relationship

Tripathi (2016)
  • Tribes live in a harmonious and interdependent relationship with their forest environment.
  • Their economic life, social organisation, and magico-religious beliefs all revolve around the forest ecosystem.
  • Existence of tribal communities is deeply linked with forests — a true symbiotic relationship.

Forest Policy Timeline

Year Act / Policy Key Provision / Significance
1865 Forest Act First formal state control over forests. Traditional practices restricted by law. Forests treated as state property.
1878 Forest Act Divided forests into Reserved, Protected, and Village Forests. Trespassing, grazing, collecting produce — prohibited or declared offences. Consolidated Forest Department power.
1894 First Forest Policy Introduced concept of "Eminent Domain" — state ultimate control over forest land. Stated: "Rights and privileges of individuals must be limited." Focused on revenue generation and timber supply for British needs.
1927 Indian Forest Act Codified previous forest laws. Strengthened powers of forest officers. Section 64 — authorised arrest without warrant. Created to serve British timber needs; overrode customary rights; declaring forests as state property. Led to clearing of millions of hectares for monoculture plantations. Forces people to choose between abandoning forest or living as "criminals."
1952 National Forest Policy Continued colonial approach. Tribals given concessions not rights. Emphasised forests as national asset — ignored livelihood dependence.
1980 National Committee on Development of Backward Areas Recommended that tribal rights and concessions be fully protected, and domestic needs be met through forest produce. Recognised importance of forest-based livelihoods.
1982 B.K. Roy Burman Committee on Forests and Tribals Headed by eminent anthropologist B.K. Roy Burman. Highlighted tribes earn nearly one-third of income from minor forest produce. Emphasised positive role of forest dwellers in forest preservation and regeneration. Suggested tribal participation for sustainable forest management.
1988 National Forest Policy — PARADIGM SHIFT Shift from revenue-oriented to people-oriented forest management. Main objective: Environmental stability and ecological balance. Recognised symbiotic relationship between tribal people and forests. Introduced Joint Forest Management (JFM). Recognised Minor Forest Produce (MFP) as a RIGHT, not a concession. Led to establishment of TRIFED. (Hiremath, 1994 — tribal communities should be actively involved.)
2006 Forest Rights Act (FRA) Corrects historical injustice. Three types of rights: Land Rights, Use Rights, Community Forest Rights. Gram Sabha as central authority. Special provisions for PVTGs.
2007 National R&R Policy Minimise displacement; adequate rehabilitation; protect rights of SCs/STs.
2019 Supreme Court Eviction Order Ordered eviction of 1.8 million forest dwellers; later stayed due to activism and protests.

③ Forest Rights Act, 2006 (FRA) — In Full Detail

Exam Note: This is a standalone question topic. FRA 2006 can appear as a 10-mark or 20-mark question. Master every section below — objectives, provisions, issues, achievements, and way forward.
▼ MIND MAP — FRA 2006
Historical Justice Land Rights (≤4 ha) Use Rights (MFP, grazing) CFR — Section 3(1)(i) PVTG Habitat — Section 3(1)(e) Cut-off date: 13 Dec 2005 14 Implementation Issues Gram Sabha as authority Mendha Lekha success Hasdeo Arand violation

I. Objectives of FRA 2006

  1. To undo the historical injustice suffered by forest-dwelling communities under colonial forest laws.
  2. To ensure land tenure, livelihood, and food security of forest-dwelling Scheduled Tribes (FDSTs) and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (OTFDs).
  3. To strengthen conservation — including responsibilities and authority for sustainable use, conservation of biodiversity, and maintenance of ecological balance.

II. Background — Why Was FRA Needed?

Conditions in Forest Areas (Pre-FRA):
  • Millions of forest dwellers subject to harassment and evictions in their own land.
  • Torture, bonded labour, extortion, and sexual assault quite common in forest areas.
  • The Indian Forest Act, 1927 declared forests as state property, overriding customary rights.
  • Single officials settled disputes — mostly recording rights of powerful communities.
  • Resulted in clearing of forests for monoculture; forced people to either abandon forests or live as "criminals."

III. Who is a Forest Dweller Under FRA?

Two-Stage Eligibility:

Stage 1 — Everyone must satisfy BOTH conditions:
  1. Primarily residing in forests or forest lands.
  2. Depends on forests and forest land for livelihood ("Bonafide livelihood needs").
Stage 2 — Must prove ONE of the following:
  • Conditions have been true for 75 years → classified as "Other Traditional Forest Dweller" (OTFD)
  • OR — Member of Scheduled Tribe AND residing in a Scheduled Area → classified as "Forest Dwelling Scheduled Tribe" (FDST)

IV. Key Provisions Under FRA 2006 — Three Types of Rights

Type of RightProvisionKey Details
A. Land Rights Individual Forest Rights
  • No rights to land not being cultivated prior to 13 December 2005.
  • Those cultivating without documents can claim up to 4 hectares, provided they cultivate it themselves for livelihood.
B. Use Rights Collection & Grazing Rights
  • Rights over collection of Minor Forest Produce (MFP) — tendu, herbs, medicinal plants (No timber).
  • Rights over grazing grounds and water bodies.
  • Traditional areas of use by nomadic or pastoralist communities.
C. Community Forest Rights (CFR) Section 3(1)(i)
  • First time in Indian law: community has right to protect and manage the forest.
  • CFRs grant legal right to protect, regenerate, conserve, and manage forest resources.
  • In several states, Forest Department's control-oriented approach has slowed or obstructed CFR recognition.
D. PVTG Habitat Rights Section 3(1)(e)
  • Special provision for Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs).
  • "Rights including community tenures of habitat and habitation."
  • Includes common habitat and habitats in reserved forests and protected forests.

V. Case Studies

POSITIVE CASE STUDY | Chhattisgarh & Odisha
Recognising CFR Rights — Kanger Ghati & Simlipal
  • Chhattisgarh has recognised nearly 4,000 Community Forest Rights (CFRs) — one of the most proactive states in India.
  • Kanger Ghati National Park (Chhattisgarh) is the second national park in India (after Simlipal in Odisha) where CFR rights have been successfully recognised.
  • In both parks, indigenous communities (e.g., Gudiyapadar) are allowed to reside within the park and create community-led regulations for sustainable forest use.
  • This model illustrates a balance between conservation and livelihood rights.
NEGATIVE CASE STUDY | Chhattisgarh
Hasdeo Arand — Revocation of CFR Titles (Adani Mining)
  • Hasdeo Aranya region — ongoing conflict between development projects and community rights.
  • Ghatbarra village — one of the first villages to receive CFR titles (810 hectares in 2013).
  • District Level Committee revoked CFR rights in 2016 to facilitate mining in the Parsa East Kete Basen (PEKB) coal block (joint venture involving Adani Enterprises).
  • Hasdeo Aranya Bachao Sangharsh Samiti (HABSS) challenged this in court — argued it violated Gram Sabha consent provisions and FRA's core principle.
  • Chhattisgarh High Court (October 2025) upheld the revocation on technical grounds — disregarding evidence of Gram Sabha resolutions and community affidavits.
  • Activists termed the judgment a "mockery of the Forest Rights Act" — warns of dangerous precedent allowing corporate interests to override legally recognised community rights.
  • Underscores the gap between law and implementation.

VI. Implementation Issues of FRA 2006

ISSUE 1
High Rejection Rates

Large number of claims rejected due to incomplete documentation, lack of proof of cultivation before 13 December 2005, or disputes between Forest and Revenue Departments. In Odisha, over 1.4 lakh claims were rejected. Most states have not completed the review ordered by the Supreme Court (2019). Genuine forest dwellers branded as encroachers.

ISSUE 2
Non-Communication of Rejections

Rejected applicants rarely informed in writing, violating Section 6(2) of the Act — denying the right to appeal within 60 days. Irregular meetings of State Level Monitoring Committees add to procedural opacity.

ISSUE 3
Weak District Support

Many district administrations fail to provide revenue or forest maps to Gram Sabhas, making verification difficult and creating a gap between land claimed and land granted.

ISSUE 4
Faulty Evictions

Thousands of families evicted from forest lands on incomplete or manipulated records. In Hasdeo Arand (Chhattisgarh), the Ghatbarra village CFR title was cancelled to allow coal mining, violating due process.

ISSUE 5
Resistance by Forest Departments

Forest officials impede CFR recognition and retain control over forest management. Section 3(1)(i) of FRA — mandating community rights — is ignored in practice. E.g.: In Tamil Nadu, Kattunayakans (honey collectors) face harassment from forest guards.

ISSUE 6
Conflicting Laws and Policies

FRA's intent conflicts with Forest Conservation Act (1980), Wildlife Protection Act (1972), and CAMPA (2016). The 2023 amendment to FCA allows activities like ecotourism and safaris on forest land without explicit Gram Sabha consent.

ISSUE 7
Institutional Turf Wars

Poor coordination between Ministry of Tribal Affairs (MoTA) and Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) — delays implementation and causes contradictory directions at state level.

ISSUE 8
Procedural Violations

Claims often rejected by Forest Guards or Patwaris who have no legal authority to do so. In places like Chittorgarh, rejections were made orally without notice, preventing any appeal.

ISSUE 9
Data Manipulation and Errors

In Chhattisgarh, reports by ATREE found duplicate entries and inflated data — only 243 Gram Sabhas actually received community rights though records claimed 1,378.

ISSUE 10
Lack of Awareness

Many Gram Sabha members and OTFDs lack awareness about FRA provisions. Despite portals like Adi Prashikshan, awareness remains low in remote areas.

ISSUE 11
Judicial Uncertainty

The 2019 Supreme Court eviction order created panic among 1.8 million forest dwellers. Although stayed, most states are yet to review rejected claims properly.

ISSUE 12
Protected Area Displacement

Over 1 lakh forest dwellers displaced from tiger reserves due to NTCA relocations without settling their FRA claims — a conservation-vs-rights conflict.

ISSUE 13
Mining and Clearance Violations

Forest clearances granted before FRA compliance. The Hasdeo Arand case (Adani-operated mine) showed cancellation of CFR titles without Gram Sabha consent — termed a "mockery of FRA."

ISSUE 14
Uneven State Progress

Only Maharashtra (36%), Chhattisgarh (24%), and Odisha (10%) have recognised substantial CFR rights. Other major states remain at near zero recognition.

VII. Significance and Achievements of FRA 2006

#AchievementDetails
1 Historical Justice Corrects "historical injustice" under colonial laws. Recognises both IFR and CFR/CFRR.
2 Comprehensive Rights Sections 3(1)(b), 3(1)(c), and 3(1)(i) legally empower communities to access, occupy, and manage forest lands, water bodies, grazing areas, and biodiversity resources.
3 Empowered Gram Sabhas FRA makes Gram Sabhas the central authority for claim verification and forest governance — promoting participatory democracy.
4 Community Stewardship Successes Mendha Lekha (Maharashtra) and Betakata (Odisha) — formed CFR Management Committees (CFRMCs) that sustainably regulate forest use, prevent illegal felling, and conserve biodiversity.
5 Economic Empowerment In Pajilibandha and Kalakani (Odisha), women now earn ₹5,000–6,000 per month through Van Dhan Kendras — linking livelihoods with conservation.
6 Ecological Benefits Soura Kurlanda — CFR village that restored degraded forests and increased biodiversity, showing community control enhances conservation.
7 Gender and PVTG Inclusion Women and PVTGs participate in Van Suraksha Samitis, ensuring equitable access and decision-making power.
8 Link with PESA States like Odisha and Maharashtra merged FRA implementation with PESA, giving legal corporate status to Gram Sabhas under Article 243G.
9 Technological Innovation In Gujarat's Narmada district, NGOs used GPS and satellite imagery for digital maps in claim verification — now replicated in other states.
10 Administrative Achievement As per MoTA (2024), about 60% of total claims recognised, covering 13 million acres of land.
11 Global Model When properly implemented, FRA balances livelihood security with biodiversity conservation — a global model of justice-based environmental management.

VIII. Way Forward and Suggestions

1. Review Rejected Claims — States must review all rejected claims transparently, issue written reasons, ensure appeal rights under Section 6.
2. Strengthen Gram Sabhas — Recognise as body corporates (as in Maharashtra and Odisha) for fund management and legal representation.
3. Joint Institutional Coordination — Establish joint MoTA-MoEFCC mechanisms for consistent policy interpretation.
4. Digital Mapping Tools — Adopt GPS and GIS mapping with BISAG and NRSC assistance to eliminate data duplication and manipulation.
5. Awareness and Training — Expand Tribal Research Institutes and Adi Prashikshan-like platforms for grassroots legal training.
6. Livelihood Integration — Link CFR areas with TRIFED, Van Dhan Yojana, and PM-JANMAN for forest-based income.
7. Legal Safeguards — Mandate Gram Sabha consent before approving mining or diversion projects (prevent Hasdeo Arand-type violations).
8. Inclusive Participation — Encourage women, PVTGs, and marginal groups in decision-making and forest management.
9. Monitoring and Transparency — Create state-level dashboards with real-time data on claims filed, approved, and pending.
10. Model Replication — Scale up successful models from Mendha Lekha and Betakata; promote peer learning across states.
11. Policy Convergence — Integrate FRA with Green India Mission, CAMPA, and National Biodiversity Strategy.
12. National FRA Mission — Launch a dedicated National FRA Mission with a separate budget and time-bound targets for claim settlement.

IX. Supreme Court 2019 Order — Current Issue

SC Order (13 February 2019):
  • Court ordered eviction of lakhs of STs and OTFDs across 16 states whose claims had been rejected under FRA.
  • Chief secretaries directed to carry out evictions by July 24, 2019.
  • On February 28, 2019, court stayed its order — though it said "the mighty and the undeserving" who encroached would be shown no mercy.
  • Stay was due to the role of anthropologists and activists raising awareness about real reasons for high rejection rates, and due to tribal protests.
  • Court decided to examine whether due processes were followed by Gram Sabhas and states before rejecting claims.
  • Most states are yet to complete the review properly.

X. Transition: Colonial Forest Control to Community-Based Governance

Bureaucratic vs. Community Governance Top-down bureaucracy; forest department in complete control FRA (2006) empowers Gram Sabhas as democratic forest managers
Exploitation vs. Ecological Justice Extractive forestry — forests as commercial asset Rights-based environmental justice; conservation needs community participation
Economic Utility vs. Ecological Balance Forests as economic resources for timber and revenue 1988 policy reframed forests as ecological assets — JFM and CFR

Conclusion on Forest Policy: Forest policies in India reflect a journey from colonial domination to democratic participation. FRA (2006) embodies this transformation by recognising forest dwellers as partners in conservation rather than intruders. For its promise to be fulfilled, India must ensure effective implementation, community awareness, and strong Gram Sabha empowerment, so that justice, sustainability, and livelihood security truly reach those who live by the forest.

④ Climate Pressures on Forest-Dwelling Communities & Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK)

▼ MIND MAP — Climate Change & TEK
Erratic monsoons / droughts — Bhil Shrinking forests & biodiversity loss Jarawa — cyclone prediction Khasi — sacred groves Apatani — rice-fish farming (Ziro) Dongria Kondh — organic farming Zabo System — Nagaland Green displacement by CAMPA FRA + PESA = climate adaptation

India's tribal and indigenous communities are among the strongest protectors of forests and biodiversity, yet they face the severest impacts of climate change. Their livelihoods, food systems, and cultural traditions are being reshaped by ecological disruption.

1. Risks and Problems Due to Climate Change

Tribal livelihoods are highly climate-sensitive because they depend on forests, rainfall, and soil fertility.

  • Erratic monsoons and droughts — threaten cultivation and forest produce. The Bhil community in Gujarat, once praying for rain, now faces destructive floods followed by prolonged dry spells.
  • Shrinking forests and biodiversity loss — reduce access to MFP such as tendu, mahua, and bamboo — undermining income and food security.
  • Soil erosion and deforestation — worsen migration, especially in Central India, while extreme heat and forest fires endanger crops and health.

2. Coping Through Tribal Ecological Knowledge (TEK)

Tribal GroupLocationTEK Practice
Jarawa Andaman Islands Predict cyclones by observing fish movement and sea behaviour — enabling timely evacuation
Khasi Meghalaya Community agroforestry and maintenance of sacred groves — preserving soil moisture and biodiversity in high-rainfall zones
Apatani Arunachal Pradesh (Ziro Valley) Rice-fish integrated farming — conserving water and enhancing soil fertility without chemicals. A UNESCO recognised system.
Dongria Kondh Odisha Organic multi-crop farming with traditional pest control and seed-saving — ensuring food and ecological security
Irular Tamil Nadu Organic multi-crop farming with traditional pest control and seed-saving practices
Zabo System Nagaland Combines rainwater harvesting, forestry, animal husbandry, and agriculture — maintaining fertility and reducing drought stress

3. Continuing Struggles and Policy Disconnect

  • National programmes like CAMPA and the Green India Mission are largely top-down and have led to "green displacement" through afforestation without community consent.
  • Gender inequalities are widening — tribal women bear the burden of resource collection and loss of autonomy.
  • Traditional rain forecasting and shifting cultivation cycles are failing as climate shifts become unpredictable — eroding both livelihoods and ancestral knowledge.
  • Institutional fragmentation between MoTA and MoEFCC and the neglect of Gram Sabha consent under FRA weaken adaptation from below.

4. Path Ahead: Integrating Rights and Resilience

  • Integrate FRA (2006) and PESA (1996) into climate-adaptation frameworks and link them with schemes like PM JANMAN.
  • Empower Gram Sabhas to manage eco-restoration and climate funds locally.
  • Document and scale up models such as Zabo and Apatani systems across eco-regions.
  • Strengthen the role of tribal women and PVTGs in decision-making and climate planning.
  • Blend community forecasting techniques with modern satellite-based early-warning systems for better preparedness.
Key Insight: The people who nurture and protect India's natural ecosystems are the ones most endangered by climate change. Their survival depends not only on external support but on recognising them as equal partners in climate adaptation and forest governance.

⑤ Industrialization and Urbanization — Impacts on Tribal Communities

▼ MIND MAP — Industrialization & Urbanization
Das & Banerji (1964) — 4 Impacts Social: Family breakdown, land loss Economic: Unemployment, wage labour Religious: Ritual decline Psychological: Powerlessness POSITIVE: Education, art, reform Keonjhar — resource rich but poor MP — India's largest tribal population Gond paintings, Warli art

Introduction

  • Tribal lands are not only rich in forest resources but also contain rich mineral deposits.
  • Post-independence government exploited minerals for raw materials and constructed many projects in tribal lands.
  • This development was supposed to uplift tribals — but instead uprooted tribals from their dwellings.
  • They were deprived of land and paid meager cash which they used up eventually — leaving them landless and moneyless.
  • Rules provide for 90% reservation for locals in employment — but reality has been exactly the opposite.

Case Study: Madhya Pradesh — Encroachment and Forest Loss

CASE STUDY | Madhya Pradesh
India's Largest Tribal Population — and India's Highest Forest Encroachment
  • MP has India's largest tribal population — about 15% of its total.
  • Two belts: Western — Bhil tribes (49%); Eastern — Gonds (36%).
  • MP leads the country in forest land encroachment — over 5,347 square kilometres lost, the highest in India.
  • MoEFCC data: MP alone lost 219.7 hectares of forest land to infrastructure — about 24% of India's total forest diversion over the last decade.
  • Dhyan Singh Case (2022, Shahdol district): Tribal man committed suicide after forest department demolished his hut built on 0.010 hectares — barely a few meters of forest land. He had already received money under Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY) to build the house. Department justified action under Section 36(1) of Indian Forest Act, 1927.
CASE STUDY | Odisha
Keonjhar — Resource Rich but People Poor
  • Keonjhar district of Odisha — one of India's richest in iron ore and manganese, yet:
  • Over 13 lakh children are stunted or underweight.
  • 74.7% of pregnant women are anaemic (NFHS-5).
  • 25.9% of women have a low BMI (NFHS-5).
  • Economic development primarily benefits corporations and outsiders, while tribal inhabitants remain impoverished.

Impacts of Industrialization and Urbanization

According to Das and Banerji (1964), the impact can be studied under four major dimensions:

1. Social Impacts

a) Loss of Home and Forests

  • Forests represent both livelihood and identity for tribal communities — food, fuel, medicinal plants, shelter, cultural and spiritual significance.
  • Expansion of mining, dam, and industrial projects → forests rapidly depleted.
  • Disappearance of forests deprives tribals of economic base and sacred landscapes.
  • Destruction of sacred groves and ecological systems weakens traditional beliefs and community bonds.
  • Result: large-scale displacement, land alienation, and loss of cultural stability.

b) Breakdown of Family and Kinship

  • Das and Banerji (1964) — industrialization and migration have weakened interpersonal ties within tribal families.
  • Earlier, joint family system was the norm — ensuring collective ownership and strong kinship.
  • Migration → fragmentation into nuclear units → weakened traditional authority structures.
  • Elders no longer hold decision-making power; younger generation adopts individualistic values.
  • Rise in divorces, inter-community marriages, and decline in community-level festivals and rituals.
  • Result: erosion of kinship solidarity and weakening of community cohesion.

2. Economic Impacts

a) Shift in Occupation

  • Before industrialization: subsistence agriculture, hunting, fishing, and forest collection — family as basic unit of production.
  • Industrialization introduced individual wage labour in factories, mines, and construction sites.
  • Factory employment: rigid hours, repetitive tasks, and impersonal relations — contrasting with flexible communal nature of traditional tribal work.
  • Das and Banerji: labour has shifted from a collective social activity to an individual economic necessity.

b) Unemployment and Casual Labour

  • Majority of skilled/semi-skilled positions occupied by non-tribal workers; tribals employed temporarily as unskilled labourers.
  • After project completion, jobs vanish → families left without income or land.
  • Migration to urban centres → tribals end up as construction workers, street vendors, or domestic helpers, living in poor settlements without sanitation or healthcare.
  • Shift from self-sufficient agrarian lifestyle to economic dependency and urban poverty.

3. Religious Impacts

  • Industrialization and urbanization have led to gradual decline in tribal religious and ritual life.
  • Rituals connected to agriculture, harvest, and forest worship are now shortened or discontinued.
  • According to Das and Banerji (1964), simplification of traditional ceremonies occurred largely due to financial hardship and lack of leisure in urban/industrial settings.
  • Community shrines and sacred groves are often inaccessible after displacement.
  • Absence of ritual spaces and collective participation → breakdown of communal and spiritual identity among displaced tribals.

4. Psychological Impacts

  • Psychological consequences are severe and long-lasting.
  • Displacement, loss of land, and cultural alienation create a deep sense of insecurity and helplessness.
  • Individuals once living as independent cultivators or forest gatherers are now dependent on employers or contractors.
  • Loss of control over their own lives often leads to low self-esteem, frustration, and anxiety.
  • Das and Banerji: described this as the emergence of a sense of "powerlessness" among tribal populations.
  • Younger generation — disconnected from traditional culture — faces identity crises and higher vulnerability to substance abuse.

5. Positive Impacts

Despite numerous adverse effects, some positive outcomes have emerged:
  • Industrialization indirectly facilitated the spread of education, healthcare, and infrastructure in several tribal regions.
  • Exposure to new ideas led to gradual social reform — declining acceptance of practices like witch-hunting, child marriage, and gender-based taboos.
  • Traditional art and craft forms gained national and international recognition:
    • Gond paintings from Madhya Pradesh
    • Warli art from Maharashtra
    • Both promoted through cultural organisations and cooperatives.
  • Several NGOs and self-help groups assist tribal communities in marketing handicrafts, forest produce, and traditional goods — enabling economic empowerment and cultural preservation.
Conclusion on Industrialization and Urbanization:
Industrialization and urbanization have brought both development and destruction to India's tribal societies. These processes expanded infrastructure and created new economic opportunities, but also displaced millions, dismantled traditional social systems, and eroded cultural values. The need of the hour is inclusive and participatory development — where tribal communities are treated as partners rather than victims. Sustainable development must integrate economic growth with cultural preservation and guarantee that the rights and welfare of tribal populations are not compromised in the pursuit of modernisation.

Hiremath 1994 Tribal communities should be actively involved in forest protection, regeneration, and development.
Das and Banerji 1964 Impact of industrialization and urbanization on tribals studied under four dimensions: social, economic, religious, and psychological. Described emergence of "powerlessness" among tribal populations.

⑩ Key One-Liners & Facts for Exam

Michel Cernea: displacement should be minimised, avoided, and rehabilitation should be "people-centric."
Tribal population = 8.6% of India but constitute 40–50% of all displaced persons.
Nalin Negi & Ganguly (2011): 50 million displaced by development projects in India.
Tripathi (2016): tribes live in a harmonious and interdependent relationship with forests.
FRA 2006 recognises three types of rights: land rights, use rights, and community forest rights.
Cut-off date under FRA for land rights: 13 December 2005.
Land ceiling under FRA: up to 4 hectares for those cultivating without documents.
PVTG special provision: Section 3(1)(e) of FRA — habitat rights.
CFR falls under Section 3(1)(i) of FRA.
B.K. Roy Burman (1982): tribes earn one-third income from minor forest produce.
1988 Forest Policy = paradigm shift; introduced JFM; MFP as right not concession.
TRIFED markets Minor Forest Produce; Van Dhan Kendras empower tribal women.
Das and Banerji (1964): four dimensions of impact — social, economic, religious, psychological.
Keonjhar (Odisha) — rich in iron ore/manganese but 74.7% of pregnant women are anaemic.
Mendha Lekha (Maharashtra) and Betakata (Odisha) — successful CFR community management models.
Hasdeo Arand: CFR title cancelled for coal mining (Adani) — "mockery of FRA."
2019 SC order: eviction of 1.8 million forest dwellers; stayed due to anthropologist/activist intervention.
MoTA (2024): 60% of total FRA claims recognised, covering 13 million acres.
MP has India's largest tribal population (15%); leads in forest land encroachment (5,347 sq km lost).
Gond paintings (MP) and Warli art (Maharashtra) — tribal art gaining global recognition.
Indian Forest Act 1927, Section 64: authorised arrest without warrant for forest violations.
Pakri Barwadih: NTPC acquired 87,000+ hectares in Jharkhand — larger than Bengaluru city.
Chaal Coal Mine, Chhattisgarh: displaced 400 Kawar tribe families from Lath village.
Sardar Sarovar Dam: 200,000 people displaced, majority Adivasis.
Chhattisgarh: recognised nearly 4,000 CFRs — most proactive state in India.
Apatani (Arunachal Pradesh): rice-fish integrated farming in Ziro Valley — conserves water, enhances soil fertility.
Jarawa (Andaman Islands): predict cyclones by observing fish movement and sea behaviour.
Zabo System (Nagaland): combines rainwater harvesting, forestry, animal husbandry, and agriculture.
CAMPA and Green India Mission criticised for "green displacement" — afforestation without community consent.
Das and Banerji: emergence of sense of "powerlessness" among tribal populations due to industrialization.
