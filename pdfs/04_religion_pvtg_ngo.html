8.1 — Impact of Hinduism on Tribal Societies

Key Statistics:
  • More than 90% of tribal population directed towards Hinduism
  • 95% among total tribal population are Hinduised

Historical Background & Entry of Hinduism

  • During British rule, cultural contact between tribal societies and urban areas was rapid
  • Significant increase in cultural contact through "Peasantization" (N.K. Bose)
  • Hinduism is smooth and more compatible — tribes can continue with original values and also adopt new ones
  • Hinduised tribal groups: Bhils, Bhumij, Gonds
  • Forms of gotras are more totemistic than Vedic → basis of the Tribe-Caste Continuum
Tribe-Rajput Continuum (N.K. Bose): Bhumij tribes studied → tribes follow the Rajput Model. Tribes like Tharu and Khasa got themselves accepted as Kshatriya. Process called "Kshatriyaization" — a popular form of Sanskritization. Hinduism created a Tribe-Caste Continuum that allowed partial absorption without full erasure of identity.
Case Study — Bhil Tribe: Bhils practiced theft and dacoity. They believe Goddess Parvati was a Bhil; when they demanded bride price from Shiva, he refused. They killed Nandi (Shiva's bullock), were cursed to poverty but given a Vardaan: theft for livelihood invites no sin. Some sub-tribes consider themselves "thieves of Mahadev" — a mythological legitimation of their economic practices.

Positive Impacts of Hinduism

ImpactDetails
Cultural Integration & Social MobilityThrough Sanskritisation (Srinivas, 1952), Gonds, Bhils, Oraons adopted Hindu customs to claim higher status
Incorporation into Civilizational EthosTribal deities absorbed into Hindu pantheon — Gond deity Pharsa Pen → Shiva; Santhal Marang Buru → Mahadeo
Spread of Agriculture & Settled LifePeasantization (N.K. Bose) — Bhumij, Bhils, Oraons moved from jhum to settled agriculture
Introduction of LiteracyRaj Gonds used Sanskrit titles (Raja/Thakur) to legitimize political authority
Religious TolerancePolytheistic worldview allowed coexistence — Navakhani (Oraons) and Holi (Bhils) celebrated jointly

Negative Impacts of Hinduism

ImpactDetails
Replacement of Simple RitualsLambadi (Banjara) now employ Brahmin priests for life-cycle rituals — economic dependence on outsiders
Decline of Traditional InstitutionsYouth dormitories — Ghotul (Muria Gonds) and Dhumkuria (Oraons) — declined under Hindu moral codes
Emergence of DowryBride price → dowry among Ho, Munda, Gond — reversed egalitarian gender norms
Child Marriage & Widow RestrictionsBhil and Gond tribes: child marriage encouraged, widow remarriage prohibited
Caste-like HierarchiesRaj Gonds developed internal stratification resembling caste; similar among Oraons and Bhils
Loss of Cultural AutonomyTribal deities subordinated as local Hindu manifestations; tribal myths reinterpreted through Hindu cosmological lens
Conclusion: Hinduism has had a significant influence leading to Tribe-Caste Continuum. Interaction resulted in assimilation of novel customs and practices. The process was gradual, often voluntary, and allowed tribes to negotiate their position within the caste hierarchy while maintaining partial cultural distinctiveness.

8.1 — Impact of Christianity on Tribal Societies

Overview:
  • Spread of Christianity began during the colonial period
  • Christianity is the second largest religion among tribal communities, next only to Hinduism
  • Northeastern region has the highest concentration of Christian tribal populations

Positive Impacts of Christianity

1. First Organized Agency for Tribal Welfare

Before state schemes, missionaries were the first organized agency — brought literacy, healthcare, and social awareness to remote forest areas.

2. Introduction of Education and Tribal Scripts
  • Mizoram: Rev. J. H. Lorrain and F. W. Savidge (1894) — established schools, books in Mizo language
  • Nagaland: American Baptist Mission — first village school at Molungyimsen (1872)
  • Jharkhand/Chhattisgarh: Rev. John Baptist Hoffmann (Munda grammar), Paul Olaf Bodding (Santal dictionary)
  • Arunachal Pradesh: Among Adi and Nyishi, mission schools were first sources of literacy
3. Westernization — Elimination of Harmful Practices
  • Nagaland: Headhunting and fertility rituals disappeared
  • If mother died during childbirth, baby was buried with her → missionaries established orphanages and infant homes
  • Improved health through dispensaries, hospitals, vaccination, maternal care
4. Prohibition of Alcohol

Young Mizo Association (YMA) campaigns against rice beer (Zu) — reduced alcoholism and family disputes.

5. Syncretism and Religious Coexistence

Jharkhand: Christian Santals and Oraons celebrate Karma and Sohrai festivals with drums and hymns — described as "religious layering" — new beliefs added without erasing old ones.

Negative Impacts of Christianity

1. Ethnocentric Approach

Missionaries saw tribal faiths as "primitive" → erosion of customs, songs, and institutions. Cultural arrogance embedded in proselytization.

2. Sense of Superiority Among Converts

Christian Oraons of Chotanagpur developed feeling of superiority over non-Christians → fractured tribal unity.

3. Massive Demographic Change
  • Arunachal Pradesh: Conversion among Wancho, Nocte, Tangsa, Nyishi rose from <5% (1970s) to ~80% (2011)
  • Nagaland: ~90% of Nagas (Ao, Angami, Lotha, Sema) became Christian via Miles Bronson and E.W. Clark's missions
  • Mizoram: Mizo population converted almost entirely by early 20th century
4. Loss of Traditional Institutions

Morung (Nagas) and Ghotul (Gonds) declined — disrupted transmission of folklore and moral instruction. Mundas and Oraons stopped celebrating Karma and Sarhul.

5. Political Identity Implications
  • IFCSAP (Arunachal Pradesh): Demands delisting of converted Christians from ST benefits
  • Nagaland/Mizoram: Christianity became base for ethnic nationalism
  • Jaipal Singh Munda: Blended Christian and tribal ideals to mobilize Adivasi political movements

Revivalist Movements in Response to Christian Influence

  • Donyi-Polo movement (Arunachal Pradesh): Institutionalized Sun-Moon worship through temples (Khumkos) and schools
  • Sarna Dharma revival (Central India): Reaffirmed nature worship (Singbonga) among Munda and Oraon; All India Sarna Samiti demands separate Census code
  • Gond Mahasabha: Promotes Koya Punem, worshipping Baradeo as supreme god
Conclusion: Christianity played a transformative yet complex role — education, literacy, healthcare, social reforms on one side; cultural loss and religious polarization on the other. The Christian mission was simultaneously liberatory and colonial.

8.1 — Impact of Buddhism on Tribal Societies

Overview:
  • Buddhism followed by around 0.5% of Indian tribal population
  • Spread linked to Mauryan and Kushan emperors; Ashoka played a key role
  • Buddhism is a way of life — tribes could follow it alongside their own beliefs
  • Impact primarily in North-west and North-east Himalayan regions

Key Tribal Groups Influenced by Buddhism

  • Bhutias (Sikkim)
  • Lepchas (Meghalaya, Arunachal Pradesh, Bhutan, Sikkim, Darjeeling)
  • Chakmas (Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh)
  • Monpa, Sherdukpen, Khowa (Arunachal Pradesh)
  • Brokpa, Changpa, Balti (Ladakh)
C. von Furer-Haimendorf (1983): Buddhist converts in Arunachal Pradesh did not renounce tribal rituals. This symbiotic relationship between Buddhism and tribal religion is the key feature distinguishing Buddhist tribal conversion from Christian conversion. Monasteries like Bapuchang in Khampti and Singpho villages established; sacred specialists are Lamas. Sherdukpen worship tribal deities alongside Lamas; Monpas have elements of ancient Bon religion coexisting with Buddhism.

Case Studies: Buddhism and Tribes

TribeRegionKey Finding
BhutiaSikkim, DarjeelingFollow Vajrayana Buddhism (17th–18th c.); Losoong (Sikkimese New Year) and animistic dances coexist → cultural continuity within conversion
LepchaSikkim, NE HimalayasBon-like healing rituals and ancestor worship alongside Buddhist ceremonies — syncretism
ChakmaArunachal, MizoramTheravada Buddhists; still worship Lakshmi and forest spirits; village councils blend Buddhist codes with customary law — accommodation, not replacement
Monpa/Sherdukpen/KhowaArunachal PradeshMahayana Buddhism; Sherdukpen worship tribal deities alongside Lamas; Bon elements among Monpas — symbiotic coexistence
Brokpa/Changpa/BaltiLadakhMahayana dominant; pre-Buddhist mountain-spirit worship (Lha/Yul-lha), animal-sacrifice rituals continue — religious coexistence
Conclusion: Buddhism integrated with indigenous cosmologies rather than replacing them. Non-dogmatic nature, emphasis on ethics over exclusivity, acceptance of local gods as Dharma protectors → syncretic evolution. Even the adoption of Buddhism could not cut through traditional beliefs. Limited but significant impact.

8.1 — Impact of Islam on Tribal Societies

Overview:
  • Islam's penetration faced challenges due to dogmatic nature and limited support from Muslim rulers
  • Dissemination fell largely upon Sufi saints and preachers

Muslim Tribal Groups in India

  • Siddis of Gujarat
  • Gujjars of North-western Himalayas
  • Bakarwals of Jammu and Kashmir
  • Bhils, Kotis, Dhankasi of Rajasthan
  • Entire population of Lakshadweep — seven Muslim tribal communities: Koya, Malmi, Malacheri, Manikfan, Thakrufan, Thakru, and Reveri

ImpactDetails
Shift to Patrilineal NormsTransition from matrilineal/bilateral to patrilineal under Sharia inheritance norms

Detailed Case Study — Islam and Matriliny in Lakshadweep

The Taravad (Matrilineal Unit) — Structure:
  • Group of individuals tracing descent through common genealogy from an ancestress in unbroken female line
  • Depth: 3 to 6 or more generations
  • Every Taravad has a name used as prefix to personal names
  • Birth in Taravad gives right to share in Taravad property (land, trees, boats, buildings) — passes through female members
  • Male members have only usufructuary rights (use but not ownership)
  • Taravad is a matrilineal exogamous unit
  • Father has no legal responsibility for children's maintenance — children belong to mother's descent group
  • Background: Matriliny brought by nearest coastal people of Kerala
Islam's Influence on Matriliny — The Tension:
  • Islamic marriage procedure adopted BUT duolocal residence pattern not given up
  • Duolocal residential arrangement: Men and women including spouses live in separate houses
  • Most religious activities (festivals, Quran chanting, fulfilment of vows) carried out within the context of the matrilineal group
  • If Taravad has an ancestor-saint, anniversary celebrated by entire matrilineage
  • Islam provides easy divorce → high frequency of divorce in Lakshadweep
  • Current Changes (Islamisation): Matrilineal practices gradually eroding — growing shift toward patriliny, nuclear family residence, and male headship
Three-Point Summary of Negative Impacts:
  1. Erosion of Traditional Kinship: Shift from matrilineal/bilateral to patrilineal under Sharia inheritance — Lakshadweep Taravad weakened
  2. Decline of Indigenous Rituals: Ancestor worship, spirit appeasement, sacred dances replaced by namaz, fasting, Islamic festivals
  3. Dual Marginalisation: Gujjars and Bakarwals perceived as socially inferior to Ashraf Muslims (Syeds, Pathans) — outside both Islamic upper layers and mainstream economy
Conclusion: Islam influenced tribal societies through new religious practices, laws, and social norms. Yet tribes like those in Lakshadweep adapted Islam within their own cultural frameworks, creating a blend of faith and indigenous identity. Islam's more dogmatic character meant deeper cultural disruption than Buddhism, yet Sufi influence allowed some syncretic adaptation.

8.2 — Tribe and Nation State

Key Definitions

ConceptDefinition
TribeSocial group with territorial affiliation, endogamous, no specialization of functions, ruled by hereditary/tribal officers, united in language/dialect
NationCommunity based on shared language, history, ethnicity, culture, society — collective identity of a group
StateCentralized political organization imposing rules over a population within a territory; maintains monopoly on legitimate use of violence (Max Weber)
Nation StatePolitical unit where state and nation are congruent; people share common identity, culture, history
Nation vs. Nation State — Examples:
  • Kurds = nation without a state
  • France, Germany, Japan = classic nation-states
  • Korea = one nation divided into two states (North and South)

Who are Indigenous and Tribal Peoples?

More than 370 million people in more than 70 countries worldwide. No universal definition — ILO Convention No. 169 takes a practical approach.

ILO Convention No. 169 — Criteria

CategorySubjective CriteriaObjective Criteria
Indigenous peoples Self-identification Descent from populations present at time of conquest/colonization; retain own social, economic, cultural, political institutions
Tribal peoples Self-identification Social, cultural, economic conditions distinguish them from national community; status regulated by customs, traditions, or special laws
India's Special Case: Division into indigenous vs settlers is NOT valid in India — India cannot be divided into "natives" and "non-natives." Other terms used globally: native peoples, aboriginal peoples, first nations, adivasi, janajati, hunter-gatherers, hill tribes. In Latin America, "tribal" is applied to some afro-descendent communities.

Challenges of Tribes vis-à-vis Nation States

  1. Tribal identity & culture: Distinct cultures, languages, traditions → conflicts over land rights and cultural preservation when nation-state imposes uniformity
  2. Self-governance & self-determination: Tribal structures clash with centralized authority; self-determination is a fundamental human right under international law
  3. Colonization & forced assimilation: History of resentment and mistrust; nation-state must acknowledge historical injustices to build trust

9.1 — History of Tribal Administration in India

British Era — Isolationist and Exploitative

Why the British pursued an Isolationist Policy (4 Rationales):
  1. Security & Political Control: Tribal groups seen as "explosive" — e.g., Santhal Uprising; isolation cut them from national movements
  2. Economic Interests: Tribal belts rich in forests/minerals; Forest laws to secure timber and land for British revenue and railways
  3. Cultural Justification: British anthropologists portrayed tribes as "noble savages" needing protection — paternalistic racism
  4. Missionary Access: Allowed missionaries and select officials; cultural penetration with political dominance
Case Study: The Malers (Paharias) of Rajmahal Hills — First Separate Administrative Model:
  • Among first tribes under separate administrative model
  • Cleveland established ring-fence of sepoys around hill tracts to isolate Paharias
  • 1782: Rajmahal Hill Tract withdrawn from ordinary judicial system; special Hill Assembly with tribal reps + British officers, could impose capital punishment
  • Codified as Regulation I of 1796, later repealed by Regulation I of 1827
  • Area known as Daman-e-Koh — indirect rule through limited tribal autonomy
  • Became prototype for later "special administrative areas"

Key Constitutional Provisions:

  • DPSP: State shall promote with special care educational and economic interests of STs
  • Sixth Schedule: Administration of northeastern states
  • Fifth Five-Year Plan: Introduced Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) for integrated development

9.1 — Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)

What are PVTGs? — Features

General Tribal Features: Primitive traits, distinctive culture, geographical isolation, shyness of contact, backwardness.

Additional Features of PVTGs (over and above general tribal features):
  • Dependency on hunting and gathering for food
  • Pre-agriculture level of technology
  • Zero or Negative growth of population
  • Extremely low level of literacy

5 Criteria for Identifying PVTGs

  1. Forest dependent livelihood
  2. Pre-agriculture level of technology
  3. Stagnant or declining population
  4. Low literacy rate
  5. Subsistence-based economy

Historical Development of the PVTG Category

YearDevelopment
1973Dhebar Commission created Primitive Tribal Groups (PTGs) as a separate category
1975Government of India identified 52 groups as PVTGs
1993Another 23 groups added → total 75 PVTGs out of 705 Scheduled Tribes
2006Government renamed PTGs as PVTGs (Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups)
75 PVTGs spread across 18 States and 1 Union Territory (2011 Census)

Population Data

Key Population Statistics:
  • 2001 Census: approximately 27,68,322
  • 12 PVTGs have population of 50,000; remaining have 1,000 or less
  • Huge variation: Great Andamanese, Onge, Sentinelese (very few individuals) to Toda of Nilgiris (~1,000+)
  • Declining PVTGs: Onge, Andamanese — biggest reason: inbreeding
  • Stagnant: Birhor in central India
  • Largest PVTG: Saharia people of Madhya Pradesh and Rajasthan — population more than 4 lakhs

Literacy Rate

  • Increased from single digit to 30–40% in many PVTGs
  • Female literacy still considerably lower than male
  • Factors for low literacy: Linguistic barriers, social exclusion, lack of cultural sensitivity, discrimination, poor infrastructure

Livelihood

  • Depend on: food gathering, NTFP, hunting, livestock rearing, shifting cultivation, artisan works
  • Forests are their life and livelihood
Forest Rights Act (FRA 2006) — Key Provisions for PVTGs:
  • Section 3(1)(e): Recognizes "rights including community tenures of habitat and habitation for primitive tribal groups and pre-agricultural communities"
  • Section 2(h): Defines 'habitat' as customary habitat in Reserved and Protected Forests of PTGs and pre-agricultural communities

Challenges to PVTG Livelihood

  • Shrinking forests, environmental change, new forest conservation policies
  • 2009: Baiga families moved out of Achanakmar Reserve — loss of livelihood, no proper compensation
  • Kattunayakans (Tamil Nadu): Prevented from collecting honey in Mudumalai Wildlife Sanctuary — harassment from forest guards
  • Exploitation by middlemen — PVTGs unaware of value of their products (honey, tendu leaves)

Health Issues

  • Poverty, lack of safe drinking water, bad sanitary conditions, difficult terrain, malnutrition, poor maternal health
  • Common diseases: Anaemia, Malaria, Gastro-intestinal disorders

Bonded Labour among PVTGs

  • Sahariya (Rajasthan): Work as bonded labour for rich landlords for generations; tricked into loans with exorbitant interest
  • Banned under Bonded Labour (Abolition) Act 1976
  • PVTGs in Odisha: Debt bondage locally called "goti" — despite Orissa Debt Bondage (Abolition and Regulation) Act, 1948

PM-JANMAN Scheme (2023) — For PVTGs

Pradhan Mantri Janjati Adivasi Nyaya Maha Abhiyan (PM-JANMAN):
  • Purpose: Reach the most marginalized and remote PVTGs with saturation of key welfare programmes
  • Ministry of Tribal Affairs implements "Development of Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)"
  • Conservation-Cum-Development (CCD): Annual plans prepared by each state/UT based on need assessment, approved by Project Appraisal Committee
  • Focus areas: housing, roads, drinking water, education, healthcare, livelihood, mobile medical units

7.1 — Denotified Tribes (DNTs)

Historical Background — The Criminal Tribes Act

Criminal Tribes Acts (CTA) — 1871 and 1911:
  • 150–200 communities deemed "Hereditary Criminals" → surveillance, confinement, gross discrimination
  • Part of colonial model: communities that resisted British rule were targeted
  • Criteria: Only "reason to believe" needed (not factual evidence) that community was "addicted" to crime
  • Notion that crime was a genetic trait passed down generations — pseudo-scientific racism
Legal Definition (CTA): "A Criminal Tribe is a gang, tribe or class of people addicted to the systematic commission of Non-Bailable Offenses and with reason to believe, a local Government could notify using the local gazette that an entire tribe, gang or class of people were criminals."

Repeal and Aftermath

  • CTA repealed in 1952 on recommendation of All India Criminal Tribes Inquiry Committee (1949)
  • 'Criminal Tribes' became 'Denotified Tribes'
  • CTA replaced by Habitual Offenders Act, 1951
  • Communities celebrate August 31 as Independence Day (denotification date), but stigma remains

Classification — Scattered Across SC/ST/OBC

Some listed as Scheduled Tribes, others as Scheduled Castes, some as OBCs. Example of the same community across states:
  • ST in Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Bihar
  • SC in Karnataka, Delhi, Rajasthan
  • OBC in Uttar Pradesh
  • Denotified Tribe in Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu
This inconsistency prevents effective welfare delivery.

Challenges Faced by DNTs

#ChallengeDetails
1Social StigmaLabelled "born criminals"; stigma not completely abolished by 1952 Act; police harassment continues
2Economic HardshipTraditional occupations (snake charming, street acrobatics, performing with animals) notified as criminal activity
3Large-scale ExclusionsSpread among SC/ST/OBC but many not classified anywhere; severe lack of data
4Poor Grievance RedressalNo permanent commission unlike NCSC/NCST
5Lack of Uniform ApproachAnomalies in identification from state to state — same community classified differently
6Declining PopulationResult of accumulated disadvantages — health, nutrition, marginalisation

Government Schemes for DNTs

#SchemePurpose
1Dr. Ambedkar Pre-matric and Post-Matric ScholarshipScholarships for DNT Boys and Girls at school and college level
2Nanaji Deshmukh Scheme of Construction of HostelsHostel facilities for DNT Boys and Girls for educational support
3Assistance for Skill Development of OBCs/DNTs/EBCsVocational and skill training to improve livelihood options

Key Committees on DNTs

Renke Commission / Idate Commission: Compiled data on 809 Nomadic and 334 Denotified Tribes. Recommended:
  • Establishment of a Permanent Commission for DNTs
  • Repeal of Habitual Offenders Act, 1952
  • Sub-quotas within SC, ST, OBC categories for DNTs
  • Socio-economic survey through reputed social science institutes (basic census data missing)
  • Centre should carve sub-categories: DNT-SC, DNT-ST, and DNT-OBC with dedicated sub-quota
Conclusion: DNTs continue to face stigma, exclusion, and economic hardship despite repeal of Criminal Tribes Act. True rehabilitation demands de-stigmatisation, accurate identification, targeted welfare measures, a permanent commission, and repeal of the Habitual Offenders Act.

9.1 — Role of NGOs / Voluntary Agencies in Tribal Development

Historical Evolution

  • Ancient times: Rishis and sannyasins — medicine, spirituality, ethical living
  • British period: Christian missionaries — education, healthcare, social reform in tribal areas
  • Post-independence: Secular social organisations and NGOs
Definition of NGO (World Bank): "Private organizations that pursue activities to relieve suffering, promote the interests of the poor, protect the environment, provide basic social services, or undertake community development." Non-profit groups functioning independently of government; also called civil societies.

9.2 — Role of Anthropology in Tribal and Rural Development

M.N. Srinivas (Simla Seminar, 1972): "I find today sharply increased awareness of the importance and urgency of associating sociologists and anthropologists in the solution of the many complex and baffling problems which confront the country today."

Why Anthropological Studies Help — Methodological Edge

MethodContribution
Cultural RelativismNeutral perspective without preconceived notions; objective work without ethnocentric bias
Field Work / Participant ObservationInsider perspective; become part of the society; authentic data
Holistic StudiesEvery aspect analyzed from multiple dimensions simultaneously
Integrated StudiesUnderstand how economic, social, cultural elements are interconnected
Historical Marginalization of Anthropologists: Anthropologists' importance first recognised by Court of Directors in 1807. Hutton, S.C. Roy, D.N. Majumdar, Elwin highlighted negative impacts of contact — labelled "no changers," "revivalists," "isolationists." Post-independence: policies did not include anthropologists → benefits did not reach tribals → led to Applied Anthropology.
Applied Anthropology — "Anthropology put to use": Sometimes called the "fifth sub-field". Application of methods and theory to analysis and solution of practical problems in development, health, conflict resolution.

Key Anthropological Contributions to Tribal Development

1. Nature-Man-Spirit Complex (L.P. Vidyarthi): Helps understand equilibrium between ecology, social organization, and superhuman component. Helps plan tribal rehabilitation with least resistance. Essential for culturally sensitive development planning.
2. Tribe-Caste Continuum: Shows why it is difficult to define tribes with specific criteria. Demonstrates how tribal and non-tribal communities have influenced each other over centuries — basis for nuanced policy design.
3. Integrationist Approach (Verrier Elwin): 'A Philosophy for NEFA', 1957 (foreword by Nehru). Tribes not forced to give up tribal identity; choice left to tribals themselves. Became India's official tribal policy framework.
4. Development of PVTG Category (1973/2006): Anthropologists contributed through in-depth understanding of socio-economic marginalization, highlighting vulnerabilities, advocating for targeted policies. Dhebar Commission's work drew on anthropological studies.
5. Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP): Expert Committee under Ministry of Education and Social Welfare, 1972. Adopted for first time in Fifth Five-Year Plan. Anthropologists highlighted socio-economic and cultural distinctiveness justifying dedicated plan resources.
6. Criminal Tribes Act Repeal & DNTs: CTA 1924 repealed by Criminal Tribes Laws (Repeal) Act, 1952. Anthropologists' role: provided understanding of history, culture, social dynamics; advocated DNTs as citizens with equal rights, not hereditary criminals.
7. Forest Rights — B.K. Roy Burman Committee on Forests and Tribals (1982): Recognised traditional forest-dwelling communities had deep understanding of forests. Advocated community-based forest management and tribal rights over forest resources. Helped shape the landmark Forest Rights Act of 2006.
8. Xaxa Committee Report (2014) — Virginius Xaxa (TISS): High-level committee; submitted to PMO in May 2014. Six thematic issues: (1) Livelihoods and employment status; (2) Education; (3) Health; (4) Land alienation; (5) Displacement and enforced migration; (6) Legal/constitutional issues; delivery of public goods and services. Key findings: Rights of tribals to refuse land acquisition; empowerment of gram sabha and tribal advisory council; rights of tribes in Scheduled Areas to mine minerals. Emphasized tribal groups have fallen behind over last 60 years despite India's rapid expansion.
9. Development of Tribal Health: Expert Committee Report: "Tribal Health in India — Bridging the Gap and a Roadmap for the Future" (first of its kind). Recommendations: Annual budget equal to 2.5% GDP per capita allocated for tribal healthcare (~INR 2500 per tribal in 2015–16); new entities: Tribal Health Council and Directorate for Tribal Health at state and union levels; government focus 70% of resources on primary care; adequate human resources and infrastructure.
10. Safeguarding Endangered Languages: Linguistic research and documentation; raising awareness about language endangerment; advocating for language revitalization programs — Anthropologists central to Endangered Languages documentation projects.

9.3 — Communalism

Definition: "An ideology based on the belief that society is divided into religious communities whose interests differ and are opposed to each other and hence antagonistic in nature" (1979). Antagonism practiced by people of one community against people of another community and religion.

Factors Responsible for Communalism

1. Divisive Politics: Religion or cultural identity used to mobilize support; "majority-minority divide" consolidated for votes. Weakens secular democracy.
2. Politics of Appeasement — Shah Bano Case (1985–86):
  • Supreme Court upheld Shah Bano's right to post-divorce maintenance under secular law
  • Rajiv Gandhi government reversed verdict through Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986
  • Textbook case of vote-bank politics and state surrender to religious orthodoxy
3. Shift to Religious Mobilisation:
  • Rath Yatra (1990): Large religious procession from Gujarat to Ayodhya
  • By 1992: Demolition of Babri Masjid — violence erupted from both sides
  • Reflected fragility of communal balance
4. Economic Causes:
  • UN MPI (2023): One in three Muslims in India is multidimensionally poor
  • Economic competition between Hindu and Muslim traders adds to distrust
  • Continued reliance on outdated madrasa education
5. Historical and Structural Factors: Communal tensions reappear in places with past history of conflict. Delays in justice or administrative gaps allow small disputes to grow into full-scale riots.
6. Psychological and Social Dimensions: Sustained by social distance and fear; arises from misunderstanding, lack of communication. Karnataka Anti-Conversion Bill (2021): Christian community protests reflected perception/communication gaps.
7. Role of Media and Social Media:
  • Sensationalist coverage inflames tensions
  • Delhi Riots (2020): Viral videos used to spread anger and misinformation
  • Social media nationalises local conflicts into pan-India communal narratives
8. Communal Violence and Civic Ties — Ashutosh Varshney (2002): Shared civic associations and neighbourhood networks reduce communal violence. Where Hindus and Muslims participate in same trade unions, sport clubs, civic organisations — riots less likely. The Civic Life Argument: Saumitra Jha found Gujarat's medieval port towns with historic Hindu-Muslim trade ties were 25% less likely to experience riots in 2002.

Instances of Communal Violence

EventYearDetails
Partition Riots1947~15 million displaced; close to 1 million lives lost; trauma of divided homeland — worst communal violence in modern India
Anti-Sikh Riots1984After assassination of PM Indira Gandhi; thousands of Sikhs killed; gurdwaras destroyed

Features of Communal Riots (Probe of Last 5 Decades)

  • More politically motivated than fuelled by religion
  • Besides political interests, economic interests play a vigorous part
  • More common in North India than South and East
  • Possibility of recurrence is stronger in towns where riots have already occurred
  • Most take place during religious festivals
  • Use of deadly weapons is on the ascendancy
Overall Assessment — India's Resilience:
  • India: 1.4 billion people of diverse faiths; 99% coexist harmoniously every day
  • India's civilisational philosophy of tolerance reflected in Vedas, Upanishads, Buddha, Mahavira, Sufis, Bhakti saints
  • These incidents are exceptions rather than the rule

Concluding Philosophy on Communalism

India: land of rich diversities — "We're all minorities in India"

"Acceptance of difference rather than mere tolerance" — the great Indian secret

Rig Veda: "Ekam Sat Vipra bahudha vadanti" — "There is only one truth and learned persons call it by many names"
