Unit 6.3 — Problems of Rehabilitation & Resettlement (R&R)

Section Overview
Background & Scale Resettlement vs Rehabilitation 7 Key Issues 4 Case Studies 3 Solutions

Background

  • R&R has been a burning issue in India even before independence.
  • After 1947, India pursued development through large multipurpose river valley projects, thermal power projects, mining, roads, and industrial hubs — requiring large land tracts with consequences of involuntary land acquisition and displacement.
  • No reliable data on the number of Project Affected Persons (PAPs); estimates vary from 50 to 100 million persons.
  • No reliable data on how many have been resettled or rehabilitated.

Resettlement vs Rehabilitation — The Critical Distinction

Dimension Resettlement Rehabilitation
Meaning Moving people from original homes to another place (for dam, mine, factory, sanctuary) Restoring the economic, social, and cultural well-being of displaced people
Core Idea Spatial adjustment — shifting people from one area to another; focus on where to move Holistic restoration — helping them regain livelihood, dignity, and standard of living
Reality Only physical relocation happens despite promises of land-for-land or house-for-house Requires livelihood training, education, healthcare, and participation in planning
What's Lost Livelihoods, culture, and belonging — rarely restored Addresses these losses through the National Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy, 2007
Example Tribals moved from forests to plains lose farming skills and spiritual ties to nature Hirakud Dam Project (1950s, Odisha) — 22,000 families displaced with little rehab; shaped better policies
How it works Logistical exercise — count families, allot plots, move them Goal: displaced persons live better than before after relocation (NRRP 2007)

Seven Key Issues in R&R

  1. Identification of Land to be Acquired

    Development projects require forest land as well as agriculture land. Government under the law of 'eminent domain' has the absolute right to acquire any private land for public purpose. Mining and hydro-electric projects cannot be shifted due to geological requirements, but a compromise on mining boundary or submergence level could substantially reduce the number of PAPs.

  2. Land Records and Payment of Compensation

    Land is a state subject; records kept by the Patwari of the village, rarely updated (supposed to be updated every 30 years). Difficult to assess real value of land (based on last traded value). Leads to legal claims continuing in courts for decades. Forest dwellers displaced have no land records — making them ineligible for compensation; whatever compensation is paid remains meagre.

  3. Identification of Project Affected Persons (PAPs)

    Major hurdle when: (i) Land owners are not traceable or not residing in the impacted area; (ii) People do not own land but cultivate it on sharing basis or as daily wage earners; (iii) Some have been cultivating forest land for generations but do not have land in their name; (iv) Outsiders buy small pieces of land to claim R&R benefits and enroll as PAPs.

  4. Loss of Livelihoods

    Loss of land = loss of livelihood. Not merely monetary — the style of earning that livelihood is equally important. Land provides economic security even for taking loans; harvested crop used for bartering; cultivation provides food security; traditional wisdom and agricultural skills passed on through generations (no formal training required). Loss of land renders skills and knowledge harnessed over decades redundant in one go.

  5. Loss of Home

    Ecological incompatibility — resettled area in stark contrast to environment they were accustomed to. Non-availability of opportunities for traditional livelihood forces alien occupations (agricultural and industrial labourers). Resettlement in non-familiar environment alienates them from host community — leads to hostility. In many cases, resettled population was not entitled to legal rights over land in the resettled area.

  6. Lack of Stakeholder Engagement, Loss of Dignity and Self-Respect

    PAPs are often illiterate but have traditional wisdom, self-respect, and dignity. Since they do not speak corporate language, they are assumed not wise enough to take decisions about their own lives — opportunity for stakeholder engagement is denied. All decisions made by project authorities and local government imposed on PAPs, assuming it is for their welfare.

  7. Grievance Redressal Mechanism

    In the absence of genuine grievance redressal, PAPs get lost as to whom to approach. Ultimately, PAPs lose trust in the process of land acquisition and R&R.

Case Studies in Rehabilitation

Case Study 1 — Forest Displacement
Baiga and Gond Tribes, Madhya Pradesh (January 2015)

Around 450 Baiga and Gond settlements were relocated from the Kanha Tiger Reserve under a forest conservation and rehabilitation program. Criticized as forced eviction, though the government claimed it was voluntary relocation aimed at providing better facilities and livelihoods outside the reserve.

Case Study 2 — Land Rights Denial
Adivasi Groups, Mysuru District, Karnataka (2020)

Adivasi groups protested delays in rehabilitation and land title deeds after displacement from forest areas. Concerns raised: non-declaration of tribal hamlets as revenue villages, lack of land allotment under Mastigudi rehabilitation, and diversion of funds by dominant groups. Around 210 families, mainly Jenu Kuruba and Soliga tribes, were affected. Appeals made to the National Commission for Scheduled Tribes for justice.

Case Study 3 — Bonded Labour Rehabilitation
Chenchu Tribals (PVTG), Karimnagar, Telangana (2018–2020)

In 2018, 45 Chenchu tribals (a PVTG) were rescued from bonded labour at a canal irrigation site. Two years later, 12 survivors received ₹1.5 lakh each under the Centrally Sponsored Rehabilitation Scheme for Bonded Labour (1978, revised 2016). Highlights how Article 23 and the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976 protect tribal workers, though delayed compensation and weak enforcement remain persistent issues.

Case Study 4 — Child Labour Exploitation
Paniya, Kattunayakan, and Adiya Tribes, Nilambur, Kerala (2023)

Reports revealed tribal children aged 8–15, mainly from Paniya, Kattunayakan, and Adiya tribes, were employed in areca nut and banana plantations near Kakkadampoyil. Earned ₹300–₹500/day while poverty, alcoholism, and lack of educational follow-up led to school dropouts — violating Articles 23 and 24. The banned CPI (Maoist) expressed solidarity with workers of the Plantation Corporation of Kerala (PCK), alleging forced overtime and wage neglect.

Solutions to R&R Problems

Solutions for Rehabilitation

Core principle: "Restoration of livelihood is not merely in monetary terms alone. People aspire to restore the process of earning that livelihood. They need much empathy rather than sympathy."
Solution Description Example / Evidence
1. Land for Land Best and obvious first choice — assures restoration of income in original lifestyle Rourkela Steel Plant Project (1956) — 30 villages (Oraon, Kharia, Bhumij tribes) displaced; resettled within 5 km of original villages with land and houses; helped maintain community networks
2. Self-Employment Schemes Most viable option; common schemes: carpet weaving, masala grinding, bamboo crafting, dairy, poultry, carpentry, goat rearing, apiculture, sericulture, pisciculture, handicraft Viable due to demand from project-dependent population influx; however, lack of education and skills may affect viability
3. Employment in the Project Addresses restoration of previous standard of living + provides significant improvement Ho community in Singhbhum, Jharkhand — employed in the Zinc Factory; brought different clans together, created social unity, improved economic condition; however, some traditions changed (restrictions on Maghe festival)

Solutions for Resettlement

PAPs have often rejected fully developed resettlement colonies, sometimes even with built-up houses. Key factors impacting acceptance of resettlement colony:

  • Access to livelihood opportunities & market
  • Access to health and education facilities
  • Availability of space for storing fodder, grains, shelter for domestic animals
  • Space for expanding the house as family expands
  • Availability of community land for pasture, fodder, village pond, wells, trees, celebrating festivals
Key Recommendation: PAPs should be given opportunities to settle at the place of their choice other than the resettlement colony.

Fifth Schedule — Article 244(1): "Scheduled areas" = areas which the President declares as scheduled areas. The Union Government has executive powers to give directions to states regarding administration of Scheduled Areas.

  2. The Scheduled Tribes and Other Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006

Identification of Scheduled Tribes — Lokur Committee (1965)

The Constitution did not define criteria for recognition of STs; hence the Lokur Committee was set up.

Five Lokur Criteria for Scheduled Tribe Status
1 Primitive traits
2 Distinctive culture
3 Geographical isolation
4 Shyness of contact with the larger community
5 General backwardness
Hrusikesh Panda Task Force (2014) — Criticisms of Lokur Criteria:
  • Criteria found obsolete, rigid, and condescending
  • Terms like "primitive" and "shy" fail to reflect present realities
  • Lack of anthropological and sociological expertise in decision-making bodies
  • Inconsistent historical records (from "tribal animists" in 1901 to "primitive tribes" in 1931)
  • Administrative inconsistencies: differing tribal names across states, exclusions due to state bifurcation or forced migration, delays in updating lists

Proposed New Criteria — Panda Committee (2014 Task Force)

Criterion Description
1 Socio-economic and educational backwardness relative to the state's population
2 Historical geographical isolation, whether continuing or not
3 Distinct language or dialect
4 Core cultural identity (life-cycle rituals, art, folklore, marriage customs)
5 Endogamy, or marriage largely within the community

(These are to be assessed holistically, not individually.)

Procedure for Inclusion in ST List

  1. Proposal from State Government/UT
  2. Review by Ministry of Tribal Affairs (MoTA)
  3. Evaluation by Registrar General of India (RGI)
  4. Recommendation by National Commission for Scheduled Tribes (NCST)
  5. Cabinet approval
  6. Presidential assent to amend the Constitution (ST Order, 1950)

Six Traditional Disabilities of Scheduled Castes

# Disability Description & Case Study
1 No Physical Contact Did not participate in village meetings and worship. Lived in a separate hamlet. Children did not attend school or play with higher-caste children. Had to play drums to pass through village streets.
2 Ban on Use of Common Wells and Tanks Not allowed to use common village wells and tanks. Had separate wells/ponds or depended on courtesy of others.

3 Prohibited from Entering the Temple Not allowed to enter temple for offering worship. Not supposed to hear religious discourses, offer prayer, or study religious texts.
4 Did Not Receive Service from Other Castes Priests, artisan castes, dhobi (washermen), and dom (death ritual performers) did not render their services to untouchable castes.
5 Non-acceptance of Cooked Food Food cooked by SCs not accepted by higher castes. Water from the hands of SCs also not acceptable.

Case Study (Uttarakhand, 2022): In May 2022, students from a government school in Sukhidhang village, Champawat district boycotted midday meals cooked by Sunita, a Dalit bhojan mata. Despite reinstatement after filing case under SC/ST Act, some students continued the boycott.
6 Status Incongruence and Dependence Proneness Persistence of stigma of untouchability and the Constitutional status of SCs were not congruent. Dependent upon others for livelihood and survival.

Political Discrimination — Case Studies

Case Study — Political Discrimination
Caste Discrimination Against Dalit Panchayat Chief, Tamil Nadu (October 2020)

Rajeshwari Saravana Kumar, Dalit panchayat president of Therku Thittai Panchayat, Cuddalore district, was forced to sit on the floor during panchayat meetings while caste Hindu members sat on chairs. The Dalit woman panchayat president of Aathupakkam Panchayat, Thiruvallur district, was prevented from hoisting the national flag during Independence Day celebrations. The District Collector intervened and organized a special flag-hoisting ceremony.

Dalit Politics — Two Forms

  1. Militant movements: Bhim Sena — caused more reactionary measures from caste Hindus.
  2. Electoral coalitions: BSP in UP — Dalits always in minority by themselves; can only benefit if they form alliance with dominant caste groups or mainstream parties. Successful Dalit parties: Lok Janshakti Party (Bihar, 2000 — Ram Vilas Paswan) and Viduthalai Puratchi Party (Tamil Nadu, 1989 — Thol. Thirumavalavan).

Constitutional Safeguards for Scheduled Castes

Key SC-specific Articles (different from STs):
  • Article 341 (vs 342 for STs): President may notify "castes, races or tribes or parts of or groups within castes, races or tribes" as SCs. For states, President must also consult with the Governor.
  • Article 366(24) (vs 366(25) for STs): Definition of Scheduled Castes.
  • Article 17: Abolition of untouchability and prohibition of any such practices.

The Six "Big Solutions" to End Untouchability

  1. Banish untouchability in educational institutions (discrimination-free atmosphere)
  2. Land for rural Dalit families (implementing pledge of land reforms, pattas to all landless Dalits, prevent grabbing of Dalit-owned land)
  3. Provide viable assets in cities (make Dalits owners of viable assets they work on, like rickshaws)
  4. Ensure food security (include all Dalit families in Antyodaya Anna Yojana/Priority households under NFSA 2013)
  5. Implement the Sukhdeo Thorat Committee Report (constitutionally guaranteed safeguards to prevent hostility against students of reserved category)
  6. Rehabilitate manual scavengers (Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers and their Rehabilitation Act, 2013 has not been enough)

Unit 7.1 — Other Backward Classes (OBCs)

Definition and Scope

  • OBCs are the middle band of castes and communities situated above the SCs and STs, excluding prominently well-advantaged groups at the top.
  • Described as "socially and educationally backward classes" under the Constitution.
  • Entitled to 27% reservations in public sector employment and higher education.
  • OBC list maintained by the Indian Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment — dynamic list.
  • OBCs found to comprise 52% of the country's population (Mandal Commission, 1980); shrunk to 41% by 2006 (National Sample Survey).

Backward Classes Commissions — Three Key Bodies

Commission Year Key Recommendation Outcome
Kalekar Commission (First Backward Classes Commission) 1955 Recommended "caste as the criteria" to determine backwardness Report was not accepted by the government
Mandal Commission (Second Backward Classes Commission) Report 1980 / Implemented 1990 Recommended 27% reservation (due to legal constraint that total reservation should not exceed 50%) Implemented in 1990; OBCs comprise 52% of population; massive political impact
Rohini Commission (Sub-Categorisation) 2017 (constituted by President Ram Nath Kovind) Sub-categorization of OBCs under Article 340; headed by Delhi HC's former CJ G. Rohini; fixed quota of 8–10% of 27% for ~1900 castes Findings: Out of 2,633 OBCs, 1900 castes received no/minimal benefits; only 10 sub-castes received 25% of OBC advantages

Rohini Commission — Detailed Findings

  • Out of 2,633 OBCs on the central list, nearly 1,900 castes have not benefited proportionately.
  • Half of these 1,900 castes have received no benefit at all; the other half received less than 3% of OBC quota.
  • Only 10 sub-castes have received 25% of the advantages from OBC reservations.
  • Profession-based castes like Kalaigars (polish tins) and Sikligars and Saranias (sharpen knives) received almost no benefits.
  • Panel suggestion: Fixed quota of 8 to 10% of total OBC quota (27%) for around 1,900 castes.

The Creamy Layer

What is the Creamy Layer? The generally small section of people that occupies the top of a marginalized community's socioeconomic hierarchy. The creamy layer test specifies that a candidate must be below a certain income ceiling in order to avail reservation in government jobs and educational institutions.
Aspect Details
Origin Supreme Court introduced the concept in 1993 through the Indira Sawhney case: "Exclusion of such socially advanced members will make the 'class' a truly backward class."
Applies to OBCs only — NOT Dalits and Adivasis (acknowledged as the country's most backward communities)
Original basis Reservation for Dalits, Adivasis, and OBCs originally had no income criteria — sole basis was caste
Counter-argument Delhi University (July 2018) did not manage to fill its reserved category seats — placing further curbs will serve no purpose other than more valuable seats going unfilled

OBCs Among Non-Hindu Communities

Since the caste system has been replicated by Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, and Buddhists, their backward sections have also been included in the OBC list under the Mandal Commission. Eg: Mappilas of Kerala (Muslims).

Muslim Stratification — Three Classes

Class Name Origin

Commoners Ajlaf "Commoners"; said to be converted from Hindu low castes

Hindu-Muslim OBC Anomaly: Communities like Darzis (tailors), Dhunias (cotton carders), and Julahas (weavers) — Muslims from these groups get reservation in central OBC list, but Hindus from the same groups have been left out. Rohini Commission has compiled data on these religiously restricted entries and is likely to recommend removal of the anomaly.
Social Movements to Abolish Casteism in Muslim Society (1990s):
  • All India Backward Muslim Morcha — Dr. Ejaz Ali
  • All India Pasmanda Muslims Mahaj — Ali Anwar from Bihar
  • All India Muslim OBC Organisation — Shabbir Ansari from Maharashtra
Case Study — Caste Among Christians
Persistence of Caste System among Indian Christians
  • Kerala: Syrian Christians practiced caste-like purity rules, including ritual baths after contact with Dalits. Intermarriage between Knanaya, Nasrani, and Dalit Christians remains rare.
  • Goa: Portuguese assigned surnames to suppress caste, but divisions persisted. Converts from Brahmin/merchant castes became Bamonns and Chardos; lower castes labelled Sudirs.
  • Tamil Nadu: Communities like Paravar, Nadar, and Adi Dravida Christians retain strong caste identities. Christian Nadars prefer marrying Hindu Nadars over Christians of lower caste status.
  • North India: Dalit Christians descended from Chuhra and Chamar converts face occupational segregation; referred to as "New Christians" (derogatorily). Report: separate seating, restricted church entry, exclusion from priesthood.

Debate on Non-Hindu Scheduled Castes

  • January 2020: Supreme Court agreed to examine a petition seeking "religion-neutral" SC reservations.
  • The plea challenges Paragraph 3 of the Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order, 1950, which limits SC status to Dalits belonging to Hindu, Sikh, or Buddhist faiths — thereby excluding Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims.
  • Petition argues this violates Articles 14, 15, and 25.
  • Justice Ranganath Misra Commission (2007) and National Commission for Minorities Report by Satish Deshpande and Geetika Bapna (2008) — both recommended extension of SC benefits irrespective of religion.
  • 2022: Union Government constituted a three-member Commission headed by former CJ K.G. Balakrishnan to re-examine the issue of SC status for Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims.

Unit 7.2 — Social Change During Pre-Independence Period

Section Overview
Definitions of Social Change BK Roy Burman Classification Historical Pace 5 Causes Surajit Sinha's 5 Movements Economy / Polity / Society Peasantization KS Singh's 2 Phases

Definitions of Social Change

Anderson and Parker: "Social change involves alteration in the structure and functioning of societal forms or processes themselves."
Surinder Jodhka: Social Change refers to the change in values, institutions, and practices.

Tribal societies, like other societies, have undergone change. Colonial transformation of tribal society is an important area of study in Anthropology. The main theme: disturbance of the traditional tribal socio-economic system.

B.K. Roy Burman's Classification (1970)

B.K. Roy Burman (1970) classified tribes based on their orientation towards Hindu society:
Type Orientation Examples
Type 1 Tribes incorporated in the Hindu society Bhil, Bhumij
Type 2 Tribes positively oriented towards the Hindu society Santhal, Oraon
Type 3 Tribes negatively oriented towards the Hindu society Mizo, Naga
Type 4 Tribes indifferent towards the Hindu society Tribal communities from Arunachal

Note: Roy Burman himself later disassociated from this approach. Useful for pre-colonial/colonial context but not for understanding social change.

L.P. Vidyarthi and S.C. Dube: To understand the colonial and post-colonial situation, classified tribes based on different levels and stages of acculturation (assimilation towards the dominant culture, typically Hindu peasantry in different regions).

Historical Pace of Social Change

Period Pace of Change
Ancient Times No social change
Medieval India Less social change
Colonial Rule More social change
Post-Independence Rapid social change

Five Causes of Social Change

  1. Introduction of outsiders in tribal areas
  2. Extension of state authority in tribal areas
  3. Commercialisation of agriculture
  4. Hinduisation and Christianity (Evangelisation)
  5. Ethnic revolts and tribal movements

Surajit Sinha (1972) — Five Types of Tribal Movements

Surajit Sinha (1972) identified five types of tribal movements as causes of social change:
# Type Period / Examples
1 Ethnic Rebellious Movements during British rule 18th–19th centuries: Birsa movement (Mundas), Kol rebellion (1832), Santhal Rebellion (1855–56, Bihar — led by Sido and Kanhu against zamindars and moneylenders; later turned anti-British), Naga rebellion (1880s)
2 Reform Movements — emulating Hindu caste patterns Bhagat movement (Oraon), Vaishnavite movement (Bhumij), Kehr movement (Santhals)
3 Political Movements for tribal states within Indian Union (post-Independence) Jharkhand movement in Chotanagpur and Orissa, Hill states movement in Assam and MP
4 Secessionist Movements Naga and Mizo movements
5 Agrarian Unrest Movements Naxalbari movement (1967), Birsadal movement (1968–69)

Social Changes in Economy (Pre-Independence)

Non-Monetised to Monetised Economy

  • Tribal markets declined; traditional barter exchange died away.
  • Reciprocity, redistribution, and socio-cultural exchange were the bases of the traditional tribal economy.
  • Some evidence that sections of tribes took to commodity production on a limited scale (K.S. Singh).

Peasantization and Depeasantization (K.S. Singh)

K.S. Singh — Two Simultaneous Processes (operated simultaneously):
  • Peasantization: Several tribal communities who were not peasants were coming into the fold of peasantry.
  • Depeasantization: A number of tribal people after losing their land were entering a different economic system. Small and marginal farmers pushed out of the agricultural sector — this process is increasing.
  • Substantial sections from Middle India — Santhals, Oraon, and Munda — had to migrate to the tea plantations of Assam and adjoining areas; others became industrial labor, share-croppers, etc.

Social Changes in Polity (Pre-Independence)

Customary law of tribal communities was replaced by the new law of the centralized state power during British rule. The new law operated through its administrative and judicial organs and helped the process of Hinduisation to some extent.

Social Changes — Social Dimension (Pre-Independence)

Ethnic Conflicts

Due to prolonged relative isolation, tribal communities developed their own social structure and mechanism of social control. They reacted to colonial changes more strongly and often violently than other populations. Most tribal movements could be covered under the headings: 'Anti-Colonial', 'Anti-Diku', and 'Pro-Ethnic'.

K.S. Singh — Two Phases of Ethnic Conflicts:

Phase 1 (1795–1860) — Rise, expansion, and establishment of British empire:
  • Chuar rebellion (1795–1800)
  • Kol and Bhumij insurrections
  • Resistance by Gond zamindars in 1819 and 1842
  • Khond resistance of the mid-1830s
  • Santhal insurrection (1855–59)
Phase 2 (1860–1920) — Intensive phase of colonialism (deeper penetration by merchant capital, higher incidence of rent):
  • Munda-Oraon Sardar Movement (1869–1895) — led by Birsa Munda
  • Tana Bhagat Movement (1895–1901)
  • Bhil's movement — led by Govind Giri

Sanskritization

Tribal society moving closer to the peasant/caste stratified system strengthened the role of Sanskritization. Some tribes of central India unitedly moved towards the Varna system and laid claim over Kshatriya status (Kshatriazation — the easiest and most popular process). Example: Santhals and the Gonds followed Sanskritization model.

Kinship Breakdown

Breakdown of kinship relations due to alienation from land and migration. Adivasi communities depend on kin relationships for access to information, assistance during health problems, child care, economic assistance, and social support. Hardships of displacement exacerbate the gendered burden of morbidities and mortality on Adivasi women.

Religion

  • Indigenous religious beliefs such as Animism, sacredness of sacred groves underwent dilution.
  • Sanskritization and conversions into Christianity were common.
  • Christianity was perhaps the earliest agency of welfare in tribal areas.
  • The modernizing role of Christian missions is an important feature of social change in tribal areas where it had deep impact.
  • Close link between Christianity and Colonialism — values and institutions of affected tribal communities wilted under Christian impact.
  • The fate of 'Youth Dormitories' — very popular tribal institution — declined (missionaries wanted churches to be the only places of congregation).
  • At the same time, Christianity gave a new sense of self-respect to the tribal peasants and sought to create a separate identity for them.
Colonial Transformation Summary: What began as limited contact turned into widespread economic dependence, displacement, and acculturation. The shift from self-sufficient tribal systems to monetized, stratified, and state-controlled societies marked one of the most significant phases of social change in India's tribal history.

Social Changes in the Post-Colonial Period

Section Overview

Chaudhary (1990) — Key Observations

Chaudhary (1990) in the introduction to 'Tribal Transformation in India' (5 Vols.) highlighted the following:
  1. Transformation from peripheral existence to the awareness of civic rights
  2. The development has not been up to expectations:
    • Census 2011: Literacy rate of STs was 59% whereas overall literacy rate was 73%
    • NFHS-4 (2015–16): Under-5 mortality among tribal population was 57.2 per 1000 live births vs 38.5 among others; IMR 44.4 per 1000 vs others at 32.1
  3. Elite class has emerged through present democratic institutions and has cornered the developmental benefits:
    • Eg: According to Mr. Prabhu, dominant groups like Valmiki and the Naika communities cornered all the developmental schemes. Primitive tribes like Jenu Kuruba and Soligas are left in the lurch.

People of India Project (Anthropological Survey of India) — Findings

  • Many tribes participated in the country in various ways — became entrepreneurs, businessmen, teachers, political leaders.
  • Educational levels doubled from 8.53% to 16.35% (1961 to 1981).
  • Literacy rate for STs improved from 47.1% in 2001 to 59% in 2011 (Census).
  • Tribes avail health and medical facilities; decline in death rate and rise in birth rate.
  • Coverage of mass media (radio and television) in tribal areas has increased.

Case Studies — Post-Colonial Change by Community Type

Case Study — Hunter-Gatherers
Jenu Kuruba, Bhil, Bhilala, Chenchu

Losing control over means of subsistence from early colonial rule; forced to other survival strategies.

  • Jenu Kuruba: With vast traditional forest knowledge, now helping the forest department in rearing elephants and collection of forest produce.
  • Bhaviskar (1995): Traced criminality among the Bhil and the Bhilala of Jhabua district, MP, to the growing control of the State over forest resources and degradation of cultivable lands.
  • Chenchu resettlement scheme failed — as a result, some have taken to crime in certain areas.
Case Study — Artisans
Agarias, Asurs, Dhurva (Dhokda Crafts)
  • Gadgil and Guha (1992): Pointed out how artisan activities like Blacksmithing are declining and the Agarias and Asurs took up agriculture.
  • Availability of raw materials for crafts like basketry; high price charged for bamboo by the forest department contributed to impoverishment of tribals.
  • Study on Dhokda crafts among the Dhurva of MP: Deficiencies in government schemes related to training, supply of tool-kits, and marketing.
Case Study — Shifting Cultivators
Jhum Cultivators

Hit hard due to government forest policies. Alternative activities like animal husbandry and plantation started gaining prominence. A class of landless agricultural workers has emerged as a result of the Jhum lands being taken over by the forest department. These people are now wage earners in the plantations.

Case Study — Settled Agriculturists
Santhals (Manapatra's study)

Tribals started adopting new methods like line sowing and manure pits. Manapatra's study on the Santhals points out that out of eight Santhal sub-groups, the Murmu (priest), the Mardi, and the Tudu — who are most influential and landed — have gained the most through the planning process, depriving the other groups. Classic case of elite capture.

Case Study — Panchayats
Gond — Modern Democratic Institutions (People of India study)

Statutory panchayats are active in village politics and close to power structure. Traditional panchayats have restricted their activities to deciding civil disputes like marital disputes. So both coexist now — no clash observed between the traditional Panchayat and the statutory panchayats.

Post-Independence Summary: Development efforts brought both progress and disparity. While education, health, and political participation improved, uneven implementation and elite capture meant that many vulnerable groups remained marginalized — social change has been uneven and incomplete.

Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) — Detailed Analysis

Background

UN Dhebar Commission (1961): TSP was first presented as the magic solution by the Dhebar Commission in response to massive tribal unrest in central-eastern India due to land alienation, poverty, indebtedness, and exploitation by non-tribal dominant groups.
BD Sharma: Asserted that the Fifth Schedule and the Tribal Subplan are two co-equal pillars of tribal empowerment.
TSP Budget Gap — Case Study

An allocation of ₹89,265.12 crore in the Union Budget accounts for only 2.26% of the total expenditure — far below the mandated 8.6% allocation needed for tribal welfare.

Planning Commission 2013 — Seven Implementation Failures of TSP

Planning Commission 2013 evaluation of TSP effectiveness — Prominent reasons for underperformance:
  1. Lack of Unified Planning, Implementation, and Monitoring Mechanism

    Multiple ministries and departments execute tribal welfare schemes independently without a single nodal agency to coordinate or monitor progress. This leads to duplication of efforts, fragmented accountability, and delays in project execution.

  2. Weak Convergence between Central and State Plan TSP Funds

    Absence of a synchronized framework to integrate Central and State-level TSP allocations has resulted in overlapping projects and inefficient fund utilization. Many states have treated TSP as an accounting formality, diverting funds to general schemes without specific tribal focus.

  3. Scattered Financial Resources and Fragmented Utilization

    Tribal welfare funds are often thinly spread across numerous small projects, reducing their transformative potential. Short-term or symbolic initiatives dominate, failing to generate measurable improvements in income, literacy, or health outcomes.

  4. Absence of Location-Specific Perspective Planning

    The TSP lacks context-sensitive micro-planning that accounts for the ecological, cultural, and socio-economic diversity of tribal regions. Uniform program designs imposed on heterogeneous tribal areas, ignoring the specific needs of PVTGs, forest-dependent communities, or pastoral tribes.

  5. Lack of Systematic Gap Analysis

    No comprehensive baseline assessment is conducted to identify development deficits between tribal and non-tribal populations. TSP allocations are not evidence-based and priorities remain misaligned with ground realities.

  6. Weakening of Dedicated Tribal Institutions

    Weakening of institutions specifically meant for delivery of goods and services: Integrated Tribal Development Agency (ITDA)/Integrated Tribal Development Projects/Tribal Research Institutes (TRI) and other Micro Projects.

  7. Misuse of TSP Funds

    Several instances where TSP funds are actually used in forcing tribal displacement from government projects or sites for mineral extraction. A major share also allocated in building highways used by mining companies to transport minerals from tribal areas. Worst cases: purchasing choppers and repairing jail buildings in Jharkhand.

Planning Commission Evaluation Conclusion: Despite significant financial allocations, ineffective coordination, poor convergence, and lack of localized planning have limited the TSP's impact — calling for institutional reform, data-driven planning, and greater accountability in tribal welfare governance.

General Way Forward

  • Involve all stakeholders — administrators, anthropologists, and most importantly the tribals themselves — to adopt a felt-needs approach.
  • As Verrier Elwin urged: Tribal development must let communities grow "according to their own genius."
  • As L.P. Vidyarthi reminded: It should balance material progress with cultural happiness, ensuring empowerment through culturally sensitive, participatory, and rights-based approaches.
