## 1. Key Concepts

### 1.1 Sex vs Gender

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Sex** | The biological and physiological characteristics (chromosomes, hormones, reproductive organs) that define males and females. It is a binary, biological category. |
| **Gender** | The socially constructed roles, behaviours, expectations, and norms that society assigns to individuals based on their sex. It **varies across cultures and time**. |

- **Key insight:** Gender is not fixed — it is constructed through socialisation. As **Simone de Beauvoir** wrote: *"One is not born, but rather becomes a woman."*
- Because gender is constructed, it can be **reconstructed** through transformation at the levels of: individual consciousness, family dynamics, community norms, institutional practices, and state policy.

### 1.2 Gender Socialisation
- The **lifelong process** through which individuals learn gender-appropriate roles, norms, and behaviours — through family, school, peers, and media.
- Creates and reproduces gendered hierarchies across generations.

### 1.3 Patriarchy
- A **social system** where men hold primary power and authority across all major institutions — family, economy, religion, and polity.
- **Operates at multiple levels:** household (division of labour, resource control), community (norms, honour), economy (wage gaps, property rights), and state (laws, representation).
- Harms men too — by enforcing rigid, often **toxic standards of masculinity** that suppress emotion and restrict caring relationships.

### 1.4 Key Conceptual Distinctions

| Concept | Definition | Focus | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gender Equality** | Equal rights, responsibilities, and opportunities for all genders | Outcome — same treatment for all | Equal pay for equal work |
| **Gender Equity** | Fairness that recognises different starting points and provides differential support | Process — targeted intervention to level the field | Providing maternity leave without career penalty |
| **Women's Empowerment** | Women gaining the capacity, agency, and access to make and act on strategic life choices | Agency — expanding capabilities | SHG training enabling rural women to access credit and lead local governance |

### 1.5 Other Key Concepts
- **Gender Mainstreaming:** Integrating a gender perspective into all stages of policy design, implementation, and evaluation.
- **Gender Budgeting:** Government practice of analysing the budget for its differential impact on women and men; allocating resources specifically to bridge gender gaps.
- **Intersectionality (Kimberlé Crenshaw):** Recognises how multiple identities (gender, caste, class, religion, region, disability) overlap to create unique and **compounded** forms of discrimination. A Dalit woman from rural Bihar faces compounded, not additive, disadvantage — qualitatively different from that of either a Dalit man or a Savarna woman.
- **Patriarchal Bargain:** When women accept and reinforce patriarchal norms in exchange for protection, status, or resources — a strategy of accommodation within an unequal system.

## 2. Current Status and Issues

### 2.1 Population / Demography

#### 2.1.1 Skewed Sex Ratios — Son Preference and "Missing Women"
- **"Missing Women"** — term coined by economist **Amartya Sen** for women absent from the population due to sex-selective abortions, female infanticide, and neglect of girls.
- India's **sex ratio at birth:** ~887–904 girls per 1,000 boys (recent years) — reflects persistent **son meta-preference**.
- **Son meta-preference:** Deep-rooted cultural and economic bias for male children, driving sex-selective abortions, daughter neglect, and skewed sex ratios.

**Economic drivers of son preference:**
- Patrilineal property transfer — sons keep wealth in the family
- Dowry system — daughters seen as financial burdens
- Sons provide old-age security (absent state welfare)
- Males dominate labour-intensive agriculture, especially North India

**Solutions:**
- Strict enforcement of the PCPNDT Act
- Annual monitoring through the Civil Registration System
- Focused district-level intervention in NCR states (Haryana, Punjab, Delhi, Uttarakhand)
- Awareness drives like Beti Bachao Beti Padhao

#### 2.1.2 Prosperity Paradox — Some Prosperous Regions Have Poor Sex Ratio
Economic prosperity does NOT automatically lead to gender equality.

**Reasons why prosperous states (Punjab, Haryana) have adverse sex ratios:**
1. **Strong Son Preference** — cultural preference persists despite prosperity
2. **Misuse of Technology** — greater access to ultrasound enables sex-selective abortions
3. **Inheritance/Property Concerns** — families prefer sons to inherit land/assets
4. **Small Family Norm** — fewer children = more pressure to ensure at least one son
5. **Patriarchal Social Values** — economic development without corresponding change in gender norms

**Prosperity Paradox (Glossary):** Wealthier states/regions have worse child sex ratios — because prosperity provides access to sex-determination technology used to act on son preference.

#### 2.1.3 Tribals Have Better Sex Ratio Than Mainstream Society
Tribal communities exhibit more favourable sex ratios than the rest of society:
1. Relatively lower patriarchy — more egalitarian gender relations
2. Lesser son preference — weaker desire for male child vs. caste-based societies
3. Higher economic role of women — active participation in agriculture, forest activities
4. Weaker influence of dowry — daughters not seen as economic burden
5. Less female foeticide — lower access to sex-selection technologies in remote areas
6. Greater social mobility for women — more freedom in marriage, work, social participation

#### 2.1.4 Early Marriage of Girls
- Mean age at marriage for women: **22.7 years** (2020); rural women: **22.2 years**
- Driven by: social pressure, poverty, lack of education
- **Strong literacy link:** Graduate women have fertility rate of **1.6** vs. **3.1** for illiterate women
- Keeping girls in school is the most powerful intervention

**Early marriage data (women married before 18, 2019–21):**

| State | % Married Before 18 |
|---|---|
| West Bengal | 41.6% |
| Bihar | 40.8% |
| Tripura | 40.1% |
| Jharkhand | 32.2% |

Once married, schooling almost always ends.

#### 2.1.5 Inter-State Demographic Divergence
- **Kerala sex ratio:** 1,084 | **Haryana sex ratio:** 879
- Southern states: low fertility, better women's indicators
- Northern states (Bihar, UP): high fertility, weaker outcomes
- Targeted, state-specific policies are essential

#### 2.1.6 Ageing Female Population and Widowhood Vulnerability
- Women outlive men: **71.4 years vs. 68.6 years** (2016–20); gap will widen further by 2036
- At age 60, women have **19.2 additional years** vs. 17.5 for men
- Rising population of elderly widows needing healthcare, pension, and social support — currently inadequate

### 2.2 Health

**Comparison Table:**

| Indicator | Male | Female | Gap / Remark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Under-5 Mortality Rate (per 1,000), 2020 | 31 | 33 | Girls still slightly more vulnerable |
| Obesity (BMI ≥ 30), NFHS-5 | 4.0% | 6.4% | Women more obese; both rising |

#### 2.2.1 Maternal Mortality
- MMR fell from **254** (2004–06) to **97** (2018–20) per lakh live births — significant progress
- **SDG target:** 70 per lakh live births
- **Lagging states (far above target):**
  - Assam: 195 | Madhya Pradesh: 173 | Uttar Pradesh: 167 | Rajasthan: 113
- Causes: weak antenatal care, neglect of women's health, poor rural health infrastructure
- Solutions: strengthen Primary Health Centres (PHCs), skilled birth attendants, free maternity services in lagging states

#### 2.2.2 Anaemia and Malnutrition
- Women carry a heavy burden of malnutrition → weak pregnancies and low-birth-weight babies
- Only **44.1% of pregnant women** consumed iron-folic acid for 100+ days (2019–21)
- **Anaemia prevalence: 57%** — a crisis
- Causes: poor diet, repeated pregnancies, unequal food distribution within families
- Solutions: nutrition programmes, school meals, iron supplementation drives

#### 2.2.3 Adolescent Reproductive Health
- Adolescent fertility rate (15–19): **11.3%** out of 1,000 girls (2020); rises to **33.9** for illiterate girls
- Causes: early marriage and low awareness
- Solutions: school retention, reproductive health awareness, contraceptive access

#### 2.2.4 Mental Health Neglect
- Women face unique health challenges: caregiving stress, gynaecological conditions, emotional burdens
- Mental health remains under-diagnosed due to: lack of trained counsellors, stigma, absence of gender-sensitive services
- Solutions: training health workers, posting female counsellors at PHCs

#### 2.2.5 Rising Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)
- Female obesity rose from **5.1% (2015–16) to 6.4% (2019–21)** — higher than male obesity (4%)
- Raises risk of heart disease, diabetes, and cancers
- Causes: poor diet, low physical activity, stress
- Solutions: regular check-ups, cancer screening, active-lifestyle promotion

#### 2.2.6 Healthcare Access Barriers
- Women find it harder to access health services due to:
  - Lack of decision-making power
  - Restricted mobility
  - Shortage of female doctors in rural areas
  - Cultural taboos restricting open discussion of female-specific issues
- Solutions: more women health workers, women-only health camps, empowering women in health decisions

#### 2.2.7 Menstrual Hygiene
- Many women and girls (especially rural and low-income) lack: affordable sanitary products, clean toilets, and adequate awareness
- Poor menstrual hygiene leads to: health problems, school absenteeism, reduced dignity and well-being
- Solutions: affordable sanitary products, adequate sanitation facilities, menstrual health awareness through schools and community outreach

### 2.3 Education

**Comparison Table:**

| Indicator | Male | Female | Gap / Remark |
|---|---|---|---|
| Literacy rate (7+ yrs), 2017–18 NSS | 84.7% | 70.3% | Gender gap of 14.4 percentage points |
| Rural literacy gap | 81.5% | 65.0% | Gap of 16.5 pp |
| Urban literacy gap | 92.2% | 82.8% | Gap of 9.4 pp |
| Female share in tertiary education teachers (2021–22) | 58.4% (male) | 41.6% | Glass ceiling at university level |

#### 2.3.1 Adolescent Dropout
- Secondary dropout fell from 21% (2013–14) to **13% (2021–22)** — but many girls still leave during adolescence
- Reasons: onset of menstruation, household work burden, lack of separate toilets, unsafe commutes
- Solutions: girls' toilets in every school, safe transport, free sanitary pads, bridge programmes

#### 2.3.2 Disciplinary Segregation — The Leaky STEM Pipeline
- Girls cluster in Arts, Education, and Nursing; heavily underrepresented in Engineering, IT, Management, and Law
- India produces the highest number of female STEM graduates globally but ranks only **19th in hiring them**

**Women in STEM:**
- 27% of India's STEM workforce (vs. 32% non-STEM)
- Only 22% of AI professionals
- Only 28% of engineering graduates

**Reasons for underrepresentation:**
- Gender stereotypes about technical roles
- Few visible role models
- Self-doubt inculcated through socialisation
- Marriage and family planning interrupting careers
- Dual burden and institutional bias

**Government initiatives for women in STEM:** Vigyan Jyoti, WISE-KIRAN, GATI, KIRAN, CURIE schemes

#### 2.3.3 Digital Gender Divide
- Men exceed women in internet usage in almost every state
- Widest gaps in Bihar, Jharkhand, and Madhya Pradesh (~15–25 percentage points)
- Causes: lack of family permission, fewer personal devices for girls, lower digital skills
- Implications: limits access to online banking, e-commerce, telemedicine, online learning
- Only **16% of Indian women used mobile internet** (GSMA 2019)
- Solutions: free digital literacy programmes, subsidised smartphones, safe internet education for girls

#### 2.3.4 Early Marriage and Dropout Linkage
- Once married, schooling almost always ends
- Solutions: strict enforcement of legal marriage age, scholarships for older girls, family incentives

#### 2.3.5 Vocational and Skill Training Gap
- Female participation in technical and skill-based courses remains low despite higher academic enrolment
- Causes: lack of awareness, family resistance to non-traditional trades, absent women-friendly training centres
- Solutions: women-only ITIs, mobile skill camps, apprenticeship reservations

### 2.4 Economy

**Comparison Table:**

| Indicator | Male | Female | Gap / Remark |
|---|---|---|---|
| LFPR (15+ yrs), 2022–23 | 78.5% | 37.0% | Gap of 41.5 pp; women's LFPR up from 23.3% (2017–18) |
| Worker Population Ratio (all ages, rural) | 54.0% | 30.0% | Rural female WPR ~half of male |
| WPR (all ages, urban) | 55.6% | 18.7% | Urban female WPR ~one-third of male |
| Workers in agriculture (rural) | 49.1% | 76.2% | Women heavily concentrated in low-productivity agri |
| Time spent in unpaid domestic work/day (6+ yrs) | 67 min | 305 min | Women spend 4.5× more |
| % of females (15–59) in unpaid domestic work | 29% | 92% | Stark gendered division |

#### 2.4.1 Female Labour Force Participation — The U-Shaped Curve
- India's Female LFPR has risen from **23.3% (2017–18) to 37% (2022–23)**, recently rising further to **~41.7% (2023–24)**
- Still far below male rate of 78.5% and global average (~47%)
- Urban women's WPR: just **18.7%** vs. 55.6% for urban men

**The U-Shaped Curve (Claudia Goldin, Nobel Prize 2023):**
Economic development and FLFP follow a U-shape:
1. At **low incomes:** women work out of necessity (informal agriculture, household work)
2. As **incomes rise:** women withdraw from work to signal social status and middle-class respectability
3. At **high income and education levels:** women re-enter in skilled professional roles

This explains why India's growing middle class first saw declining FLFP despite rising incomes — a trend now partially reversing.

**Reasons for low FLFPR:**
- Patriarchal norms restricting mobility and work
- Disproportionate unpaid care burden
- Unsafe workplaces and harassment
- Pink-collarisation — women concentrated in low-paying feminised sectors
- Marriage and motherhood creating career breaks
- Strict maternity provisions making firms hesitant to hire women

#### 2.4.2 The Double Burden — Unpaid Care and the Second Shift
- **"Second Shift" (Arlie Hochschild):** Working women come home from paid jobs to a full second shift of cooking, childcare, eldercare, and cleaning — single biggest barrier to full economic participation

**Key data:**
- Women spend **8.4 times more time on unpaid care** than men (NSSO 2019)
- Time Use Survey 2019: women aged 6+ spend **305 minutes/day** on unpaid work vs. men's 67 minutes
- 81% of females do nearly 5 hours of unpaid domestic work daily; **92% of 15–59-year-olds**
- Women's unpaid work, if valued, would contribute **15–17% of India's GDP**
- During COVID-19, women's unpaid care burden rose by **3–4 hours daily**

**ILO's 5R Framework for the Care Economy:**
1. **Recognition** — formal recognition of unpaid care
2. **Reduction** — infrastructure (piped water, clean fuel) to reduce drudgery
3. **Redistribution** — equitable sharing between men, women, and state
4. **Rewarding** — decent pay and protection for paid care workers
5. **Representation** — care workers' voices in policymaking

> **Economic Survey 2024:** 2% of GDP invested in the care sector could generate **11 million jobs**, 70% benefiting women.

#### 2.4.3 Workplace Inequality — Glass Ceiling and Sticky Floor
- **Glass Ceiling** — invisible barrier preventing women from rising to senior leadership; women hold only **15–20% of leadership positions** in Indian companies
- **Sticky Floor** — forces keeping women confined to low-paying, low-mobility jobs (clerical, administrative, care work)
- **Pink-Collarisation** — occupational segregation in "feminised" sectors (teaching, nursing, domestic work) that are systematically undervalued
- **Leaky Pipeline** — women progressively dropping out at each stage of STEM and professional careers

#### 2.4.4 Wage Gap
**Casual labourers' average daily wages (April–June 2023):**
- Rural: Men ₹416, Women ₹287 (~69% of male)
- Urban: Men ₹515, Women ₹333 (~65% of male)
- Disparity exists across all occupations
- Solutions: equal pay enforcement, transparent wage records, stronger labour inspections

#### 2.4.5 Informalisation of Female Work
- Most working women stuck in the informal economy without social security, maternity benefit, or pension
- **71% of rural women workers are self-employed**, but **43.1% work as unpaid helpers** in household enterprises (vs. 11% of men)
- Solutions: bring women into formal records, extend social security, register household enterprises

#### 2.4.6 Feminisation of Agriculture
- **Feminisation of Agriculture:** Rising involvement of women in farming as men migrate to cities for non-farm work

**Drivers:**
- Male outmigration to urban centres
- Agrarian distress
- Decline of rural crafts and alternative livelihoods
- Lack of non-farm employment for women

**The Paradox:** Women contribute **~47% of agricultural labour** but:
- Own very little land (land in men's names; inheritance goes to male heirs)
- Have negligible access to institutional credit
- Lack access to extension services, markets, technology
- Remain vulnerable to harassment in fields
- Bear the dual burden of farming and domestic work

#### 2.4.7 Safety at Workplace
- Discrimination, harassment, and weak grievance redressal force many women into low-paid informal work
- Women in unorganised sectors (domestic, agriculture, construction) remain especially exposed
- Solutions: strict POSH Act enforcement, Internal Complaints Committees, gender sensitisation

#### 2.4.8 Maternity Penalty
- Pregnancy, childbirth, and caregiving force many women to permanently leave jobs
- Reflected in 305 minutes/day of unpaid work
- Solutions: paid maternity leave enforcement, return-to-work programmes, on-site crèches, shared parental leave

#### 2.4.9 Care Infrastructure Deficit
- Working-age women (15–59) spend over six hours daily on unpaid services
- Women NOT in the labour force devote the most time of all
- Solutions: universal crèches, eldercare centres, safe public transport, shared family responsibility

#### 2.4.10 Women and the Gig Economy
**Opportunities:**
- Flexible hours enable caregiving–paid work balance
- Work-from-home options reduce commuting risks
- Digital platforms provide entry to new income streams
- Platforms like Urban Company enable self-employed beauticians to earn more

**Challenges:**
- No minimum wage, EPF, health insurance, or maternity benefits — workers treated as "independent contractors"
- **Algorithmic bias** — AI hiring tools may embed gender discrimination (Amazon's AI tool famously favoured male resumes)
- Digital gender divide — only 16% of Indian women used mobile internet (GSMA 2019)
- Safety risks in mobility roles (delivery, ride-sharing)
- 90% of gig workers had no emergency savings (2024)

**Case Study — Digital Strike (2024):** Women gig workers organised by the Gig and Platform Workers Union (GIPSWU) went into "digital silence" on Diwali to protest high commissions, low pay, unsafe conditions, and absence of social security.

**Comparison Table:**

| State Legislative Assemblies — women MLAs | ~92% avg. | ~8% avg. | Highest in Chhattisgarh/Bihar/WB, lowest in Mizoram/Nagaland |

| Total judiciary (SC+HC) | 781 | 110 | ~14% women |
| Total police strength | ~90.75% | 9.25% | Far below 33% target |

#### 2.5.1 Severe Under-Representation in Legislatures
- Women in Central Council of Ministers: **13.7% (2023)**, peaked at 17.8% in 2015
- Share in 17th Lok Sabha: ~14%
- Speedy implementation of the Women's Reservation Bill is essential

#### 2.5.2 Tokenism in Local Bodies — Sarpanch Pati Syndrome
- Over **1 million women** elected to three-tier local bodies
- **22 states** mandate 50% reservation
- However, many women representatives still function under male relatives — the **"Sarpanch Pati" phenomenon** — converting formal representation into proxy representation

**Structural barriers:**
- High cost of elections disadvantages women
- Parties give women "unwinnable" seats
- Safety concerns and physical violence against women candidates
- Social stigma around women in public life

**Evidence of impact (Esther Duflo):** Studies show that women-led Panchayats invest significantly more in drinking water, sanitation, and health — proving genuine representation improves outcomes for all.

**Global best practices:**
- **Rwanda's Zipper Method** — alternating male/female candidates on party lists → over 60% female parliament (world's highest)
- **Bolivia's Parity Law (2009)** — equal participation in elected bodies → representation rose from 13% to 50% in a decade

#### 2.5.3 Police Force Under-Feminisation
- Women: **14.07%** of civil police and just **4.04% of CAPFs** (January 2022)
- By force: BSF (3.01%), CRPF (3.18%), ITBP (2.91%), NSG (0.68%)
- Overall: women make up only **9.25% of total police strength**, well below the 33% target

### 2.6 Cross-Cutting Issues

#### 2.6.1 Violence and Safety

**NCRB 2022 Data:**

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| Total crimes against women (cases) | 4,45,256 |
| Conviction rate (court disposal) | only 1.61% |

**Structural Violence Against Women:**
Harm caused not by direct assault but by unjust social structures — skewed sex ratios, maternal mortality, pay gaps, and property deprivation are all systemic forms.

**Forms of Structural Violence:**
- **Demographic violence** — skewed sex ratios
- **Economic violence** — denying property rights, unequal pay, devaluing unpaid care
- **Institutional violence** — unequal access to food, healthcare, education
- **Symbolic violence** — normalisation of stereotypes and sexist language

**Gender-Based Violence (GBV)** — GBV is both a cause and consequence of patriarchy:
- **Domestic violence** — most reported crime; UN called the COVID-19 surge a "shadow pandemic"
- **Dowry deaths** — IPC Section 304B; thousands annually
- **Honour killings** — violent enforcement of caste endogamy targeting women's marriage choices
- **Sexual violence** — including POSH Act workplace harassment and cyber violence
- **Acid attacks** — often from rejected proposals and dowry disputes
- **Tech-Facilitated GBV (TFGBV)** — cyberstalking, deepfakes, non-consensual image sharing; over **20% of cybercrimes (2021) targeted women** (NCRB)

**Case Study — Women Wrestlers' Protest (2023):** Top Indian wrestlers protested against the WFI president for alleged sexual harassment, exposing systemic failures of POSH Internal Complaints Committees and power dynamics that deter women from speaking out.

#### 2.6.2 Women and Climate Change — Ecofeminism
- Climate change is **not gender-neutral**; women — especially poor rural women — are disproportionately affected:
  - Primarily responsible for water, food, and fuel collection
  - Women and children are **14 times more likely to die** in climate-related disasters
  - With 76.2% of rural women workers in agriculture, drought/floods/extreme heat directly threaten incomes
  - Increased heatwaves = more hours of unpaid care work

**Ecofeminism (Vandana Shiva):** Argues that the domination of women and the domination of nature are interconnected — both stem from hierarchical systems that reduce living beings to exploitable resources.

**Chipko Movement** — Classic example of ecofeminism in action: local rural women physically embraced trees to prevent deforestation, linking their survival to environmental protection.

**Solutions:** Climate-smart farming training, drought-resistant crops, easy water access, inclusion of women in disaster planning.

#### 2.6.3 Unresolved Legal Debates
- **Marital Rape Exception:** Exception 2 to Section 375 IPC exempts husbands from rape charges. **Justice J.S. Verma Committee (2013)** recommended its deletion — not implemented. Remains the central frontier of bodily autonomy within marriage and the single most contested legal gap in women's rights.
- **Anti-Conversion Laws:** Laws in UP, MP, Gujarat, Karnataka, Himachal Pradesh regulate religious conversion for marriage — chilling effect on women's personal choices, particularly inter-faith relationships.
- **Surrogacy (Regulation) Act, 2021:** Restricts commercial surrogacy (only altruistic surrogacy by close relatives). Raises concerns about reproductive autonomy, exclusion of single parents and LGBTQ+ individuals, and exploitation within the fertility industry.

## 3. Women's Movements in India

### Phase 1 — Reform and Anti-Colonial Era (Late 19th Century – 1947)
Largely characterised by **male-led social reform** targeting specific practices:

| Reformer / Organisation | Contribution |
|---|---|
| Raja Ram Mohan Roy and Brahmo Samaj | Abolition of Sati |
| Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar | Widow remarriage |
| Jyotirao and Savitribai Phule | Women's education; Jyotirao established India's **first girls' school in 1848** |
| Arya Samaj | Opposed child marriage; promoted women's education |
| All India Women's Conference (1927) | Advocated political rights and legal reforms |

- Women gradually mobilised into the independence movement — from passive participants to frontline leaders in Gandhian campaigns and agrarian revolts like the **Tebhaga Movement** and the **Telangana Movement**.

---

### Phase 2 — Quest for Legal and Social Equality (1960s–1980s)
**Decisive catalyst:** The **Towards Equality Report (1974)** — authored by **Vina Mazumdar and Lotika Sarkar** — exposed the stark post-independence reality of declining sex ratios, economic marginalisation, and discriminatory personal laws. This report **galvanised autonomous women's groups**.

**Key developments:**
- Rise of **autonomous women's organisations** — independent of state and political parties; shift from welfare to rights-based feminism
- **SEWA (Self-Employed Women's Association)** — founded by **Ela Bhatt**; 2.9 million members; organised informal sector women combining trade union struggle with developmental programmes
- **AIDWA (All India Democratic Women's Association)** — advocated political rights and legislative equality
- **Mathura rape case (1972)** — triggered the first sustained legal reform campaign; led to amendments in rape laws
- Campaigns against dowry deaths and domestic violence leading to **IPC Section 498A (1983)**

---

### Phase 3 — Decentralisation, Intersectionality, and Digital Movements (1990s onwards)
- **73rd and 74th Amendments (1992)** — mass expansion of women's political participation at grassroots level
- **Growth of women-led NGOs** — legal aid, awareness campaigns, community support
- **Intersectional feminism emerged** — challenged the notion of a uniform "woman's experience"; highlighted caste, class, and regional inequalities
- Rise of **Dalit women's movements** and **Adivasi women's groups** — addressing double marginalisation
- **#MeToo movement (2018)** — exposed normalised harassment across industries; brought previously silenced voices into public discourse
- **#PinjraTod** — campus movement against patriarchal hostel regulations
- **Digital feminism** — feminist content creators and influencers expanding gender discourse to Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities
- **Naga Mothers Association** — women's peace-building in insurgency-affected Northeast
- **LGBTQ+ rights discourse** gaining visibility post-*Navtej Singh Johar* (2018) and *Supriyo vs. Union of India*

**Intersectionality — The Essential Analytical Lens:**
Recognises that a woman's experience is not shaped by gender alone but by the simultaneous interaction of multiple identities — caste, class, religion, region, disability, and sexual orientation. Upper-caste feminist movements have historically overlooked this — which is a key critique of the women's movement in India.

## 4. Women's Organisations

### 4.1 Types

**Grassroots and Developmental Organisations:**
- **SEWA** — organises informal sector women; provides banking, healthcare, childcare, insurance
- **Kudumbashree (Kerala)** — poverty eradication through micro-enterprises and community development; model for women's collective leadership
- **Shri Mahila Griha Udyog (Lijjat Papad)** — collective ownership and decision-making empowering women through cooperative enterprise

**Advocacy and Rights-Based Organisations:**
- **AIDWA** — legislative reform and political rights
- **BMMA (Bhartiya Muslim Mahila Andolan)** — legal aid for Muslim women; fought for abolition of instant triple talaq

**State-Supported Mechanisms:**
- **Self-Help Groups (SHGs) under DAY-NRLM** — over 90 million women organised; beyond economic empowerment, they foster political awareness and grassroots leadership
- **Lakhpati Didi Programme** — targets ₹1 lakh+ annual income for rural women through SHGs

### 4.2 SHGs as Engines of Empowerment
SHGs have emerged as the most scalable grassroots empowerment tool in India. Beyond providing micro-credit, they:
- Build social capital and collective confidence
- Create platforms for taking collective action on alcoholism, domestic violence, and local governance
- Generate political awareness and leadership skills among rural women
- **55.6% of PM Jan Dhan Yojana account holders are women**
- **10.05 crore women** mobilised into **90.87 lakh SHGs** till October 2024 (DAY-NRLM)

**Risk of patriarchal co-optation:** SHGs face the danger of being captured by dominant community interests or used as instruments of state welfare delivery without genuine autonomy.

### 4.3 Critiques of Women's Organisations and Movements

**Internal limitations:**
- **Urban and middle-class bias** — movement has prioritised workplace rights and legal reform while rural concerns (agrarian distress, unpaid care, informal labour) remain under-represented
- **Leadership concentration** — elite, educated women dominate leadership, restricting genuine grassroots participation
- **Representation gap** — Dalit, Adivasi, Muslim, and disabled women represented symbolically rather than substantively
- **Issue-based silos** — organisations work in thematic isolation with limited cross-issue coordination
- **Insufficient engagement with men** — gender equality treated as a women's issue rather than a societal one, reducing transformative potential

**External constraints:**
- **Patriarchal backlash** — social resistance at family, community, and institutional levels
- **Policy implementation gaps** — progressive laws undermined by poor enforcement and administrative apathy
- **Digital harassment** — activists face trolling, doxxing, and online abuse, silencing marginalised voices
- **Tokenism** — online movements generate visibility without sustained institutional follow-through

## 8. Way Forward — Six-Pronged Strategy

### 8.1 Social and Behavioural Change
- Curriculum reform — gender-neutral textbooks, challenging stereotypes from early schooling
- Mandatory gender sensitisation in schools, colleges, and workplaces
- Community and media campaigns challenging patriarchal norms
- Engaging men and boys as active allies — not targets of blame, but partners in transformation

### 8.2 Economic Empowerment
- Equal pay legislation with enforcement teeth — mandatory pay gap reporting (on UK's model)
- Investment in the care economy — public childcare and eldercare infrastructure to redistribute the unpaid burden
- Recognition of unpaid care work in national accounts — Time-Use Surveys institutionalised
- Women's land rights — ensuring Hindu Succession Act provisions are actually implemented
- Extending social security to gig and informal workers

### 8.3 Political Representation
- Timely implementation of **Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam** — delimitation and census must not delay indefinitely
- Capacity-building for elected women representatives — beyond symbolic presence to substantive participation
- Political party internal democracy — genuine commitment to winnable seats for women
- A **"quota within quota"** for OBC, SC, and ST women

### 8.4 Legal Reform and Justice
- Close the **marital rape exception** — bodily autonomy does not end at the threshold of marriage
- Fast-track courts for gender-based violence cases
- Police sensitisation — women-friendly reporting processes
- Strengthen POSH Act implementation — currently undermined by weak ICC systems
- Legal aid for the most marginalised women — Dalit, Adivasi, and minority women face the greatest barriers to justice

### 8.5 Human Capital and Capabilities
- Address anaemia and maternal nutrition — 57% anaemia prevalence is a crisis
- Mental health support — rising suicide rates among young women require structural, not merely clinical, responses
- Digital literacy for women — bridge the mobile and internet gender gap
- Girls' retention in secondary and higher education — conditional cash transfers, safe transport, sanitation facilities

### 8.6 The Case for Male Membership
Genuine gender transformation requires engaging men. Reasons:
- Patriarchy harms men too — by enforcing rigid, often toxic standards of masculinity that cause psychological harm, suppress emotion, and restrict caring relationships
- Gender relations involve both men and women; meaningful change requires participation from both
- Men can help promote attitudes and practices that support equality in families, workplaces, and communities
- Greater dialogue between men and women can reduce misunderstandings and build mutual respect
- Shared responsibility in childcare, household work, and caregiving can improve outcomes for both
- Broad-based participation makes gender equality a collective social goal rather than the concern of any one group alone

**Example:** **Mardon Wali Baat (MAVA — Men Against Violence and Abuse):** An initiative using workshops, street plays, and peer discussion to help young men critically examine masculinity and become active allies for gender equality.

## 9. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Simple Definition |
|---|---|
| **Algorithmic Bias** | When AI or digital tools reproduce and amplify gender (or caste/race) discrimination in hiring, credit scoring, or service allocation |
| **Bodily Autonomy** | The fundamental right of a person to self-governance over their own body — encompassing reproductive, sexual, and health choices without coercion |
| **Care Economy** | The full spectrum of paid and unpaid work devoted to caring for people — including childcare, eldercare, and domestic labour; largely performed by women and uncounted in GDP |
| **Compulsory Heterosexuality** | The societal assumption and enforcement of heterosexuality as the only "natural" or acceptable orientation, marginalising all others |
| **Digital Gender Divide** | The gap between men's and women's access to digital devices, internet, and technology skills — creating new intersectional inequalities |
| **Double Burden / Second Shift** | The situation where women bear the dual responsibility of paid employment plus the primary load of unpaid domestic and care work |
| **Ecofeminism** | A feminist framework linking the oppression of women with the exploitation of nature — arguing both stem from the same hierarchical worldview |
| **Feminisation of Agriculture** | The increasing proportion of women in agricultural work, primarily due to male outmigration, without commensurate land ownership or decision-making power |
| **Feminisation of Labour** | The rising proportion of women in the paid workforce — often concentrated in low-wage, insecure, informal jobs |
| **Feminisation of Poverty** | The trend of women experiencing poverty at significantly higher rates than men, due to wage gaps, property denial, and care burdens |
| **Gender** | The socially constructed roles, behaviours, expectations, and norms that society assigns to individuals based on their sex — varies across cultures and time |
| **Gender Budgeting** | Government practice of analysing the budget for its differential impact on women and men; allocating resources specifically to bridge gender gaps |
| **Gender Equity** | The process of fairness — recognising historical disadvantages and providing differential, targeted support to achieve a level playing field |
| **Gender Equality** | The end state where all genders have equal rights, resources, responsibilities, and opportunities |
| **Gender Mainstreaming** | Integrating a gender perspective into all stages of policy design, implementation, and evaluation |
| **Gender Pay Gap** | The difference in average earnings between men and women in the workforce |
| **Gender Socialisation** | The lifelong process through which individuals learn gender-appropriate roles, norms, and behaviours — through family, school, peers, and media |
| **Glass Ceiling** | The invisible barrier preventing women from rising to senior leadership positions in organisations |
| **Intersectionality** | A framework (Kimberlé Crenshaw) recognising how multiple identities (gender, caste, class, religion, region) overlap to create unique and compounded forms of discrimination |
| **Leaky Pipeline** | The progressive drop-out of women from STEM and professional careers at each successive stage, due to institutional bias and social expectations |
| **Manosphere** | Online communities promoting anti-feminist, male supremacist ideologies — framing gender equality as a threat to men |
| **Patriarchal Bargain** | When women accept and reinforce patriarchal norms in exchange for protection, status, or resources — a strategy of accommodation within an unequal system |
| **Patriarchy** | A social system where men hold primary power and authority across all major institutions — family, economy, religion, and polity |
| **Pink-Collarisation** | The concentration of women in low-paying, socially "feminised" occupations (nursing, teaching, domestic work) that are systematically undervalued |
| **Prosperity Paradox** | The phenomenon where wealthier states/regions have worse child sex ratios — because prosperity provides access to sex-determination technology used to act on son preference |
| **Sarpanch Pati Syndrome** | When male relatives exercise de facto power on behalf of elected women Panchayat representatives, converting formal representation into proxy representation |
| **Second Shift** | Arlie Hochschild's term for the unpaid domestic labour that working women perform after returning from paid employment |
| **Sex** | The biological and physiological characteristics (chromosomes, hormones, reproductive organs) that define males and females |
| **Son Meta-Preference** | Deep-rooted cultural and economic bias for male children, driving sex-selective abortions, daughter neglect, and skewed sex ratios |
| **Sticky Floor** | Forces that keep women confined to low-paying, low-mobility jobs with limited career advancement |
| **Structural Violence** | Harm caused not by direct physical assault but by unjust social structures — skewed sex ratios, maternal mortality, and wage gaps are all forms of structural violence |
| **TFGBV (Tech-Facilitated GBV)** | Technology-enabled gender-based violence — including cyberstalking, deepfakes, non-consensual image sharing, and online harassment |
| **Toxic Masculinity** | The rigid, harmful aspects of traditional masculinity that suppress emotion, demand dominance, and discourage men from caregiving roles |
| **U-Shaped FLFP Curve** | Claudia Goldin's theory that female labour force participation first falls as incomes rise modestly, then rises again at higher development levels — explaining middle-income FLFP dips |
| **Women's Empowerment** | The process by which women gain the agency, capacity, and access to make and act on strategic life choices across economic, social, and political spheres |
