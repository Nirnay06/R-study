## PART 1: SANSKRITIZATION, WESTERNIZATION AND MODERNIZATION

---

### A. SANSKRITIZATION

#### Definition
**M. N. Srinivas** — coined the concept.
- Key work: **"Religion and Society among the Coorgs of South India" (1952)** — first systematic study to define, analyze, and understand social change in Indian society.
- Core argument: The caste system in India has **never been so rigid** that individuals or castes cannot alter or raise their status.

**Definition of Sanskritization (Srinivas):**
> "The process by which a low caste or tribe or other group takes over the customs, rituals, beliefs, ideology and lifestyle of a higher caste and in particular 'twice born' (dwija) caste."

#### Changes Adopted During Sanskritization (Cultural Emulation)
- Giving up non-vegetarianism, alcohol consumption, animal sacrifice
- Imitating Brahmin lifestyle in food, dress, and rituals
- Donning of sacred thread
- Giving up sacrifice of pigs at weddings
- System of **dowry replacing bride price**
- All of the above = **Cultural Emulation** → leads to **Cultural Erosion**

#### Role of Dominant Castes
- Srinivas notes: mediation of Sanskritization models is shaped by **local dominant castes**
  - Brahmin-dominated areas → Brahminical model
  - Rajput or Bania-dominated areas → Kshatriya or Vaishya models

#### Regional Examples
| Region | Community | Claim/Action |
|---|---|---|
| Bihar | Rajwars (SC) | Claim Kshatriya status |
| Bihar | Koyeris (BC) | Identify as Kushwaha Kshatriya |
| Bihar | Dusadh (SC) | Adopt identity of Gahlout Kshatriya |
| Maharashtra & Rajasthan | Mahar and Pasi castes | Abandon polluting occupations |
| Central India | Gonds, Cheros, Sudha Saoras, Paudi Bhuiyans | Undergo Sanskritization |
| Rajasthan | Meena | Absorbed into Hindu fold |
| Bihar & West Bengal | Santhal, Munda | Absorbed into Hindu fold |
| UP | Jounsari, Khasas, Tharu | Absorbed into Hindu fold |

#### Impact of Cultural Emulation
- Autonomy of women and freedom to choose life partner is **lost**
- Family structure shifts to **orthodox Hindu joint family** with strong paternal authority and monogamy
- Food habits change (beef, pork, liquor abandoned)
- Dowry system **replaces bride price**

#### Hostility Faced by Lower Castes
- Middle-strata castes resist upward mobility of lower castes
- **Example — Travancore:** Lower caste women (Nadars, Ezhavas) had to pay ***mulakkaram*** ("breast tax") to cover their upper bodies
  - Legendary story of **Nangeli** — an Ezhava woman who cut off her breasts in protest and died of blood loss
  - For men, equivalent was ***talakkaram*** (head tax) or ***meeshakkaram*** (moustache tax)

#### Negative Effects of Sanskritization
- Lower castes are **ironically more liberal** in matters of women's rights, sexual conduct, freedom of marriage — but Sanskritization makes them **conservative** and retrograde
- Examples of regressive adoption: **dowry, early marriage**

#### The Paradox of Sanskritization
- "Lower" castes that become prosperous/educated claim **higher status** through Sanskritization
- At the same time, they **fiercely retain "backward" status** to avail reservations/jobs
- "Modern India is full of contradictions"

#### Other Motives — Harold Gould (1988)
> "The prime motive force behind Sanskritization is the factor of **repressed hostility** which manifests itself not in the form of rejecting the caste system but in the form of its victims trying to **seize control of it** and thereby expiate their frustrations on the same battlefield where they acquired them."

- In short: it is not just to gain status, but to **show capability** equal to former oppressors (upper castes)

#### New Agents of Sanskritization
- Srinivas: Festivals of village deities and calendrical festivals are increasingly Sanskritized
- **Newspapers, microphone, radio/television** = new agents of Sanskritization
- Example: Booking a Tirupati pooja online → indirectly strengthens Sanskritization

#### Sanskritization Among Non-Hindu Communities
- Cultural emulation for status elevation is the prime motive even among non-Hindu communities
- **Tribal Absorption into Hindu fold:** A tribe undergoing Sanskritization becomes a caste, establishing regular relations with local castes

### VARIANTS OF SANSKRITIZATION

#### a. Resanskritization
- **Meaning:** A group temporarily moves away from Sanskritic practices, then **returns** to them
- **Explanation:** Groups adopt reformist/modernist/Western practices, then re-adopt Brahmanical practices due to social pressure, revivalist movements, or identity reassertion
- **Examples:**
  - **Lingayats in Karnataka** — Originally a radical bhakti movement by **Basavanna** (12th century) that rejected Brahminical orthodoxy; later sections incorporated idol worship and temple rituals → classic example of resanskritization
  - **Arya Samaj followers** — Rejected idol worship, but later generations reintroduced Sanskritic practices

#### b. Desanskritization
- **Meaning:** Groups that had adopted Sanskritic practices **deliberately abandon** them to revive indigenous traditions
- **Explanation:** Arises from disillusionment with Sanskritization (it fails to deliver actual mobility), or from tribal assertion and identity movements
- **Examples:**
  - **Ramnamis of Chhattisgarh** — Dalit sect adopting Vaishnav bhakti, but tattooed "Ram" on bodies and rejected Brahminical/priestly mediation
  - **Jharkhand tribal movements (Santhal, Munda, Ho, Oraon)** — **Sarna revivalism** emphasizes worship of nature spirits and ancestral rituals, rejecting Hindu deities and temples
  - **Adi Dharma Movement among Dalits in Bengal** — Rejected Brahmanical Hinduism and reclaimed distinct cultural identity outside Hinduism

### SCHOLAR VIEWS ON SANSKRITIZATION

| Scholar | Key Argument |
|---|---|
| **M. N. Srinivas** | Coined concept; multiple models depending on dominant caste; Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya all possible models |
| **Milton Singer (1968)** | Multiple models of Sanskritization exist; contemporary mobility less about Sanskritization, more about **political participation** |
| **Yogendra Singh** | Extended Sanskritization beyond castes to *varna* framework; critiqued it for focus on hierarchy; said it manifests **repressed inter-class hostility** |
| **William Rowe** | Sanskritization less functional today compared to political participation as a means of mobility |
| **Harold Gould (1988)** | Prime motive = **repressed hostility**; victims seize control rather than reject the system |

**Key Takeaway:** Sanskritization gains are largely **symbolic rather than structural**. Contemporary India increasingly uses education, politics, and law for dignity and equality beyond Sanskritic models.

### B. WESTERNIZATION

#### Definition
**M. N. Srinivas** coined the term.
- Refers to all **cultural changes and institutional innovations** in India due to more than **150 years of British rule** and contact with Western nations
- Wider plane: scientific approach, materialism (over spiritualism), individualism, liberal approach, humanism, equality, egalitarianism, rationalism

#### Impacts of Westernization in India
| Domain | Impact |
|---|---|
| Scientific & Educational | Modern schools, colleges, universities, scientific institutions |
| Political | Rise of nationalism, modern leadership, democratic institutions, new political culture |
| Caste & Family | Lessening of caste rigidity, disintegration of joint families, social reform movements |
| Economic | Decline of cottage industries, promotion of commercial crops, new land management |
| Social Values | Democracy, equality, social justice, uniform administration |

- Humanitarianism and rationalism → social reform movements: **abolition of sati, widow remarriage**
- Rise of social reformers like **Raja Ram Mohan Roy**

#### Westernization and Sanskritization — The Link (Srinivas)
- **Westernization accelerates Sanskritization:** Railways, post, newspapers increased pilgrimages, caste meetings, religious propaganda
- **Westernization weakens Sanskritization:** Highly Westernized people live in the "world of class rather than caste"; drop caste names, intermarry, give up sacred thread
- **Higher castes and Westernization:** Many Brahmins and Rajputs shifted to Western education as a way to maintain social distance
  - **Harold Gould:** Once a caste is fully Sanskritized, it cannot rise further; so higher castes used **Western lifestyles as an alternative form of distinction**

#### Three Global Views on Westernization

| View | Scholar | Key Argument |
|---|---|---|
| **Conflict View** | **Samuel Huntington** | Westernization brings **cultural conflicts between West and Rest**. Three responses: isolation, band-wagoning (openly joining), or balancing (modernize while retaining cultural identity) |
| **Optimistic View** | **Kishore Mahbubani** | A new **global civilization** is emerging; non-Western nations admire Western standards but the world must adopt shared policies. In *The New Asian Hemisphere*, observes Western influence is **unraveling** as Eastern powers (China) rise |
| **Critical View** | **Edward Said** (*Orientalism*) | Westernization = **colonial domination**; West essentialized subject populations, reinforced own superiority. **Balfour** argued Egyptians needed British guidance. Knowledge and domination intertwined |

#### Indian Context
- **John McLane** — Indian nationalist movement drew heavily from Western ideas of democracy and rights
- **B. R. Nanda** — Observed that **Jawaharlal Nehru** carried such "Britishness" that he once called himself "the last Englishman to rule India"
- Westernization gave India the ideological foundation for nationalism, democracy, and social reform

#### Limitations of Westernization
1. **Over-generalised concept** — Clubs together very different influences (railways, education, democracy, dress) under one label
2. **Eurocentric bias** — Assumes Western values are inherently progressive; ignores indigenous reforms
3. **Neglects selective adoption** — Communities accepted modern science but resisted Christianization
4. **Ignores resistance** — Swadeshi movement, revivalist reform movements not adequately accounted for
5. **Static view of the West** — Treats "the West" as a fixed model, ignoring its internal contradictions and evolving nature

### C. MODERNIZATION

#### Definitions
- **Eisenstadt:** "From a historical viewpoint, modernization is the process of change towards those types of social, economic, and political systems which were developed in Western Europe and North America from the 17th to 19th centuries, and later spread to South America, Asia, and Africa."
- **Yogendra Singh:** "Modernization symbolizes a **rational attitude** towards issues and their evaluation, not from a particularistic point of view."

- Rests on the idea of **progress** — scientific, technological, and institutional advancement improves the human condition
- Implies decline of feudalism, superstition, and rigid dogmatism

#### Key Features of Modernization
- Based on progress in science, technology, and social organization
- Decline in feudalism, superstition, and rigid dogmatism
- Promotes rationality, equality, democracy, and secularism

#### Historical Roots of Modernization

**1. Neolithic Revolution (c. 10,000 BCE)**
- Development of ploughs and refined stone tools → raised land productivity
- Nomadic bands settled into permanent villages
- Surplus production → specialization (artisans, merchants, priests, administrators)
- First leap toward organized societies and proto-civilizations

**2. Industrial Revolution (18th-19th Century)**
- Steam engine, electricity, telegraph, internal combustion engine, factory system
- Agricultural modernization: Denmark and New Zealand industrialized farms → "rural factories"
- Social impact: dismantled agrarian hierarchies, created new classes and urban cultures
- Political revolutions: American and French revolutions introduced democracy and self-determination

**3. Cultural Dimension — Max Weber**
- **"The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism" (1904)**
- Protestantism encouraged **rational discipline and work ethic** ("this-worldly orientation")
- Unlike Catholicism and Eastern religions (more otherworldly), suited for industrial capitalism

#### Modernization vs Westernization
**S. C. Dube (1988):** "Modernization is **less value-loaded** than Westernization. While Westernization implies borrowing Western practices, modernization emphasizes rational and scientific progress without threatening cultural identity."

| Westernization = Modernization | Westernization ≠ Modernization |
|---|---|
| Railways, telegraphs, postal services, English education | Blind imitation of fashion, fast food, Valentine's Day |
| Constitutional democracy modernized political structures | Wearing suits in Indian summers lacks rationality |

**Modernization Beyond the West:**
- Borrowing from other societies: **Green Revolution** in India drew from Mexico and Philippines
- Indigenous reform: **Arya Samaj, Brahmo Samaj, Gandhi's Khadi and Panchayati Raj** = modernization from within

**Key Takeaway:** Westernization may sometimes lead to modernization, but modernization is broader — can come from Western, non-Western, or indigenous sources.

#### Features of a Modernized Personality — Daniel Lerner
1. **Empathy** — Ability to see from another's perspective
   - Example: B. R. Ambedkar's temple entry and anti-untouchability struggles
   - Example: Saudi Arabia's relaxation of rigid dress codes
2. **Mobility** — Both geographical and social mobility
   - Example: Ambedkar's rise from untouchable origins to chief framer of the Constitution
   - Example: Lifting of driving ban for Saudi women
3. **High Participation** — Greater involvement in political, economic, and social life
   - Example: Dalits mobilizing through Ambedkar's leadership
   - Example: Saudi women joining workforce and politics under Vision 2030

#### Indian Model of Modernization
**S. C. Dube (1974):** "There is no standard model of modernization and no fixed path for its attainment. Developing societies can adopt a model of their choice."
- India's chosen path: **democracy, secularism, and social justice**
- Adoption of modern science and technology is central
- Example: **Directive Principle Article 48** — modernizing agriculture and animal husbandry
- Challenges: regional imbalances, corruption, nepotism, uneven diffusion of benefits

#### Contemporary Issues in Modernization
- **Superstition vs Rationality:** NCRB data (2021) showed **74 deaths from witchcraft and human sacrifice** → reveals gaps in modernization. Activist **Narendra Dabholkar** campaigned for a national anti-superstition law
- **Women's Rights:** Demands for entry into mosques, equal inheritance laws
- **Religious Conservatism:** Women activists or minorities branded as "Westernized" or "anti-religion"

#### Limitations of Modernization Theory
1. **Not a uniform or linear process** — IT hubs of Bengaluru coexist with witchcraft-related deaths
2. **Neglect of inequality and exclusion** — Green Revolution raised productivity but widened class divides; tribal farmers and rainfed areas left behind
3. **Resistance from cultural/religious conservatism** — Modern reforms branded as "Western" or "anti-tradition"
4. **Overemphasis on progress, underestimation of costs** — Environmental degradation, urban alienation, consumerism
5. **Alternative modernities overlooked** — Gandhian decentralization and indigenous tribal sustainable models sidelined; Gandhi's Panchayati Raj vision dismissed as "backward" during Nehru's focus on heavy industry

**Conclusion:** Modernization is multidimensional — economic, political, social. India has been shaped by Western models, non-Western emulation, and indigenous reforms. At its core, modernization is about **progress, dignity, and inclusivity**.

### RELIGIOUS MINORITIES

#### Census Data — Religious Communities of India (2011)
| Religious Group | Population (in million) | % of Total |
|---|---|---|
| Hindus | 966.3 | 79.8% |
| Muslims | 172.2 | 14.2% |
| Christians | 27.8 | 2.3% |
| Sikhs | 20.8 | 1.7% |
| Buddhists | 8.4 | 0.7% |
| Jains | 4.5 | 0.4% |
| Other Religions & Persuasions (ORP) | 7.9 | 0.7% |
| Religion Not Stated | 2.9 | 0.2% |

#### Constitutional Provisions for Minorities
- Term "Minorities" appears at **only four places** in the Indian Constitution
- **No definition** of the term is given in the Constitution
- Key articles:
  - **Article 29** — Protection of interests of minorities; speaks of "any sections of citizens having a **distinct language, script or culture**"
    - **Lord Acton** famously stated: *"The most certain test by which we judge whether a country is really free is the amount of security enjoyed by minorities."*
  - **Article 30** — Right of minorities to **establish and administer educational institutions** (religious OR linguistic minorities)
  - **Article 350-A** — Instruction in **mother tongue** at primary stage for linguistic minority children
  - **Article 350-B** — **Special Officer for linguistic minorities** appointed by the President (inserted by **Constitution 7th Amendment Act, 1956**)

#### Who are Religious Minorities?
- A group whose religion is held by a minority of a population
- Initially **five** communities notified: **Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Zoroastrians (Parsis)**
- On **27th January 2014**, **Jains** were also notified as a minority community

#### Problems of Religious Minorities

1. **Problem of Identity** — Socio-cultural differences, history and background; difficulty adjusting with majority community

2. **Problems of Security** — Feelings of insecurity about life, assets, well-being; accentuated when majority-minority relations are strained

3. **Problem of Equity** — Deprived of development opportunities due to discrimination
   - **UNDP and Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative (OPHI) — MPI 2018:** "Every second ST, every third Dalit and Muslim in India poor, not just financially"

4. **Problem of Communal Tension and Riots**
   - **2013 Muzaffarnagar riots** — widespread violence and displacement
   - **2002 Godhra Riots** — Train burning on 27 February 2002, death of 69 Hindu pilgrims returning from Ayodhya, triggered communal violence

5. **Lack of Sufficient Representation in Civil Services and Politics**
   - **Sachar Committee** highlighted: Muslims = 14% of population, but only **2.5% of Indian bureaucracy** and **5% in politics**

6. **Politicization of Religious Issues**
   - **Case Study: Karnataka Hijab Controversy (2022)**
     - February 2022: Muslim students denied entry to Karnataka junior college for wearing hijabs
     - Hindu students counter-protested in saffron scarves
     - Karnataka government directed uniforms without exceptions
     - **15 March 2022**: Karnataka High Court **upheld the ban** (hijab not an essential religious practice)
     - Criticized by **Malala Yousafzai** and **Human Rights Watch**

7. **Fear Mongering by the Majority Community**
   - **CAA 2019** — Excluded Muslims from fast-tracked citizenship → media and political narratives painted Muslim protesters (Shaheen Bagh women) as "anti-national" or "funded by outsiders"

#### Important Cases Relating to Religious Minorities
- **Ashwini Upadhyay's PIL** — Court said no new religious group to be recognised as a minority; NCM must work towards reducing the list. Court's approach called "assimilation" — but the Constitution wants **integration, not assimilation**

#### Justice Ranganath Mishra Commission — Key Recommendations
- **10% reservation** for Muslims and **5%** for other minorities in Central and State Government jobs
- In 27% OBC quota, an **8.4% sub-quota** earmarked for minorities (since minorities = 8.4% of OBC population per Mandal Commission)
- Internal breakup: **6% for Muslims** (73% of minority population nationally) and **2.4% for other minorities**
- Carefully examine extent of minority presence in ST category
- **Delink SC status from religion** — abrogation of 1950 Scheduled Caste Order which excludes Muslims, Christians, Jains, and Parsis from SC net (only includes Hindus, Buddhists, Sikhs)
- Establish **Morarji Desai Residential Schools** and PU colleges for minorities at taluk level; minority women hostels and degree colleges

### LINGUISTIC MINORITIES

#### Definition
- A linguistic minority = people whose **mother tongue is different from the majority in the state or part of a state**
- Determined on a **state-wide basis** (not national basis)

#### Important Court Cases
- **1971 — DAV College etc. vs. State of Punjab:** Supreme Court defined linguistic minority as a minority that at least has a **spoken language, regardless of having a script or not**
- **TA Pai Foundation and Others vs. State of Karnataka:** Status of linguistic minority to be determined in the **context of states and not India as a whole**

#### Problems of Linguistic Minorities
1. **Lack of funds to teach minority language in schools**
   - **XAXA Committee on Tribes:** Low literacy among PVTGs due to poor infrastructure, poorly trained teachers, **lack of teaching in tribal languages**, irrelevant curriculum
   - Rapid shift to English medium in tribal schools → indigenous linguistic minorities cannot understand curriculum and lose their own language
2. **Language Diversity** — Challenge to preserve languages due to dominance of major languages
3. **Having to use majority's language for grievance redress** — Barrier to effective communication and representation
4. **Social Discrimination** — Made to feel like outsiders in social and professional settings
5. **Cultural Erosion** — Influence of major languages leads to erosion of unique linguistic and cultural identities
6. **Economic Disparities** — Economic opportunities correlate with proficiency in major languages; disadvantage for linguistic minorities

**Conclusion:** "A civilisation can be judged by the way it treats its minorities." Empowerment through affirmative action, inclusive development, equitable share in education, employment, and economy. Implementing **Justice Ranganath Mishra Commission** recommendations is the way forward.

## PART 3: GROWTH OF ANTHROPOLOGY IN INDIA

---

### Overview
Development of anthropological thought in India was **slow but steady**. Four generally accepted phases:

---

### Phase 1: Formative Phase (1774–1919)
- Emphasis on **tribal life and diversity of customs**
- Many handbooks, ethnographies, and gazettes on tribes and castes published
- Revenue reports of **Dalton, Buchanon, and Lord Baden Powell** examined socio-cultural situation
- **1774:** Foundation of **Asiatic Society of India** by **Sir William Jones** (became Asiatic Society of Bengal in 1784; started publishing journal)
- **H. H. Risley** published report on *Tribes and Castes of Bengal* (1891); later became Head of Census operations and conducted ethnographic surveys

#### Contribution of Indian Anthropologists (Formative Phase)
- **1912 — S. C. Roy** published the monograph **"Munda and Their Country"** and wrote on other tribes of Central India

---

### Phase 2: Constructive Phase (1920–1949)
- Anthropology introduced in Sociology Department of **Bombay University (1919)**
- Entered PG curriculum of **Calcutta University by 1920**
- Publication of journal **"Man in India" (1921)** under editorship of **S. C. Roy**
- **25th Indian Science Congress (1938, Calcutta):** Focal theme "Anthropology in India" — first review of anthropological research in India (joint session of Indian Science Congress and British Association)
- **1939 — Verrier Elwin** published book ***"The Baiga"*** — proposed that tribes should be **left alone** to develop in isolation from the mainstream (Isolationist approach)
- **1945:** Establishment of **Anthropological Survey of India (AnSI)** — first Director: **B. S. Guha** at Calcutta

---

### Phase 3: Analytical Phase (1950–1990)
- Indian anthropologists began interacting regularly with **American anthropologists** — collaborative works
- **Shift from descriptive studies of preliterate societies to analytical studies of complex societies**
- Foreign anthropologists came to India; villages they studied became famous
- Large number of **village study monographs** published in 1950s and 1960s (Redfield, Singer, Marriott and others)
- New concepts and ideas generated; distinct research methodologies developed
- **L. P. Vidyarthi** — studied sacred complexes, **nature-man-spirit complex** in tribal societies

---

### Phase 4: Evaluative Phase (1990 to Present)
- New areas and subfields of Anthropology emerging
- Increased interest in **Medical Anthropology, Religion, Development Studies, and Psychological Studies**
- Research areas:
  - Tribal identity, integration, vanishing culture and planning
  - **Industrial Anthropology**
  - Tribal demography
  - Integrated study of tribal religions
- Important contributions to **Action Research, Socio-Psychological Research, Folk-lore Research, Studies of Power Structure and Leadership, Anthropology of Religion**

**Key Takeaway:** Growth moved from documenting indigenous traditions to critically analysing theories and methods. Today anthropology remains dynamic and relevant, continuously adapting to India's changing socio-cultural realities.

## PART 4: PANCHAYATI RAJ AND SOCIAL CHANGE

---

### What is Social Change?
- "Any major change in behaviour patterns, cultural values, and customs over time" = social change
- By "significant" modification, anthropologists mean changes yielding **profound social consequences**
- **Anderson and Parker:** Social change involves **alteration in the structure and functioning** of societal forms or processes themselves
- **Surinder S. Jodhka:** Social change refers to **changes in values, institutions and practices** of society

### What is a Panchayat?
- **Panchayati Raj** = oldest system of local government in the Indian subcontinent
- "Panchayat" = assembly (*ayat*) of **five (*panch*)** and *raj* = "rule"
- A group of elders chosen by local community to settle disputes; leader called **Mukhya or Sarpanch**
- Type of **direct democracy** (exercises all powers of government at village level)

### Three-Tier PRI Structure
- **Gram Panchayat** — village level
- **Block Panchayat / Panchayat Samiti** — intermediate level
- **Zilla Panchayat** — district level

### Panchayati Raj and Social Change

#### 1. Leadership from Less Privileged Sections
- Gram Sabha — all eligible voters can participate → includes less privileged sections
- Reservation for SCs and STs as chairpersons in panchayats
- **Women's Reservation:**
  - **Bihar** = first state to reserve **50% panchayat seats for women**, followed by 15 others (Uttarakhand, HP, MP, Kerala, Tripura, Maharashtra)
  - Challenge: "**Sarpanch pati**" phenomenon — husband of woman sarpanch calling the shots (PM Modi raised this on National Panchayati Raj Day, 24 April)

#### 2. Tribals and Social Change — PESA
- **PESA (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas Act)** — addresses tribal customary rights, cultural rights, language and identity, rights to land, water, forests, and minerals
- Emphasizes **right of tribal people to maintain and strengthen their own institutions, cultures and traditions** and pursue development according to their own aspirations

#### 3. Other Social Change Initiatives
- Tracking health outcomes: preventing childhood malnutrition, nutritious food for pregnant women below poverty line, preventing deaths during childbirth
- Social peace, farmer welfare, rural development, urban utilities to villages, open-defecation-free communities, sufficient toilets for women

### Case Studies of Panchayati Raj in Action

| Case Study | Location | Achievement |
|---|---|---|
| **Ban on Alcohol** | Rajasthan (Rajsamand's Bhim tehsil, 2015) | Women campaign → permanent closure of liquor shops in Barar and Hamela Ki Ver villages. Rule: 51% registered voters can close a liquor shop |
| **Decentralized Planning** | Latak, Assam's Dhemaji district | Village of 300 houses built a **bamboo bridge** to connect flood-affected areas — funded entirely from own resources |
| **Social Welfare** | Digampur, West Bengal | 'Direct-to-home' drinking water supply, doorstep garbage collection, handicrafts from organic manure. Won **first prize under Gram Panchayat Vikas Yojana** |
| **Environment Conservation** | Odisha Gram Panchayats | Plantation activities in 2020-21 for green cover expansion, climate change mitigation, and income generation (though critiqued as top-down/non-participatory) |
| **Social Development** | ACNielsen ORG-MARG Survey (Ministry of Panchayati Raj) | PRI representatives taking up social issues: child marriage discouragement, gambling, alcoholism |

**Conclusion:** Panchayati Raj is not just local governance but a **tool of social change**. It empowers marginalized groups, promotes health, education, and development, safeguards tribal rights through PESA. By decentralising power, it drives inclusive growth and strengthens grassroots democracy.

## PART 5: MEDIA AND SOCIAL CHANGE

---

### What is Media?
- **Marshall McLuhan:** *"The medium is the message"* — media not only carries information but **shapes how people think and act**, directly influencing social change
- Types of Media:
  - **Print Media:** Newspapers, magazines, pamphlets
  - **Broadcast Media:** Radio and television
  - **Digital Media:** Internet, social media, online platforms

### Positive Impacts of Media

| Impact | Example |
|---|---|
| **Giving voice to people** | Media coverage of **Dongria Khond** tribe's agitation against Vedanta's bauxite mining in **Niyamgiri range** → Supreme Court order (18 April 2013): mining clearance only if Dongrias agree |
| **Awareness about wrongs** | Coverage of honour killings, **khap panchayats**, lynchings → mobilised action against them |
| **Uplifting marginal sections** | Uplifting **Ghasi** (SC community of Odisha) by preventing social discrimination; Exposing FRA 2006 loopholes (rejection of appeals at patwari level); **#MeToo**, **#BlackLivesMatter** |
| **Revival of dying languages** | **Asur Community** using mobile radio to spread Asur language — language listed in **UNESCO Interactive Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger** (only 7,000-8,000 Asur speakers left). Led by **Sushma Asur** |
| **Women empowerment** | **Subhash** — study *"Impact of Mass Media on Women: A Sociological Study of Gulbarga District"* — mass media enabled women's empowerment, improved learning, advocated gender equality |
| **Media and democracy** | Ensures informed choices by citizens; "checking function" ensuring elected representatives uphold their oath |
| **Media and social justice** | Educating, raising awareness, questioning caste discrimination |

### Negative Impacts of Media

| Negative Impact | Details / Examples |
|---|---|
| **Dangers to Socialization** | Fixed to TV screens; reduced time with family/children; developing generation gap |
| **Fake news** | Creating tensions between communities |
| **Yellow Journalism / Sensationalism** | Former CJI **N. V. Ramana** said media is running **"kangaroo courts"** — ill-informed, biased, agenda-driven debates affecting justice delivery |
| **Distortion of elections** | Paid news distorting free and fair elections |
| **Impact on diet and body image** | Mass media shapes ideal body images → impact on mental health; promotes junk food consumption |
| **Media and criminal justice** | Law enforcement under pressure to selectively reveal investigation details or mischaracterize incidents |
| **Bhima Koregaon violence (2018)** | Motivated arrests of popular dissenters critical of Government |
| **Disha (Priyanka Reddy) murder case, 2019** | Media organized rallies demanding "encounter" justice → all four accused killed in alleged police encounter on 6 December 2019 (trial by media) |

**Conclusion:** Media is both a **mirror and a motor of social change**. It informs, empowers, and challenges social hierarchies, while also shaping new values and practices. Despite risks like fake news, its role in democracy, social justice, and cultural transformation makes it a **central driver of change** in contemporary society.
