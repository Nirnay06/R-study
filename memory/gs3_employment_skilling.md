## 2. Concurrent Ageing, Longevity Dividend & Silver Economy

**Longevity dividend** = the **economic and social gains** that arise when people live longer, healthier and more productive lives. Aging should not be seen only as a burden — older persons can contribute as **workers, consumers, mentors, caregivers and community participants**.

### Reasons for Longevity Dividend
- A **concurrent ageing trend**: working-age population expands while overall population gradually ages
- Total fertility rate falling **below replacement level**
- Life expectancy **steadily increasing** → raises median age

### Harnessing Longevity Dividend for Silver Economy
1. **Healthier lives** through preventive healthcare, NCD screening/management
   - Eg: **Ayushman Arogya Mandirs** — comprehensive primary healthcare + elderly/palliative care
2. **Flexible jobs & second-career opportunities** — converts elderly from passive dependents to active economic participants
   - Eg: **SACRED Portal** (Senior Able Citizens for Re-Employment in Dignity)
3. **Reskilling, digital literacy & financial literacy** for older adults
   - Eg: **PMGDISHA** (Pradhan Mantri Gramin Digital Saksharta Abhiyan)
4. **Trained caregivers, geriatric care, assisted living, physiotherapy & community care** — new employment-generating sector
   - Eg: **PM Special Training of Geriatric Care Givers**
5. **Assistive devices**, health products, elder-friendly housing, mobility aids, wellness services, insurance & retirement products
   - Eg: **Rashtriya Vayoshri Yojana** — assisted-living devices to eligible senior citizens
6. **Elder-friendly transport**, barrier-free public spaces, accessible housing, assisted digital services
   - Eg: **Atal Vayo Abhyuday Yojana (AVYAY)** — shelter, food, medical care, entertainment for senior citizens

### Case Study: Telangana Silver Economy Leadership
- By 2047, Telangana aims to be a **global leader of Silver Economy** through **ElderX** (tech platform)
- Connecting innovation, inclusion & investment to **serve 150 million seniors across 30 countries**
- Digital hubs for accessible housing startups, telehealth, financial products for ageing

### Challenges in Transforming Longevity Dividend into Silver Economy
- Rising **NCDs**, disability & chronic illnesses → dependency burden instead of productivity gain
- Lack of adequate **geriatric hospitals**, trained caregivers, home healthcare, palliative care
- **Rigid retirement norms, age bias**, lack of flexible jobs, weak second-career platforms
- **Digital illiteracy** among elderly, online fraud risks, inaccessible apps, unsafe streets, poor public transport

### Way Forward ✏️ (Handwritten annotations expand on these)
- Invest in **biomedical research & preventive healthcare**; control NCDs
- **Lifelong learning ecosystems** — reskilling, digital & financial literacy
- **Age-friendly work arrangements** — flexible jobs, phased retirement, consultancy, mentoring
- **Targeted programmes for informal & unorganised workers** — pension, insurance, social security, skilling

## 3. Employment Overview & Macro Landscape

### Key Surveys
- **PLFS** (Periodic Labour Force Survey): monthly & quarterly data — improving LFPR, falling unemployment
- **ASI** (Annual Survey of Industries): organised manufacturing — **6% YoY increase** in employment in FY24, adding **10 lakh jobs** vs FY23
- **QBUSE** (Quarterly Bulletin of Unincorporated Sector Enterprises): vast non-agricultural unorganised sector — **7.9 crore establishments** employing **12.9 crore individuals**

### Sectoral & Industrial Distribution
- **Agriculture** employs **42.4%** of total workforce (Q2 FY26) — due to seasonal agrarian cycles
- **Gendered Sectoral Divergence**: **59.1%** of female workforce concentrated in agriculture; males dominate **Tertiary/Services (36.7%)** and **Secondary/Industry (28.5%)**

### Employment Status Distribution & Gender Dynamics ✏️ TIME POVERTY, MOTHERHOOD PENALTY, PINK TAX
- **Female Workforce**: distinct concentration in **informal and self-employed roles**
  - Rural women: **'own account workers/employers' (37.5%)** or **'helpers in household enterprises' (34.2%)**
  - Only **10.8%** of rural women in regular wage jobs
- **Male Workforce**: more formalised; many self-employed, but urban males significantly in **regular wage/salaried jobs**
- **Rural-Urban Divide**:
  - Rural: agricultural workers (57.7%), self-employment (62.8%)
  - Urban: services sector (62.0%), formal regular wage/salaried (49.8%)

### Socioeconomic Rationale for Female Employment Patterns
- **Flexible Independent Work**: women prefer independent entrepreneurship, own-account work, helper roles due to **limited urban mobility** and **lack of affordable housing/childcare**
- **The "Dual Burden"**: **Time Use Survey (TUS) 2024** — women are predominant caregivers, managing **unpaid domestic/care work** alongside economic roles
  - Females: **363 minutes/day on unpaid activities** (males: only **123 minutes**)
  - Males: **414 minutes on paid activities** (females: only **302 minutes**)
  - Women's **combined total** (paid + unpaid) significantly exceeds men's

### Insights from National Time Use Survey (TUS) 2024
- **Employment Activity Participation** (15–59 years, 24-hour reference):
  - 2024: **75% males** and **25% females** participated (up from 70.9% and 21.8% in 2019)
- **Caregiving Imbalance**: females ~**140 minutes daily** caregiving vs males ~**74 minutes**
- **The Dual Burden**: while women spend less time in formal paid work, their massive unpaid load means combined time spent by females significantly exceeds males

## 4. Monetizing the Care Sector

**ILO definition**: care work = activities meeting **physical, psychological, and emotional needs** of adults & children, old & young, frail & able-bodied

### Economic Potential
- Care services investments → estimated **475 million jobs globally by 2030**
- India: direct public investment of **2% of GDP** → potential to generate **11 million jobs**, nearly **70% to women**

### Why India Needs a Care Economy
- By **2050**, India will need to care for nearly **64.7 crore persons** (30 crore children + 34.7 crore elderly)
- Women face severe **"time poverty"** — working-age women spend **5.6 hours/day** on unpaid work (men: only 30 minutes)
- **Motherhood penalty** — pushes women into informal jobs or out of labour force
- Women's unpaid domestic & care work valued at **15–17% of GDP**

### Key Interventions & Way Forward
1. **Expand childcare & creche infrastructure** — Revamped Palna Scheme, Anganwadi-Cum-Creche initiative, workplace-linked creches
2. **Enhance female paid employment** — flexible work policies, removal of statutory restrictions, skill development aligned with industry
3. **Professionalise the care sector** — create **Sector Skill Council for Care Services**, standardised skilling modules
4. **Public-Private Partnerships** — mobile creches in offices, hospitals, public spaces
5. **Support care start-ups** — through Start-up India and Self-Help Groups
6. **Quality monitoring & rating systems** — similar to UK's **OFSTED model**
7. **Financial support** — vouchers, tax rebates, income-based assistance
8. **Strengthen elderly care** — in-situ ageing, multigenerational households, geriatric infrastructure
9. **Community-led models** — eg: **Jeevika in Bihar** for self-managed childcare

### Case Study: Multidimensional Impact of Creches
- **Mexico**: public childcare → dual effect: (a) low-income mothers found paid employment, (b) created **45,000 paid positions** for caregivers
- **India**: quality creche facilities → positive impact on women's wellbeing, income, child well-being, family relationships

### State-Level Best Practices
- **17 states/UTs** removed prohibitions on women in 'hazardous' industries
- **22 states/UTs** (incl. UP) permit night-time work for women with mandatory safety protocols
- **Tamil Nadu's 'Thozhi Hostels'** — gender-responsive working women's hostels with creches (PPP model)
- **Telangana's WE-Hub** — connects women to start-up ecosystems
- **Kerala's Kudumbashree** — women in non-traditional roles (construction/logistics) via microfinance
- **Maharashtra's Mahila Arthik Vikas Mahamandal** — links SHGs to formal credit

**Viksit Bharat 2047** demands "women-led" progress; increasing female participation to **~55% by 2050** is critical for GDP growth

## 5. Strategic Interventions to Enhance Female LFPR ✏️

1. **STEM Encouragement**: women account for only **43% of STEM enrollment (2021–22)**; gap due to perceptions, care responsibilities, early marriage, high education costs
2. **The "Pink Tax" on Urban Mobility**: 2021 World Bank study — **31% of women** consider commuting a barrier; **13%** cited childcare, **19%** domestic duties as barriers
   - Solutions: visible women police (Kochi's Women Police Control Room vans, Hyderabad's SHE Teams), women driver training (NCRTC's 21-day course), **gender-responsive infrastructure** (safe crossings, lighting, emergency buttons — Chennai's inclusive street manual, Bhubaneswar's Janpath Street)
3. **Affordable Housing Infrastructure**: secure working women's hostels & affordable rental housing
   - Eg: **Sakhi Niwas scheme**, Tamil Nadu's 'Thozhi Hostels'
4. **Institutionalising the Care Economy**: expand Anganwadi centres, integrate community creches, professionalise care work
5. **Targeted Skilling Ecosystems**: align training with manufacturing, renewable energy, digital services, agro-processing
   - **'Back to Work'** and **'Returnship programmes'** for women returning from career breaks
   - **Digital literacy**, hybrid work capabilities, green job training
   - Integrate **SHGs with MSME ecosystem** → transition from subsistence to growth-oriented businesses
6. **Legislative Flexibility & Labour Codes**:
   - **Section 59(5), CSS 2020**: women can work from home
   - **Section 60, CSS 2020**: maternity benefits
   - **Section 67, CSS 2020**: mandates creches
   - **Section 3, Code on Wages 2019**: gender equality through equal wages
7. **PPP Models**: Tamil Nadu Thozhi Hostels, Telangana WE-Hub, Kerala Kudumbashree, Maharashtra Mahila Arthik Vikas Mahamandal
8. **Behavioral Change & Social Campaigns**: gender sensitisation in schools, community initiatives promoting shared caregiving

## 6. Unorganised Workforce

- **Code on Social Security, 2020 (CSS)** defines **unorganised workers** as home-based, self-employed or wage workers in unorganised sector + workers in organised sector not covered by Industrial Disputes Act, 1947

### eShram Portal
- **National Database of Unorganised Workers**
- As of Jan 2026: **31 crore workers** registered; **women comprise 54%**
- Each registrant gets **Universal Account Number (UAN)** linked to Aadhaar
- Interstate portability of **18 integrated welfare schemes** (incl. One-Nation-One-Ration Card, pensions)

### National Career Service (NCS)
- Launched 2015; comprehensive **employment-matching** institutional framework
- Job fair module connecting career centres, nodal officers, job seekers, employers
- Integrates with **SIDH, EPFO, ESIC, eShram, eMigrate**
- **5.9 crore registered job seekers**, **53 lakh job providers**, ~8 crore vacancies mobilised

## 7. Organised Manufacturing Sector: Scale & Spatial Concentration

### Structural Asymmetry (Small vs Large Factories) — ASI FY24
- **Small factories** (<100 employees): **77%** of total factories but only **21%** of workforce
- **Large factories**: only **22%** of factories but absorb **79%** of manufacturing workforce
- Continuous rise in **Net Value Added (NVA) per person engaged** — improved labour productivity
- **Dominance of Large Factories**:
  - (a) **More employment**: employment in large factories grew at **CAGR of 6%** vs **2%** in smaller units
  - (b) **More wages**: large factories pay higher wages, higher NVA per person
  - (c) **More increase in numbers**: large factories increased by **97%** over FY14–FY24 (small: only 26%)

### Spatial Concentration ✏️ 60% in 7 states
- Just **7 states** contribute ~**60%** of total manufacturing employment
- Led by: **Tamil Nadu (15%)**, Gujarat (13%), Maharashtra (13%), UP (8%), Karnataka (6%), Haryana (6%), Telangana (5%)
- States with higher share of large factories → higher TPE and superior labour productivity

## 8. Boosting the Manufacturing Workforce

### Labour Participation as an Expansion Imperative
- Manufacturing = central pillar for India's **strategic resilience, national self-reliance, global value chain integration**
- Cannot achieve global competitiveness through capital & technology alone — **non-negotiable focus on labour participation**
- Currently manufacturing: **11.4%** of total employment (**1.9 crore** in organised, **3.3 crore** in unorganised)

### Strategic Focus on Workplace Conditions
- Factory worker's experience defined not just by working hours but by **quality of working conditions** and adequate space for living/working — especially vital for **migrant workers**

### Tri-Dimensional Worker Well-Being Framework

#### (a) Financial Well-Being via Earned Wage Access (EWA)
- EWA = access part/all wages as earned, before next payday
- Financial stress severely reduces cognitive bandwidth
- **Impact (RCT, 800+ women workers)**: substantial reduction in **high-interest informal borrowings** and **20% decline** in forgoing essential expenditures

#### (b) Mental Well-Being via the Buddy System
- For new **migrant women workers** — paired with experienced peers ('buddies') sharing language/background
- Buddies trained in **cognitive-behavioural techniques** for emotional first-aid
- **Impact**: **5.3% reduction in anxiety**, **5.9% reduction in depression**, productivity boosts (**6.4%** new workers, **12%** senior buddies)

#### (c) Physical Well-Being via SRH Training
- **Sexual and Reproductive Health (SRH)** training directly at workplace
- Dismantles information barriers, restrictive social norms
- **Impact**: improved health behaviors, increased contraceptive use, significant increase in **reporting of sexual harassment**, greater awareness/confidence

## 9. Catalysing Job Growth: New Labour Codes

### Core Legislative Architecture
- Consolidates **29 central laws** into 4 comprehensive Labour Codes:
  1. **Code on Wages, 2019**
  2. **Industrial Relations Code, 2020**
  3. **Code on Social Security (CSS), 2020**
  4. **Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code, 2020**
- Core features: formal recognition for **gig workers**, mandatory **appointment letters** for all workers, **portable social security** for migrant workers, **equal benefits** for fixed-term & contract workers, **minimum wage**, timely payments, **national floor wage**

### Flexibility vs Regulation Trade-off
- **Flexibility for employers** (entrepreneurship, productive employment) + **Regulation & protection for workers** (social security, minimum wages, fair conditions)
- States with flexible labour laws → significantly higher employment, fixed capital, output

### Compliance Rationalisation & Streamlining
- Replace multiple overlapping jurisdictions with simplified compliance
- **Single pan-India registration/license/return** + single licence for contract staffing
- 'Inspector' → **'Inspector-cum-Facilitator'** — guidance, awareness, advisory roles alongside enforcement

### Case Study: Uttar Pradesh Labour Reform Blueprint
- Amended Factories Act 1948 — women can work in all **29 hazardous sectors** + **night shifts**
- Mandatory safeguards: employer-provided transportation, health facilities, **CCTV, security personnel**, anti-harassment squads, police beats
- **Double wages for overtime**, quarterly overtime cap raised to **144 hours**

### Multi-Dimensional Impact of Labour Codes

| Dimension | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Women Workforce** | Equal wages (Sec 3, Code on Wages), creches mandated (Sec 67 CSS), work from home (Sec 59(5) CSS), maternity benefits (Sec 60 CSS). Could raise FLFPR to **33.7%** (Indian Staffing Federation) |
| **Formalisation** | Mandatory appointment letters, equalising benefits for **Fixed-Term Employment (FTE)** & contract workers. SBI projects formalisation increase from **60.4% to 75.5%**. **Curbing 'Voluntary Dwarfism'**: higher thresholds for layoff/retrenchment/closure reduce incentives to stay small. Compliance simplification reduces costs **30–40% for SMEs** |
| **Social Security Infrastructure** | Aggregators must contribute **1–2% of annual turnover** (capped at 5% of worker payouts) for gig workers' life/health insurance & pensions — potentially covering **2.35 crore workers by 2030**. Portable benefits via **UAN** for interstate migrant workers |
| **Aggregate Incomes & Growth** | Could contribute **1.25% to GDP by 2029–30**. SBI: medium-term employment gains of **1.0–2.2%** in organised sector → ~**77 lakh jobs**, UR down by 0.3–1.3%. Code on Wages → boost disposable income, ~**₹75,000 crore into aggregate consumption** |

## 10. Gig Economy & Informal Workforce Dynamics

### Background: Changing Employer-Worker Relationship
- Shift from **fixed employer, fixed workplace, fixed salary, fixed hours** → **flexible, task-based, platform-mediated, demand-driven work**

### Work Relationship Features (Comparison Table)
| Feature | Regular Wage/Salaried | Casual | Self-employed | Gig/Platform | Fixed-term/Contract |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Employer connection | Direct | Direct (Bipartite) | No employer | Via digital platform (Tripartite) | Bipartite or Tripartite |
| Nature of work | Ongoing | Casual/Seasonal | Ongoing | Task Based | Ongoing |
| Payment | Fixed | Specific | Profits | Task based | Fixed |
| Hours | Fixed | Demand based | Self-determined | Self-determined | Fixed |
| Social Security | Yes | Not covered | Not covered | Not covered | Mixed |
| Leave | Paid Leave | No paid leave | Self-determined | No formal paid leave | Mixed |

### Data on Emergence of Gig Workers
- Increased from **77 lakh in FY21 to 120 lakh in FY25** — a **55% rise** in 4 years
- Now account for **over 2%** of India's total workforce
- Non-agricultural gig work projected to form **6.7% of workforce by 2029–30**

### Definition
- **ILO**: typically performed by self-employed/independent contractors, obtain clients through online platforms, paid upon completion of tasks
- **CSS 2020**: a person participating in a work arrangement outside traditional employer–employee relationship; aggregators = digital intermediaries connecting service providers with consumers

### Factors Behind Rise of Gig Economy
- **Economic restructuring**: firms prefer cost efficiency, outsourcing, flexible hiring
- **Technological progress**: **80 crore** smartphone users, **15 billion UPI transactions/month**
- **Rise of digital platforms**: delivery, transport, logistics, home services, online freelancing
- **Changing consumer demand**: fast, cheap, on-demand services
- **Worker-side flexibility**: supplementary income, flexible timing, labour market entry

### Importance of Gig Economy
- Quick work opportunity for **youth, migrants, semi-skilled, urban service workers**
- **Supplementary income** source
- **Flexibility** — useful for students, women, those who can't take fixed-hour jobs
- **Urban service delivery backbone** — delivery, mobility, e-commerce, logistics
- **Digital formalisation potential** — payment trails, work history, worker identity → access to credit, insurance, social security

### Problems/Challenges of Gig Economy
- **Income uncertainty**: ~**40% earn below ₹15,000/month**
- **Weak social security**: no regular insurance, pension, paid leave, accident cover, maternity benefits
- **Algorithmic control**: platforms decide work allocation, ratings, incentives, deactivation through opaque algorithms
- **Credit & financial exclusion**: irregular income → **thin-file credit problem**
- **Limited upward mobility**: stuck in low-skill, low-paying gigs due to limited skilling; fear of replacement by **AI and Machine Learning**

### Definition Problem in Gig Work
- CSS 2020 defines gig worker as **one broad group** — but reality is highly differentiated (high-skilled freelancer vs cab driver vs delivery worker face very different risks)
- **NITI Aayog projection**: by 2030, **high-skilled gig workers = 27.5%**, **low-skilled = 33.8%**

### McKinsey Categorisation of Gig Workers
1. **Free agents** — primary income, prefer gig work
2. **Casual earners** — supplementary income, skills/side income
3. **Reluctants** — rely on gig work but prefer regular jobs
4. **Financially strapped** — gig work to make ends meet
- **Policy aim**: move (iii) Reluctants + (iv) Financially strapped → (i) Free agents + (ii) Casual earners — **make gig work a choice, not a necessity**

### Measures Taken for Gig & Platform Workers

| Measure | Details |
|---|---|
| **CSS 2020** | First formal definition of gig & platform workers; welfare measures: life/disability cover, accident insurance, health/maternity benefits, old-age protection |
| **Social Security Fund** | To finance welfare schemes for gig/platform workers |
| **National Social Security Board** | Board to support welfare framework |
| **eShram portal** | Launched **26 Aug 2021**; national database incl. gig/platform workers; UAN on self-declaration. **eShram One-Stop Solution** launched **21 Oct 2024** |
| **Aggregator module on eShram** | Launched **12 Dec 2024**; **12 major aggregators** (Zomato, Swiggy, Uber, Ola, Amazon, Zepto, Urban Company) onboarded |
| **Budget 2025–26** | eShram registration, identity cards, **Ayushman Bharat-PMJAY health coverage** for online platform workers |
| **PM-JAY interoperability** | Data mapping, verification & information exchange between eShram and AB-PMJAY |
| **Registration drives** | 3 nationwide drives in 2025; ~**5.12 lakh platform workers** registered by 1 Dec 2025 |
| **Stakeholder consultations** | With aggregators, platform worker unions, knowledge partners, states/UTs |
| **State-level initiatives** | Rajasthan: welfare-board model with aggregator contribution; Karnataka: proposed similar framework |

### Way Forward: Making Gig Work a Choice, Not Compulsion
- **Move from recognition to delivery** under CSS → actual insurance, health cover, accident cover, maternity benefit, old-age protection
- **Portable social security** — benefits follow worker across platforms via **eShram, identity cards, UAN**
- **Income protection** — minimum per-hour or per-task earnings, compensation for waiting time
- **Co-investment in assets & training** — platforms/employers co-invest in vehicles, tools, digital devices, skilling
- **Financial resilience** — low-cost emergency savings, budgeting support, financial literacy, flexible credit/insurance products
- **Algorithmic & platform accountability** — **algorithmic transparency, data access, competition rules, grievance redressal**
- **Better data visibility** — PLFS should identify **gig workers separately**, not merge them with self-employed/casual labour

### Global Best Practices on Gig Work
- **Spain — Rider Law, 2021**: food-delivery riders recognised as **employees**, not self-employed contractors; attention to algorithms at workplace
- **EU — Platform Workers' Directive, 2024**: correcting **misclassification of workers**, regulating algorithmic & data-based decision-making
- **ILO, 2025**: global standards for **decent work in platform economy** — fair wages, working conditions, social protection

### Conclusion
- Gig work reflects changing nature of work in digital economy
- Policy goal: make flexibility more secure → workers enter gig work by **real choice**, not because of weak demand, skill mismatch, or absence of safety net
- Gig work must evolve into **dignified, protected and upwardly mobile employment**

## 11. Skill Ecosystem

### Current Situation: Training Happening, But Marketable Skills Weak ✏️ Output vs Outcome
- Training coverage widened: vocational/technical training (ages 15–59) rose from **8.1% in 2017–18 to 34.7% in 2023–24**
- But **formal skilling still limited**: among youth 15–29, only **4.9%** received formal vocational/technical training; **21.2%** depended on informal sources
- **PARAKH Rashtriya Sarvekshan 2024**: only **47% of schools** offer skill-based courses at Grade IX+; student participation only **29%**
- A **12 percentage-point rise** in skilled workforce through formal skilling → raise employment in labour-intensive sectors by **over 13% by 2030**
- **Outcome gap remains**: not just whether people are being trained, but whether training leads to **employment, retention, higher earnings, job quality**

### Challenges in India's Skilling Ecosystem
1. **Supply-driven skilling**: programmes chase enrolment, training numbers, certification — not employment outcomes
2. **Weak foundational readiness**: gaps in **literacy, numeracy, digital ability, soft skills**
3. **Industry mismatch**: training doesn't match actual vacancies, emerging sectors, local investment pipelines
4. **Limited employer ownership**: employers not deeply involved in **curriculum design, apprenticeships, assessment**
5. **Poor placement & retention support**: placement treated as target not professional service; weak counselling, unrealistic wage expectations
6. **Weak TVET**: **outdated curricula, inadequately trained instructors, infrastructure gaps**
7. **Accountability gaps**: inflated numbers, weak attendance checks, data gaps, fund delays, low placement outcomes
8. **Low employability despite certification**: youth employability ~**56.3%** — certification ≠ job readiness
9. **Low social status of vocational work**: traditional sectors (weaving, dairy, entry-level manufacturing) seen as **low-status, low-paying**
10. **Financing constraints**: public funding insufficient; credit constraints, information gaps
11. **Fragmented governance**: multiple ministries, schemes, levels of government; without convergence → duplication, less responsiveness to local demand

### Measures Taken for Improving India's Skilling Ecosystem

#### Scheme-Based

| Scheme | Details |
|---|---|
| **PMKVY 4.0** | NSQF-aligned job roles by Sector Skill Councils; focus: **digital tech, green energy, healthcare, advanced agriculture, financial services, e-commerce** |
| **Rozgar Melas & Apprenticeship Melas** | Improve interface between employers & job seekers |
| **Flexi-MoU model** | Firms customise training per evolving needs; ~**10,000 trainees** through onboarded industrial partners |
| **PM Vishwakarma Kaushal Samman Yojana** | **18 traditional trades**; skill upgradation, digital certificate, toolkit incentive, digital transaction incentive, concessional credit; **30 lakh** registered, **23.09 lakh** trained |
| **ITI Upgradation / PM SETU** | Upgrading **1,000 govt ITIs** (200 hub + 800 spoke); smart classrooms, modern labs, digital content, industry-aligned courses |
| **Future-skills courses in ITIs** | **169 NSQF-compliant trades** including **31 future-skills courses** (AI, IoT, renewable energy, 3D printing) |
| **National Centres of Excellence** | 5 sector-specific centres with global institutional partners |
| **National Credit Framework** | Credit accumulation & transfer across **academic, vocational & work-based learning** |
| **SWAYAM Plus** | Industry-aligned digital courses: **AI, data analytics, robotics** |
| **NATS 2.0** | Apprenticeship-based learning; **12 lakh DBT transfers**; stipend support |
| **SIDH of MSDE** | Centralised information hub for all govt initiatives in skill, education, employment, entrepreneurship |

#### Financing Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Details |
|---|---|
| **NAPS 2.0** | Govt transfers **25% of prescribed stipend directly to apprentices** via DBT |
| **Direct stipend support** | ~**₹1,110.64 crore** released as stipend by 31 Oct 2025 |
| **Model Skill Loan Scheme, 2024** | Credit-based access to skill training; increased loan limits, widened eligible lenders |
| **Skill loans via SIDH-linked courses** | Extended to eligible **non-NSQF courses** on SIDH |
| **Skill Impact Bond** | Outcome-based financing: payment linked to **placement & retention outcomes**, not just enrolment |
| **Employer co-investment** | Firms co-invest via voluntary/mandatory contributions → training matches workplace needs |
| **Industry funding via Sector Skill Councils** | In **FY25**, ~**1.13 lakh candidates** trained through industry funding |
| **Skill vouchers** | **Demand-side financing**: learner gets voucher, chooses provider; payment linked to completion/certification/placement; Eg: **Maharashtra's Vikalp Skill Voucher Programme** → **60% job retention** |

### How to Fix Skilling: From Training Numbers to Labour-Market Value

#### Making Skilling 'Work' for Upskilling & Employability
1. **Shift incentives from training numbers to outcomes**: evaluate trainees' full journey — Baseline → Certification + placement + job role → Retention (6/12 months) → Earnings improvement → Employer demand → Rank courses & institutions → Expand high performers / Phase out weak ones
2. **Make employers core partners**: involve in **curriculum design, workplace exposure, assessment**
3. **Use local labour-market intelligence**: map **district-level vacancies, investment pipelines, migration corridors, sectoral shifts**; phase out low-demand courses
4. **Professionalise placement services**: aptitude counselling, verified interviews, **wage expectation-setting, mobility support, post-placement follow-up**
5. **Strengthen credibility of certification**: digital attendance, audit trails, third-party assessment, action against ghost centres
6. **Upgrade ITIs**: better trainers, modern labs, updated curricula, stronger industry linkage — make them aspirational

#### Other Important Measures
- **Make Skilling Modular & Lifelong**: short, stackable, flexible modules for continuous upgrading
- **Whole-of-Government Coordination**: horizontal (ministries use common standards, shared data) + vertical (centre-state-district alignment with local demand)
- **Link Financing with Outcomes**: Skill loans, employer co-investment, skill vouchers, Skill Impact Bonds tied to placement, retention, earnings

#### Skill Vouchers (Detail)
- **Demand-side financing tool**: learner receives voucher, chooses course/institute
- Training institute redeems after meeting **course completion, certification or placement** conditions
- Creates competition among providers; pushes quality improvement
- Eg: **Maharashtra's Vikalp Skill Voucher Programme** — linked payments to outcomes, **60% job retention**

### Digital Public Infrastructure for Skill Tracking: Skill-Employment Data Loop
- Integrated loop connecting: SIDH (training + credentials) → NCS (vacancies + employer demand) → eShram (informal/unorganised workers) → EPFO (formal job verification) → ESIC (employment/social security verification) → APAAR (education records + skill pathway) → PM Vishwakarma (traditional artisans/skilling support)
- Result: **better tracking, matching and accountability** → move from scattered schemes to integrated **skill-employment information system**

### Best Practices in Skilling Reform

#### Odisha's "Skill in Odisha" Model — Fix, Scale and Accelerate
- **Fix the ITIs**: improved image through alumni success stories, better uniforms, ITI fests, global exposure. **10-6-4-2 formula**: 10 successful alumni (6 working outside Odisha, 4 women achievers, 2 entrepreneurs)
- **Scale short-term training**: expanded DDU-GKY with better trainee support, rural outreach, alumni-led awareness
- **Accelerate advanced training**: created **World Skill Centre, Bhubaneswar** with international collaboration — advanced training, scholarships, safety & life skills

#### Global Evaluation Practices: Europe & East Asia
- **Singapore**: track post-course **employability & wage effects** → shape future course design & budget
- **Germany & Switzerland**: employers structurally involved in **curriculum design, workplace learning, assessment** → natural feedback loop
- **Korea**: active labour-market programmes periodically reviewed; weak interventions pruned, effective ones expanded

## 12. Future-Ready Workforce through Apprenticeships

### About
- Bridge between **classroom learning and real workplace skills**
- Must move beyond traditional trades → **green manufacturing, logistics, digital services, new-age sectors**

### Progress So Far
- **PM-NAPS**: **43.47 lakh apprentices** across **36 states/UTs**, **51,000 establishments**
- Female participation: ~**20%**
- **NATS**: **5.23 lakh apprentices** in FY25
- **NAPS 2.0**: improved transparency via DBT for stipend share

### Key Gaps
- **Low enterprise participation**: only **6,100 enterprises** active under NAPS vs **5.2 lakh SMEs** registered nationally
- **Fragmented governance**: NAPS & NATS under different ministries (MSDE vs Ministry of Education)
- **Regional imbalance**: Maharashtra & UP have high registrations; North-East very low
- **Weak post-training pathway**: need clearer links to jobs, entrepreneurship, credit, further skilling

### Way Forward
- **Unified apprenticeship framework** across NAPS, NATS, similar schemes
- **District Skill Committees** for outreach in low-registration areas
- **MSME cluster models** with graded incentives for smaller firms
- Expand into **green jobs, logistics, digital services, gig-linked sectors**
- Provide **insurance, travel & accommodation support**
- Link with **Recognition of Prior Learning under PMKY** and formal credit pathways

## 13. Vocational Education: From Classroom to Career Readiness

### Current Gaps
- **Late exposure to skills**: starts too late, close to labour market entry
- **Low school-level coverage**: only **47% of schools** offer skill courses at Grade IX+; participation only **29%**
- **Academic bias**: narrow focus on general academic routes → vocational pathways look less aspirational
- **Weak industry connect**: students lack workplace exposure, career counselling
- **Skill mismatch**: 2025 National Skill Gap Study — weak foundational skills, mismatch with industry, difficulty adapting to emerging tech
- **Limited mobility across pathways**: vocational, academic, work-based learning not fully integrated

### What India Is Doing
- **Samagra Shiksha**: vocational learning linked with general education
- **Employability Skills**: communication, self-management, ICT, entrepreneurship, green skills in vocational courses
- **CBSE from 2025–26**: Skill Education in Grades VI–VIII through **Kaushal Bodh** textbooks (project-based, hands-on)
- **National Credit Framework**: credit transfer across academic, vocational, work-based learning
- **Multiple entry-exit provisions**: re-enter higher education with prior credits
- **SWAYAM Plus**: industry-aligned digital courses (AI, data analytics, robotics)
- **STARS programme** (World Bank): Madhya Pradesh's Skill GPS app, Rajasthan's Comprehensive Career Education Programme, Maharashtra's Career Portal

### Way Forward
- **Start early, make it practical**: skill exposure from middle/secondary levels through **skill labs, project-based learning**
- **Link vocational education with job-potential sectors**: agriculture, agro-processing, advisory services, retail, IT, banking (World Bank report)
- **Strengthen teacher preparation**: updated technical training, industry exposure
- **Ensure mobility across pathways**: National Credit Framework, multiple entry-exit, SWAYAM Plus
- **Make vocational learning aspirational**: showcase successful skilled workers, entrepreneurs, alumni

### Global Best Practices
- **Germany, China, Australia, USA**: vocational choices at school level
- **Germany & Switzerland**: dual/hybrid models combining classroom + workplace training; employers participate in curriculum, training, assessment
- **South Korea's "Job First, University Later"** + Germany's mobility routes: vocational education need not block higher education

## 14. Prime Minister Internship Scheme (PMIS)

### About
- Announced in **Union Budget 2024–25**
- Aims to provide **1 crore internships in top 500 companies over 5 years**
- Bridges India's education–employment gap through structured workplace exposure

### Key Features
- **Target group**: Youth aged **21–24 years**, not in full-time employment/regular education
- **Minimum qualification**: Class 10 and above (incl. ITI, Polytechnic, graduates)
- **Duration**: **12 months**, at least half in real workplace/job-based learning
- **Financial support**: **₹5,000/month stipend** + one-time grant of **₹6,000**
- **Social protection**: Insurance under **PM Jeevan Jyoti Bima Yojana** and **PM Suraksha Bima Yojana**
- **Excluded**: elite institution students, professional/PG degree holders, those already in NAPS/NATS, families with income >₹8 lakh, govt employee families

### Concerns from Pilot Experience
- **Low acceptance**: ~**1.65 lakh offers** across two phases, acceptance remained low (<one-third accepted)
- **Mismatch problem**: candidates declined due to **location, role & duration** concerns
- **Poor completion**: ~one-fifth of accepted offers left before completion
- **Weak financial incentive**: ₹5,000/month may be inadequate in urban centres (relocation + living costs)
- **Underutilisation of funds**: large budgetary allocation but low spending → gap between policy intent and execution
- **Credibility risk**: target of **1 crore internships** may lose credibility if pilot outcomes remain weak

### Way Forward
- **Improve matching**: skill profiling, location preference, sector fit before offers
- **Recalibrate stipend**: link to local living costs, esp. for urban/outstation internships
- **Mandate learning outcomes**: internships end with recognised certification aligned with **NSQF**
- **Strengthen industry accountability**: companies report completion, skill acquisition, post-internship outcomes as **CSR-linked disclosure**
- **Decentralise outreach**: ITIs, polytechnics, district employment offices → reach **non-elite and semi-urban youth**
- **Reward completion & hiring**: companies with higher completion and post-internship hiring get incentives
