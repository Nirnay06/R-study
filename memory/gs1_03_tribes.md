## 1. Who Are the Tribes?

- Tribal communities — popularly known as **Adivasis** (original inhabitants) — are distinct social and cultural groups whose identity is rooted in an ancestral relationship with land, forests, and a specific territory.
- Unlike caste society (organized around ritual hierarchy and occupational stratification), tribal identity rests on **kinship, territoriality, and collective solidarity**.
- The Indian Constitution uses the administrative label **Scheduled Tribes (STs)**, defined under **Article 342**, which empowers the President to notify tribal communities.

### Lokur Committee (1965) — Five Criteria for ST Identification
1. Primitive traits
2. Distinctive culture
3. Geographical isolation
4. Shyness of contact with the broader community
5. Backwardness

### Why Are They Called 'Scheduled Tribes'?
- Their names are officially scheduled/listed in the **Constitution (Scheduled Tribes) Order, 1950** (amended from time to time).
- The Order was issued by the President after consultation with state Governors; amended several times by Parliament under Article 342.
- This listing gives formal legal recognition as a distinct social group, enabling reservation, welfare schemes, and development programs.

## 2. Economic and Social Features of Tribal Society

### Economic Features
- Livelihoods are pre-agricultural or semi-agricultural — hunting, gathering, pastoralism, and shifting cultivation (**Jhum**)
- Land is **communally owned** and collectively managed, not held as individual private property
- Economic exchange operates through **reciprocity and barter** rather than profit-oriented market transactions
- Largely subsistence economies with minimal surplus generation
- Use of primitive and simple tools and technology

### Social and Cultural Features
- Each tribe has its own distinct language, dress, customs, and systems of marriage and inheritance
- Religion is **animistic** — centred on nature-worship, ancestor-worship, and spirit-worship
- Tribal societies are often **egalitarian**, with minimal social stratification compared to caste-based systems
- Social organisation combines **exogamous clans** (must marry outside the clan) within an **endogamous tribe** (must marry within the tribe)
- Governance rests with **clan councils and village elders** — community-based, not state-delegated
- Rich folk traditions — dance, music, oral literature, and festivals — tied to agricultural seasons and ecological cycles
- Women generally enjoy **greater economic participation and social visibility** than in mainstream caste Hindu society
- Some tribes maintain **youth dormitories** — e.g., **Ghotul** among the Gonds and **Dhumkuria** among the Oraons — spaces for socialisation, cultural education, and collective training

### The Immense Internal Diversity

**Population scale:**
- Gond tribe: over 12 million — one of the world's largest tribal communities
- Great Andamanese: fewer than 50, on the verge of extinction
- This range illustrates the absurdity of treating tribes as a uniform group

**Linguistic diversity — four major language families:**
- **Austroasiatic**: Santhali, Mundari, Ho
- **Dravidian**: Gondi, Kondh, Toda
- **Tibeto-Burman**: Naga languages, Bodo, Mizo
- **Indo-Aryan**: Bhili, Halbi
- Only **Santhali** has been recognised in the **Eighth Schedule** — the sole tribal language with official constitutional status

**Social and kinship diversity:**
- **Khasi of Meghalaya**: practice **matriliny** — property and clan identity pass through the mother's line; youngest daughter inherits the ancestral home (though men may retain ritual authority). Matriliny is also found among the **Nairs of Kerala** and the Minangkabau communities
- **Gonds and Bhils**: patrilineal
- **Todas of the Nilgiris**: historically practiced **fraternal polyandry** (a woman marrying multiple brothers), tied to land-resource management

**Economic diversity:**

| Livelihood | Example Tribes |
|-----------|---------------|
| Shifting cultivation (Jhum) | Nagas, Mizos |
| Settled agriculture | Gonds, Bhils |
| Forest-based gathering | Chenchus, honey collectors |
| Pastoralism | Todas (cattle), Bakarwals (sheep/goat) |
| Plantation labour | Tea tribes of Assam (historically indentured) |
| Fishing | Coastal tribes of Andaman, Kerala, Odisha |

**Racial diversity:**
- Dravidian racial group: Gonds, Konds, Todas
- Mongoloid group: Bhutias of Sikkim, most Northeast tribes
- Proto-Australoid: Mundas, Santhals, Ho — among India's oldest inhabitants

**Developmental diversity:**
- Meenas of Rajasthan: emerged as politically and administratively influential
- Shompen of Andaman Islands: remain almost entirely isolated from administrative systems

## 4. The Single Category Debate — Diversity vs. Unity

### When the Single-Category Framework Is Valid
- **Constitutional Safeguards**: Notification as STs under Article 342 and uniform protections under Articles 15(4), 16(4), 330, 332, Fifth and Sixth Schedules, PESA, and FRA require a collective category to function legally
- **Shared Structural Vulnerability**: All tribal communities share common disadvantages in poverty, literacy, health, and asset ownership
- **Common Historical Experience**: History of geographical isolation, colonial dispossession, marginalisation, and post-Independence displacement
- **Institutional Mechanisms**: Bodies like Tribal Advisory Councils, Tribal Research Institutes, and NCST depend on a unified category

### When the Single-Category Framework Must Be Modified
- **Reservation & Welfare**: Sub-classification necessary so most deprived sub-groups are prioritised, preventing "winner-takes-all" scenarios
- **Education**: Uniform regional language policy erodes distinct tribal identities; teaching in native languages improves learning outcomes
- **Urban vs. Rural tribal concerns**: Forest-dwelling tribes need community forest rights; urban tribal youth need skill development and property rights — fundamentally different needs
- **Customary Law**: Imposing a uniform tribal law risks overriding legitimate customary rights
- **Political Representation**: Tribes that remain political minorities in states like Jharkhand or Odisha need far stronger safeguards than those who already hold demographic/political majority in Mizoram or Meghalaya

**Guiding principle: "Protect together, develop differently."**

## 5. Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)

- **75 tribal communities** identified as PVTGs — the most marginalised among the marginalised
- Earlier called **Primitive Tribal Groups**, renamed in **2006** to reflect greater sensitivity

### Defining Characteristics of PVTGs
- Pre-agricultural or stagnant technology — dependent on hunting, gathering, or shifting cultivation
- Declining or stagnant population
- Extremely low literacy and minimal contact with mainstream society
- Economic backwardness

### Government Scheme: PM JANMAN (2023)
- Dedicated **PVTG Development Mission** under PM JANMAN scheme (2023)
- Focuses exclusively on: housing, roads, clean water, and healthcare for PVTG communities
- Acknowledgement that the most vulnerable need targeted, not trickle-down, attention

## 6. Denotified Tribes (DNTs) — India's Forgotten Communities

- Communities criminalized by the British under the **Criminal Tribes Act, 1871** — branded as "born criminals" simply by birth
- After Independence, the Act was **repealed in 1952**, and these communities were "denotified"
- But the stigma never left — DNTs living largely on the margins of both mainstream society and official welfare systems

### Core Challenges of DNTs
- Constant police suspicion and harassment
- High illiteracy, poverty, and lack of land rights

### Commissions on DNTs
- **Renke Commission (2008)**: Documented the plight of DNTs
- **Idate Commission (2017)**: Further documented their conditions
- Yet systematic policy action remains largely incomplete

## 7. Unique Features of Tribal Society

### The Sex Ratio Paradox — More Equal Than Mainstream India
- Tribal sex ratio: approximately **990 females per 1,000 males**
- National average: 943 females per 1,000 males
- A powerful counter-example to the assumption that India's social inequalities are universal

### Why Is the Tribal Sex Ratio More Favourable?
1. **Less rigid patriarchy**: Women participate visibly in economic and social life — not restricted or policed as in upper-caste households
2. **Economic value of women**: Women are central to tribal agriculture, forest gathering, and household economy — reduces son preference
3. **Bride price instead of dowry**: Groom's family pays the bride's family — daughter's birth becomes economically advantageous rather than a financial burden, fundamentally altering the incentive structure
4. **Limited access to sex-selective technology**: Remote tribal areas historically had less access to sex-determination technology

**Social Significance**: In this specific dimension, tribal social structures are demonstrably more equitable than those of mainstream Hindu society — challenges the simplistic "backwardness" narrative.

## 8. Tribal Knowledge Systems — Wisdom at the Margins

Tribal knowledge is **fundamentally different** from mainstream knowledge — not in quality or validity, but in **mode of production, transmission, ownership, and application**. It is transmitted through **folklore, songs, myths, and ritual** — ensuring intergenerational continuity through oral traditions embedded in lived practice.

### Ecological Wisdom
- Deep, intergenerational knowledge of local ecosystems — plant species, animal behaviour, seasonal cycles, soil conditions, water sources
- **Bishnoi community of Rajasthan**: Practiced conservation of trees and wildlife for over five centuries; Chipko movement of the 1970s drew directly on this tradition
- **Soliga tribes of Karnataka's Biligiri Rangana (BRT) Hills**: Documented knowledge of more than 100 medicinal plant species
- **Sacred Groves**: Forests protected by tribal communities for religious reasons — significant for biodiversity conservation
  - Deorais (Odisha), Orans (Rajasthan), Kovils (Tamil Nadu)

### Traditional Medicine
- Tribal medicine uses plants, roots, barks, and minerals — knowledge accumulated and refined over generations
- **Irula tribe of Tamil Nadu**: Unique expertise in snakes and their venom; knowledge actively used by antivenom research institutions
- Many modern pharmaceutical compounds derived from plants first identified and used by tribal healers
- This has led to the issue of **bio-piracy** — external corporations patenting tribal knowledge without consent, acknowledgment, or benefit-sharing

### Sustainable Resource Management
- Traditional **Jhum (shifting cultivation)**: When practiced with adequate fallow periods of **10–15 years** — ecologically sustainable and maintains soil fertility
- Problem arises when population pressure and legal restrictions reduce fallow periods to 2–3 years
- Community-managed forests under tribal governance often maintain higher biodiversity than state-managed or commercially leased forests

### Comparison: Tribal vs. Mainstream Knowledge Systems

| Dimension | Tribal Systems | Mainstream Systems |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Connection to nature | Very strong; lived, local, ecosystem-specific | More abstracted; generalised and decontextualised |
| Knowledge generation | Practice, community consensus, oral validation over generations | Formal research, peer review, empirical verification |
| Transmission | Oral — stories, songs, rituals, apprenticeship | Written — textbooks, institutions, digital archives |
| Scope | Holistic — medicinal, ecological, spiritual interwoven | Specialised — disciplines strictly compartmentalised |
| Ownership | Communal; embedded in community identity | Individual/institutional; governed by patents and IPR |
| Vulnerability | High — erodes with loss of elders or cultural disruption | Better archived; institutionally durable |
| Sustainability orientation | Tailored to local ecosystems; often inherently sustainable | Can prioritise productivity over sustainability |

### Significance and Challenges
- Failures of industrial agriculture, accelerating biodiversity loss, and the climate crisis have made tribal ecological wisdom **more relevant, not less**
- Key challenges:
  1. **Bio-piracy**: Corporate and pharmaceutical interests have repeatedly patented tribal knowledge — from neem to turmeric — without community consent or compensation
  2. **Erosion of Living Knowledge**: Displacement, urbanisation, and declining use of tribal languages causing irreversible knowledge loss

### Way Forward for Knowledge Systems
- **Legal Protection**: Enforce the **Nagoya Protocol** (under the Convention on Biological Diversity) to prevent misappropriation and ensure benefit-sharing
- **Community-led Documentation**: Knowledge must be recorded with communities through **free, prior, and informed consent** — not extracted from them
- **Complementarity, not Replacement**: Integrate indigenous practices into mainstream policy rather than treating them as primitive alternatives
  - *"Protecting tribal knowledge is not an act of cultural charity — it is an ecological and civilisational necessity."*

## 9. Models of Integration — The Historic Debate

| Model | Core Idea | Critique |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Isolation** | Tribes are "pristine" — protect them from any outside contact | Freezes tribes in time; romanticises poverty; denies communities the right to choose change |
| **Assimilation** | Tribes are "backward Hindus" — absorb them into mainstream society | Destroys cultural identity; forces the dominant culture; erases distinct constitutional rights |
| **Integration with Identity (Nehru-Elwin Model)** | Tribes develop on their own terms, retaining culture and rights while accessing education and development | The constitutional approach — endorsed as the right balance |

## 10. Nehru's Tribal Panchsheel — Five Guiding Principles

Nehru articulated five principles for tribal development — the constitutional and moral foundation of tribal policy:
1. Tribes should develop according to their own **genius** — no external blueprint imposed
2. Tribal **land rights** must be protected; no land alienation
3. Tribal **language and culture** must be respected and supported
4. **Tribal administration** should be through tribals themselves, not outsiders
5. The **Governor's special responsibility** under the Fifth Schedule for tribal welfare

## 11. Constitutional and Legal Framework for Tribal Welfare

### Constitutional Provisions

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Article 342** | President notifies STs by public notification; Parliament may include/exclude by law |
| **Article 244** | Fifth Schedule (10 states) and Sixth Schedule (Northeast) administration |
| **Article 244A** | Power to create an autonomous state within Assam for certain tribal areas |
| **Article 275(1)** | Special grants to states for tribal welfare and Scheduled Area development |
| **Article 338A** | National Commission for Scheduled Tribes (NCST) with advisory and oversight functions |
| **Articles 15(4) & 16(4)** | Reservation in education and employment for STs |
| **Articles 330 & 332** | Reservation of seats in Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies |
| **Article 46** | DPSP — State to promote educational and economic interests of STs, protect from exploitation |
| **Articles 23 & 24** | Prohibition of human trafficking, forced labour, and child labour — acutely relevant for tribal vulnerability |
| **Article 164(1)** | Dedicated Minister for tribal welfare in Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, MP, and Odisha |
| **Article 19(5)** | Permits restrictions on movement and settlement rights in Scheduled Areas to protect tribal land |

### Fifth Schedule (Article 244)
- Applies to **10 states** with Scheduled Areas
- Gives the **Governor special powers** to oversee tribal administration, including:
  - Reporting to the President
  - Making regulations to restrict land transfer and money-lending to non-tribals

### Sixth Schedule (Article 244)
- Applies to tribal areas in **Assam, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and Tripura**
- Provides **Autonomous District Councils (ADCs)** with legislative, executive, and judicial powers for local self-governance

### Key Legislations

#### 1. PESA — Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996
- Extended Panchayati Raj to tribal Scheduled Areas
- Gave **Gram Sabhas** (village assemblies) powers over:
  - Control over natural resources
  - Minor forest produce
  - Land acquisition decisions
  - Regulation of money lending
  - Oversight of local development plans
- Recognised customary tribal law and traditional governance as legally valid
- **Challenge**: Suffered from weak implementation — several states delayed or incompletely framed the required rules; land acquisition and mineral projects in Scheduled Areas often proceeded without effective Gram Sabha participation, especially for minor minerals

#### 2. Forest Rights Act (FRA), 2006 — The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act
- Landmark tribal law recognising the **historical injustice of colonial forest control** and restoring rights to forest-dwelling communities
- Provides:
  - Individual rights over occupied forest land
  - Community rights over forest resources and sacred areas
  - Empowers Gram Sabhas to protect and manage community forests
- **Challenge**: "Process as punishment" crisis — about **52 lakh claims filed**, nearly **36% rejected** due to strict evidence rules. Issue is not just land titles but also about restoring governance, as Gram Sabhas struggle to exercise control over forest resources amid industrial and conservation pressures

#### 3. SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 (Amended 2015)
- Provides stringent penalties for crimes against SCs and STs
- Mandates special courts for speedy trial
- Places burden of proof on the accused in certain categories of offences
- **Challenge**: Conviction rates remain very low; many cases not registered due to fear, social pressure, or police reluctance
- The **2018 Supreme Court judgment** that diluted arrest provisions under the Act (later reversed by Parliament) exposed political contestation around tribal and Dalit protection laws

#### 4. Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act (LARR), 2013
- Mandates **Social Impact Assessment**, consent from affected communities, and stronger rehabilitation requirements before land acquisition
- Significantly strengthened protections compared to the colonial-era Land Acquisition Act of 1894
- **Challenge**: Implementation remains uneven; exemptions for certain infrastructure and defence projects reduce protective scope

## 12. Major Problems and Structural Vulnerabilities

### Displacement — The Defining Crisis of Tribal India

*"If one concept defines the tribal experience of post-Independence India, it is displacement."*

Tribal communities inhabit precisely the regions that the dominant development model most aggressively targets: **mineral-rich plateaus, river valleys, and forest lands**.

#### Why Tribes Are Disproportionately Displaced
- Tribal lands sit above India's richest deposits — coal, iron ore, bauxite, and uranium across Central and Eastern India
- Oral land tenure traditions are unrecognised by state systems — making acquisition legally easier
- Forest-based livelihoods directly conflict with both commercial forestry and conservation models
- Political marginalisation limits capacity to resist through legal or electoral channels

#### Why Rehabilitation Fails
- Displacement is rarely a one-time disruption — it creates **cycles of permanent poverty**
- Cash compensation cannot replace land, community networks, or cultural identity
- Tribal communities uprooted from forests cannot replicate forest-based livelihoods in urban resettlement sites
- Studies on **Narmada, POSCO, and Vedanta-affected areas** consistently show displaced families worse off — economically and socially — even two decades later

### Cultural Erosion and Identity Threats
- **Language extinction**: Rapid mainstreaming through migration, formal education, and media is weakening tribal languages; hundreds of tribal dialects face extinction within a generation
- **Religious and social divisions**: Missionary activity in tribal areas has been a force of both genuine education and cultural transformation — creating internal community divisions in Jharkhand, Odisha, and Northeast India; the conversion debate intersects with tribal identity, land rights, and political mobilisation
- **Left-wing extremism**: Maoist/Naxalite movement exploited genuine tribal grievances about displacement and land alienation to draw tribal youth into armed resistance; tribal communities are caught between security forces and armed groups — it is also a symptom of the state's failure to address tribal land and livelihood rights through legitimate institutional channels
- **Demographic displacement**: Mainstream migration into tribal homelands — particularly in Assam and Jharkhand — threatens tribal cultural and political dominance; underpins "Sons of Soil" politics and demands for Inner Line Permit protections
- **Commodification of culture**: Tribal art forms — Warli paintings, Gond art, tribal dance — risk being reduced to tourist products and exotic spectacle, stripped of their cultural, spiritual, and community significance

### Health — A Triple Burden
Tribal communities face three simultaneous and compounding health crises:
1. **Malnutrition**: NFHS-5 data shows under-five stunting among ST children at around **40.9%**
2. **Communicable diseases**: Tribal areas carry a disproportionate burden of **malaria** and elevated tuberculosis risk
3. **Sickle Cell Disease (SCD)**: A genetic blood disorder disproportionately affecting tribal communities across Central India
   - **PM-JUGA scheme (2024)** includes SCD screening as a specific targeted component — first major policy acknowledgment of this community-specific health burden

## 13. Contemporary Challenges and Emerging Issues

### Climate Vulnerability
- Tribal communities inhabit ecologically sensitive regions — forests, hills, river basins, and coastal areas — most severely affected by climate change
- They are simultaneously the **least responsible** for carbon emissions and **among the most vulnerable** to its consequences
- Specific impacts:
  - Irregular rainfall disrupts subsistence agriculture and Jhum cultivation cycles
  - Forest degradation and diversion threaten Minor Forest Produce that constitutes tribal livelihoods
  - Tribal communities in the Andaman Islands and coastal areas face sea-level rise
- At the same time, tribal ecological knowledge offers critical insights for climate adaptation strategies

### Demands for Territorial Autonomy
- Many tribal communities demand separate administrative units to govern themselves according to their own customs and traditions
- **Bhil Pradesh Demand**: Demand for a separate state carved out of tribal-majority districts across **Rajasthan, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, and Maharashtra**
  - **Bhils** are India's **second-largest tribal community**
  - Their argument: within current state boundaries, their cultural identity, language, and developmental needs consistently take a back seat
  - A separate state would give them real control over decisions affecting their lives

### Inter-Tribal Conflicts
- Conflicts typically revolve around land, identity, political representation, and access to resources
- **Karbi–Kuki conflict in Assam** and **Meitei–Kuki tensions in Manipur** are clear examples of how overlapping land claims and identity disputes can turn violent
- Resolution requires community dialogue, fair resource distribution, recognition of customary rights, and inclusive governance structures

### Customary Law vs. Uniform Civil Code
- The **UCC debate** has specific and acute implications for tribal communities
- Tribal societies are governed by **customary laws** that regulate marriage, inheritance, land transfer, and community governance in ways fundamentally different from both Hindu personal law and any envisaged unified civil code
- Tribal customary laws are explicitly recognised and protected by the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, PESA, and FRA
- Many tribal communities view the UCC as a **direct threat** to the constitutional protections of their distinct socio-legal identity
- Tension between the universalist aspiration of a unified civil code and the constitutional commitment to protecting tribal distinctiveness remains unresolved

## 14. Globalization Impact on Tribal Society

*"Globalisation has produced its most unequal outcomes for tribal communities. They bear its costs most acutely while receiving the fewest of its benefits."*

### Economic Impact — Resource Extraction and Displacement
- Global demand for minerals — bauxite, iron ore, coal, uranium — has driven large-scale corporate land acquisition in tribal areas, accelerating displacement from **jal, jangal, zameen** (water, forest, land)
- **Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta** case: Defining symbol of a tribal community successfully resisting corporate globalisation — at least temporarily

### Cultural Impact — Erosion and Commodification
- Global cultural homogenisation, mediated by smartphones and streaming media, is accelerating erosion of tribal languages, oral traditions, and distinctive customs
- Tribal art is simultaneously threatened (loss of authentic community practice) and globally commodified
- **Warli and Gond paintings** now find markets on global e-commerce platforms — positive when communities retain authorship, control, and economic benefit
- **NITI Aayog's Global Tribal Entrepreneurship Summit**: Emerging policy attempt to channel globalisation toward tribal economic empowerment

### Technological Impact — Digital Divide and Bio-Piracy
- The digital divide is sharpest for tribal communities: low smartphone penetration, poor internet connectivity in remote areas, digital content almost entirely in non-tribal languages
- However, digital platforms are creating genuine new markets for tribal artisans — e-commerce access bypasses exploitative middlemen
- **Bio-piracy**: Global pharmaceutical companies patenting tribal medicinal knowledge without consent or benefit-sharing — requires stronger enforcement of the **Nagoya Protocol**

### Political Impact — Rights Bypass
- PESA and FRA, constitutional protections hard-won through decades of struggle, are routinely bypassed when global capital and state development interests converge on tribal resource areas
- **Global conservation frameworks** — including the ambitious **30×30 biodiversity target** (protecting 30% of land and ocean by 2030) — risk displacing tribal communities from forests in the name of conservation, repeating the injustice of colonial forest exclusions in a new ideological framework

### Positive Global Dimensions
- **UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007)**: Provides normative international support for tribal land, cultural, and self-governance rights
  - India voted **against** it initially before endorsing it
- International civil society attention — as in the Vedanta case — can amplify tribal resistance against powerful corporate-state coalitions
- Tribal ecological knowledge is gaining genuine recognition in international climate and biodiversity policy frameworks

## 15. Case Studies

### 1. Narmada Bachao Andolan — When Resistance Reshapes Policy
- Led by **Medha Patkar**
- Combined legal battles, hunger strikes, and international advocacy to contest displacement caused by the **Sardar Sarovar Dam**
- Though the dam was completed, the movement directly contributed to the **LARR Act, 2013** — which for the first time mandated **Social Impact Assessment and community consent** before land acquisition

### 2. Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta — When FRA Actually Worked
- The **Dongria Kondh** of Odisha's **Niyamgiri Hills** resisted UK-based **Vedanta Resources**, which sought to mine bauxite from hills sacred to the community
- In a landmark **2013 Supreme Court judgment**, the Court held that affected Gram Sabhas had the right to decide
- All **12 Gram Sabhas unanimously rejected** the mining project
- Defining example of FRA working as intended

### 3. Bishnoi Community — Conservation Tradition
- Community of Rajasthan practiced conservation of trees and wildlife for over five centuries
- The **Chipko movement of the 1970s** drew directly on this tradition

### 4. Soliga Tribes — Medicinal Knowledge
- Soliga tribes of Karnataka's Biligiri Rangana (BRT) Hills
- Possess documented knowledge of more than **100 medicinal plant species** and their applications

### 5. Irula Tribe — Snake Expertise
- Irula tribe of Tamil Nadu has unique expertise in snakes and their venom
- Their knowledge is actively used by antivenom research institutions

### 6. Sentinelese — Uncontacted Protection
- North Sentinel Island, Andaman & Nicobar
- World's most isolated community; India maintains strict non-contact protection
- 2018 incident: American missionary John Allen Chau killed after illegally attempting contact

## 16. Way Forward — Xaxa Committee and the Rights-Based Approach

### Virginius Xaxa Committee (2014)
The most comprehensive recent policy framework for tribal development — focused on social, economic, health, educational, and other development indicators.

**Key Recommendations:**
1. **Strengthen last-mile delivery** of welfare schemes in remote tribal regions — addressing the governance gap between policy design and ground reality
2. **Enhance quality of education and healthcare infrastructure** — crucially, including instruction in **tribal mother tongues** to address the language barrier that drives dropout rates
3. **Protect tribal land and forest rights rigorously** as the foundation of all other development — displacement prevention is more effective than post-displacement rehabilitation
4. **Design livelihood and skill development programmes** that respect tribal knowledge and socio-cultural context — not generic urban skilling programmes transplanted to tribal areas
5. **Promote inclusive research and institutional support** — documenting tribal culture, languages, and traditional knowledge in partnership with communities
6. **Ensure genuine community participation** in planning and execution of development initiatives — cultural sensitivity and community ownership are preconditions for effectiveness, not soft additions

**Overarching principle**: True tribal development must be grounded in a **rights-based approach** — one that:
- Protects tribal interests
- Preserves cultural identity
- Strengthens community governance
- Ensures tribal communities are active partners in shaping their own developmental trajectories

*"The goal is not to bring development to tribal communities as outsiders conceive it, but to create the conditions under which tribal communities can exercise their own vision of a good life."*

| **Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta (2013)** | Landmark SC case; all 12 Gram Sabhas rejected bauxite mining — FRA working as intended |
| **Irula Tribe** | Tamil Nadu; snake/venom expertise used by antivenom research |
| **Bishnoi Community** | Rajasthan; five centuries of conservation; inspired Chipko movement |
| **Soliga Tribes** | Karnataka's BRT Hills; knowledge of 100+ medicinal plant species |

## 19. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Adivasi** | Literally "original inhabitant"; preferred self-designation of tribal communities asserting prior historical claim to land |
| **Animism** | Religious belief system centred on the spiritual significance of nature, animals, and ancestors; predominant in tribal religions |
| **Bio-piracy** | Patenting of traditional tribal or indigenous knowledge by external corporations without community consent or benefit-sharing |
| **Bride price** | Practice in many tribal communities where the groom's family pays the bride's family — the opposite of dowry; makes daughters economically valuable |
| **Customary law** | Unwritten, community-specific legal norms governing marriage, inheritance, land, and governance in tribal societies |
| **Denotified Tribes (DNTs)** | Communities formerly listed as "criminal tribes" under colonial law (Criminal Tribes Act, 1871), denotified after Independence but still stigmatised |
| **Dhumkuria** | Youth dormitory among the Oraon tribe — space for socialisation and cultural education |
| **Fifth Schedule** | Constitutional provision (Article 244) governing administration of Scheduled Areas in 10 states; gives Governors special powers to protect tribal land and governance |
| **Forest Rights Act (FRA)** | The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006; vests individual and community forest rights in tribal communities |
| **Ghotul** | Youth dormitory institution among the Gond tribe — space for socialisation, cultural education, and collective training |
| **Gram Sabha** | Village assembly of all adult voters; under PESA and FRA, empowered to make decisions on natural resources, land acquisition, and local governance in tribal areas |
| **Jal, Jangal, Zameen** | "Water, forest, land" — the three pillars of tribal livelihood; the phrase captures what is at stake in every displacement |
| **Jhum** | Shifting cultivation — a traditional agricultural practice of clearing forest land, cultivating for 2–3 seasons, then leaving it fallow for ecological regeneration |
| **Lokur Committee** | Committee (1965) that laid down five criteria for identifying Scheduled Tribes — primitive traits, geographical isolation, shyness of contact, backwardness, tribal origin |
| **Matriliny** | Social system where clan identity and property pass through the mother's line; practiced by the Khasi of Meghalaya |
| **Nagoya Protocol** | International protocol (2010) under the Convention on Biological Diversity requiring prior informed consent and benefit-sharing for access to traditional knowledge |
| **PESA** | Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996; extends Panchayati Raj to tribal areas and empowers Gram Sabhas over natural resources |
| **PVTG** | Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Group; 75 notified communities with pre-agricultural technology, very low literacy, and stagnant or declining populations |
| **Sacred Groves** | Patches of forest protected by tribal communities for religious and cultural reasons (Deorais, Orans, Kovils); significant for biodiversity conservation |
| **Scheduled Tribe (ST)** | Administrative category under Article 342; communities notified by the President based on Lokur Committee criteria for protective policies |
| **Sixth Schedule** | Constitutional provision for Northeast tribal areas — provides Autonomous District Councils with legislative, judicial, and administrative powers |
| **Tribal Panchsheel** | Nehru's five principles for tribal development: own genius, land rights, language support, tribal administration, Governor's special responsibility |
| **UNDRIP** | UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007) — provides normative international support for tribal land, cultural, and self-governance rights |
| **Vanvasi** | "Forest dweller"; a term used by some political groups to describe tribal communities that critics argue is reductive and strips away Adivasi land claims |
