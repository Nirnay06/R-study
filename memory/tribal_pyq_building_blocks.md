## BB-01: CONCEPT OF TRIBE — DEFINITIONS & COLONIAL CONSTRUCT

**PYQ Hook:** "Tribe as colonial construct" (2016/10), "Tribe and Scheduled Tribe" (2018/10), "Concept of tribe and Indian census" (2021/10), "Tribes in India are not homogeneous group" (undated/20)

### Core Definitions

| Scholar | Definition |
|---|---|
| **D.N. Majumdar** | Social group with territorial affiliation, endogamous, no functional specialization, ruled by tribal officers, united by language/dialect, recognizes social distance with other tribes/castes |
| **Imperial Gazetteer of India** | Group of families bearing common name, common dialect, common territory, observing marriage taboos |
| **L.P. Vidyarthi** | Group of people speaking common dialect, observing uniform social customs, living in a particular geographical area |

### Tribe as Colonial Construct
- British needed administrative categories → created rigid "tribe" label to classify non-caste groups
- **Criminal Tribes Act (1871):** Branded entire communities as "criminal" — Pardhis, Sansis, Kanjars → socially stigmatized for generations
- Census classifications (1891 onwards) froze fluid identities into fixed categories
- **André Béteille:** "Tribe" is a colonial administrative category, not a natural social formation
- **G.S. Ghurye (1943):** Referred to tribes as "backward Hindus" — denied separate identity, saw them on Hindu continuum
- Pre-colonial reality: Tribe-caste boundaries were fluid (e.g., Rajput–Bhil interaction in Rajasthan, Gond kingdoms of Central India)

### Tribe vs Scheduled Tribe

| Aspect | Tribe | Scheduled Tribe |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Anthropological/sociological concept | Legal/constitutional concept |
| Basis | Shared culture, territory, language, kinship | Presidential notification under Article 342 |
| Criteria | Academic — no single definition | Lokur Committee: 5 criteria (primitive traits, distinctive culture, geographical isolation, shyness of contact, backwardness) |
| Overlap | Not all tribes are STs | Not all STs are "tribes" in strict anthropological sense |
| Example gap | Many nomadic groups (DNTs) missed ST list | Some groups got ST status for political reasons — **pseudo-tribalism** (e.g., Gujjar demand in Rajasthan) |

### Tribe and Indian Census
- 1871 Census: First enumeration of tribes under "forest tribes"
- 1891: Separate "animist" category
- 1931: Last caste-based census — listed 25 million tribals
- Post-independence: Census counts STs, not "tribes" — conflates anthropological and legal categories
- **Problem:** Communities not notified as ST become statistically invisible
- 2011 Census: 10.43 crore STs (8.6% of population)
- **Xaxa Committee (2014):** Recommended revision of ST list, criticized outdated Lokur criteria

### "Tribes Are Not Homogeneous" Argument
- **Linguistic diversity:** 4 language families (Austro-Asiatic, Dravidian, Tibeto-Burman, Indo-Aryan)
- **Economic diversity:** Hunter-gatherers (Chenchu) → settled farmers (Santhals) → artisans (Asur) → pastoral (Toda)
- **Racial diversity:** Negrito (Onge), Proto-Australoid (Gond), Mongoloid (Naga)
- **Religious diversity:** Animist, Hindu-syncretic, Christian converts, Buddhist (Chakma)
- **Regional variation:** NE tribes (clan-based, democratic) vs Central tribes (hierarchical, caste-influenced) vs Island tribes (isolated, pre-Neolithic)

## BB-02: BIO-GENETIC VARIABILITY

**PYQ Hook:** "Bio-genetic variability of Indian tribes" (2014/10, 2016/10)

### Definition
Genetic differences among human populations arising from **gene–environment interaction** over evolutionary time. Reflected in blood groups, hemoglobin types, skin color, cranial measurements, DNA markers.

### Racial Classification of Indian Tribes

| Race | Physical Traits | Examples | % of Tribal Pop |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Negrito** | Short stature, dark skin, flat-broad nose, woolly hair, thick lips | Onge, Jarawa, Sentinelese, Great Andamanese | Smallest |
| **Proto-Australoid** | Medium stature, dark skin, curly hair, low forehead, muscular | Chenchu, Gond, Santhal, Munda, Oraon, Koya | **Largest** |
| **Mongoloid** | Medium stature, yellowish-brown skin, flat face, epicanthic fold, straight hair | Garo, Naga, Kuki, Khasi, Lepcha, Mizo | NE India |

### Key Studies

| Scholar/Study | Sample | Finding |
|---|---|---|
| **B.S. Guha (1935)** | Pan-India racial survey | Classified Indian population into 6 racial types; tribal groups mapped to Negrito, Proto-Australoid, Mongoloid |
| **mtDNA Studies** | 115 individuals across linguistic groups | Austro-Asiatic tribes show **greater genetic diversity** than Dravidian tribes → older ancestry, longer separation |
| **Vasava & Kotwalia study (1985)** | Same ethnic group, Surat, Gujarat | No major genetic difference within same group in same region → **regional homogeneity** due to endogamy |
| **AP tribal study (1981)** | 11 major tribal groups of Andhra Pradesh | Geographically proximate tribes share genetic similarities → **regional gene flow** despite endogamy |
| **Sickle Cell studies** | Central & Western Indian tribes | High frequency of sickle cell trait among Gonds, Bhils → natural selection favoring malaria resistance |
| **ABO Blood Group studies** | Various Indian tribes | Higher frequency of B allele in tribes of Eastern India; O allele dominant in isolated tribes (Andaman) |

### Exam-Ready Conclusion
- Indian tribes = **"living laboratory of human genetic variation"**
- Isolation + endogamy → genetic drift → unique allele frequencies
- But gene flow (especially Proto-Australoid belt across Central India) prevents total genetic isolation
- Modern challenge: Rapid acculturation threatening genetic distinctiveness

## BB-03: LINGUISTIC CLASSIFICATION

**PYQ Hook:** "Linguistic classification of Indian tribes" (2020/15, undated/10)

### Four Language Families (Grierson's Linguistic Survey of India, 1903–28)

| Family | Region | Tribes | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Austro-Asiatic** (Munda & Mon-Khmer) | Chotanagpur Plateau, Central-East India; also Meghalaya (Khasi) | Santhals, Mundas, Ho, Kharia, Korku, Savara, Juang, **Khasi** (Mon-Khmer) | Oldest linguistic stratum; agglutinative; Santali = largest Munda language (7M+ speakers); Santali has Ol Chiki script |
| **Dravidian** | Central & South India | Gond (Gondi), Oraon (Kurukh), Toda (Toda), Malto (Maler), Chenchu | Pre-Aryan; Kurukh (Oraon) = northernmost Dravidian tribal language; Brahui (Balochistan) = geographic outlier |
| **Tibeto-Burman** (Sino-Tibetan) | NE India, Himalayan belt | Naga, Mizo, Bodo, Garo, Lepcha, Kuki, Karbi, Adi, Apatani | Tonal languages; enormous internal diversity (Naga alone has 60+ dialects); many languages endangered |
| **Indo-Aryan** | Scattered — adopted by contact | Bhil (Bhili), Warli, Tharu | Originally non-IA speakers who shifted; Bhili retains archaic Rajasthani features |

### Key Scholars
- **George A. Grierson:** Linguistic Survey of India (1903–28) — classified 179 languages, 544 dialects
- **S.K. Chatterji:** Proposed Austro-Asiatic speakers as earliest linguistic group in India
- **Suniti Kumar Chatterji:** Traced Munda-Dravidian substratum in Indo-Aryan languages

### Endangered Tribal Languages
- **UNESCO:** 197 Indian languages endangered; many tribal
- **Great Andamanese:** <10 speakers — critically endangered
- **Toda:** ~1,500 speakers
- **Bo language:** Extinct (2010) — last speaker Boa Sr died
- Causes: Dominant language adoption, lack of script, education in non-tribal languages, migration
- **8th Schedule:** Only 2 tribal languages included — Santali, Bodo (out of 22 scheduled languages)

### Exam Application
- Linguistic diversity proves tribes are NOT homogeneous
- Language = strongest marker of tribal identity (stronger than territory or economy)
- Language loss = cultural loss (oral traditions, TEK, cosmology encoded in language)

## BB-04: SHIFTING CULTIVATION

**PYQ Hook:** "Socio-economic characteristics of shifting cultivators" (2015/10), "Balance between livelihood and environmental degradation — shifting cultivation" (2022/15)

### Definition & Process
- Also called: **Jhum** (NE India), Podu (AP/Odisha), Bewar/Dahiya (MP), Kumari (Western Ghats), Penda (Chhattisgarh)
- Slash-and-burn agriculture: clear forest patch → burn → cultivate 2–3 years → abandon → move to new patch → original patch regenerates (fallow period 10–20 years)

### Socio-Economic Characteristics of Shifting Cultivators
1. **Subsistence economy:** Grow food for family, not market — rice, millets, vegetables, tubers
2. **Community-based:** Clan/village collectively decides which patch to clear; communal land ownership
3. **Simple technology:** Dao (machete), digging sticks, hoe — no plough, no irrigation
4. **Ecologically embedded:** Intimate knowledge of soil, seasons, forest regeneration
5. **Mixed cropping:** Plant 20–40 crops together (polyculture) — natural pest control, nutritional diversity
6. **Egalitarian social structure:** No landlord–tenant; equal access to land
7. **Cultural-ritual integration:** Agricultural calendar linked to festivals (e.g., Wangala of Garos)
8. **Low population density required:** Needs large forest tracts for rotation

### Environmental Debate

| View | Argument |
|---|---|
| **Degradation argument** | Reduced fallow periods (from 20 to 3–5 years) → deforestation, soil erosion, loss of biodiversity; contributes to CO₂ emissions |
| **Sustainability argument** | Traditional long-fallow jhum is ecologically sustainable; polyculture preserves agro-biodiversity; **Ramakrishnan (1992):** jhum with adequate fallow is comparable to secondary succession |
| **Middle ground (2022 PYQ angle)** | Problem is not jhum itself but **compressed fallow** due to population pressure, restricted forest access, government policy |

### Livelihood vs Environment Balance (2022 PYQ)
- **NITI Aayog Report (2018):** Recommended gradual transition, not sudden ban
- **Alternatives tried:** Settled terrace farming (Apatani model — Arunachal), horticulture (rubber, oil palm), agroforestry
- **Apatani system (Ziro Valley):** Wet rice + fish culture in terraced fields — globally recognized sustainable model (proposed UNESCO Heritage)
- **Way forward:** Combine TEK with modern agroforestry; recognize community forest rights under FRA 2006; invest in alternative livelihoods without criminalizing jhum

## BB-05: LAND ALIENATION

**PYQ Hook:** "Impact of land alienation on tribes of Central India" (2014/15), "Constitutional provisions for land alienation" (2015/20), "Problems of land alienation among tribals" (2020/15)

### Definition
Transfer of tribal land to non-tribals through legal, semi-legal, or illegal means — leading to loss of livelihood, identity, and cultural moorings.

### Causes (6 major)
1. **Colonial land policies:** Permanent Settlement (1793), Ryotwari — imposed individual ownership on communal land systems; tribals couldn't prove ownership
2. **Moneylender–trader nexus:** Usurious loans → land mortgage → forfeiture; tribals unfamiliar with written contracts
3. **Market penetration:** Cash economy undermined subsistence; tribals sold land for immediate cash needs
4. **Government acquisition:** Dams, mines, industries, sanctuaries — "development" projects displaced millions
5. **Encroachment by non-tribals:** In-migration of settlers, merchants; benami transactions circumventing protective laws
6. **Weak legal literacy:** Tribals unaware of protective legislation; revenue officials complicit in illegal transfers

### Consequences
- **Economic:** Landlessness → bonded labor, migration, poverty trap (B.D. Sharma: "destitution spiral")
- **Social:** Breakdown of community bonds; loss of clan/village identity tied to land
- **Cultural:** Sacred groves, ancestral burial sites, totemic landscapes lost
- **Psychological:** Alienation, anomie, identity crisis → alcoholism, suicide (high rates among displaced Adivasis)
- **Political:** Tribal unrest — Santhal Rebellion (1855), Birsa Munda movement (1899), contemporary Naxalism

### Constitutional & Legal Protections

| Provision | Protection |
|---|---|
| **Fifth Schedule** | Governor can prohibit/restrict land transfer in Scheduled Areas; Tribes Advisory Council |
| **Sixth Schedule** | Autonomous District Councils control land allotment in NE tribal areas |
| **PESA (1996)** | Gram Sabha must approve land acquisition in Scheduled Areas |
| **FRA (2006)** | Recognizes individual forest rights (IFR) and community forest rights (CFR) |
| **CNTA (1908)** | Chotanagpur Tenancy Act — restricts transfer of tribal land in Jharkhand |
| **SPTA (1949)** | Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act — same for Santhal Pargana |
| **State-level laws** | Andhra Pradesh (1 of 70 Act), Rajasthan, MP, Maharashtra, Odisha — all have anti-alienation laws |

### Key Case Study — Jharkhand
- Pre-colonial: Communal khuntkatti land system (Mundas)
- British: Zamindari imposed → Munda Ulgulan (1899, Birsa Munda) against land grab
- Post-independence: Mining, dams (Subarnarekha, Koel-Karo) → massive alienation
- Despite CNTA, illegal transfers continue through benami transactions
- **Koel-Karo movement (1970s–2010):** Munda and Ho tribes successfully resisted dam project — rare victory

### Scholars
- **B.D. Sharma:** "Unfinished Task" — documented systematic tribal dispossession
- **Walter Fernandes:** Estimated 40 million displaced by development projects (1947–2004), 85% not rehabilitated; tribals = 40% of displaced but 8% of population
- **K.S. Singh (People of India Project):** Land alienation as primary driver of tribal movements

## BB-06: TRIBAL EDUCATION

**PYQ Hook:** "Socio-cultural, economic and psychological constraints for low literacy" (2015/15), "Issues related to tribal education" (2019/10), "Problems and challenges in educational attainment of STs" (2021/15)

### Statistics
- ST literacy rate: **59%** (Census 2011) vs national average 73%
- ST female literacy: **49.4%** vs national female average 65.5%
- Drop-out rate (Class I–X): ~62% for STs vs ~47% national average
- Exception: **Mizoram** — 91.3% literacy (highest among tribal-majority states)

### Constraints (categorized for exam)

**Socio-Cultural:**
1. Medium of instruction in dominant language — not mother tongue (language barrier)
2. Curriculum alien to tribal worldview — no reflection of tribal history, ecology, culture
3. Schools run on calendar incompatible with agricultural/forest-gathering cycles
4. Gender discrimination in some patriarchal tribes — girls kept home for domestic/agricultural work
5. Early marriage traditions reduce educational years
6. Fear of cultural alienation — education seen as path away from community

**Economic:**
1. Poverty → children needed as labor force
2. Hidden costs — uniform, books, transport — despite "free" education
3. Opportunity cost of sending children to school instead of forest/field
4. Malnutrition → poor cognitive development → learning difficulties
5. Remote locations → no accessible schools within walking distance

**Psychological:**
1. Discrimination by non-tribal teachers and students → feeling of inferiority
2. Hostel environment culturally alien → homesickness, isolation
3. Language of instruction creates comprehension gap → repeated failure → dropout
4. First-generation learners — no academic support system at home

### Government Interventions
- **EMRS (Eklavya Model Residential Schools):** High-quality residential schools in tribal areas — 740 sanctioned as of 2023
- **Ashram Schools:** Residential schools in Scheduled Areas
- **Post-Matric Scholarships:** For ST students in higher education
- **National Fellowship for STs:** For M.Phil/PhD
- **Multilingual Education (MLE):** Pilot programs in tribal mother tongues (Odisha model — Saora, Kui languages)

### Way Forward (exam conclusion)
- Mother-tongue based multilingual education (UNESCO recommendation)
- Culturally rooted curriculum — integrate tribal knowledge systems
- Recruit tribal teachers (role models + language bridge)
- Bridge courses instead of mainstreaming overnight
- Address poverty-nutrition nexus first — "hungry child cannot learn"

## BB-07: HEALTH & NUTRITION

**PYQ Hook:** "Impact of displacement on health and nutritional status" (2017/20), "Factors responsible for malnutrition in tribal India" (2021/15), "Causes of stunting and wasting among tribal children" (2024/10), "Holistic health for marginalized — COVID-19" (2024/20)

### Key Statistics
- **IMR:** 44.4 per 1000 (STs) vs 34.9 national (NFHS-5)
- **Under-5 mortality:** 50.3 (STs) vs 41.9 national
- **Stunting:** 40.3% among ST children vs 35.5% national (NFHS-5)
- **Wasting:** 23.2% among ST children vs 19.3% national
- **Anemia:** 67.6% among ST women vs 57% national
- Sickle cell disease: Endemic in tribal belts of Central India (Gonds, Bhils — prevalence 1–40%)

### Causes of Stunting & Wasting (2024 PYQ)
1. **Chronic malnutrition:** Inadequate caloric/protein intake; shift from diverse forest foods to mono-crop dependence
2. **Loss of traditional food sources:** Deforestation → loss of minor forest produce (tubers, greens, mushrooms, honey)
3. **Poverty:** Cannot afford supplementary nutrition; food insecurity
4. **Poor WASH (water, sanitation, hygiene):** Open defecation, contaminated water → repeated infections → nutrient malabsorption
5. **Maternal malnutrition:** Undernourished mothers → low birth weight babies → intergenerational cycle
6. **Inadequate healthcare access:** Remote areas, shortage of ANMs/AWWs; cultural barriers to institutional delivery
7. **Displacement effect:** Disrupts food systems, social networks, traditional medicine

### Traditional Health Knowledge (TEK)
- Tribes possess rich ethnomedicinal knowledge — over 7,500 plant species used
- **Irula tribe (Tamil Nadu):** Snake venom extraction — contribute to 90% of India's anti-venom production
- **Kani tribe (Kerala):** Arogyapacha plant discovery → TBGRI benefit-sharing model
- **Baiga:** Extensive knowledge of medicinal plants for bone-setting, fever, childbirth
- TEK threatened by deforestation, displacement, lack of intergenerational transmission

### COVID-19 Impact on Tribals (2024 PYQ)
- Digital divide → couldn't access telemedicine or information
- Lockdown disrupted MFP collection → livelihood and nutrition crisis
- ASHA/ANM workers couldn't reach remote areas → immunization, antenatal care disrupted
- But: Some isolated tribes were naturally protected by geographic remoteness
- **Lesson:** Need decentralized, community-based health systems; TEK integration with modern medicine

### Government Schemes
- **NRHM/NHM:** Sub-centres, PHCs in tribal areas
- **Janani Suraksha Yojana:** Institutional deliveries
- **National Sickle Cell Anemia Mission (2023):** Screening + management in tribal districts
- **ICDS (Anganwadi):** Supplementary nutrition for children and pregnant women
- **Van Dhan Vikas Kendra:** Supports MFP processing (indirect nutrition security)

## BB-08: FOREST POLICY & FRA 2006

**PYQ Hook:** Asked almost every year. "Forest policies 1878 to 2006" (2024/15), "FRA 2006 significance & implementation" (2018/15), "FRA impact on livelihood & culture" (2021/20), "Difficulties with FRA implementation" (2016/15), "Forest policy and tribes" (2014/10)

### Evolution of Forest Policy

| Year | Policy/Act | Impact on Tribes |
|---|---|---|
| **1865** | Indian Forest Act | First state control over forests; "government forests" concept introduced |
| **1878** | Indian Forest Act (Amended) | Created Reserved/Protected/Village forests; **criminalized** tribal forest use; tribes became "encroachers" on their own land |
| **1894** | Forest Policy | Forests for state revenue (timber) — no tribal rights recognized |
| **1927** | Indian Forest Act | Consolidated colonial forest law; restricted shifting cultivation, grazing, MFP collection |
| **1952** | National Forest Policy | 33% forest cover target; national interest > local rights; continued colonial approach |
| **1980** | Forest Conservation Act | States cannot divert forest land without central approval; further restricted tribal access |
| **1988** | National Forest Policy | **First recognition** of tribal dependence on forests; Joint Forest Management (JFM) concept |
| **1996** | PESA | Gram Sabha's role in forest management in Scheduled Areas |
| **2006** | **Forest Rights Act (FRA)** | **Historic correction of "historical injustice"** — recognized tribal forest rights |

### FRA 2006 — Detailed Block

**Key Provisions:**
1. **Individual Forest Rights (IFR):** Title to land being cultivated before Dec 13, 2005 — max 4 hectares per family
2. **Community Forest Rights (CFR):** Right to collect, use, sell MFP; community ownership of forests traditionally used
3. **Habitat rights for PVTGs:** Entire habitat recognized as community forest resource
4. **Right to protect & manage:** Community forest resource (CFR) — empowers Gram Sabha as forest management authority
5. **Conversion of forest villages:** To revenue villages — grants full amenities (schools, roads, healthcare)
6. **No eviction without settlement:** Forest dwellers cannot be evicted until rights recognition process is complete

**Who is eligible:**
- STs and **Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (OTFDs)** — anyone residing in forest for 3 generations (75 years) before Dec 2005

**Implementation Challenges:**
1. **Low claim settlement:** As of 2023 — ~47 lakh individual claims filed, only ~22 lakh titles distributed; massive rejection rates
2. **CFR titles lag far behind IFR:** Community rights harder to process — bureaucratic resistance, no clear process
3. **Forest bureaucracy resistance:** Forest department sees FRA as threat to control; officers delay or obstruct
4. **Gram Sabha capacity:** Supposed to verify claims but lacks resources, training, awareness
5. **State variation:** Odisha, Maharashtra progressed; NE states barely implemented
6. **Conflict with other laws:** FCA 1980, Wildlife Protection Act 1972 — conservation vs rights tension
7. **Eviction despite FRA:** Supreme Court order (Feb 2019) directing eviction of rejected claimants — later stayed, but exposed implementation gaps

**Achievements:**
- Where implemented well (e.g., Gadchiroli, Maharashtra), CFR led to:
  - Community forest management → better forest conservation than state-managed forests
  - MFP-based livelihoods → increased income (tendu, bamboo, mahua)
  - Women's empowerment through CFR management committees
  - Reduced Naxal influence (Gadchiroli model)

### Scholars/Committees
- **Kailas Sarup Committee (2006):** Reviewed tribal forest rights → paved way for FRA
- **Saxena Committee (2010):** Implementation review — found systemic non-implementation, bureaucratic hurdles
- **N.C. Saxena:** Argued forest conservation and tribal rights are compatible, not contradictory

## BB-09: DISPLACEMENT & REHABILITATION

**PYQ Hook:** "Development induced displacement" (2020/20), "Problems in R&R of tribals" (2021/15), "Displacement due to dams — impact on women" (2023/15), "Impact of displacement on health" (2017/20)

### Scale of Displacement
- **Walter Fernandes (2007):** 60 million displaced by development projects (1947–2004)
  - **40% are tribal** (though tribals = 8% of population) → disproportionate burden
  - **75% not rehabilitated** — "development refugees"
- Major causes: Dams (16.4M), mines (2.55M), industries (1.25M), wildlife sanctuaries (0.6M)

### Cernea's Impoverishment Risks & Reconstruction (IRR) Model
**8 risks of displacement:**

| Risk | Tribal Context |
|---|---|
| 1. **Landlessness** | Primary risk — land is identity, not just asset |
| 2. **Joblessness** | Forest-dependent skills not transferable to cash economy |
| 3. **Homelessness** | Relocated to unfamiliar terrain; housing inadequate |
| 4. **Marginalization** | Economic + social — displaced tribals become lowest rung in new location |
| 5. **Food insecurity** | Loss of forest foods, fishing grounds, agricultural land |
| 6. **Increased morbidity** | Exposure to new diseases; loss of traditional medicine access; mental health crisis |
| 7. **Loss of access to common property** | Forests, rivers, grazing lands — not compensated in cash-based R&R |
| 8. **Social disarticulation** | Community bonds shattered; clan/village unit destroyed; cultural practices disrupted |

### Impact on Tribal Women (2023 PYQ)
- Loss of forest access → loss of economic autonomy (women are primary MFP collectors)
- Displacement to urban peripheries → domestic confinement (from forest worker to unpaid domestic labor)
- R&R compensation given to male head → women excluded from economic decision-making
- Increased vulnerability: trafficking, sexual exploitation in displacement camps
- Loss of traditional support networks → increased domestic violence
- **Case study — Sardar Sarovar Dam:** Adivasi women of Narmada Valley lost access to forest resources; many became wage laborers in unfamiliar urban settings

### Key Case Studies

| Project | Tribes Affected | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Sardar Sarovar Dam** (Gujarat) | Bhils, Bhilalas, Tadvis | 40,000+ families displaced; Narmada Bachao Andolan (Medha Patkar) |
| **Hirakud Dam** (Odisha) | 22,000 families | First major dam displacement; many still not rehabilitated (1957) |
| **Polavaram Dam** (AP/Telangana) | Koya, Konda Reddi | 200+ villages submerged; ongoing displacement |
| **POSCO** (Odisha) | Betel vine cultivators | Massive resistance → project shelved |
| **Koel-Karo Dam** (Jharkhand) | Munda, Ho | 30+ years of resistance → project dropped (2010) |
| **Mining in Jharkhand** | Santhal, Ho, Munda | Coal, iron, uranium mining → land loss, water contamination |
| **Niyamgiri** (Odisha) | Dongria Kondh (PVTG) | Vedanta mining rejected by Gram Sabha (2013) under FRA — **landmark case** |

### R&R Legislation

| Act | Key Features | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **Land Acquisition Act (1894)** | Colonial era; "eminent domain" — no consent, no R&R | No rehabilitation provision; grossly inadequate compensation |
| **National R&R Policy (2007)** | First R&R policy; voluntary, not mandatory | Not legally binding; poorly implemented |
| **RFCTLARR Act (2013)** | **Landmark reform:** 70%/80% consent (PPP/Govt); R&R mandatory; Social Impact Assessment; enhanced compensation (2–4x market); retrospective application | Implementation challenges; 2015 amendment attempts (withdrawn after opposition); still weak on CPR recognition |

### Exam-Ready Arguments
- **Development vs displacement is a false binary** — sustainable alternatives exist (run-of-river hydro, decentralized industry)
- **Tribals bear cost of "national development" they don't benefit from** — electricity goes to cities, minerals to corporates
- **R&R must go beyond cash compensation** — recognize CPR loss, cultural disruption, community bonds
- **FRA 2006 + PESA 1996 + RFCTLARR 2013 = institutional framework exists** but implementation deficit persists

## BB-10: CONSTITUTIONAL SAFEGUARDS — V & VI SCHEDULES

**PYQ Hook:** Asked almost every year. "V and VI Schedules" (2016/20), "Role of Governor in V Schedule areas" (2015/10, 2022/10), "Provisions under VI Schedule" (2020/15), "Status of VI Schedule areas" (2024/10), "Scheduled Areas" (2023/10)

### Fifth Schedule

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Applicability** | 10 states: AP, Telangana, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, MP, Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan, Odisha, HP |
| **Governor's role** | Direct responsibility; can modify/annul laws; direct transfer of land prohibited; regulate moneylending; report annually to President on tribal administration |
| **Tribes Advisory Council (TAC)** | 20 members, 3/4 ST; advises Governor on tribal welfare — but advisory only, not binding |
| **Scheduled Areas** | Declared by President; criteria: preponderance of tribal population, compactness, under-developed, lack of administrative infrastructure |
| **Key limitation** | Governor's discretionary power rarely used proactively; TAC recommendations often ignored; states resist central intervention |

### Sixth Schedule

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Applicability** | 4 NE states: Assam (Karbi Anglong, Dima Hasao, Bodo), Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram — 10 Autonomous District Councils |
| **Autonomous District Councils (ADCs)** | Elected bodies (30 members); legislative, judicial, executive powers |
| **Legislative powers** | Laws on land, forests, water, shifting cultivation, village councils, marriage, inheritance, customs |
| **Judicial powers** | Can constitute village/district courts for disputes between tribals |
| **Revenue** | Can levy and collect taxes, tolls; can receive grants from state |
| **Land protection** | ADC controls allotment, use of land; can restrict non-tribal land ownership |
| **Key limitation** | Limited financial autonomy; dependent on state grants; political factions; VI Schedule not extended to all NE states (Arunachal, Nagaland, Manipur have different arrangements — Article 371) |

### Fifth vs Sixth Schedule Comparison (useful for 15/20 mark answers)

| Aspect | Fifth Schedule | Sixth Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| Region | Peninsular/Central India | NE India |
| Governance | Governor + TAC (advisory) | Elected ADCs with real powers |
| Legislative power | Governor can modify laws | ADCs can make laws |
| Judicial power | None | Village/district tribal courts |
| Land control | Governor can prohibit transfer | ADCs control allotment |
| Autonomy | Limited — executive discretion | Substantial — legislative + judicial + executive |
| PESA link | PESA (1996) extends to V Schedule areas | Not applicable (ADCs serve similar purpose) |

## BB-11: ETHNICITY & ETHNIC CONFLICTS

**PYQ Hook:** "Concept of ethnicity" (2020/10), "Ethnic identity vs ethnicity" (2023/15), "Ethno-nationalism" (2014/15, 2024/15), "Ethnic conflicts in tribal areas" (2023/15)

### Three Theoretical Perspectives

| Approach | Core Idea | Key Scholar | Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Primordial** | Ethnic identity is given — based on blood, kinship, language, religion; deeply emotional, non-rational | **Clifford Geertz** | Can't explain why ethnic identity fluctuates or is selectively activated |
| **Instrumentalist** | Ethnicity is a political tool — elites mobilize ethnic identity for power, resources | **Paul Brass, Abner Cohen** | Reduces identity to manipulation; ignores genuine emotional bonds |
| **Situated Primordial** (Constructivist) | Ethnic identity is real BUT is context-dependent — activated in specific situations | **Fredrik Barth** (ethnic boundaries) | Most nuanced; best for exam |

### Fredrik Barth's Contribution
- **"Ethnic Groups and Boundaries" (1969):** Ethnicity is defined not by cultural content but by **boundary maintenance** — the social boundary between "us" and "them"
- Groups may change culture, language, economy — but maintain ethnic boundary
- Applied to Indian tribes: Bhils adopted Hindu practices but maintained Bhil identity through endogamy and clan markers

### Ethnicity in Indian Tribal Context
- **Surajit Sinha:** Tribal identity strengthened through contact with state formation — not weakened
- **K.S. Singh:** Tribes increasingly asserting ethnic identity as political tool (instrumentalist reading)
- Ethnic mobilization in India: language (Bodo), territory (Jharkhand), race (Mongoloid identity in NE)

### Ethnic Conflicts & Tribal Unrest — Causes
1. **Land and resource alienation:** Core grievance — non-tribals encroaching on tribal territory
2. **Cultural domination:** Mainstream society's values imposed through education, media, administration
3. **Political marginalization:** Despite reservation, tribals remain peripheral in governance
4. **Exploitative intermediaries:** Moneylenders, contractors, forest officials
5. **Identity threat:** In-migration of non-tribals (NE — "outsider" issue; Jharkhand — diku sentiment)
6. **Development-induced displacement:** Dams, mines — forcible uprooting without adequate R&R
7. **State repression:** AFSPA (NE), paramilitary operations (Central India) → alienation, radicalization

### Major Ethnic/Tribal Movements

| Movement | Region | Tribe/Group | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Jharkhand Movement** | Bihar/Jharkhand | Santhal, Munda, Ho, Oraon | Longest sustained movement; demanded separate state → achieved 2000 |
| **Naga Movement** | Nagaland | Nagas | Armed separatism since 1950s; Naga Club (1918), NNC, NSCN; ceasefire since 1997; Naga Accord 2015 (still unresolved) |
| **Mizo Movement** | Mizoram | Mizos | Mizo National Front revolt (1966); Mizo Accord (1986) → peace + statehood; **most successful resolution** |
| **Bodo Movement** | Assam | Bodos | Demand for Bodoland; Bodo Accord (2020) → BTR (Bodoland Territorial Region) |
| **Gorkhaland** | West Bengal | Gorkhas | Demand for separate state; GTA (Gorkhaland Territorial Administration) — not satisfied |
| **Santhal Rebellion (1855)** | Bihar | Santhals | Against zamindars and moneylenders (dikus); led by Sidho-Kanho; 10,000+ killed |
| **Birsa Munda Ulgulan (1899)** | Chotanagpur | Mundas | Against land alienation + Christian missionaries; Birsa = "Dharti Aba" (Father of Earth) |

### Ethno-Nationalism (2024 PYQ)
- **Definition:** Nationalism based on ethnic identity — aspiration for political self-determination of an ethnic group
- **Resurgence factors:** Globalization paradoxically strengthens local identity; democratic politics gives platform; historical grievances unresolved; social media amplifies ethnic consciousness
- **Indian examples:** Naga nationalism (Naga identity transcends Indian borders — Nagas in Myanmar too), Bodo nationalism, Kashmir (ethno-religious), Meitei identity assertion
- **Anthropological lens:** Not simply "law and order" problem — reflects deep structural inequity; state must address root causes (identity recognition, resource sharing, political autonomy) rather than just securitize

## BB-12: IMPACT OF RELIGIONS ON TRIBES

**PYQ Hook:** One religion asked every year. "Impact of Hinduism" (2015, 2020), "Christianity" (2014, 2017, 2021), "Buddhism" (2019), "Islam" (2016), "Social and religious consequences of contact" (2022)

### A) HINDUISM

**Process:** Sanskritization — tribes adopt Hindu customs, deities, rituals to gain higher status in regional hierarchy.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Positive** | Social mobility aspiration; access to larger civilization; pilgrimage networks; temple-based education |
| **Negative** | Loss of egalitarian ethos; introduction of caste hierarchy within tribes; women's status often declines (e.g., veil adoption); devaluation of animistic traditions |
| **Tribe-Rajput Continuum** | Rajput–Bhil interaction in Rajasthan — intermarriage, ritual exchange, political alliances; boundary between Rajput and Bhil was fluid |
| **Key scholar** | **Surajit Sinha:** "Tribe-caste continuum" — tribes and castes are not separate systems but poles of a continuum |
| **Case study — Bhils** | Adopted Hindu deities alongside tribal spirits; celebrate Holi/Diwali but retain Bhil-specific rituals; some Bhil groups have Brahmin priests alongside traditional bhopas |
| **Impact on women** | Central Indian tribal women had relative freedom — after Hinduization, restrictions on widow remarriage, female seclusion introduced |

### B) CHRISTIANITY

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Positive** | Education (mission schools); healthcare; script development for unwritten languages; abolition of superstitious practices (witch-hunting); empowerment of lower-status clans; individual dignity |
| **Negative** | Cultural alienation — converts disconnect from tribal festivals, totemic practices; intra-tribal division (converts vs non-converts); loss of indigenous worldview; dependency on mission aid |
| **Demographic change** | NE India: Nagaland 88%, Mizoram 87%, Meghalaya 75% Christian; Jharkhand: significant Oraon, Kharia, Munda conversion |
| **Revivalist movements** | Reaction to Christianity → **Tana Bhagat Movement** (Oraon, 1914), **Sarna Movement** (demand for separate "Sarna" religion code in Census), **Donyi-Polo** (Arunachal) |
| **Case study — Nagas** | Christianity unified diverse Naga tribes through common religion + English language; but replaced morung (youth dormitory), head-hunting rituals, traditional authority structures |

### C) BUDDHISM

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Historical** | Syncretic absorption — tribes in eastern India (Jharkhand, Bengal) incorporated Buddhist elements alongside animism |
| **Neo-Buddhist movement** | Ambedkar's conversion (1956) — primarily SC, but some tribal communities also adopted (Gonds in Vidarbha) |
| **Tribal connection** | Many tribal practices (non-violence, nature worship) compatible with Buddhist philosophy → easier adoption |
| **Case study — Chakma** (Tripura, Mizoram) | Theravada Buddhist tribal community; maintain distinct identity; face refugee issues (CHT, Bangladesh) |

### D) ISLAM

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Examples** | Siddis (African-origin, Gujarat/Karnataka — Sufi Islam), Bakarwals (J&K — pastoral), Lakshadweep islanders (matrilineal + Muslim) |
| **Process** | Trade contact, Sufi influence, political incorporation — less systematic than Christian missions |
| **Impact** | Mixed with tribal customs — e.g., Lakshadweep: Islam + matriliny (property through mother's line — unusual in Islamic context) |
| **Limited** | Compared to Hinduism and Christianity, Islamic influence on tribal populations is geographically restricted |

### "Social and Religious Consequences of Contact" (2022 — Broader Question)
- **Religious syncretism:** Most tribes don't fully convert — they add new elements to existing beliefs (e.g., Sarna + Hindu + Christian elements among Oraons)
- **Social stratification:** Contact introduces hierarchy into egalitarian tribal society
- **Market integration:** Religious contact often brings market economy — disrupts subsistence
- **Language shift:** Adoption of Hindi/English through religious institutions
- **Identity politics:** Religion becomes a marker for political mobilization (Sarna code demand, Christian tribal politics in NE)

## BB-13: TRIBAL DEVELOPMENT APPROACHES — ELWIN–GHURYE DEBATE

**PYQ Hook:** "Elwin-Ghurye debate" (2015/10), "Views of Ghurye and Elwin" (2022/20), "Relevance of Tribal Panchsheel today" (2015/10), "Shifting terrains of tribal policies" (2023/20)

### Three Approaches

| Approach | Champion | Core Idea | Criticism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Isolationist** | **Verrier Elwin** (early phase) | Protect tribes from outside contact; create "national parks" for tribal culture | Keeps tribes as museum pieces; denies development; patronizing |
| **Assimilationist** | **G.S. Ghurye** | Tribes are "backward Hindus" — should merge into Hindu mainstream through education, Sanskritization | Denies tribal identity; cultural imperialism; ignores structural exploitation |
| **Integrationist** | **Nehru, Elwin** (later) | Integrate tribals into national life while respecting their culture — develop along their own genius | Middle ground; adopted as official policy; basis for Tribal Panchsheel |

### Elwin vs Ghurye — Detailed

| Aspect | Verrier Elwin | G.S. Ghurye |
|---|---|---|
| **View of tribes** | Unique, independent cultures worth preserving | "Backward Hindus" — part of Hindu civilization |
| **Solution** | Protection from exploitation; tribal areas as sanctuaries | Education, intermarriage, cultural assimilation |
| **Work** | Lived with Baigas and Gonds; ethnographic fieldwork | Academic analysis; "The Scheduled Tribes" (1943) |
| **Critique of other** | Ghurye's approach = cultural destruction | Elwin romanticizes tribes; prevents progress |
| **Influence on policy** | Shaped Nehru's Tribal Panchsheel; later became advisor to NEFA | Influenced Hindu nationalist view of tribes |
| **Evolution** | Moved from isolationist to integrationist after independence | Remained assimilationist |

### Nehru's Tribal Panchsheel (5 Principles)

1. **Develop along their own genius** — don't impose alien values
2. **Respect tribal rights in land and forests** — don't take away their resources
3. **Train tribal people for administration** — build their own leadership, not outsider bureaucrats
4. **Don't over-administer** — work through tribal institutions, avoid excessive bureaucracy
5. **Judge results by human character, not statistics** — quality of life > GDP numbers

**Relevance Today (2015 PYQ):**
- Principle 1: Violated — mainstream curriculum, Hindi imposition
- Principle 2: Violated — continued land alienation, mining, dam displacement
- Principle 3: Partially achieved — reservation helps but not sufficient
- Principle 4: Violated — over-bureaucratized ITDP/TSP machinery
- Principle 5: Violated — government measures success by expenditure, not outcomes
- **Conclusion:** Tribal Panchsheel = excellent principles but poor implementation; remains relevant as guiding framework

### History of Tribal Administration

**Colonial Period:**
- 1874: Scheduled Districts Act — "backward tracts" under special administration
- 1919: Government of India Act — "backward tracts" excluded from reforms
- 1935: Government of India Act — "excluded" and "partially excluded" areas
- Agent of Governor administered these areas — no elected representation

**Post-Independence:**
- Constitution: V & VI Schedules (see BB-10)
- Tribal Panchsheel (1950s)
- TSP (1974), ITDP, MADA, Cluster approach
- FRA 2006, PESA 1996 — rights-based shift
- **Trajectory:** Protection → Development → Rights-based (but all three coexist messily)

## BB-14: PVTGs (PARTICULARLY VULNERABLE TRIBAL GROUPS)

**PYQ Hook:** "Concept of PTG/PVTG" (2014/10, 2017/10), "Problems of PVTGs" (2023/20), "Recent welfare measures for PVTGs" (2024/20)

### Definition & Nomenclature
- Originally called **Primitive Tribal Groups (PTGs)** — coined by Dhebar Commission (1960–61)
- **Renamed PVTGs in 2006** — "primitive" considered derogatory, evolutionist, colonial; "particularly vulnerable" is rights-based, empathetic terminology

### Identification Criteria (3)
1. **Pre-agricultural level of technology** — hunter-gatherers, food gatherers, shifting cultivators
2. **Declining or stagnant population** — reproductive health issues, high mortality, ecological stress
3. **Very low literacy** — significantly below even ST average

### Statistics
- **75 groups** across 18 states and Andaman & Nicobar
- Identified in 3 phases: 1975–76 (52 groups), 1993 (23 groups), total = 75
- **Smallest:** Great Andamanese (43 as per 2011 Census), Sentinelese (~50–150 estimated)
- **Largest PVTG:** Saharia (Rajasthan/MP) — ~4.5 lakh

### Important PVTGs (exam must-know)

| PVTG | State | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|
| **Sentinelese** | North Sentinel Island | Uncontacted; hostile to outsiders; protected under Andaman & Nicobar Protection of Aboriginal Tribes Regulation (1956) |
| **Jarawa** | Andaman Islands | Semi-contacted since 1998; vulnerable to disease; Andaman Trunk Road controversy |
| **Great Andamanese** | Andaman Islands | Near-extinct; settled in Strait Island; 10 languages → only 1–2 partially spoken |
| **Onge** | Little Andaman | ~100 population; tsunami-affected (2004); honey gatherers |
| **Chenchu** | Telangana/AP | Forest gatherers in Nallamala Hills; threatened by tiger reserves |
| **Birhor** | Jharkhand | Rope-making, monkey hunting; nomadic; ~10,000 |
| **Baiga** | MP/Chhattisgarh | Resist plough agriculture (believe wounding Mother Earth); bewar cultivators |
| **Toda** | Tamil Nadu (Nilgiris) | Pastoral buffalo herders; polyandrous; sacred dairy temples; unique embroidery |
| **Dongria Kondh** | Odisha (Niyamgiri) | Niyamgiri hills — resisted Vedanta mining; fruit orchards on hilltops |
| **Saharia** | Rajasthan/MP | Largest PVTG; semi-nomadic; extreme poverty |

### Problems of PVTGs (2023 PYQ)
1. **Demographic decline:** Disease vulnerability (no immunity to modern illnesses), low fertility, high infant mortality
2. **Habitat destruction:** Mining, deforestation, dam projects threaten their ecological niche
3. **Food insecurity:** Loss of forest foods; not integrated into PDS effectively
4. **Health:** No healthcare access; high prevalence of malaria, TB, malnutrition; sickle cell in some groups
5. **Isolation paradox:** Need protection from exploitation but also need healthcare, nutrition support
6. **Cultural erosion:** Language death, loss of traditional knowledge, youth migration
7. **Administrative neglect:** Development schemes don't reach them — designed for mainstream population
8. **Land rights:** Even FRA implementation is poor in PVTG areas

### Government Schemes for PVTGs (2024 PYQ)
- **PVTG Development Mission (PM-JANMAN) — 2023:** ₹24,000 crore; holistic development — housing, clean water, healthcare, education, road connectivity, mobile medical units; covers all 75 PVTGs across 220+ districts
- **Conservation-cum-Development (CCD) Plans:** Micro-plans for each PVTG — tailored to local needs
- **Special Central Assistance (SCA) to TSP:** Part earmarked for PVTGs
- **FRA 2006 — Habitat Rights:** Specifically provides habitat rights for PVTGs — recognizes entire area used for habitation, livelihood, cultural practices
- **National Commission for STs:** Monitors PVTG welfare

### Why "Primitive" Was Problematic (2024 PYQ)
- Rooted in **19th-century evolutionism** (Morgan, Tylor) — linear progression from "primitive" to "civilized"
- Implies tribal societies are frozen in time, inferior, pre-modern
- Ignores agency, knowledge systems, sustainable practices of these communities
- "Particularly Vulnerable" shifts focus from cultural judgment to **structural vulnerability** — a rights-based framing
- Reflects anthropology's own evolution from colonial science to reflexive, ethical discipline

## BB-15: PESA & GRAM SABHA

**PYQ Hook:** "PESA empowering local self-governance & women's political participation" (2024/15), "Traditional Tribal Council vs Gram Sabha under PESA" (2022/15), "Salient features of PESA — comparison with VI Schedule" (2018/15), "Role of Gram Sabha under PESA" (undated/10)

### PESA (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996

**Background:** 73rd Amendment (1992) extended panchayati raj but excluded Scheduled Areas. Bhuria Committee (1995) recommended extending PRI with modifications for tribal areas → PESA enacted.

**Key Features:**
1. **Gram Sabha is the foundation** — every village's collective decision-making body
2. Gram Sabha must approve plans, identify beneficiaries, certify utilization of funds
3. **Consent for land acquisition** — Gram Sabha must be consulted before land acquisition in Scheduled Areas
4. **MFP ownership:** Gram Sabha owns Minor Forest Produce
5. **Moneylending regulation:** Gram Sabha can control moneylending, liquor sale
6. **Dispute resolution:** Traditional dispute resolution mechanisms preserved
7. **Customary law:** Gram Sabha can safeguard tribal customs, cultural identity
8. **Mining leases:** Gram Sabha consultation mandatory before granting mining lease for minor minerals

### Traditional Tribal Council vs Gram Sabha (2022 PYQ)

| Aspect | Traditional Tribal Council | Gram Sabha under PESA |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | Customary, evolved over centuries | Statutory, created by law |
| **Membership** | Clan/tribe elders, hereditary chiefs | All adult residents of village |
| **Authority** | Based on custom, consensus, social sanctions | Based on legal mandate |
| **Dispute resolution** | Customary law, oral tradition | Both customary + formal mechanisms |
| **Women's role** | Varies — excluded in many tribal councils; exceptions (Khasi, some NE tribes) | Legally mandated 1/3 reservation for women |
| **Decision-making** | Consensus-driven; elder authority | Democratic voting |
| **Land matters** | Communal land management by tradition | Statutory powers over land acquisition consultation |
| **Accountability** | Social — to community | Legal — to state + community |
| **Strength** | Deep cultural legitimacy | Legal backing + financial powers |
| **Weakness** | Can be conservative, gender-unequal | Bureaucratic capture; poor awareness among tribals |

### PESA vs VI Schedule (2018 PYQ)

| Feature | PESA | VI Schedule |
|---|---|---|
| Region | V Schedule Areas (10 states, Central/Peninsular India) | 4 NE states |
| Body | Gram Sabha + Panchayats | Autonomous District Councils |
| Legislative power | No — only consultative + consent | Yes — ADCs can make laws |
| Judicial power | Limited (customary dispute resolution) | Full — village and district courts |
| Land control | Consultation required for acquisition | ADC controls allotment |
| Scope | Village level | District level |
| Financial autonomy | Depends on state devolution | Can levy taxes |

### Impact on Women's Political Participation (2024 PYQ)
- 1/3 reservation in panchayats → tribal women entering formal governance for first time
- But: Sarpanch pati (husband/male relative rules through female proxy) is widespread
- Where effective: Women in Gram Sabha using MFP rights to generate income (Odisha, Jharkhand)
- Challenge: Patriarchal tribal councils resist women's authority in Gram Sabha
- **Way forward:** Capacity building for women panchayat members; convergence of SHG movement with PESA

## BB-16: NGOs IN TRIBAL DEVELOPMENT

**PYQ Hook:** "Role of NGOs in tribal development" (2014, 2016, 2019), "NGOs critiqued for interfering" (2015/20), "NGOs empowering tribal women" (2021/15), "NGOs in health & education" (2014/15)

### Positive Contributions
1. **Education:** Barefoot College (Bunker Roy) — trained tribal women as solar engineers; Adivasi Academy (Tejgadh, Gujarat) — culturally rooted education
2. **Health:** Jan Swasthya Sahyog (Chhattisgarh) — community health workers trained from tribal communities; SEARCH (Gadchiroli) — Abhay Bang's work on neonatal mortality reduction
3. **Legal empowerment:** FRA implementation — Vasundhara (Odisha), Kalpavriksh helped tribals claim forest rights
4. **Livelihood:** PRADAN — introduced SHGs among tribal women; sericulture, lac cultivation
5. **Documentation:** Survival International — advocacy for Sentinelese, Jarawa protection
6. **Women's empowerment:** Seva Mandir (Udaipur) — SHGs among Bhil women; SEWA — organized tribal women informal workers

### Criticisms (2015 PYQ — "NGOs critiqued for interfering")
1. **Foreign funding agenda:** Some NGOs accused of pursuing donor priorities rather than tribal needs; FCRA restrictions tightened
2. **Religious conversion:** Missionary NGOs — education/health as vehicle for conversion; creates intra-tribal division
3. **Development disruption:** Environmental NGOs blocking industrial projects → tribals lose employment opportunities (counter-argument: tribals themselves often resist — see Niyamgiri)
4. **Dependency creation:** Tribals become dependent on NGO programs rather than self-reliant
5. **Unaccountable:** Not elected, not transparent — who do they represent?
6. **Cultural imposition:** Some NGOs impose urban middle-class values (gender norms, consumption patterns) on tribal communities
7. **Intelligence Bureau Report (2014):** Accused several NGOs (Greenpeace, etc.) of stalling India's GDP growth — controversial, but reflected government stance

### Balanced View for Exam
- NGOs fill **vacuum where state fails** — remote areas, culturally sensitive work
- But need **accountability framework** — transparency, community ownership
- Best model: **NGOs build capacity, then withdraw** — community takes over (PRADAN model)
- Partnership model: NGO + State + Community (tripartite) works best

## BB-17: ROLE OF ANTHROPOLOGY IN DEVELOPMENT

**PYQ Hook:** Asked EVERY YEAR. "Role of anthropology in tribal and rural development" (2018/20), "Anthropological perspective on development" (2022/15), "Anthropological knowledge in rural development" (2016/15), "Role in nation building" (2023/15), "Strengths and weaknesses" (2018/20)

### Why Anthropology is Essential for Development

1. **Holistic understanding:** Views community as whole system — economy, kinship, religion, ecology interconnected; development planners see only "sectors"
2. **Emic perspective:** Insider's viewpoint — understands what development means TO the community, not just FOR them
3. **Participant observation:** Long-term fieldwork reveals ground realities that surveys miss
4. **Cultural sensitivity:** Identifies cultural barriers and facilitators for development programs
5. **Community participation:** Anthropological methods (PRA, PLA) ensure beneficiary voice in planning
6. **Conflict mediation:** Understands inter-community dynamics — can prevent/resolve conflicts arising from development interventions

### Contributions to Tribal Development

| Area | Anthropological Contribution |
|---|---|
| **FRA implementation** | Mapping of community forest resources using ethnographic methods; documenting traditional use patterns |
| **PVTG programs** | Micro-plans based on ethnographic understanding of each PVTG's specific needs |
| **Health programs** | Understanding ethno-medicine → integrating traditional + modern healthcare; SEARCH Gadchiroli model |
| **Education** | Multilingual education based on linguistic anthropology; culturally appropriate curriculum design |
| **R&R programs** | Social Impact Assessment (SIA) mandated under RFCTLARR 2013 — anthropologists conduct these |
| **Disaster management** | 2004 Tsunami — Onge, Jarawa survived due to traditional knowledge; anthropologists documented this |
| **Land rights** | Documenting customary land tenure systems to support legal claims |

### Contributions to Rural Development

| Contribution | Example |
|---|---|
| **PRA/PLA methodology** | Robert Chambers — participatory rural appraisal; used in all rural development programs |
| **Understanding peasant economy** | Chayanov, Scott — "moral economy"; why farmers resist "rational" innovations |
| **Green Revolution critique** | Anthropologists documented social costs — inequality, ecological damage, cultural disruption |
| **Panchayati Raj** | Studying local power structures to make PRI genuinely participatory |
| **Watershed development** | Community-level water management through anthropological understanding of commons |

### Key Scholars in Applied Anthropology (India)

| Scholar | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **S.C. Dube** | "India's Changing Villages" (1958) — community development study |
| **L.P. Vidyarthi** | "Applied Anthropology in India" — advocated for anthropology in planning |
| **T.N. Madan** | Cultural analysis of development programs |
| **K.S. Singh** | People of India Project — largest anthropological survey; provided data for development planning |
| **Virginius Xaxa** | Xaxa Committee (2014) — reviewed tribal development policies; recommended rights-based approach |

### Strengths and Weaknesses (2018 PYQ)

**Strengths:**
- Only discipline that combines biological, cultural, linguistic, archaeological perspectives
- Long-term fieldwork → deep understanding vs survey-based snapshot
- Comparative method → can learn from cross-cultural solutions
- Ethical commitment to community welfare (AAA Code of Ethics)

**Weaknesses:**
- Small-scale focus — difficulty scaling insights to policy level
- Time-intensive fieldwork — development planners want quick answers
- Sometimes romanticizes tradition — resists modernization even when communities want it
- Academic language can be inaccessible to policy makers
- Limited quantitative skills — policy world demands numbers

### Exam Conclusion Template
"Anthropology provides the **cultural compass** that technical development programs lack. Without it, development becomes imposition; with it, development becomes empowerment. The discipline's strength lies in its insistence on understanding before intervening — what **S.C. Dube** called the 'human dimension of development.'"

## BB-18: COMMUNALISM & REGIONALISM

**PYQ Hook:** "Anthropological understanding of communalism" (2019/10), "Regionalism as opportunity and threat" (2022/10), "Secularism vs religiosity vs fundamentalism vs spiritualism" (2024/15), "Theocratic vs secular state" (2024/15), "Regionalism and communalism in ethno-political movements" (undated/20)

### Communalism — Anthropological Understanding

**Definition:** Ideology that treats religious community as a political entity — assumes all members of a religion have common secular interests opposed to other religious communities.

**Anthropological Perspective:**
- **T.N. Madan:** Secularism as conceived in India is "impossible, impractical, and impotent" for deeply religious societies — need to engage religion, not banish it from public sphere
- **Clifford Geertz:** Religion as cultural system — provides meaning-making; becomes communal when politicized
- **Veena Das:** Communal violence is not "irrational mob frenzy" but organized political action using religious symbols
- Communalism = manipulation of primordial religious sentiments for instrumental political ends (combines primordial and instrumentalist approaches)

**Tribal Context:**
- Tribes are generally LESS susceptible to communalism — animistic traditions, syncretic beliefs
- But: Conversion politics creates communal tensions (Hindu-Christian in tribal Jharkhand/Odisha)
- Cow vigilantism has affected tribal communities who traditionally eat beef
- Ghar Wapsi campaigns target tribal Christians — communal overtones

### Regionalism

**Definition:** Loyalty to one's region over nation; demand for greater autonomy or separate statehood based on regional identity.

**Types:**
1. **Positive/Accommodative:** Celebrates regional diversity within national framework; drives decentralization
2. **Negative/Secessionist:** Threatens national integration; separatism (early Naga movement, Kashmir)
3. **Autonomist:** Demands more powers within existing system (Bodo, Gorkha)

**Causes in Tribal Context:**
- **Cultural distinctiveness:** NE tribes are racially, linguistically, religiously distinct from mainland
- **Economic deprivation:** Resource-rich tribal regions remain undeveloped (Jharkhand paradox — mineral wealth but mass poverty)
- **Administrative neglect:** Distant central/state governments don't understand tribal reality
- **In-migration threat:** Outsiders taking jobs, land (Jharkhand "diku" sentiment, NE "insider-outsider" issue)
- **Historical memory:** Pre-colonial autonomy → colonial subjugation → post-colonial marginalization

**As Opportunity (2022 PYQ):**
- Drives federal decentralization — new states (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Uttarakhand) brought governance closer
- Preserves cultural diversity — pluralism strengthens democracy
- Regional demands lead to protective legislation (V/VI Schedules, PESA, Inner Line Permit)

**As Threat:**
- Secessionism — Naga, Mizo movements involved armed conflict
- Ethnic violence — Bodo–Santhal, Naga–Kuki clashes
- Undermines national integration narrative
- Can be manipulated by elites for power

### Secularism vs Religiosity vs Fundamentalism vs Spiritualism (2024 PYQ)

| Concept | Definition (Anthropological) |
|---|---|
| **Secularism** | Separation of religion from governance; equal treatment of all religions by state. Indian secularism ≠ absence of religion but **equal respect for all** (sarva dharma sambhava) |
| **Religiosity** | Personal devotion and practice of religion; spiritual engagement without political dimension. Tribes are deeply religious (animism, nature worship) but historically not fundamentalist |
| **Religious Fundamentalism** | Rigid adherence to literal interpretation of religious texts; resistance to modernity; political mobilization around religious identity. Often anti-pluralist |
| **Spiritualism** | Inner quest for meaning beyond organized religion; personal transcendence. Tribal animism is closer to spiritualism — no scripture, no hierarchy, no orthodoxy |

**Tribal angle:** Tribal religion = spiritualism + religiosity (nature worship, ancestor worship, animism — deeply personal, communal, non-dogmatic). When tribes encounter organized religions (Hinduism, Christianity, Islam), the encounter can produce either enriching syncretism or destructive fundamentalism.

## BB-19: DEVELOPMENTAL PROGRAMMES

**PYQ Hook:** "Tribal Sub Plan" (2017/15), "ITDP objectives" (2022/20), "Tribal development programmes — social transformation" (2016/15), "Welfare plans and programmes" (2019/15)

### Key Programmes

| Programme | Year | Key Features | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tribal Sub Plan (TSP)** | 1974 | Proportional allocation of plan funds to ST development (as per population %); implemented through state plans | Funds often diverted to general schemes; "notional" allocation — not actual spending; no accountability |
| **Integrated Tribal Development Projects (ITDP)** | 1974 | Area-based approach — blocks with 50%+ ST population; infrastructure + livelihood package | Top-down planning; ignored tribal knowledge; one-size-fits-all |
| **MADA (Modified Area Development Approach)** | 1979 | For areas with 50%+ ST population but not covered under ITDP; smaller scale | Underfunded; bureaucratic |
| **Cluster Approach** | 1982 | For scattered tribal pockets with 50%+ ST population | Limited coverage |
| **EMRS (Eklavya Model Residential Schools)** | 1998 | Model schools for ST students (like Navodaya); 740 sanctioned | Quality varies; far from home; cultural gap |
| **Van Bandhu Kalyan Yojana** | 2014 | Umbrella scheme; outcome-oriented; convergence of 12 ministries | Still evolving; monitoring challenges |
| **MFP-MSP (Minimum Support Price for MFP)** | 2014 | Fair price for forest produce (tendu, mahua, sal seed, bamboo, etc.) through TRIFED | Procurement infrastructure weak in many states |
| **Van Dhan Vikas Kendra** | 2018 | SHG-based MFP processing and value addition; tribal entrepreneurship | Capacity building needed; market linkages weak |
| **PM-JANMAN** | 2023 | ₹24,000 crore for PVTGs — housing, water, healthcare, connectivity | Too early to assess |

### Common Criticisms of Tribal Development Programmes
1. **Top-down approach:** Designed in Delhi, implemented without local consultation
2. **Fund leakage:** TSP funds routinely diverted to general population schemes
3. **Bureaucratic:** Multiple departments, no convergence — tribals run from pillar to post
4. **Welfare vs rights:** Programmes treat tribals as beneficiaries, not rights-holders (shift needed: welfare → rights — FRA model)
5. **Ignores heterogeneity:** Treats all 705 ST communities as one; PVTGs need different approach than larger tribes
6. **Measurement failure:** Success measured by expenditure, not outcomes (violates Nehru's Tribal Panchsheel Principle 5)
7. **Intermediary capture:** Benefits captured by tribal elites, contractors, officials — not grassroots tribals

### Impact on Social Transformation (2016 PYQ)
**Positive:** Improved literacy (ST literacy rose from 10.3% in 1961 to 59% in 2011), health infrastructure in tribal areas, political representation through reservation, TRIFED improved MFP marketing

**Negative:** Tribal identity under threat from mainstreaming; consumerism replacing subsistence ethos; traditional authority structures weakened by panchayati raj; increased inequality within tribes (educated/employed vs marginalized)

## BB-20: TRIBE & NATION STATE

**PYQ Hook:** "Tribal unrest as incompatibility between tribes and nation-state" (2019/20), "Tribe vs indigenous in other countries" (2017/20), "Relationship between tribal communities and nation-state on governance" (undated/15)

### Core Tension
- **Nation-state logic:** Uniform citizenship, territorial sovereignty, development, integration, resource extraction for "national interest"
- **Tribal logic:** Community-based identity, customary law, communal resource ownership, autonomy, self-determination
- **Conflict arises when:** State claims eminent domain over tribal lands; imposes uniform laws on customary systems; views tribal areas as resource peripheries

### ILO Convention 169 (1989) — Indigenous and Tribal Peoples (2024 PYQ)

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Indigenous and Tribal Peoples Convention |
| **Key principles** | Self-identification, consultation, participation, consent (FPIC — Free Prior Informed Consent) |
| **Land rights** | Recognize collective rights to land and natural resources |
| **Consent requirement** | Governments must consult and seek consent before projects affecting indigenous peoples |
| **Relocation** | Only with free and informed consent; right to return when circumstances permit |
| **Ratifiers** | 24 countries (mostly Latin American) |
| **India's position** | **NOT a signatory** — India argues its constitutional provisions (V/VI Schedule, FRA, PESA) are sufficient; also resists "indigenous" label — argues all Indians are indigenous |

### "Indigenous" vs "Tribe" — Comparative

| Country | Term | Status | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **India** | Scheduled Tribe | Constitutional category; not called "indigenous" | Government rejects "indigenous" label — argues no Indian is non-indigenous (settler-native dichotomy doesn't apply) |
| **Australia** | Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander | Recognized as indigenous; Native Title Act (1993); Mabo judgment | Stolen Generation; land rights movement; 2023 Voice referendum rejected |
| **USA/Canada** | Native American / First Nations | Treaty-based sovereignty; reservation system | Self-governance rights; Indian Gaming Regulatory Act; Truth and Reconciliation |
| **Latin America** | Pueblos Indígenas | ILO 169 ratified; constitutional recognition | Bolivia — plurinational state; FPIC implemented (unevenly) |
| **Africa** | Varies — indigenous peoples, pastoralists, hunter-gatherers | ILO 169 not widely ratified; African Charter recognizes | San (Bushmen), Maasai, Pygmies — dispossession continues |

### India's Argument Against "Indigenous" Label
1. India has no settler–native dichotomy (unlike Americas, Australia)
2. All communities are "indigenous" — who came first is anthropologically debatable
3. Constitutional framework (Art. 342, V/VI Schedule) provides sufficient protection
4. "Indigenous" creates legal obligation under international law that India wants to avoid (especially FPIC, which would slow "development")
5. **Counter-argument:** UN Declaration on Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007) — India voted in favor but with reservations; tribes fit UN Working Group definition regardless of India's terminology

### Governance Incompatibility (the core exam argument)
- State imposes **individual property rights** — tribes have **communal ownership**
- State demands **written law** — tribes follow **customary law** (oral, elder-interpreted)
- State extracts **resources for national GDP** — tribes see resources as **sacred, inalienable**
- State measures **progress in economic indicators** — tribes value **ecological sustainability and cultural continuity**
- **Way forward:** Not "tribe OR state" but negotiated coexistence — V/VI Schedule, PESA, FRA show the path but implementation is key
