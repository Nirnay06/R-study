## 2. What is Caste?

Caste is a **social group in India into which a person is born**. Members share common customs, follow similar traditions, and marry within their own group. Caste is linked to traditional occupations and forms part of a larger system of social hierarchy — including **varna** and **jati** — which influences social relations, marriage patterns, and status across generations.

---

## 3. Key Features of the Caste System

1. **Hereditary Identity**: Caste is determined entirely by birth — inherited from parents, impossible to change. It is a classic **ascriptive identity** — fixed for life regardless of personal achievement.

2. **Hierarchy**: Castes are arranged in a fixed ladder from pure to impure — ranked through notions of **ritual purity and pollution** linked to occupation, food, and behaviour. This hierarchy, not wealth or power, governed social status.

3. **Endogamy**: Marriage within one's own Jati was a strict social rule — inter-caste marriages were prohibited and often punished. Though slowly increasing today, endogamy remains dominant.

4. **Occupational Specialisation**: Every Jati was tied to a hereditary occupation. The **Jajmani system** institutionalised this economically within villages. Industrialisation has loosened this in cities, but the caste-occupation link persists in rural India.

5. **Restrictions and Discrimination**: The purity-pollution logic governed everyday interactions — food sharing, temple entry, and use of public spaces. At the extreme, Untouchables (now Scheduled Castes) faced the harshest exclusions. **Untouchability was abolished under Article 17**, but its social reality has not fully disappeared.

6. **Internal Governance**: **Caste Panchayats** served as community courts — settling disputes over marriage, conduct, and inter-caste tensions — giving the system a self-regulating mechanism that further entrenched its hold over social life.

---

## 4. Varna and Jati — Two Distinct Realities

| | **Varna** | **Jati** |
|--|-----------|----------|
| **Nature** | Ancient, textual model | Lived, ground-level reality |
| **Division** | Four broad categories | Thousands of local communities |
| **Categories** | Brahmin (priests/scholars), Kshatriya (warriors/rulers), Vaishya (traders/merchants), Shudra (labourers/service providers) | Each with own traditional occupation, social rank, rules of interaction |
| **Scope** | Pan-Indian, ideological framework from scriptures | Local, regional |
| **Reality** | Theoretical blueprint — never fully operational | The **real and effective unit** of the caste system |

**Key point:** Scholars widely agree that it is the **Jati**, not the Varna, that is the real and effective unit of the caste system.

---

## 5. Caste as a Closed System — vs. Class

- **Open system** (e.g., class-based Western societies): individuals can move up or down based on education, income, and effort.
- **Caste is a closed system**: birth determines everything. No amount of individual effort could change one's caste. Generations performed the same occupations, married within the same group, and occupied the same social position — not by choice, but by the accident of birth.

## 6. Gandhi vs. Ambedkar — On Caste and Untouchability

| Dimension | Mahatma Gandhi | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Basic Approach** | Moral reform, change of heart, non-violence | Structural reform, legal safeguards, institutional change |
| **On Varna** | Accepted a reinterpreted, non-hierarchical varna; opposed untouchability | Insisted both caste AND varna must be dismantled |
| **Methods** | Social harmony, constructive work, temple entry for "Harijans" | Rights, legal protections, political power, modern education |
| **Terminology** | "Harijan" (children of God) | "Dalit" — emphasising oppression, resistance, and self-respect |
| **Political position** | Opposed separate electorates — prioritised national unity (**Poona Pact**) | Demanded separate electorates for autonomous political voice |
| **Religious position** | Worked within Hinduism — hoped to reform it from inside | Rejected Hinduism (*Annihilation of Caste*, 1936) — converted to Buddhism (1956) |

**Both shared the goal** of ending untouchability and ameliorating the condition of the downtrodden.

## 7. Historical Phases of Dalit Assertion

### Phase I — The Colonial Foundation

- **Jyotiba and Savitribai Phule**: Founded the **Satyashodhak Samaj (1873)**; opened the first schools for untouchables and women, directly challenging Brahminical control over education.

- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar**: The most pivotal figure. Led the **Mahad Satyagraha (1927)** for water rights; secured political representation through the **Poona Pact**; drafted the Indian Constitution. Ended his journey with a mass **conversion to Buddhism (1956)** to escape the caste hierarchy.

- **Periyar (E.V. Ramasamy)**: Launched the **Self-Respect Movement** in South India, using rationalism and anti-caste rhetoric to challenge North Indian cultural dominance.

### Phase II — Post-Independence Radicalism

- **Namdeo Dhasal and J.V. Pawar (Dalit Panthers)**: Inspired by the Black Panthers (USA), they used radical poetry and street protest in the **1970s** to fight police brutality and rural violence.

- **Kanshi Ram**: Shifted the focus from "protest" to "power." Founded **BAMCEF** (Backward and Minority Communities Employees Federation, 1978) for employees and the **BSP** (Bahujan Samaj Party), teaching Dalits that they are the "rulers" (Bahujans), not just victims.

- **Mayawati**: As head of the BSP, became a symbol of "Dalit Pride" — became Chief Minister of UP **four times**, proving a Dalit woman could lead India's political heartland.

### Phase III — The Modern Wave of Dalit Movements

- **Bhim Army (Chandrashekhar Azad)**: Grassroots leaders combining social media savvy with rapid physical mobilisation.

- **Bhima Koregaon (2018)**: Dalits reclaiming historical battles to build a narrative of "valor" rather than just "victimhood."

- **Institutional murders**: Institutional deaths no longer seen as individual tragedies. Nationwide demand for the **Rohith Act** to protect students in higher education (following suicide of Rohith Vemula).

- **Una Protests (2016)**: Dalits refused to perform caste-assigned jobs (handling carcasses), hitting the traditional village economy — a structural shift.

- **Digital activism**: #DalitLivesMatter bypasses mainstream media bias.

- **Intersectional movements**: Dalit women's activism addressing double marginalisation (gender + caste).

---

## 8. Limitations of Dalit Movements

- **Internal Fragmentation**: Debate around sub-classification within SC/ST categories has exposed sub-caste rivalries, weakening the broader unified front.

- **Political Appropriation**: Mainstream political parties have repeatedly co-opted Dalit icons like Ambedkar into broader religious or nationalist narratives, often diluting the movement's radical anti-caste core.

- **The Wealth Gap**: Despite the rise of Dalit Capitalism and initiatives like **DICCI** (Dalit Indian Chamber of Commerce and Industry), the majority of the community remains trapped in the informal economy and hazardous sanitation work.

- **Resistance and Backlash**: Dalit assertion often faces violent backlash and everyday discrimination from dominant castes.

- **Reinforces Caste Consciousness**: Mobilisation as "Dalits" strengthens solidarity but can also keep caste as a primary marker of identity, making politics caste-based even though it is anti-caste in intent.

- **The Saviour Narrative**: Representation behind the camera remains thin.

| Feature | The Upper-Caste "Saviour" Narrative | Subversive Anti-Caste Cinema |
|---------|-------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Example | *Article 15*, *Aarakshan* | *Pariyerum Perumal*, *Sairat*, *Jai Bhim* |
| Protagonist | Upper-caste, educated "outsider" | Member of the oppressed community |
| Tone | Empathy and charity | Assertion and self-respect |
| Message | "We must help them." | "We must claim our rights." |

- **Brahminical Cultural Dominance**: Hinduism and its scriptures remain the governing ideological framework. Alternative frameworks like **Neo-Buddhism** (Ambedkar's 1956 movement, focused on equality, social reform, and liberation from the Hindu caste system) have limited mass appeal.

## 9. Forces That Have Weakened Caste

| Force | How it Challenged Caste |
|-------|------------------------|
| **Constitutional and Legal Reform** | Article 17 abolished untouchability; Articles 15 and 16 banned discrimination; reservations under Articles 15(4) and 16(4); Universal Adult Franchise gave lower-caste communities real political power |
| **Urbanisation** | Cities offer anonymity; shared spaces (metros, offices, restaurants) make strict caste rules difficult; new bonds form around profession, neighbourhood, interest |
| **Education and Economic Mobility** | Post-1991 liberalisation created occupational opportunities not tied to caste; a small but growing Dalit and OBC middle class emerged |
| **Sanskritisation** | Lower castes adopting rituals and lifestyle of upper castes weakened rigid occupational and ritual boundaries |
| **Westernisation** | Rational outlook, English education, urban lifestyles loosened caste rigidity in cities |
| **Rising Inter-Caste Marriages** | Slowly but directionally increasing. Supreme Court (2018, *Shafin Jahan* case) reaffirmed right to choose partner is fundamental under Article 21 |
| **Anti-Caste Social Movements** | Dalit and backward-caste movements have collectively challenged caste hierarchy |

## 10. Forces That Have Sustained Caste

1. **Endogamy**: The vast majority of Indians continue to marry within their caste. Matrimonial websites openly offer caste filters. **Ambedkar called intermarriage the "real remedy"** because endogamy is the biological mechanism through which caste reproduces itself across generations.

2. **Caste in the Private Realm**: Even where caste has retreated from public life, it quietly persists — seating arrangements at weddings, choice of priest, food service during ceremonies, and inheritance practices.

3. **Everyday Discrimination — The "Caste Gaze"**:
   - People routinely sized up by name, dialect, or appearance to determine caste.
   - Separate utensils for Dalits remain a reality in many households.
   - Many Dalit employees **conceal their caste identity at work** to avoid discrimination (33% — Equality Labs Report, 2023).
   - **27% of Indian households** practise untouchability (IHDS-II, 2011-12): 30% rural, 20% urban.

4. **The Caste-Wealth Nexus**:
   - SCs own only **~9% of India's total agricultural land**, and 92% of all land held by SCs is "marginal" (0-2 hectares).
   - **90% of manual scavengers** belong to Scheduled Castes (Oxfam).
   - Dalits are **12.6%** of the urban population but constitute **20.3%** of slum residents (Census 2011).
   - SC and ST workers earn on average **15% lower wages** than other workers in both public and private sectors (Oxfam).

5. **Caste in Politics — Reinvention, Not Decline**:
   - Caste has evolved from a social hierarchy into a **political interest group**.
   - Caste alliances continue to drive electoral outcomes.
   - Sociologists describe: caste has shifted from a **vertical ritual hierarchy** to a **horizontal political solidarity bloc** — the political reinvention of caste.

6. **Caste Associations**:
   - Modern organisations formed by caste members to promote collective interests and welfare.
   - Provide educational, financial, and employment support to caste members.
   - Mobilise members for political representation and reservations.
   - Strengthen caste identity through meetings and community events.
   - Facilitate marriage alliances within the caste community.
   - Act as pressure groups for social, economic, and political benefits.

## 11. Dominant Castes and Reservation Demands

A **dominant caste** (concept by **M.N. Srinivas**) exercises disproportionate power at the regional level — not through ritual status, but through a combination of **numerical strength, land ownership, and political influence**.

Examples: **Marathas** (Maharashtra), **Jats** (Haryana, UP, Rajasthan), **Patels/Patidars** (Gujarat), **Yadavs** (Bihar, UP), **Vokkaligas** (Karnataka).

**Why dominant castes demand reservations:**

| Cause | Explanation |
|-------|-------------|
| Agrarian distress | Economic dominance was land-based; farm income stagnation has eroded this |
| Unemployment crisis | Intense competition for government jobs makes General Category status a disadvantage |
| Relative deprivation | Affirmative action has enabled OBC/SC upward mobility — dominant castes feel their relative status has declined |
| Vote-bank politics | Politicians exploit these numerically large communities with reservation promises |

**Why this is problematic:**
- Extending reservations to dominant castes dilutes benefits for genuinely marginalised SCs and STs.
- Reservations are a **social justice tool** for historically oppressed groups — not an employment benefit scheme.
- Reinforces caste identities rather than addressing root causes — agrarian crisis and jobless growth.

**Case Study — Maratha Reservation**: Maharashtra's SEBC Act (2018) granting reservation to Marathas was **struck down** by the Supreme Court in *Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil v. Chief Minister* **(2021)** as unconstitutional — it exceeded the **50% ceiling** set by the **Indra Sawhney judgment (1992)** without sufficient justification.

---

## 12. Reservation and Religious Conversion — A Legal Debate

The Supreme Court has reaffirmed: **SC reservation benefits apply exclusively to Hindus, Sikhs, and Buddhists** (as defined under the Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order, 1950). Individuals who convert to Christianity or Islam lose their SC status and reservation benefits.

**The sociological debate:**

- **Against the judgment**: Caste discrimination does not disappear with religious conversion — a Dalit Christian or Dalit Muslim continues to face social stigma, occupational exclusion, and economic deprivation rooted in their caste origin, regardless of their faith.

- **For the judgment**: Reservation was designed to address caste-based oppression within the Hindu social order — extending it to religions that doctrinally reject caste hierarchy may dilute its original purpose.

**Fundamental question**: Is caste a **social reality** or a **religious construct**? If caste follows a person beyond religion, as sociological evidence suggests, then denying reservation purely on grounds of faith raises serious concerns around **Articles 14, 15, and 21**.

---

## 13. Urban Segregation

Urbanisation was expected to dissolve caste — instead, it has made caste **more subtle and harder to confront**.

- Housing discrimination has shifted from open untouchability to coded exclusion.
- **"Pure vegetarian only"** housing societies or **separate service entrances** for domestic workers often act as proxies for caste-based exclusion without explicitly mentioning caste.

---

## 14. Caste in Higher Education

- Dalit students in IITs and central universities face social isolation, academic discrimination, and higher dropout rates.
- **Language barrier**: Fluency in English is often used as a marker of social acceptability; students from vernacular or rural backgrounds are frequently mocked or sidelined.
- This creates a **form of social hierarchy based on language and cultural capital** — caste discrimination adapted into subtle, modern forms.

## 15. Recent Trends and Issues Related to Caste

### Caste in Digital Spaces
- Matrimonial platforms like Shaadi.com allow users to filter matches by caste. **Over 60% of profiles** specify caste preferences (EPW Study, 2019).
- Social media caste groups provide solidarity but also serve as echo chambers.
- **Surname SEO**: people instantly search a celebrity or politician's caste online.
- Digital Dalit assertion: #DalitLivesMatter and #JusticeForRohithVemula challenge discrimination, bypassing mainstream media.

### Sub-Classification Within SC/ST — A New Legal Turn
- **State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)**: 7-judge Constitutional Bench held that **states can sub-classify within SC/ST categories** to prioritise the most marginalised sub-groups.
- Acknowledges: (a) **"elite capture"** — reservation benefits disproportionately went to better-off SC sub-groups; (b) intra-group inequality is real; (c) the most deprived among the marginalised need targeted intervention.
- This has fuelled the rise of **sub-caste politics** — shift from broad Dalit politics to **micro-identity politics**.

### Globalisation of Caste — The Diaspora Factor
- Caste has **travelled beyond India's borders** with the Indian diaspora.
- **Cities like Seattle and states like California** have seen intense debates over legally banning caste discrimination.
- **Cisco lawsuit**: caste-based networks influence hiring and promotions even in global workplaces.
- Caste hierarchies are not a product of Indian soil alone — they are a **social habit that travels with people**.

### The Caste Census — Data as a Political Tool
- Demand for a **National Caste Census** is one of the most significant political debates of the mid-2020s.
- Slogan: **"Jitni Abadi, Utna Haq"** (Rights proportional to population).
- Major shift: from caste as a **ritual identity** to caste as a **numerical and political resource**.
- Caste is increasingly being leveraged through **data, statistics, and democratic arithmetic**.

### Cultural Assertion — Reclaiming Caste Identity
- **Anti-Caste Cinema and Music**: Nagraj Manjule (*Sairat*) and Neeraj Ghaywan (*Masaan*) challenge stereotypes and tell stories of resistance.
- **Dalit Pop** and rap: musicians pioneering a new genre of cultural assertion.
- **Aesthetic Identity**: Youth using fashion, tattoos, and social media bios to proudly display caste identity — turning what was once "hidden" into a brand of pride or "swag."

### Algorithmic Bias and Digital Harassment
- **Algorithm bubbles**: social media platforms push users into caste silos by recommending groups based on surnames or regional content — reinforcing caste echo chambers.
- **Digital harassment** of anti-caste activists: doxing, coordinated trolling, AI-generated misinformation — new tools of caste-based intimidation.

### Environmental Casteism — Who Bears the Burden?
- The impact of environmental hazards and urban planning is **not caste-neutral**.
- Historically marginalised communities are disproportionately concentrated near landfills, toxic industrial zones, and flood-prone areas.
- During natural disasters, the **speed and quality of government relief** often vary based on the caste composition of neighbourhoods.
- This emerging dimension — **environmental casteism** — shows that caste inequality extends into questions of ecology, climate resilience, and spatial justice.

## 16. The Aspiration of a 'Casteless' Society

- A **casteless society** envisions an inclusive social order where caste identity becomes irrelevant — equal opportunity regardless of birth.
- It is not merely the absence of caste labels but the **dismantling of structural inequalities** that caste has historically produced.
- However, "casteless" may be a myth in the Indian context — even those who claim to be casteless are often unconsciously shaped by caste.

**Ambedkar's Prescription:**
- **Inter-Caste Marriages**: the most direct and real remedy against caste — because **endogamy is the biological mechanism** through which caste reproduces itself across generations.
- Breaking endogamy fosters shared identities, dismantles purity myths, and enables genuine social and cultural integration.

A truly casteless society remains **a work in progress**.

## 17. Key Government Measures Against Caste Inequality

### Constitutional Safeguards

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Articles 15 and 16** | Prohibit caste-based discrimination in public life and employment; empower State to make special provisions for SCs, STs, and backward classes |
| **Article 17** | Abolishes untouchability in all forms |
| **Article 25(2)(b)** | Opens Hindu religious institutions to all castes |
| **Article 46** | DPSP — State to protect educational and economic interests of SCs, STs, and weaker sections |
| **Articles 330 and 332** | Political reservations in Lok Sabha and State Legislatures |
| **Article 338** | National Commission for Scheduled Castes (NCSC) |
| **Article 340** | Empowers President to appoint a commission to investigate conditions of backward classes |

### Key Legal Protections
- **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955** and **SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989**: enforce Article 17 and penalise caste-based violence and discrimination.
- **Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers Act, 2013**: seeks to eradicate manual scavenging and rehabilitate affected workers.

### Welfare Measures
- Targeted scholarships, hostels, coaching, and fee waivers for SC/ST/OBC students.
- Skill development and livelihood programmes for economic uplift.
- Public awareness campaigns to promote inter-caste harmony and reduce social stigma.

| **Social Morality** | Norms upheld by community consensus — often rooted in caste, patriarchy, and religious hierarchy — contrasted with constitutional morality |

## 22. Who Are the Tribes?

- **Adivasis** (literally "original inhabitants") — distinct social and cultural groups whose identity is rooted in an **ancestral relationship with land, forests, and a specific territory**.
- Unlike caste society (organised around ritual hierarchy and occupational stratification), tribal identity rests on **kinship, territoriality, and collective solidarity**.
- The Indian Constitution uses the administrative label **Scheduled Tribes (STs)**, defined under **Article 342**, which empowers the President to notify tribal communities.

**Why are tribes called Scheduled Tribes?**
- Their names are officially scheduled/listed in the **Constitution (Scheduled Tribes) Order, 1950** (as amended from time to time).
- This Order was issued by the President after consultation with state Governors.
- The listing gives them formal legal recognition as a distinct social group.
- Purpose: to help the government clearly identify communities that have remained backward due to isolation, difficult living conditions, and limited access to education and development.

**Lokur Committee (1965)** — Five criteria for identification of STs:
1. Primitive traits
2. Distinctive culture
3. Geographical isolation
4. Shyness of contact with the broader community
5. Backwardness

## 23. Economic and Social Features of Tribal Society

### Economic Features:
- Livelihoods are pre-agricultural or semi-agricultural — **hunting, gathering, pastoralism, and shifting cultivation (Jhum)**.
- Land is **communally owned** and collectively managed, not held as individual private property.
- Economic exchange operates through **reciprocity and barter** rather than profit-oriented market transactions.
- Largely **subsistence economies** with minimal surplus generation.
- Use of primitive and simple tools and technology.

### Social and Cultural Features:
- Each tribe has its own distinct language, dress, customs, and systems of marriage and inheritance.
- Religion is **animistic** — centred on **nature-worship**, ancestor-worship, and spirit-worship.
- Tribal societies are often **egalitarian**, with minimal social stratification.
- Social organisation combines **exogamous clans** (must marry outside the clan) within an **endogamous tribe** (must marry within the tribe).
- Governance rests with **clan councils** and **village elders** — community-based, not state-delegated.
- Rich folk traditions — dance, music, oral literature, and festivals — tied to agricultural seasons and ecological cycles.
- Women generally enjoy greater economic participation and social visibility than in mainstream caste Hindu society.
- Some tribes maintain **youth dormitories**: the **Ghotul** (among the Gonds) and the **Dhumkuria** (among the Oraons) — spaces for socialisation, cultural education, and collective training.

## 24. Demographics and Diversity — The Architecture of Tribal India

**India is home to 705 Scheduled Tribes, constituting 8.6% of its population (~10.45 crore — Census 2011).**

### Geographic Distribution

| Region | Major Tribes |
|--------|-------------|
| Central India (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, MP) | Gonds, Mundas, Santhals, Oraons — India's largest concentration |
| Northeast India | Nagas, Mizos, Khasis, Garos, Bodos — ethnically and linguistically most distinct |
| Western India (Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra) | Bhils, Meenas, Warlis |
| Eastern India (West Bengal, Odisha) | Santhals, Ho, Lodhas |
| Southern India (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh) | Todas, Irulas, Chenchus, Koyas |
| Andaman and Nicobar Islands | Jarawas, Sentinelese, Great Andamanese — world's most isolated communities |

**Contemporary Note**: The **Sentinelese** of North Sentinel Island are among the last uncontacted peoples in the world. India maintains a strict non-contact protection policy under the **Andaman and Nicobar Islands (Protection of Aboriginal Tribes) Act, 1956**. The 2018 killing of an American missionary who illegally attempted contact renewed debate about enforcement adequacy.

### The Immense Internal Diversity

- **Population scale**: Gond tribe numbers over **12 million** — one of the world's largest tribal communities. The Great Andamanese number **fewer than 50**, on the verge of extinction.

- **Linguistic diversity**: Tribal languages span four major language families:
  - **Austroasiatic**: Santhali, Mundari, Ho
  - **Dravidian**: Gondi, Kondh, Toda
  - **Tibeto-Burman**: Naga languages, Bodo, Mizo
  - **Indo-Aryan**: Bhili, Halbi
  - Only **Santhali** has been recognised in the **Eighth Schedule** — the sole tribal language to receive official constitutional status.

- **Social and kinship diversity**:
  - **Khasi** of Meghalaya: **matriliny** — property and clan identity pass through the mother's line; youngest daughter inherits ancestral home.
  - Gonds and Bhils: patrilineal.
  - **Todas** of the Nilgiris: historically practiced **fraternal polyandry** (a woman marrying multiple brothers) — tied to land-resource management.

- **Economic diversity**:

| Livelihood | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| Shifting cultivation (Jhum) | Nagas, Mizos |
| Settled agriculture | Gonds, Bhils |
| Forest-based gathering | Chenchus, honey collectors |
| Pastoralism | Todas (cattle), Bakarwals (sheep/goat) |
| Plantation labour | Tea tribes of Assam (historically indentured) |
| Fishing | Coastal tribes of Andaman, Kerala, Odisha |

- **Racial diversity**: Dravidian racial group (Gonds, Konds, Todas); Mongoloid group (Bhutias of Sikkim, most Northeast tribes); Proto-Australoid (Mundas, Santhals, Ho — among India's oldest inhabitants).

- **Developmental diversity**: **Meenas** of Rajasthan: politically and administratively influential. **Shompen** of Andaman Islands: almost entirely isolated from administrative systems.

## 25. The Single Category Debate — Diversity vs. Unity

India's 700+ tribal communities are clubbed under a single "Scheduled Tribe" category — both an **administrative necessity and an analytical oversimplification**.

### When the Single-Category Framework Is Valid:

- **Constitutional Safeguards**: Notification under Article 342 and uniform protections under Articles 15(4), 16(4), 330, and 332, along with the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, PESA, and FRA, require a collective category to function legally.
- **Shared Structural Vulnerability**: All tribal communities share common disadvantages in poverty, literacy, health, and asset ownership.
- **Common Historical Experience**: All tribal communities share a history of geographical isolation, colonial dispossession, marginalisation by mainstream society, and post-Independence displacement.
- **Institutional Mechanisms**: Bodies like Tribal Advisory Councils, Tribal Research Institutes, and the NCST depend on a unified category to operate effectively.

### When the Single-Category Framework Must Be Modified:

- **Reservation and Welfare**: Sub-classification is necessary so that the most deprived sub-groups are prioritised.
- **Education**: A uniform regional language policy erodes distinct tribal identities. Teaching in native languages preserves culture while improving learning outcomes.
- **Urban vs. Rural tribal concerns**: Different needs requiring different responses.
- **Customary Law**: Imposing uniform tribal law risks overriding legitimate customary rights.
- **Political Representation**: Tribes political minorities in Jharkhand/Odisha need far stronger safeguards than those who hold demographic majority in Mizoram/Meghalaya.

**Principle: Protect together, develop differently.**

## 26. Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)

- Within the broader ST category, **75 tribal communities** are identified as PVTGs — the most marginalised among the marginalised.
- Earlier called "Primitive Tribal Groups," renamed in **2006** to reflect greater sensitivity.

**Defining characteristics:**
- Pre-agricultural or stagnant technology.
- Declining or stagnant population.
- Extremely low literacy and minimal contact with mainstream society.
- Economic backwardness.

**Government scheme**: Dedicated **PVTG Development Mission under PM JANMAN scheme (2023)** — focusing on housing, roads, clean water, and healthcare exclusively for these communities.

## 27. Denotified Tribes (DNTs) — India's Forgotten Communities

- Communities that were **criminalised by the British** under the **Criminal Tribes Act, 1871** — branded as "born criminals" simply by birth.
- After Independence, this Act was **repealed in 1952**, and these communities were "denotified" — but **the stigma never left**.

**Core challenges today:**
- Constant police suspicion and harassment.
- High illiteracy, poverty, and lack of land rights.
- Living largely on the margins of both mainstream society and official welfare systems.

**Commissions:**
- **Renke Commission (2008)**: documented the plight of DNTs.
- **Idate Commission (2017)**: further documented — yet systematic policy action remains largely incomplete.

## 28. The Sex Ratio Paradox — More Equal Than Mainstream India

- Tribal sex ratio: approximately **990 females per 1,000 males** (vs. **943** national average).

**Why is the tribal sex ratio more favourable?**

| Reason | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| Less rigid patriarchy | Women participate visibly in economic and social life |
| Economic value of women | Women are central to tribal agriculture and forest gathering; reduces son preference |
| **Bride price instead of dowry** | Groom's family pays the bride's family — direct opposite of dowry; daughter's birth becomes economically advantageous |
| Limited access to sex-selective technology | Remote tribal areas historically had less access to sex-determination technology |

**Social significance**: In this specific dimension, tribal social structures are demonstrably **more equitable** than those of mainstream Hindu society. This challenges the simplistic narrative that associates tribal life with universal "backwardness."

## 29. Tribal Knowledge Systems — Wisdom at the Margins

Tribal knowledge systems are fundamentally different from mainstream scientific and academic knowledge — not in quality or validity, but in their **mode of production, transmission, ownership, and application**. Tribal knowledge is transmitted through **folklore, songs, myths, and ritual**.

### Ecological Wisdom:
- Deep, intergenerational knowledge of local ecosystems — plant species, animal behaviour, seasonal cycles, soil conditions, and water sources.
- **Bishnoi community** of Rajasthan: practiced conservation of trees and wildlife for over five centuries. The **Chipko movement** of the 1970s drew directly on this tradition.
- **Soliga tribes** of Karnataka's Biligiri Rangana (BRT) Hills: documented knowledge of more than **100 medicinal plant species** and their applications.
- **Sacred Groves** (**Deorais** in Odisha, **Orans** in Rajasthan, **Kovils** in Tamil Nadu) — forests protected by tribal communities for religious reasons — have preserved significant biodiversity.

### Traditional Medicine:
- Tribal medicine uses plants, roots, barks, and minerals.
- **Irula tribe** of Tamil Nadu: unique expertise in snakes and their venom; knowledge actively used by antivenom research institutions.
- Many modern pharmaceutical compounds derived from plants first identified by tribal healers.
- This has led to the issue of **bio-piracy** — external corporations patenting tribal knowledge without consent, acknowledgment, or benefit-sharing.

### Sustainable Resource Management:
- Traditional **Jhum** (shifting cultivation) — when practised with adequate fallow periods of **10-15 years** — is ecologically sustainable. Problem arises when population pressure and legal restrictions reduce fallow periods to **2-3 years**.
- Community-managed forests under tribal governance often maintain higher biodiversity than state-managed or commercially leased forests.

### Comparison with Mainstream Knowledge Systems:

| Dimension | Tribal Systems | Mainstream Systems |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Connection to nature | Very strong; lived, local, ecosystem-specific | More abstracted; generalised and decontextualised |
| Knowledge generation | Practice, community consensus, oral validation over generations | Formal research, peer review, empirical verification |
| Transmission | Oral — stories, songs, rituals, apprenticeship | Written — textbooks, institutions, digital archives |
| Scope | Holistic — medicinal, ecological, spiritual interwoven | Specialised — disciplines strictly compartmentalised |
| Ownership | Communal; embedded in community identity | Individual/institutional; governed by patents and IPR |
| Vulnerability | High — erodes with loss of elders or cultural disruption | Better archived; institutionally durable |
| Sustainability orientation | Tailored to local ecosystems; often inherently sustainable | Can prioritise productivity over sustainability |

### Key Challenges:
1. **Risk of Bio-piracy**: Corporate and pharmaceutical interests have repeatedly patented tribal knowledge — from neem to turmeric — without community consent or compensation.
2. **Erosion of Living Knowledge**: Displacement, urbanisation, and declining use of tribal languages are causing irreversible knowledge loss.

### The Way Forward:
- **Legal Protection**: Instruments like the **Nagoya Protocol** under the Convention on Biological Diversity must be enforced to prevent misappropriation and ensure benefit-sharing.
- **Community-led Documentation**: Knowledge must be recorded with communities — through **free, prior, and informed consent** — not extracted from them.
- **Complementarity, not Replacement**: Tribal and modern knowledge systems should reinforce each other.

*"Protecting tribal knowledge is not an act of cultural charity — it is an ecological and civilisational necessity."*

## 30. Models of Integration — The Historic Debate

| Model | Core Idea | Critique |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| **Isolation** | Tribes are "pristine" — protect them from any outside contact | Freezes tribes in time; romanticises poverty; denies communities the right to choose change |
| **Assimilation** | Tribes are "backward Hindus" — absorb them into mainstream society | Destroys cultural identity; forces the dominant culture; erases distinct constitutional rights |
| **Integration with Identity (Nehru-Elwin Model)** | Tribes develop on their own terms, retaining culture and rights while accessing education and development | The constitutional approach — endorsed as the right balance |

### Nehru's Tribal Panchsheel — Five Guiding Principles:

1. Development based on tribal people's **own genius**
2. Respect for **tribal land rights** and forest rights
3. **Language support** — supporting tribal mother tongues
4. Strengthening tribal **administration** through tribal people themselves
5. **Governor's special responsibility** for tribal welfare

### Key Legislations

| Law | Key Provisions | Challenge |
|-----|---------------|-----------|
| **PESA (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas Act), 1996** | Extended Panchayati Raj to tribal Scheduled Areas; gave Gram Sabhas control over natural resources, minor forest produce, land acquisition decisions, regulation of money lending; recognised customary tribal law as legally valid | Weak implementation; several states delayed or incompletely framed required rules; land acquisition and mineral projects proceed without effective Gram Sabha participation |

## 32. Major Problems and Structural Vulnerabilities

### Displacement — The Defining Crisis of Tribal India

- Tribal communities constitute **nearly 40% of all people displaced** by development projects, despite being only 8.6% of the population.

**Why tribes are disproportionately displaced:**
- Tribal lands sit above India's richest deposits — coal, iron ore, bauxite, and uranium across Central and Eastern India.
- **Oral land tenure traditions are unrecognised** by state systems — making acquisition legally easier.
- Forest-based livelihoods directly conflict with both commercial forestry and conservation models.
- Political marginalisation limits capacity to resist through legal or electoral channels.

**Why rehabilitation fails:**
- Cash compensation cannot replace land, community networks, or cultural identity.
- Studies on **Narmada, POSCO, and Vedanta-affected areas** consistently show displaced families worse off — economically and socially — even two decades later.

### Cultural Erosion and Identity Threats:
- **Language extinction**: Rapid mainstreaming is weakening tribal languages. Hundreds of tribal dialects face extinction within a generation.
- **Religious and social divisions**: Missionary activity in tribal areas has been a force of both genuine education and cultural transformation — creating internal community divisions in Jharkhand, Odisha, and Northeast India.
- **Left-wing extremism**: The Maoist/Naxalite movement has exploited genuine tribal grievances about displacement and land alienation to draw tribal youth into armed resistance.
- **Demographic displacement**: Mainstream migration into tribal homelands (especially in Assam and Jharkhand) threatens tribal cultural and political dominance — underpins "Sons of Soil" politics and demands for **Inner Line Permit** protections.
- **Commodification of culture**: Tribal art forms — Warli paintings, Gond art, tribal dance — risk being reduced to tourist products and exotic spectacle.

### Health — A Triple Burden:
- **Malnutrition**: NFHS-5 data shows under-five stunting among ST children is around **40.9%**.
- **Communicable diseases**: Tribal areas carry a disproportionate burden of malaria and elevated tuberculosis risk.
- **Sickle Cell Disease (SCD)**: A genetic blood disorder disproportionately affecting tribal communities across Central India. The **PM-JUGA scheme (2024)** includes SCD screening as the first major policy acknowledgment of this community-specific health burden.

## 33. Contemporary Challenges and Emerging Issues

### Climate Vulnerability:
- Tribal communities inhabit ecologically sensitive regions (forests, hills, river basins, coastal areas) — most severely affected by climate change.
- They are the **least responsible** for carbon emissions causing it and **among the most vulnerable** to its consequences.
- Tribal ecological knowledge offers critical insights for **climate adaptation strategies**.

### Demands for Territorial Autonomy:
- **Bhil Pradesh Demand**: Demand for a separate state carved out of tribal-majority districts across Rajasthan, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, and Maharashtra. The Bhils (India's **second-largest tribal community**) argue that within current state boundaries, their cultural identity, language, and developmental needs consistently take a back seat.

### Inter-Tribal Conflicts:
- **Karbi-Kuki conflict** in Assam and **Meitei-Kuki tensions** in Manipur are clear examples of how overlapping land claims and identity disputes can turn violent.

### Customary Law vs. Uniform Civil Code:
- Tribal customary laws are explicitly recognised and protected by the **Fifth and Sixth Schedules, PESA, and FRA**.
- Many tribal communities view the **UCC as a direct threat** to the constitutional protections of their distinct socio-legal identity.

### Impact of Globalisation on Tribal Society:
- Tribal communities bear globalisation's costs most acutely — through accelerated resource extraction, cultural erosion, and institutional bypass.

**Economic impact**: Global demand for minerals (bauxite, iron ore, coal, uranium) has driven large-scale corporate land acquisition in tribal areas, accelerating displacement from **jal, jangal, zameen** (water, forest, land). The **Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta** case is the defining symbol.

**Cultural impact**: Global cultural homogenisation accelerating erosion of tribal languages, oral traditions, and customs. Tribal art simultaneously threatened and globally commodified: **Warli and Gond paintings** now find markets on global e-commerce platforms.

**Technological impact**: Digital divide is sharpest for tribal communities. **Bio-piracy** requires stronger enforcement of the **Nagoya Protocol**.

**Political impact**: PESA and FRA are routinely bypassed when global capital and state development interests converge on tribal resource areas. Global conservation frameworks — including the **30x30 biodiversity target** (protecting 30% of land and ocean by 2030) — risk displacing tribal communities from forests, repeating the injustice of colonial forest exclusions.

**Positive global dimensions**:
- **UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007)**: normative international support for tribal land, cultural, and self-governance rights — India voted against it initially before endorsing it.
- International civil society attention (as in the Vedanta case) can amplify tribal resistance against powerful corporate-state coalitions.

## 34. Case Studies

### 1. Narmada Bachao Andolan — When Resistance Reshapes Policy
- Led by **Medha Patkar**, the NBA combined legal battles, hunger strikes, and international advocacy to contest displacement caused by the **Sardar Sarovar Dam**.
- Though the dam was completed, the movement directly contributed to the **LARR Act, 2013** — which for the first time mandated Social Impact Assessment and community consent before land acquisition.

### 2. Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta — When FRA Actually Worked
- The **Dongria Kondh** of Odisha's Niyamgiri Hills resisted UK-based **Vedanta Resources**, which sought to mine bauxite from hills sacred to the community.
- In a landmark **2013 Supreme Court judgment**, the Court held that affected Gram Sabhas had the right to decide.
- **All 12 Gram Sabhas unanimously rejected** the mining project.

## 35. The Way Forward — Xaxa Committee and the Rights-Based Approach

The **Virginius Xaxa Committee (2014)** on the social, economic, health, educational, and other development indicators of tribal communities remains the most comprehensive recent policy framework.

**Recommendations:**
- Strengthen last-mile delivery of welfare schemes in remote tribal regions.
- Enhance quality of education and healthcare — including instruction in **tribal mother tongues** to address the language barrier that drives dropout rates.
- Protect tribal land and forest rights rigorously as the foundation of all other development — **displacement prevention is more effective than post-displacement rehabilitation**.
- Design livelihood and skill development programmes that respect tribal knowledge and socio-cultural context.
- Promote inclusive research and institutional support — documenting tribal culture, languages, and traditional knowledge in partnership with communities.
- Ensure genuine community participation in planning and execution of development initiatives.

**Overarching principle**: True tribal development must be grounded in a **rights-based approach** — protecting tribal interests, preserving cultural identity, strengthening community governance, and ensuring tribal communities are active partners in shaping their own developmental trajectories.

*The goal is not to bring development to tribal communities as outsiders conceive it, but to create the conditions under which tribal communities can exercise their own vision of a good life.*

## 39. Exam-Ready Frameworks

### For "Displacement and Rehabilitation as Two Axes of Tribal Development" (2025 PYQ):
**YES, but incomplete:**
- 8.6% of population = ~40% of displaced by development projects.
- Oral land tenure → easy acquisition; mineral-rich areas targeted.
- Rehabilitation fails: cash cannot replace land, community, culture.
- NBA → LARR 2013; Dongria Kondh → Gram Sabha rights.
**BUT beyond displacement:**
- Cultural erosion, health crises (malnutrition, SCD, malaria), education gaps, bio-piracy, climate vulnerability.
- Xaxa Committee: displacement prevention more effective than rehabilitation.
- Rights-based approach: Jal, Jangal, Zameen as foundation.

### For "Tribal Knowledge Systems vs. Mainstream" (2021 PYQ):
- Uniqueness: oral transmission, holistic, communal ownership, ecosystem-specific, intergenerational.
- Examples: Bishnoi conservation, Soliga medicinal knowledge, Irula snake expertise, Sacred Groves.
- Challenges: bio-piracy (neem, turmeric cases), erosion with displacement.
- Way forward: Nagoya Protocol, free prior informed consent, complementarity not replacement.

### For "Why Should Tribes Be Considered a Single Category?" (2022 PYQ):
- **Single category valid for**: constitutional safeguards, shared vulnerability, common historical experience, institutional mechanisms.
- **Must be modified for**: sub-classification for most deprived, language-specific education, urban/rural differences, customary law recognition, differential political needs.
- **Formula**: "Protect together, develop differently."

### For "Affirmative Action Not Fully Benefiting Underprivileged" (2024 PYQ):
- FRA: 36% claims rejected due to strict evidence rules.
- PESA: weak implementation, Gram Sabhas bypassed.
- SC/ST Act: low conviction rates, police reluctance.
- PVTG: 75 communities need targeted attention beyond generic reservations.
- Elite capture within categories (Davinder Singh 2024).
- Way forward: Xaxa Committee recommendations; rights-based approach; last-mile delivery.

### For "Caste Identity — Both Static and Fluid" (2023 PYQ):
- **Static**: endogamy (90-95% marriages within caste), caste-based atrocities (57,582 cases NCRB 2022), untouchability still practised (27% households IHDS 2011-12), matrimonial platform filters (60%+).
- **Fluid**: Sanskritisation, urbanisation loosening ties, post-1991 economic mobility for Dalits/OBCs, inter-caste marriages slowly increasing, caste shifting from ritual to political identity, sub-classification debate.
- **Conclusion**: Caste is simultaneously weakening and reinventing itself — losing traditional functions (occupational heredity, ritual hierarchy) while acquiring new avatars (political solidarity, digital identity, diaspora networks).
