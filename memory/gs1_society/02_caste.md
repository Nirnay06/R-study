### Limitations of Dalit Movements

Despite significant gains, Dalit movements face serious internal and external challenges:

- **Internal Fragmentation:** The debate around sub-classification within SC/ST categories has exposed sub-caste rivalries, where smaller groups compete against more established ones for reservation benefits, weakening the broader unified front.
- **Political Appropriation:** Mainstream political parties have repeatedly co-opted Dalit icons like Ambedkar into broader religious or nationalist narratives, often diluting the movement's radical anti-caste core without addressing structural inequality.
- **The Wealth Gap:** Despite the rise of **Dalit Capitalism** and initiatives like **DICCI**, the majority of the community remains trapped in the informal economy and hazardous sanitation work, largely excluded from the benefits of liberalisation.
- **Resistance and Backlash:** Dalit assertion often faces violent backlash and everyday discrimination from dominant castes, revealing the deep resilience of caste power despite visible protest and activism.
- **Reinforces Caste Consciousness:** Mobilisation as "Dalits" strengthens solidarity but can also keep caste as a primary marker of identity, making politics caste-based even though it is anti-caste in intent.
- **The Saviour Narrative:** While Dalit stories have gained visibility in mainstream cinema, representation behind the camera remains thin. Films often centre an upper-caste "saviour" figure, subtly reinforcing the very hierarchy they claim to challenge.
- **Brahminical Cultural Dominance:** Hinduism and its scriptures remain the governing ideological framework for most Indians. Alternative frameworks like **Neo-Buddhism** (a modern reinterpretation of Buddhism led by B. R. Ambedkar in 1956, focused on equality, social reform, and liberation from the Hindu caste system) have limited mass appeal.

#### Cinema: Saviour Narrative vs. Subversive Anti-Caste Cinema

| Feature | The Upper-Caste "Saviour" Narrative | Subversive Anti-Caste Cinema |
|---|---|---|
| **Example** | Article 15, Aarakshan | Pariyerum Perumal, Sairat, Jai Bhim |

## 4. Key Government Measures Against Caste Inequality

### Constitutional Safeguards

The Constitution provides the strongest legal foundation against caste discrimination:

| Article | Provision |
|---|---|
| **Articles 15 & 16** | Prohibit caste-based discrimination in public life and employment; empower the State to make special provisions for SCs, STs, and backward classes |
| **Article 17** | Abolishes untouchability in all forms |
| **Article 25(2)(b)** | Opens Hindu religious institutions to all castes |
| **Article 46** | Directs the State to protect the educational and economic interests of SCs, STs, and weaker sections |
| **Articles 330 & 332** | Provide political reservations in Lok Sabha and State Legislatures |
| **Article 338** | Establishes the National Commission for Scheduled Castes to safeguard constitutional protections |
| **Article 340** | Empowers the President to appoint a commission to investigate conditions of backward classes |

### Legal Protections

- **Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955** and **SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989** — enforce Article 17 and penalize caste-based violence and discrimination.
- **Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers Act, 2013** — seeks to eradicate manual scavenging and rehabilitate affected workers.

### Welfare Measures

- Targeted **scholarships, hostels, coaching, and fee waivers** for SC/ST/OBC students.
- **Skill development and livelihood programmes** for economic uplift of marginalised communities.
- **Public awareness campaigns** to promote inter-caste harmony and reduce social stigma.

## 5. Persistence of Caste

Despite challenges and contrarian forces, caste has not disappeared. It has **adapted**. In contemporary India, caste operates in at least three visible ways:

- **In marriage** — caste preferences dominate matrimonial choices, even on modern digital platforms.
- **In politics** — caste-based voting blocs, caste arithmetic, and caste-based political parties remain central to Indian electoral democracy.
- **In discrimination** — caste-based atrocities, manual scavenging, and wage discrimination continue to affect millions.

> Understanding caste is not an exercise in studying the past. It is essential to understanding India's present.

**Key Insight:** Caste is simultaneously **weakening and reinventing itself**. Sociologists describe caste today as *fluid in some domains and stubbornly persistent in others*. Both realities coexist — and neither tells the complete story alone.

---

### Forces That Have Sustained Caste

Despite modernization and legal reform, caste remains deeply embedded in Indian life — not always visibly, but consistently.

#### 1. Endogamy
The vast majority of Indians continue to marry within their caste. Matrimonial websites openly offer caste filters; family pressure and social sanctions keep endogamy the most resilient feature of the caste system. This is why **Ambedkar called intermarriage the "real remedy"** — it directly strikes at caste's biological and social reproduction.

#### 2. Caste in the Private Realm
Even where caste has retreated from public life, it quietly persists in private spaces — seating arrangements at weddings, choice of priest, food service during ceremonies, and inheritance practices.

#### 3. Everyday Discrimination — The "Caste Gaze"
Caste is, for many, a lived daily experience:
- People are routinely sized up by their name, dialect, or appearance to determine caste.
- Separate utensils for Dalits remain a reality in many households.
- Many Dalit employees conceal their caste identity at work to avoid discrimination.
- A significant share of Indian households — disproportionately rural — continue to practice untouchability despite it being a punishable offence.

#### 4. The Caste-Wealth Nexus
Economic inequality in India remains sharply **caste-correlated**:
- SCs own around only **9% of India's total agricultural land** and 92% of all land held by SCs is "marginal" (small plots between 0–2 hectares).
- **90% of manual scavengers** belong to Scheduled Castes — a stark example of the hereditary trap of stigmatized labour (Oxfam).
- Dalits are **12.6% of the urban population** but constitute **20.3% of slum residents** (Census 2011).
- SC and ST workers earn on average **15% lower wages** than other workers in both public and private sectors (Oxfam).

#### 5. Caste in Politics — Reinvention, Not Decline
Rather than fading, caste has become more central to electoral democracy:
- Caste has evolved from a social hierarchy into a **political interest group** — elections are frequently contested through caste-based social engineering of vote banks.
- Caste alliances continue to drive electoral outcomes.
- As sociologists have observed, caste has shifted from a **vertical ritual hierarchy** to a **horizontal political solidarity bloc** — resulting in the political reinvention of caste.

#### 6. Caste Associations
Modern organizations formed by members of a caste to promote their collective interests. They keep caste alive by:
- Providing educational, financial, and employment support to members.
- Mobilizing members for political representation, reservations, and other group demands.
- Strengthening caste identity and solidarity through meetings, community events, and social networks.
- Facilitating marriage alliances and social interactions within the caste community.
- Acting as pressure groups to secure greater social, economic, and political benefits.

### Dominant Castes and Reservation Demands

**Dominant Caste** — a caste group that exercises disproportionate power at the regional level, not necessarily through ritual status, but through a combination of **numerical strength, land ownership, and political influence** (M.N. Srinivas's concept). Unlike the traditional hierarchy based on purity-pollution, dominance is rooted in economic and political muscle.

**Examples:** Marathas (Maharashtra), Jats (Haryana, UP, Rajasthan), Patels/Patidars (Gujarat), Yadavs (Bihar, UP), Vokkaligas (Karnataka).

#### Why Dominant Castes Now Demand Reservations

| Cause | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Agrarian distress** | Their economic dominance was land-based; farm income stagnation has eroded this |
| **Unemployment crisis** | Intense competition for government jobs makes General Category status a disadvantage |
| **Relative deprivation** | Affirmative action has enabled OBC/SC upward mobility — dominant castes feel their relative status has declined |
| **Vote-bank politics** | Politicians exploit these numerically large communities with reservation promises |

#### Why This Is Problematic
- Extending reservations to dominant castes dilutes benefits for genuinely marginalised SCs and STs.
- Reservations are a social justice tool for historically oppressed groups — not an employment benefit scheme.
- It reinforces caste identities rather than addressing root causes — agrarian crisis and jobless growth.

#### Case Study — Maratha Reservation
Maharashtra's **SEBC Act (2018)** granting reservation to Marathas was struck down by the Supreme Court in **Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil v. Chief Minister (2021)** as unconstitutional — it exceeded the **50% ceiling** set by the **Indra Sawhney judgment (1992)** without sufficient justification.

---

### Reservation and Religious Conversion — A Recent Legal Development

The Supreme Court has reaffirmed that **SC reservation benefits apply exclusively to Hindus, Sikhs, and Buddhists**, as defined under the **Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order, 1950**. Individuals who convert to Christianity or Islam lose their SC status and reservation benefits, as these religions are considered not to recognise the caste-based discrimination that the reservation policy was designed to address.

#### The Debate

| Against the Judgment | For the Judgment |
|---|---|
| Caste discrimination does not disappear with religious conversion — a Dalit Christian or Dalit Muslim continues to face social stigma, occupational exclusion, and economic deprivation rooted in their caste origin, regardless of faith | Reservation was designed to address caste-based oppression within the Hindu social order — extending it to religions that doctrinally reject caste hierarchy may dilute its original purpose |

**Constitutional tension:** If caste follows a person beyond religion (as sociological evidence suggests), then denying reservation purely on grounds of faith raises concerns around **Articles 14, 15, and 21**. This remains one of the most contested intersections of **religion, caste, and social justice** in contemporary India.

---

### Urban Segregation

Urbanisation was expected to dissolve caste — instead, it has made caste **more subtle and harder to confront**. Housing discrimination has shifted from open untouchability to coded exclusion. **"Pure vegetarian only"** housing societies or separate service entrances for domestic workers often act as proxies for caste-based exclusion, without ever explicitly mentioning caste.

---

### Caste in Higher Education

Elite educational institutions have become an arena for caste-based exclusion.
- Dalit students in IITs and central universities face social isolation, academic discrimination, and higher dropout rates.
- The **language barrier**: Fluency in English is often used as a marker of social acceptability; students from vernacular or rural backgrounds are frequently mocked or sidelined.
- This creates a **form of social hierarchy based on language and cultural capital** — caste discrimination adapted into subtle, modern forms that are harder to identify and challenge.

---

### Caste-Based Violence

Assertions of dignity by lower castes continue to trigger violent backlash:
- **57,582 crimes against Scheduled Castes** were reported in 2022 (NCRB).
- The **Una attack (2016)** — where Dalits were flogged for skinning dead cattle — illustrated how even basic assertions of livelihood can invite organised violence.

## 6. Recent Trends and Issues Related to Caste

Caste in India is not disappearing — it is **transforming**. It no longer functions only as a traditional village hierarchy. In the 21st century, caste has shed much of its ritual character and taken on more **modern, organised, and self-aware forms**.

Today, caste has become a basis of **assertive identity**, not just an inherited status. Communities increasingly use caste as a source of **political assertion**, **social recognition**, and **collective bargaining power**. Instead of hiding it, people often openly express caste identity through surnames, public demands for rights, online groups, and caste-based campaigns — marking a shift from **stigma to strategy**.

Caste now operates across technology, institutions, urban spaces, and even global diaspora networks.

### Caste in Digital Spaces

Technology has not weakened caste — in many ways, it has made caste **more efficient and more visible**.

- **Matrimonial platforms** like Shaadi.com allow users to filter matches by caste, making caste-based endogamy easier than ever. Over **60% of profiles specify caste preferences**, showing that modern tools are being used to preserve traditional boundaries.
- **Social media caste groups** provide community solidarity but also serve as echo chambers that spread casteist content and reinforce in-group bias.
- **Surname SEO** — the moment a celebrity or politician rises to prominence, people instantly search their caste online, reflecting a deep-rooted social curiosity that technology has made effortless and instantaneous.
- On the other side, **digital Dalit assertion** — through movements like **#DalitLivesMatter** and **#JusticeForRohithVemula** — has used the same digital spaces to challenge discrimination, bypassing mainstream media and reaching national and global audiences.

---

### Sub-Classification Within SC/ST — A New Legal Turn

**State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)** — judgment by a 7-judge Constitutional Bench:

- The Supreme Court held that **states can sub-classify within SC/ST categories** to prioritise the most marginalised sub-groups within the reserved categories.
- **Findings:**
  - Reservation benefits have disproportionately gone to relatively better-off SC sub-groups — a phenomenon called **"elite capture."**
  - Intra-group inequality is as real as inter-group inequality.
  - The most deprived among the marginalised need targeted intervention.
- **Political consequence:** Fuelled the rise of **sub-caste politics** — smaller communities within broader categories now mobilise separately to demand their specific share within quotas, marking a shift from broad Dalit politics to **micro-identity politics**.

---

### Globalisation of Caste — The Diaspora Factor

Caste has **travelled beyond India's borders** with the Indian diaspora:
- In global tech hubs, cities like Seattle and states like California have seen intense debates over legally banning caste discrimination.
- High-profile cases (like the **Cisco lawsuit**) have shown that caste-based networks influence hiring and promotions even in global workplaces.
- Caste hierarchies are not a product of Indian soil alone — they are a **social habit that travels with people**.

---

### The Caste Census — Data as a Political Tool

The demand for a **National Caste Census** has become one of the most significant political debates of the mid-2020s:
- Communities are demanding exact population numbers to justify their share in government jobs, welfare schemes, and political seats.
- The slogan **"Jitni Abadi, Utna Haq"** (Rights proportional to population) signals a major shift — from caste as a **ritual identity** to caste as a **numerical and political resource**.
- Caste is increasingly being leveraged not through tradition but through **data, statistics, and democratic arithmetic**.

---

### Cultural Assertion — Reclaiming Caste Identity

- **Anti-Caste Cinema and Music:** A new wave of filmmakers — including **Nagraj Manjule** (*Sairat*) and **Neeraj Ghaywan** (*Masaan*) — along with musicians pioneering "Dalit Pop" and rap, are using their art to challenge stereotypes and tell stories of resistance, moving beyond mere victimhood.
- **Aesthetic Identity:** Youth are using fashion, tattoos, and social media bios to proudly display their caste identity, turning what was once "hidden" into a brand of pride or "swag."

---

### Algorithmic Bias and Digital Harassment

Technology presents a **double-edged challenge** for caste equality:
- **Algorithm bubbles** — social media platforms tend to push users into caste silos by recommending groups based on surnames or regional content, reinforcing caste echo chambers rather than breaking them.
- **Digital harassment of anti-caste activists** — including doxing, coordinated trolling, and AI-generated misinformation — has become a new tool of caste-based intimidation in the digital age.

---

### Environmental Casteism — Who Bears the Burden?

The impact of environmental hazards and urban planning is **not caste-neutral**:
- Historically marginalised communities are disproportionately concentrated near landfills, toxic industrial zones, and flood-prone areas.
- During natural disasters, the **speed and quality of government relief** often vary based on the caste composition of neighbourhoods.
- This emerging dimension — called **environmental casteism** — shows that caste inequality now extends into questions of ecology, climate resilience, and spatial justice.

---

## 7. The Aspiration of a 'Casteless' Society

### What Does 'Casteless' Mean?

A casteless society envisions an **inclusive social order where caste identity becomes irrelevant** — where every individual has equal opportunity regardless of birth. It is not merely the absence of caste labels but the **dismantling of the structural inequalities that caste has historically produced**.

However, the idea remains deeply contested. Sociological thinking suggests that "casteless" may be a myth in the Indian context — because even those who claim to be casteless are often unconsciously shaped by caste in their choices, associations, and attitudes.

### Ambedkar's Prescription

Ambedkar identified a fundamental remedy for dismantling caste — not reform from within, but a structural break from its very foundations:

- **Inter-Caste Marriages:** Ambedkar considered inter-caste marriage the most direct and real remedy against caste — because **endogamy is the biological mechanism** through which caste reproduces itself across generations. Breaking endogamy fosters shared identities, dismantles purity myths, and enables genuine social and cultural integration across caste boundaries.

> A truly casteless society remains a **work in progress**. Constitutional safeguards, reservations, and social movements have created pathways — but structural inequalities rooted in birth continue to persist.

## 9. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Simple Definition |
|---|---|
| **BAMCEF** | Backward and Minority Communities Employees Federation — founded by **Kanshi Ram (1978)** to politically mobilise educated Dalit employees |
| **Caste** | A hereditary, endogamous social group defined by birth, traditional occupation, and ritual status in a purity-pollution hierarchy |
| **Caste Gaze** | The subtle, non-verbal way individuals are sized up based on name, appearance, or dialect to locate their caste |
| **Constitutional Morality** | Values enshrined in the Constitution — equality, dignity, liberty — as opposed to inherited social customs |
| **Dalit** | Term meaning "broken/oppressed" — used to describe those outside the varna system, formerly called untouchables; emphasises resistance and self-respect |
| **Dalit Panthers** | Radical Dalit movement founded in **1972** in Maharashtra, inspired by the American Black Panthers, focused on anti-atrocity and cultural assertion |
| **Dominant Caste** | Srinivas' concept — a caste with large numerical strength, land ownership, and political-economic power at the regional level |
| **Endogamy** | The practice of marrying within one's own caste or sub-group |
| **Harijan** | Term used by Gandhi meaning "children of God" for untouchables — **rejected by Ambedkar as patronising** |
| **Horizontal Solidarity** | The transformation of caste from a vertical ritual hierarchy to a political identity bloc — all members of a caste acting together for collective interests |
| **Jajman** | The upper-caste patron in the Jajmani system who receives services from service castes |
| **Jajmani System** | Traditional socio-economic arrangement of hereditary, inter-caste service exchange in rural India |
| **Jati** | The actual, lived caste community — local, endogamous, occupation-based; the real unit of the caste system |
| **Mandal-Kamandal** | Political strategy combining OBC reservation politics (Mandal Commission) with Hindu identity mobilisation (Kamandal/Ram Mandir) — used by parties to build electoral coalitions |
| **Purity-Pollution** | The ideological basis of caste hierarchy — certain occupations, foods, and people deemed ritually pure or impure, determining social interaction |
| **Sanskritisation** | Process by which lower castes adopt upper-caste rituals, dietary practices, and customs to claim higher social status (M.N. Srinivas) |
| **Social Morality** | Norms upheld by community consensus — often rooted in caste, patriarchy, and religious hierarchy — contrasted with constitutional morality |
| **Sub-classification** | Dividing SC/ST reservation categories into sub-groups to ensure the most deprived within the category receive benefits — upheld by SC in *Davinder Singh* (2024) |
| **Untouchability** | The most extreme form of caste discrimination — treating certain castes as ritually polluting by mere touch or presence; abolished by Article 17 of the Constitution |
| **Varna** | The textual, four-fold division of Hindu society (Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra) — normative ideal, not the operational reality |

## 13. Quick-Revision: Core Analytical Framework

### Three Paradoxes of Caste in Modern India

1. **Technology reinforces caste** (matrimonial filters, surname SEO, algorithm bubbles) while also enabling Dalit assertion (#DalitLivesMatter).
2. **Affirmative action strengthens caste identity** (through reservation politics and caste census demands) while aiming to reduce caste inequality.
3. **Urbanisation was expected to dissolve caste** but has instead made it more subtle and harder to confront (coded housing discrimination, English-barrier hierarchies).

### The Core Tension
- Caste is losing its traditional functions (occupational heredity, public untouchability, rigid ritual hierarchy).
- But it is acquiring new avatars: political solidarity, digital spaces, diaspora networks, environmental distribution of hazards.

### Ambedkar's Insight (exam-ready quote)
> Endogamy is the biological mechanism through which caste reproduces itself across generations. Breaking endogamy — through inter-caste marriage — is the "real remedy."

### Srinivas's Insight
> Caste has shifted from a **vertical ritual hierarchy** to a **horizontal political solidarity bloc** — resulting in the political reinvention of caste.
