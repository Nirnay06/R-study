## 1. Definition of Poverty

### Income/Monetary Definition
- Poverty in its simplest sense = a condition where individuals lack sufficient income or resources to meet basic needs (food, clothing, shelter).
- Conventionally measured in **monetary terms**: a person is poor if income/consumption falls below a minimum level.
- In India: poverty historically estimated via **Monthly Per Capita Consumption Expenditure (MPCE)**.
- **Tendulkar Committee (2009)**: Recommended a poverty line based on expenditure required for basic consumption (food, education, health, clothing, housing, and other essentials).

### Multidimensional Definition
- Poverty is much more than lack of income — it involves **deprivation of opportunities, capabilities, dignity, health, education, and social inclusion**.
- **Amartya Sen's Capabilities Approach**: Poverty = absence of freedom — the inability to lead a dignified human life. A person is poor not just when income is below a threshold, but when they lack the capability to be well-nourished, healthy, educated, and able to participate meaningfully in community life.
- **World Bank**: Defines poverty as "pronounced deprivation in well-being" — deliberately refusing to reduce it to income, recognising that people can be simultaneously income-poor, asset-poor, health-poor, and socially excluded.

### India's Poverty Story (Progress and Paradox)
- India's poverty rate at **$3.65/day** fell from **61.8% (2011–12) to 28.1% (2022–23)** (World Bank, Spring 2025).
- Extreme poverty at **$2.15/day**: Rural declined from 18.4% → 2.8%; Urban from 10.7% → 1.1%.
- Yet nutritional deprivation persists, regional inequalities are deep, and the gap between economic growth and human development remains wide.

## 2. Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)

- Developed by **Sabina Alkire and James Foster** (Oxford), adopted by **UNDP**.
- Captures acute poverty across **3 dimensions** and **10 indicators**:

| Dimension | Indicators |
|-----------|------------|
| Health | Nutrition; Child mortality |
| Education | Years of schooling; School attendance |
| Standard of Living | Cooking fuel; Sanitation; Drinking water; Electricity; Housing; Assets |

- **India's National MPI (NITI Aayog)** adds two additional indicators: **Maternal Health** and **Bank Account**, aligning with national priorities.

### India's MPI Progress
- Multidimensional poverty declined from **29.17% (2013–14) to 11.28% (2022–23)** — a reduction of nearly 18 percentage points.
- **24.8 crore individuals** escaped multidimensional poverty in 9 years.
- Largest absolute declines: **Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Madhya Pradesh** — demonstrating that even historically backward states can achieve rapid progress with focused intervention.

## 3. Causes of Poverty

> Poverty is not random. It is structurally produced and reproduced through interlocking economic, social, political, and environmental mechanisms.

### Economic Causes
- **Low agricultural productivity**: Most of India's poor are rural and dependent on farming. Fragmented landholdings, limited irrigation, outdated technology, and climate risk keep agricultural incomes chronically low. Average farm size declining to **1.08 hectares** — makes productivity investment unviable.
- **Unemployment and underemployment**: Economy fails to generate adequate quality formal employment. India's **jobless growth paradox** — high GDP growth without proportionate formal job creation — is the central economic cause of persistent poverty.
- **The poverty trap**: Low income → low savings → inability to invest in education, health, skills → low productivity → perpetuation of poverty. Self-reinforcing mechanism especially powerful in tribal and remote rural areas.
- **Inflation's asymmetric impact**: Price surges hit the poor disproportionately — they spend the largest share of income on food and basic goods. UNDP estimates that in 2022, **71 million people** were pushed into poverty globally by food and energy price spikes following the Ukraine war.

### Political and Administrative Causes
- **Corruption and leakages** — welfare benefits diverted before reaching intended beneficiaries.
- **Exclusion and inclusion errors** — genuine poor excluded due to documentation failures; ineligible households receive benefits.
- **Weak local governance** — Panchayati Raj Institutions and Urban Local Bodies often lack financial and administrative capacity.
- **Colonial legacy** — systematic extraction, deindustrialisation, and land revenue systems disrupted indigenous economies and impoverished communities across generations.

### Social and Structural Causes
- **Caste-based exclusion**: Dalits and Adivasis face systemic discrimination in labour markets, land ownership, and service access. **94% of manual scavengers are SC/ST** — poverty is not a random condition but a structural product of social hierarchy.
- **Gender inequality**: Women's unequal access to assets, employment, and income concentrates poverty among women — the **feminisation of poverty**.
- **Landlessness**: The primary rural productive asset is denied to Dalits and Adivasis by historical dispossession — trapping communities in agricultural labour dependency.
- **Intergenerational transmission**: Poor nutrition and education in childhood reduce adult productivity — poverty reproduces itself across generations through compromised human capital formation.

### Environmental Causes — The Climate-Poverty Nexus
- The **Global MPI 2025** (UNDP and OPHI) establishes that poverty and climate vulnerability are not separate problems — they are deeply intertwined.
- Floods, droughts, cyclones, and heatwaves disproportionately hit the already poor — who live in flood plains, farm rain-dependent crops, and have the least adaptive capacity.
- **887 million** multidimensionally poor people live in areas exposed to at least one climate hazard, with South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa accounting for the largest shares.
- **Climate-driven migration** increases urban vulnerability — migrants moving to cities due to climate stress often settle in areas like Dharavi or Govandi, where they face poor housing, insecure jobs, and lack of basic services.
- The **Bengaluru water crisis (2024)** illustrates the urban dimension: explosive IT-driven population growth, encroachment of lakes, and groundwater depletion produced acute water shortage — hitting informal workers and slum residents hardest.

## 4. Effects / Consequences of Poverty

The effects of poverty manifest across all dimensions of human life and perpetuate themselves through multiple channels:

- **Health deprivation**: Lack of nutrition, healthcare access, and clean water leads to high child and maternal mortality, stunting, and anaemia.
- **Educational deprivation**: Poor children drop out early, limiting lifetime earning potential and perpetuating intergenerational poverty.
- **Social exclusion**: Poverty combined with caste and gender discrimination creates cumulative disadvantage, limiting social mobility.
- **Low social mobility**: Fewer opportunities and resources make it difficult to improve standard of living — poverty continues across generations.
- **Urban complexity**: Around slums and informal settlements, many lack proper housing, water, sanitation, and secure jobs — poverty in cities is not just about income but also about lack of quality living conditions.
- **Economic stagnation**: Poverty reduces aggregate demand, limits human capital formation, and constrains national productivity.
- **Nutritional poverty (Hidden Hunger)**: Even when income poverty declines, micronutrient deficiencies persist — India's "hunger amidst plenty" paradox.

## 5. Key Dimensions of Poverty in India

### A. Feminisation of Poverty

**Definition**: Women are disproportionately affected by poverty compared to men, both in numbers and intensity. Poverty is increasingly concentrated among women due to structural disadvantages.

**Key Reasons:**
- **Lower income & wage gap**: Women often earn less than men for similar work.
- **Limited access to resources**: Unequal property and inheritance rights; less ownership of land, property, credit.
- **Unpaid care work**: Women spend more time in household and caregiving roles, reducing earning opportunities. This invisible labour sustains households and economies but creates no economic security.
- **Low education & skills**: Limits access to better jobs.
- **Vulnerability in the informal sector**: Many women work in low-paying, insecure jobs.
- **Social norms & discrimination**: Patriarchal structures restrict mobility and decision-making; gender-based violence creates economic dependency.

**Key Stat**: Women contribute only **17% to India's GDP** despite constituting nearly **48% of the population** — a massive loss of productive potential rooted in structural exclusion, not capability deficit.

---

### B. Ruralisation of Poverty

**Definition**: A large share of poor people in India live in rural areas, and poverty is more intense there than in cities.

**Causes:**
- Rural areas often have limited access to quality education, healthcare, roads, and markets — reducing income opportunities.
- A large number of people depend on **agriculture**, which is uncertain due to weather changes and price fluctuations.
- **Unequal land ownership**, especially with land mostly owned by men, further limits economic security for many households, particularly women.

**Impact:**
- Rural poverty affects marginalised groups like tribals and lower castes the most, as they are heavily concentrated in villages.
- Fewer opportunities and resources → low social mobility → poverty continues across generations.
- Urban poverty is becoming more visible and complex — around slums and informal settlements, many people lack proper housing, water, sanitation, and secure jobs.

---

### C. Nutritional Poverty / Hidden Hunger

**Definition**: A condition where people may get enough calories to survive but lack essential micronutrients like iron, protein, vitamins, and minerals. Called "hidden hunger" because it is not always visible like starvation, yet severely affects health and development.

Nutritional deprivation frequently persists even when income poverty declines — India's **"hunger amidst plenty" paradox**.

**Why It Persists in India:**
- **Income constraints**: Poor families prioritise quantity over quality, choosing filling food over nutritious food.
- **Lack of dietary diversity**: Limited access to fruits, vegetables, pulses, and animal protein.
- **Gender inequality**: Women and girls often eat last and least, worsening malnutrition.
- **Poor awareness**: Limited knowledge about balanced diets and nutrition.
- **Public distribution bias**: Food systems focus more on cereals than on diversified nutrition.

**Policy Responses:**
- Public Distribution System (PDS): Ensures access to subsidised food grains.
- Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS): Provides nutrition to children and mothers.
- POSHAN Abhiyaan: Focuses on reducing malnutrition through convergence and awareness.
- Mid-Day Meal Scheme (PM POSHAN): Improves child nutrition and school attendance.

### D. Economic Growth vs. Human Development

India has sustained high GDP growth for decades — yet its **HDI rank stands at 134/191 (HDR 2023)**. The disconnect has structural explanations:

- **Jobless Growth**: Despite rapid GDP growth, employment generation has not kept pace. Much of the growth has occurred in capital-intensive sectors that create relatively few jobs.
- **Inadequate public spending**: India's health expenditure at **~1.8% of GDP** and education at **~2.9% of GDP** remain critically below the needs of a population of 1.4 billion.
- **Structural inequalities** of caste, gender, and region prevent marginalised groups from accessing growth opportunities.
- **Informal sector dominance**: **90% of workers** in informal jobs lack social security, creating a massive "working poor" who grow the economy without benefiting from it.

**Key Scholars:**
- **Mahbub ul Haq** (architect of HDI): Human development is the goal; economic growth is only a means.
- **Amartya Sen**: Poverty is not just lack of income, but lack of capabilities — such as the ability to be educated, healthy, socially included, and politically heard.

---

### E. K-Shaped Economy — India's Central Paradox

**Definition**: The K-shaped development pattern captures India's defining structural inequality — the economy splits into two diverging trajectories post-shock or growth.

- **Upper arm (rising)**: IT, finance, pharmaceuticals, premium services — urban, skilled, English-speaking professionals benefit from globalisation.
- **Lower arm (stagnating or falling)**: Informal workers, small farmers, tribal communities, daily wage labourers — bypassed by or harmed by growth.

This explains how India simultaneously has a **booming stock market** and a **Global Hunger Index rank of 105th (2023)** — growth is concentrated, not distributed.

**Oxfam Report (2024)**: India's richest **1% own 40.5% of national wealth**; the top 10% hold 57% of national income; the bottom 50% share is down to 13%.

---

### F. Resource Curse in Tribal Areas

India's tribal heartland — **Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha** — is extraordinarily rich in minerals, forests, and water, yet these are among India's most poverty-stricken regions.

The resource curse operates as follows:
- Mineral wealth generates profits for corporations and state revenues.
- While tribal communities face **displacement, environmental degradation, livelihood loss, and persistent poverty**.
- The gap between mineral revenue and tribal welfare is one of India's most serious social justice failures.

---

### G. Poverty vs. Environmental Protection

**The Conflict:**
1. **Short-Term Needs vs. Long-Term Sustainability**: Poverty reduction focuses on immediate improvements; environmental protection emphasizes long-term conservation. This creates pressure to prioritise present needs over future sustainability.
2. **Economy-Environment Trade-off**: Poverty alleviation is frequently associated with rapid industrialization, mining, infrastructure, and resource extraction. Strict environmental regulations may slow such activities.
3. **Restrictions on Livelihoods**: Many poor and tribal communities depend directly on forests, rivers, and other natural resources. Conservation measures may restrict jhum cultivation, hunting, grazing, or collection of forest produce.
4. **Conservation-Induced Displacement**: Creation of protected areas and wildlife reserves may require relocation of local communities — leading to livelihood loss and social insecurity.

**Harmonizing the Two Goals:**
1. **Environmental Protection Supports the Poor**: The poor depend most directly on natural resources for survival. Environmental degradation affects them disproportionately and can deepen poverty.
2. **Environmental Justice for Marginalized Communities**: Environmental safeguards can protect local populations from indiscriminate displacement and resource loss caused by large infrastructure projects.
3. **Creation of Green Jobs**: Sectors such as renewable energy, afforestation, sustainable agriculture, and eco-tourism can create employment while protecting the environment.

> "We must do two difficult things at once: lift hundreds of millions out of poverty and protect the environment." — The sustainable development approach seeks to combine social justice, poverty reduction, and environmental sustainability.

## 7. Critical Assessment — Why Poverty Persists Despite Programmes

Despite decades of schemes and significant budget allocations, poverty persists in structural pockets. The reasons are analytical, not incidental:

- **Implementation gaps**: Benefits fail to reach beneficiaries due to middlemen, corruption, documentation barriers, and exclusion errors. The gap between scheme design and ground reality is India's most persistent governance challenge.
- **Schemes address symptoms, not causes**: Without tackling land inequality, caste discrimination, gender exclusion, and educational quality, welfare transfers create temporary relief without structural transformation. Dependency is created without empowerment.
- **Skill-market mismatch**: Despite multiple skilling programmes, only **~45% of PMKVY-trained youth** are assessed as industry-ready (industry feedback). Training does not align with real market demands — producing certificates, not capabilities.
- **Jobless growth paradox**: India's GDP grows in capital-intensive sectors while labour-intensive manufacturing remains underdeveloped. Quality jobs at scale — the actual mechanism of mass poverty reduction — are not being created proportionately.
- **Nutrition-growth disconnect**: India ranks **105th on the Global Hunger Index (2023)** despite being one of the world's largest food producers — indicating that food security failures are primarily distributional, not productive. Economic growth has not translated to nutritional security.
- **Algorithmic exclusion**: The shift to digital welfare has introduced new exclusion mechanisms — biometric failures, connectivity gaps, and documentation barriers that disproportionately affect the most vulnerable.

## 8. Universal Basic Income (UBI) Debate

**Definition**: UBI is a proposed policy measure that will provide every citizen a regular, unconditional cash transfer regardless of income, employment, or social status. Its core promise: ensure that no one falls below a minimum standard of living.

### Case For UBI
- **Income security**: Provides a guaranteed financial floor, protecting individuals from unemployment, illness, and economic shocks.
- **Empowerment**: Gives people the freedom to pursue education, entrepreneurship, or leave exploitative employment.
- **Evidence**: SEWA Pilot Project (Madhya Pradesh, 2011–13) shows improvements in nutrition, school attendance, health outcomes, and local economic activity.
- **Simplification of welfare**: Replaces multiple schemes, reducing administrative costs, leakages, and exclusion errors; strengthening direct benefit transfer.

### Case Against UBI
- **Fiscal sustainability concerns**: Providing a meaningful income to all citizens requires significant public expenditure, which could instead support targeted programmes.
- **Inflationary pressure**: Especially in local markets.
- **Work disincentive**: Although evidence from the **Finland UBI experiment** shows limited impact on employment behaviour while improving mental health.
- **One-size-fits-all problem**: May not suit India's regional variation in living costs and poverty levels, making uniform transfers inadequate in some areas.

### Conclusion
UBI is a promising idea but not a standalone solution. A more practical path for India may lie in **quasi-UBI approaches** — narrowing eligibility to the poorest households while gradually expanding coverage. The **Economic Survey 2016-17**, which formally introduced UBI into India's policy conversation, suggested exactly this. Combined with investments in health, education, and infrastructure, targeted basic income can be a powerful component of a larger anti-poverty strategy.

## 9. Role of Different Stakeholders in Poverty Alleviation

The most productive collaboration model is **community-centred, state-enabled, and market-supported**:

| Stakeholder | Role |
|-------------|------|
| **Communities** | Rights-holders, not beneficiaries — participatory planning, social audits |
| **State** | Providing enabling frameworks, rule of law, and universal services |
| **Civil Society / NGOs** | Filling accountability and last-mile delivery gaps |
| **Private Sector** | Channelled through incentive structures (CSR, PPP) toward social priorities |

## 10. Way Forward on Poverty

- **Liberate from deprivation, not just income** — Sen's Capabilities Approach demands investment in education, health, and social participation, not just cash transfers.
- **Quality formal employment creation** — labour-intensive manufacturing, green jobs, and digital economy pathways that reach the poor.
- **Universal, portable social protection** — extend formal social security to informal workers, gig workers, migrants, and the self-employed.
- **Address structural inequalities directly** — land reform, caste discrimination legislation enforcement, women's property rights.
- **Nutrition as national emergency** — 57% anaemia prevalence among women and 35% child stunting are not statistics; they are the cognitive and productive future of India's next generation.
- **Climate-poverty integration** — participatory planning, community land rights, and green livelihoods must be mainstreamed, not treated as add-ons.
- **Fix welfare delivery** — real-time MPI tracking, social audits, and Aspirational Districts model to close implementation gaps.
- **Universal Basic Income debate** — merits consideration as supplemental income floor while recognising its fiscal challenges and the need for complementary structural interventions.

## 13. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Absolute Poverty** | Condition where income falls below a minimum threshold needed to meet basic needs — food, shelter, clothing |
| **Algorithmic Welfare Exclusion** | When digital welfare systems exclude genuine beneficiaries due to biometric failures, data errors, or poor connectivity |
| **Aspirational Districts Programme** | Government initiative targeting India's 112 most backward districts through convergence, collaboration, and competition on 49 KPIs |
| **Capabilities Approach** | Amartya Sen's framework: poverty = deprivation of the capability to achieve basic human functionings — not just low income |
| **Dependency Ratio** | Ratio of dependents (under 15 and over 64) to working-age population (15–64); lower ratio = stronger demographic dividend |
| **Exclusion Error** | Genuinely poor households failing to receive welfare benefits they are entitled to — due to documentation or targeting failures |
| **Feminisation of Poverty** | Disproportionate concentration of poverty among women due to structural gender inequalities in work, property, and care |
| **Gig Economy** | Work based on short-term, platform-mediated contracts — income flexibility without employment security or social protection |
| **Hidden Hunger** | Nutritional deficiency (micronutrients, anaemia, stunting) that persists even when caloric intake appears adequate |
| **Inclusion Error** | Ineligible households receiving welfare benefits that should go to the genuinely poor |
| **Intergenerational Poverty** | Self-reproducing cycle: poor childhood outcomes reduce adult productivity, transmitting poverty across generations |
| **K-Shaped Growth** | Economic pattern where skilled, urban, formal sectors rise while informal, rural, agricultural sectors stagnate or decline |
| **Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)** | Composite measure of poverty across 3 dimensions (health, education, living standards) and 10 indicators — captures non-income deprivation |
| **New Precariat** | Emerging class of workers in insecure, informal, or gig employment — lacking both welfare protection and genuine economic independence |
| **One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC)** | Policy enabling migrant workers to access subsidised food anywhere in India — not just their native district |
| **Poverty Trap** | Self-reinforcing mechanism: low income → low savings and investment → low productivity → continued poverty |
| **Resource Curse** | Paradox where regions rich in natural resources (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha) remain among the poorest due to corporate extraction without community benefit |
| **Ruralisation of Poverty** | The phenomenon that a large share of poor people live in rural areas and poverty is more intense there than in cities |
| **Social Capital** | The economic and social benefits derived from networks of trust, reciprocity, and relationships — enables opportunity access and collective action |
| **Universal Basic Income (UBI)** | Unconditional regular cash transfer to all citizens regardless of income or employment — debated as a floor for poverty elimination |
| **Vicious Circle of Poverty** | Self-reinforcing pattern: poverty → high fertility → more poverty; broken by education, healthcare, and women's empowerment |
