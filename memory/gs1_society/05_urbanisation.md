## 1. What is Urbanisation?

> *(The formal definition and types section precedes page 61 in the original PDF and is not in the extracted text. Key contextual references follow.)*

**Core Concept:**
- Urbanisation = the process by which more people move from villages to cities, changing how they live, work, and relate to each other
- **Urbanism** = the distinct way of life in cities — marked by anonymity, individualism, and weak community ties; about *culture*, not just numbers

**Key Distinction:**

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Urbanisation | Demographic/spatial process — population shift from rural to urban |
| Urbanism | Sociological concept — the *experience* and *culture* of city life |

**Urban Classification in India (Census definition, since 1961):**
- Based on: population size, density, and percentage of male workforce in non-agricultural activities
- This definition is considered **outdated** — many fast-growing town-like areas still classified as rural
- **Census Town:** A settlement meeting urban criteria (population, density, non-farm work) but with no formal civic body — governed as a village

**Types of Urban Settlements:**
- **Statutory Town:** Has a formal urban local body (municipal corporation, town council)
- **Census Town:** Meets urban criteria statistically but lacks civic body
- **Peri-Urban:** Fringe areas around cities — neither fully urban nor fully rural; urban in lifestyle but rural in governance
- **Conurbation:** Large urban region formed when multiple cities grow outward and merge — e.g., Delhi NCR

## 2. Key Drivers of Urbanisation in India

> *(This section precedes page 61 in the original PDF. Key drivers are referenced contextually throughout the text.)*

**Pull Factors (cities attract people):**
- Employment opportunities in formal and informal sectors (IT, manufacturing, services)
- Better education, healthcare, and social infrastructure
- Higher wages compared to agriculture
- Aspirations for a modern lifestyle

**Push Factors (villages push people out):**
- Agricultural distress and low farm incomes
- Lack of non-farm employment in rural areas
- Poor rural infrastructure and services
- Land fragmentation and family pressure

**Structural Drivers (referenced in text):**
- Growth of IT and service sector hubs (Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Pune, NCR)
- Industrial corridors and SEZs attracting labour
- Improved transport and digital connectivity (UDAN, broadband)
- Reclassification of Census Towns and peri-urban growth

## 3. Uniqueness of Indian Urbanisation

### 1. Urbanisation Without Urbanism
- Urban characteristics — technology, consumption, aspirations — are spreading widely
- But a majority still reside in rural areas and most cities lack adequate infrastructure and governance
- Only about **35% live in urban areas** (Census 2011), much lower than the global average of ~56%
- India is urbanising demographically but not fully in terms of culture, infrastructure, or civic governance

### 2. Informality of the Urban Economy
- Unlike Western urbanisation (industrial and formal-sector led), India's urban economy thrives on **informal labour**: street vending, domestic work, construction, casual labour
- In Delhi, **over 75% of employment is in the informal sector**
- Creates a **"dual economy"** — modern and traditional sectors coexist in the same city

### 3. Over-Urbanisation Without Industrialisation
- Cities grow demographically **without sufficient economic absorption capacity**
- Leads to: slums, congestion, and urban unemployment
- Mumbai has high GDP contribution but faces critical slum issues like **Dharavi** — housing over **1 million people in just 2.4 sq. km.**
- Unlike the Western model where industrialisation preceded and drove urbanisation, India's cities absorb more people than their economies can productively employ

### 4. Urban Primacy and Regional Imbalance
- Urbanisation concentrated in a few big cities: Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru
- These cities get most jobs and investment; smaller towns and cities are often ignored
- **Primate City Syndrome**: Largest city (e.g., Delhi/Mumbai) so dominant it dwarfs all others in population, economy, and political power
- Creates skewed regional development — widening spatial inequality

## 4. Impact of Urbanisation on Indian Society

> "Urbanisation is reshaping India in ways that go far beyond skyscrapers and metro lines."

### 1. Economic Impact

**Urban areas as growth engines:**
- Urban areas contribute approximately **65% of India's GDP** despite occupying only about **3% of the land**
- The 12th Five Year Plan called cities "engines of economic growth"
- Economic Survey 2025–26: by **2036**, urban areas will contribute almost **70% of India's GDP**
- Attract FDI, investment, skilled labour, and drive innovation

**But prosperity comes with sharp inequality:**
- Formal sector creates well-paid jobs in IT, banking, corporate services
- Informal sector absorbs the majority of migrants — street vendors, construction workers, domestic helpers, gig workers
- Around **90% of India's workforce is informal** — no social security, no fixed wages, no job protection
- Schemes like **PM SVANidhi** (for street vendors) and **e-Shram portal** (registered over **30 crore** informal workers) are early steps toward inclusion

### 2. Impact on Family and Kinship

**Biggest shift:** Joint families → Nuclear families
- Small urban flats, expensive housing, and jobs pulling people far from hometowns make extended family living difficult
- Increasing share of urban households are nuclear, much higher than in rural areas

**Cities offer real benefits but also bring real costs:**

| Benefits | Costs |
|----------|-------|
| Better access to schools, hospitals, modern parenting | Intergenerational bonding weakens; elderly parents live alone |
| Family planning awareness significantly higher | Work-life imbalance; long commutes cut family time |
| Dual-income households strengthen women's role | Traditional rituals and shared meals slowly fade |

### 3. Impact on Women

**A doorway to freedom and a zone of new dangers:**

**Positive Side:**
- Cities open up education, jobs, and independence
- Female workforce participation in urban India (though still low) is rising in IT, healthcare, education, retail
- Women in cities marry later, make their own reproductive choices
- Stronger voices in governance and activism
- Movements like **#MeToo** and rise of women-led startups began largely in urban spaces

**Negative Side:**
- **NCRB 2022:** over **4.45 lakh crimes against women** in India; metros like Delhi consistently top the list
- Working women carry a **double burden** — full-time jobs plus most housework and childcare
- **Gender pay gap:** women earn around **20-30% less** than men for similar work (ILO estimates)
- The **glass ceiling** still blocks senior leadership roles
- Lack of childcare and safe public transport adds to strain

### 4. Impact on Caste and Social Identity

**Cities have softened caste, but not erased it:**
- Urban anonymity and occupational diversity loosen the rigid grip of caste
- IT engineers, doctors, entrepreneurs come from every background
- **Inter-caste marriages**, though still a minority, are more common in cities than in villages

**But caste has changed shape, not disappeared:**
- Discrimination in **rental housing** — landlords often refuse Dalit or Muslim tenants
- **Caste networks** still shape hiring, especially in informal sectors
- Caste-based residential clusters visible in Mumbai, Ahmedabad, Bengaluru
- **Vote-bank politics** keeps caste alive in the public sphere

**Religious Segregation:**
- Around **26% of Muslims in Indian cities** live in Muslim-concentrated neighbourhoods
- Often driven by fear and exclusion, not voluntary choice
- Example: **Mumbra near Mumbai** grew rapidly after 1992-93 riots as Muslim families relocated seeking safety
- Involuntary clustering weakens integrative potential of cities and deepens social divides

### 5. Impact on Children

**Opportunities:**
- Access to better schools, libraries, healthcare, digital tools, extracurricular activities
- **Urban literacy (84.1%)** significantly higher than rural literacy (67.8%) — Census 2011
- Career awareness and social mobility expand sharply for urban middle class

**Risks:**
- India has an estimated **10.1 million child labourers** (Census 2011); many in urban informal sectors like dhabas, garages, construction
- **Slum dwellers** (~6.5 crore people, or 17% of urban India) live with poor sanitation, disease, overcrowding
- Even well-off urban children battle screen addiction, academic pressure, shrinking play spaces

### 6. Impact on Rural Areas

**Urbanisation transforms villages too:**

**Positive Spillovers:**
- **Migrant remittances** boost rural incomes and fund education, healthcare, home construction
- Better roads, mobile networks, markets bring villages closer to cities
- Urban exposure spreads modern values, education, and aspirations

**Negative Effects:**
- **Brain drain** — strips villages of educated youth, leaving behind the elderly and very young
- Traditional agricultural knowledge erodes
- **Family fragmentation** — fathers, brothers, sons leave for city, often for years
- New rural inequality emerges — between families with migrant earners and those without

### 7. Coexistence of Tradition and Modernity

- Indian cities carry their past with them: IT campuses and centuries-old temples, global malls and traditional bazaars coexist
- India's urbanism is modern — but on its own terms, shaped by local culture and history
- **"Palimpsest" quality** — old and new layered together — makes Indian cities distinctly Indian

### 8. Impact on Culture and Lifestyle

- Cities create a **new cultural mix** — acculturation shapes migrants (new languages, fashions, routines)
- Anonymity and impersonal relations replace village familiarity — freeing some, isolating others
- A new **urban middle class** has emerged, especially in Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities (Indore, Coimbatore, Lucknow, Visakhapatnam)
- Rising incomes power demand for cars, branded goods, online shopping, eating out, entertainment
- India's **e-commerce market** now valued at over **USD 100 billion**, driven largely by urban consumption boom

**But the same lifestyle brings troubles:**
- Stress, loneliness, and lifestyle diseases on the rise
- **NIMHANS estimates** nearly **14% of Indians** need active mental health interventions; urban rates higher than rural
- Pollution, traffic, waste add heavy environmental costs

### Urbanism — The Social Experience of City Life

Sociologists argued that large, dense, and diverse cities produce a distinct social psychology:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Anonymity** | Most urban interactions are functional and impersonal — you may not know your neighbour's name |
| **Individualism** | Personal achievement replaces community and collective values |
| **Heterogeneity** | People of different castes, religions, backgrounds live side by side — creating both energy and tension |
| **New associations** | Resident welfare groups, trade unions, online communities replace kin-based bonds of village life |

**City life — Opportunity vs. Burden:**

| Opportunity | Burden |
|-------------|--------|
| Freedom from caste and gender labels | Psychological stress and loneliness |
| Greater social and economic mobility | Anomie — normlessness and disorientation |
| Exposure to diverse ideas and cultures | Weakening of traditional support networks |
| Better education and healthcare access | High cost of living and housing unaffordability |

**The Loneliness Epidemic:**
- A 2025 study in *Discover Mental Health* (Springer Nature): loneliness and social isolation have reached epidemic levels in Indian cities — especially among the **urban elderly and youth**
- Depression among urban elderly estimated at **10-20%**
- The city offers freedom — but often at the cost of belonging

## 5. Issues and Challenges of Urbanisation in India

### 1. Housing Crisis and Growth of Slums

- India faces an urban housing shortage of about **10 million units** (MoHUA, 2022)
- Shortage hits the poor hardest: high land prices, unaffordable formal housing, outdated rent control laws, slow approval processes
- Poor pushed into slums and informal settlements

**Key Facts:**
- **65 million slum dwellers** in India (Census 2011); UN-Habitat estimates **104 million**
- **Mumbai's Dharavi** houses about **1 million people** in just 2.4 sq km — Asia's largest slum
- Many slums are **non-notified** — don't appear on official records and miss welfare schemes
- Higher **tuberculosis rates** in slums compared to non-slum areas
- Slum residents face insecure land tenure, fear of eviction, poor sanitation, disease exposure

### 2. Segregation and Marginalization in Urban India

**Forms of Urban Segregation:**
- **Ghettos:** Marginalized communities pushed into specific localities due to social/economic pressures
- **Enclaves:** People choose to live together to preserve culture — still creates separation
- **Gated communities:** Elite isolation — richer groups in secure, exclusive spaces — rising inequality

**Drivers of Segregation:**
- Housing discrimination based on caste, religion, gender
- Economic inequality creating class-based neighbourhoods
- Social preferences and fear encouraging in-group clustering
- Weak urban planning and housing policies deepening divides

**Impact:**
- Poor access to schools, healthcare, water, sanitation, transport in segregated areas
- Physical separation limits job opportunities and upward mobility
- Social distance, prejudice, weakened community bonds
- Marginalized groups remain underrepresented in urban governance

### 3. Urban Infrastructure and Environmental Stress

#### Strained Basic Services

| Service | Status |
|---------|--------|
| Piped water coverage | Only **58%** of urban households |
| Safe tap water | Barely **6%** receive safe tap water |
| Sewage treatment | Covers only about **30%** of urban waste |
| Solid waste | Over **60% of 1.5 lakh tonnes** generated daily remains untreated |
| Power cuts, garbage collection, drainage | Routine failures across Tier-1 and Tier-2 cities |

- These gaps hit the poor, women, and children hardest — women spend hours fetching water; children fall sick from poor sanitation

#### Shrinking Urban Commons
- Urban commons = parks, lakes, playgrounds, wetlands, footpaths, public squares
- Support recreation, ecology, community life, and livelihoods for street vendors and fisherfolk
- **Disappearing fast** across Indian cities

**Key concerns:**
- Encroachment by real estate and infrastructure projects
- Pollution and neglect — Bengaluru's **Bellandur and Varthur lakes** choked with foam and sewage
- Privatisation and exclusion — gated communities, gentrification limit poor people's access

**Citizen movements:** Save Aarey (Mumbai) shows rising public concern. Protecting urban commons is essential for inclusive, healthy, and ecologically resilient cities.

#### Traffic Congestion and Poor Mobility
- Mumbai commuters lose over **121 hours a year** to congestion
- Bengaluru consistently ranked among the **world's most congested cities**
- **NITI Aayog estimates 6% GDP loss every year** due to traffic delays alone
- Cities lack last-mile connectivity, safe pedestrian paths, cycling lanes
- Low-income workers on periphery face long, expensive, unsafe commutes

**Why Efficient Urban Mass Transport Matters:**
- Reduces congestion, travel time, transportation costs → improves productivity
- Enhances access to jobs, education, healthcare, markets for lower-income groups
- Reduces fuel consumption, pollution, environmental costs
- Well-connected cities attract investment and strengthen economic role

#### Air and Water Pollution — The Silent Killer
- **WHO:** **14 of the world's 20 most polluted cities** are in India
- Air pollution causes **over 1.6 million deaths annually**
- In Delhi, **PM2.5 levels** often run **10 times above WHO safe limits**; main culprits: vehicles, industries, construction dust, waste burning
- Rivers like **Yamuna and Ganga** choked with untreated sewage and industrial effluents
- Groundwater near industrial zones increasingly contaminated
- Urban poor bear the heaviest health burden — live closest to industrial areas and waste dumps

#### Urban Flooding

**An annual reality:** Mumbai, Chennai, Hyderabad, Gurugram, Bengaluru flood almost every monsoon
- Major cities suffer combined losses of around **₹5,000-6,000 crore every year**

**Causes:**
- Unplanned urbanisation: encroachment of floodplains, wetlands, lakes, natural drainage channels
- Excessive concretisation: prevents rainwater infiltration, increases surface runoff
- Inadequate drainage infrastructure: stormwater drains insufficient, poorly maintained, or clogged
- Climate change and extreme rainfall: intense rainfall events overwhelm urban infrastructure
- Loss of urban water bodies: lakes, ponds, wetlands encroached upon or degraded

**Consequences:**
- Disruption of transport, economic activities, essential services
- Damage to homes, infrastructure, public property
- Increased health risks from water-borne diseases
- Loss of livelihoods, especially for urban poor in vulnerable areas

**Remedial Measures:**
- Strengthen drainage systems: upgrade stormwater drains, ensure regular desilting and maintenance
- Promote rainwater harvesting and permeable surfaces: increase groundwater recharge, reduce runoff
- Restore urban water bodies: rejuvenate lakes, wetlands, drainage channels
- Climate-resilient infrastructure: incorporate flood-risk assessment into urban planning and Smart City projects
- Early warning and disaster preparedness: improve forecasting, monitoring, emergency response

> Historical reminders: **2005 Mumbai floods** and **2015 Chennai floods** revealed the fragility of Indian cities.

#### Groundwater Depletion
- Indian cities drawing water faster than nature can replace it
- Concrete surfaces block rainwater from seeping into ground
- Unchecked borewells lower water tables every year
- **Chennai water crisis 2019** — a major metro effectively ran dry — a warning sign

**Causes:**
- Rising population and increasing domestic, industrial, commercial water demand
- Over-extraction through borewells and tube wells
- Concretisation reducing natural groundwater recharge
- Encroachment and degradation of lakes, ponds, wetlands

**Consequences:**
- Declining water availability and water insecurity
- Increased cost of water extraction
- Land subsidence and ecological degradation
- Greater dependence on distant water sources

**Solutions — Water Harvesting:**
- Mandatory rooftop rainwater harvesting in residential, commercial, institutional buildings
- Recharge wells and percolation pits to channel rainwater into underground aquifers
- Revival of urban lakes, ponds, and wetlands to enhance natural groundwater recharge
- Permeable pavements and green spaces to increase water infiltration and reduce runoff
- Integration of water harvesting with urban planning and building regulations
- Public awareness and community participation for maintenance and implementation

### 4. Health Challenges in Urbanisation

**Urban India faces a dual health burden:** old problems of poverty + new diseases of modern lifestyles

| Health Challenge | Details |
|-----------------|---------|
| **Communicable diseases** | Overcrowding, poor sanitation, contaminated water make slums hotspots for TB, dengue, cholera, diarrhoea; TB prevalence significantly higher in Dharavi |
| **Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)** | Sedentary work, processed food, constant stress drive diabetes, hypertension, heart disease, obesity; NCDs account for over **60% of deaths in India** |
| **Air pollution** | Over **1.6 million deaths annually** (Lancet 2020); Delhi's PM2.5 often 10x above WHO limits |
| **Water pollution** | Sewage and industrial waste spread disease |
| **Noise and heat island effects** | Worsen daily stress |
| **Mental health** | Loneliness, competition, fast-paced life fuelling anxiety and depression; **NIMHANS: 14% of Indians need active mental health support**; urban rates higher |
| **Unequal access** | Urban poor often cannot afford quality care; women and children in slums face greatest neglect |

### 5. Vulnerabilities of Urban Migrants in India

- Migrants form the backbone of urban India — build homes, drive cabs, deliver food, clean offices
- Yet remain **invisible in policy and unprotected in practice**
- **Census 2011:** recorded **45.36 crore internal migrants**

**Key Vulnerabilities:**
- **Economic precariousness:** Most work in informal sector with low wages, no job security, no social protection (~90% of India's workforce is informal)
- **Housing insecurity:** Pushed into slums or overcrowded tenements — often without water, sanitation, or legal tenure
- **Exclusion from welfare:** Lack of local IDs blocks access to PDS, banking, and government schemes
- **Political voicelessness:** Many cannot vote in their city of work, weakening say in governance
- **Social and cultural alienation:** Language barriers, discrimination, isolation create stress and rootlessness

> The **2020 COVID-19 lockdown** — when millions walked home on foot — brutally exposed this neglect. Making migrants visible in urban planning is essential for inclusive cities.

**High Cost of Living — The "Livability Paradox":**
- Cities offer jobs but make daily life a struggle
- Housing costs — soaring rents and property prices push poor and middle class to distant peripheries
- Transport, healthcare, education are expensive and often privatised
- Stagnant wages in informal/low-skilled jobs fail to match rising costs
- Result: reduced purchasing power, longer commutes, compromised quality of life — especially for migrants, women, fixed-income workers

### 6. The Problem of Hidden Urbanisation

- Many areas in India **function like cities but are still officially counted as villages**
- **Census 2011:** only 31.2% of India classified as urban
- **Economic Survey 2025-26:** India is much more urban in reality than official data shows
- **Satellite data (GHSL)** estimated urban population at approximately **63% in 2015** vs. official ~31%

**Why Does This Happen?**
1. **Definition Issue:** India uses an outdated and strict definition of "urban" (since 1961) based on population, male workforce, and density; fast-growing town-like areas still classified as rural
2. **The Peri-Urban Challenge:** Peri-urban areas around cities are functionally urban but officially rural; they follow an urban lifestyle but receive rural-level services

**Consequences:**
- Lack of access to urban schemes and infrastructure (water, roads, sanitation)
- Mismatch between needs and governance
- Weak local bodies unable to handle urban pressures
- Rise of **Subaltern Urbanism** — urbanisation happening informally, led by the poor, without proper recognition

### 7. Weak Decentralisation

- Despite the **74th Constitutional Amendment (1992)**, urban local bodies (municipal corporations) are still **weak in terms of money, powers, and administration**
- Most important decisions still taken by state governments and separate government agencies

**Example — Bengaluru's "Institutional Jungle":**
- **BBMP** (city administration), **BDA** (planning), **BWSSB** (water supply) handle different services
- Divided responsibilities → difficult coordination → delays and inefficiency
- Too many agencies create confusion instead of effective governance

## 6. Recent Trends in Urbanism in India

India's urbanism is shifting in new and visible ways. Four key trends stand out.

### 1. Growth of IT Hubs: Impact and Challenges

**Major IT/service hubs:** Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Chennai, Pune, NCR — large-scale jobs in software development, BPO, R&D

**Opportunities Created by IT Hubs:**
- **Employment Generation:** Large-scale direct and indirect employment
- **Economic Growth:** Significant contribution to exports, GDP, tax revenues
- **FDI and Investment:** Attract domestic and foreign investment due to skilled manpower
- **Innovation Ecosystem:** Encourage startups, entrepreneurship, technological innovation
- Rise of **gig economy** — cab drivers, delivery agents (flexible work, often without job security)
- Even **Tier-2 cities** now emerging as new job centres

**Problems and Challenges:**

| Challenge | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Infrastructure Pressure | Rapid growth leads to traffic congestion, water shortages, overloaded public services |
| High Cost of Living | Rising house prices and rents push low-income workers to distant areas |
| Job Inequality | High-paying IT jobs benefit only a small group; many others in low-paid or insecure gig jobs |
| Social Inequality | Modern IT areas often exist alongside slums and poor living conditions for migrant workers |
| Environmental Issues | Urban expansion causes loss of green spaces, pollution, water body depletion |

### 2. The Rise of Tier-2 and Tier-3 Cities

**Why people are moving out of metros:**
- High cost of living — property prices in **13 major cities rose by 18.8% in 2023**
- Severe pollution — Delhi ranked **4th most polluted city globally** (IQAir 2022)
- Overcrowding, traffic, and intense competition

**Why Tier-2/3 cities are attractive:**
- Better quality of life at lower cost
- About **50% of India's recognised startups** are now based in Tier-2/3 cities
- Improved air connectivity under the **UDAN scheme**
- Expanding digital infrastructure — e-commerce, fintech, ed-tech reaching smaller cities
- Strong cultural and community identity

**Key examples:** Indore, Surat, Coimbatore, Lucknow have become major growth poles

- **Post-COVID reverse migration (2020-21):** Many professionals who went home during lockdowns chose to stay back, seeding local enterprise
- **NITI Aayog Gig Economy Report (2022):** These cities are the next hubs for gig work — Zomato, Swiggy, Blinkit report fastest order growth in non-metros since 2022

### 3. Urban Sprawl

**Definition:** Unplanned, low-density spread of cities into surrounding farmland and villages; happens when inner-city land is too costly, public transport is weak, and planning rules are missing

**Visible examples:**
- **Whitefield (Bengaluru)** and **Gurugram** grew rapidly as IT and residential hubs but with poor roads and water supply
- **Delhi's edges** (Greater Noida, Faridabad), **Pune's Hinjawadi**, **Hyderabad's Cyberabad** expanded faster than governance could manage
- Around **65% of India's 7,933 urban settlements have no Master Plan** (NITI Aayog 2024)

**Consequences:**
- Low-income communities pushed to distant peripheries, far from jobs and services — with **40% less access to critical infrastructure** (MoHUA/NITI Aayog 2025)
- Farmland and wetlands disappear — Indian city wetlands have shrunk by **one-third since 1990**
- Greater dependence on private vehicles, worsening traffic and air pollution

**Solution: Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)**
- An urban planning approach building compact, walkable, mixed-use neighbourhoods around high-quality public transport hubs (metro, BRT stations)
- Brings people, jobs, homes, and shops close to transit — live, work, travel without depending on private vehicles

**Key features of TOD:**
- High-density, mixed land use within walking distance of transit stations
- Pedestrian-friendly streets with cycling tracks and green spaces
- Affordable housing integrated near transport corridors
- Land value capture to fund public infrastructure

**Why TOD matters for India:**
- Reduces urban sprawl, congestion, and air pollution
- Cuts commute times and boosts productivity
- Makes cities more inclusive by connecting the poor to jobs

### 4. Conspicuous Consumption in Urban Life

- **Conspicuous consumption** = buying things mainly to display status rather than for need
- Shows up in: luxury cars, branded clothing, destination weddings, premium housing, foreign holidays
- Not just about wealth — it's about **identity**: as traditional markers like caste and lineage lose grip in cities, people signal their place in society through lifestyle and spending
- **"What you own defines who you are"** — the defining culture of new urban India

- **Household Consumption Expenditure Survey (HCES) 2022-23:** confirmed a sharp rise in urban per capita spending — but also a **widening urban-rural gap**, showing this boom is not reaching everyone equally

## 7. Government Schemes for Urban Development

### Major Urban Development Schemes

| Scheme | Year | Nodal Ministry | Objective and Key Features |
|--------|------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Smart Cities Mission (SCM)** | 2015 | MoHUA | Develop 100 smart cities with ICT-driven governance, smart mobility, clean energy, e-services, and citizen participation. Around **90% of 8,067 projects completed** with **Rs.1.64 lakh crore invested** (May 2025) |
| **AMRUT and AMRUT 2.0** (Atal Mission for Rejuvenation and Urban Transformation) | 2015 / 2021 | MoHUA | Ensure piped water, sewerage, drainage, green spaces, and non-motorised transport in 500+ cities. AMRUT 2.0 aims at **universal water supply** |
| **PMAY-U** (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana – Urban) | 2015 | MoHUA | Affordable housing for EWS/LIG/MIG groups. **122.06 lakh houses sanctioned, 96.02 lakh delivered** (Nov 2025) |
| **Swachh Bharat Mission – Urban (SBM-U)** | 2014 | MoHUA | Eliminate open defecation, promote solid waste management. Door-to-door waste collection now covers **98% of urban wards** |
| **DAY-NULM** (Deen Dayal Antyodaya Yojana – National Urban Livelihoods Mission) | 2013 | MoHUA | Skill training, self-employment, and shelters for the urban poor |
| **PM SVANidhi** (PM Street Vendor's Atmanirbhar Nidhi) | 2020 | MoHUA | Collateral-free loans of **Rs.10,000-50,000** to urban street vendors to restore livelihoods |
| **PM e-Bus Sewa** | 2023 | MoHUA | Deploy **10,000 electric buses** under PPP model with **Rs.20,000 crore** central support |
| **HRIDAY** (Heritage City Development and Augmentation Yojana) | 2015 | MoHUA | Revitalise heritage city cores with sensitive urban planning |
| **Shyama Prasad Mukherji RURBAN Mission** | 2016 | MoRD | Develop village clusters with urban-like amenities to reduce migration pressure |
| **National Urban Transport Policy** | 2006 (revised) | MoHUA | Promote safe, affordable, sustainable urban mobility |

### Smart Cities Mission — Urban Poverty and Distributive Justice

**The Mission's Pro-Poor and Inclusive Elements:**
- Basic services for all — clean water, sanitation, electricity, waste management aim to reach all sections including slums
- Affordable housing focus — integration with PMAY-Urban to provide pucca houses to low-income groups
- Inclusive area-based development — slum upgrading in cities like **Pune and Bhubaneswar**
- Better access to opportunities — improved roads, public transport, digital services help poor reach jobs, schools, hospitals
- Livelihood support — convergence with DAY-NULM and PM SVANidhi for street vendors and informal workers
- Smart governance — e-services and grievance redressal aim to reduce corruption and information asymmetry
- Public spaces and walkability — cycle tracks, parks, pedestrian zones improve quality of life for those who cannot afford private vehicles

**Where the Mission Falls Short (Equity Criticism):**
- **"Smart enclaves"** — improvements often concentrated in select Area-Based Development (ABD) zones; rest of city untouched. **Pune's Aundh-Baner-Balewadi** project showed visible upgrades but informal settlements elsewhere were neglected
- **Limited reach to slums** — many projects bypass slum dwellers who form a significant share of urban India
- **Top-down planning** — citizen participation, especially of the poor, has been weak in most cities
- **Risk of displacement** — beautification drives and infrastructure projects sometimes evict vulnerable communities without proper rehabilitation
- **Uneven implementation** — some cities completed most projects; others lag badly
- **One-size-fits-all model** — fails to capture diverse needs of cities with different socio-economic profiles

### Why Smart Villages Are Essential

Urban and rural development are closely interconnected. Smart cities can achieve sustainable growth only when supported by prosperous, connected, and developed rural areas.

**Smart Village Concept:** Rural areas that are self-sustaining, digitally connected, and economically vibrant through better roads, internet, schools, healthcare, and clean energy.

**Examples:**
- **Punsari (Gujarat)** — Wi-Fi and CCTV
- **Dharnai (Bihar)** — Solar power
- **Hiware Bazar (Maharashtra)** — Watershed management

**Why Smart Villages Are Necessary for Effective Urban Growth:**
1. **Reducing Rural-Urban Migration:** Better opportunities in villages reduce distress migration and ease pressure on urban infrastructure
2. **Balanced Regional Development:** Prevents excessive concentration of population and economic activity in a few cities
3. **Food, Labour, and Resource Security:** Cities depend on villages for food, labour, water, and raw materials
4. **Reducing Urban Problems:** Improved rural education, healthcare, employment, connectivity can help reduce slums, congestion, informal settlements
5. **Integrated Growth:** Smart villages with digital infrastructure complement and strengthen smart cities

> Smart cities and smart villages are not competing models — they are **complementary pillars** of sustainable and inclusive development.

## 9. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Simple Definition |
|------|------------------|
| **Urbanisation** | The process by which more people move from villages to cities, changing how they live, work, and relate to each other |
| **Urbanism** | The distinct way of life in cities — marked by anonymity, individualism, and weak community ties; about culture, not just numbers |
| **Hidden Urbanisation** | When places function like towns but are still officially counted as villages due to outdated classification rules |
| **Census Town** | A settlement that meets urban criteria (population, density, non-farm work) but has no formal civic body and is governed as a village |
| **Over-Urbanisation** | When a city's population grows faster than its jobs and infrastructure can absorb — leading to slums, unemployment, and congestion |
| **Peri-Urban** | Fringe areas around cities that are neither fully urban nor fully rural — urban in lifestyle but rural in governance and services |
| **Urban Sprawl** | Unplanned, low-density outward expansion of cities into surrounding farmland and villages, driven by rising land prices and poor planning |
| **Urban Primacy** | The excessive dominance of one or two large cities — like Delhi and Mumbai — that attract most investment while smaller cities are neglected |
| **Primate City Syndrome** | When a country's largest city is so dominant that it dwarfs all others in population, economy, and political power |
| **Conurbation** | A large urban region formed when multiple cities grow outward and merge — e.g., Delhi NCR |
| **Agglomeration Economies** | Productivity gains that arise when businesses and people cluster in cities — sharing infrastructure, labour, and ideas |
| **Dual Economy** | The coexistence of a modern formal sector (IT, banking) and a large informal sector (street vendors, construction) in the same city |
| **Informal Economy** | Economic activity outside government regulation — no contracts, no fixed wages, no social security; covers ~90% of India's workforce |
| **Gig Economy** | Short-term, flexible, platform-based work (cab drivers, delivery workers) that offers flexibility but no job security or benefits |
| **Circular Migration** | The repeated movement of people between villages and cities — going to cities for work and returning home seasonally |
| **Slum** | A densely packed, poorly serviced urban settlement with insecure tenure, limited water/sanitation, and high risk of eviction and disease |
| **Gentrification** | When wealthier people move into poor neighbourhoods, raise property prices, and gradually displace the original residents |
| **Subaltern Urbanism** | Urbanisation driven by the poor in informal, overlooked spaces — recognising that the urban poor actively build cities, not just inhabit them |
| **Segregation** | The physical separation of social groups in cities by caste, religion, or income — often reinforcing inequality and social distance |
| **Intersectionality** | The idea that overlapping identities (caste + gender + class) create compounded disadvantages that are greater than each one alone |
| **K-Shaped Urban Divide** | Growing urban inequality where skilled workers rise while unskilled workers fall further behind — the city benefits some and burdens others |
| **Conspicuous Consumption** | Buying goods primarily to display social status rather than for genuine need — a defining feature of new urban middle-class culture |
| **Anomie** | A feeling of normlessness and rootlessness experienced when someone loses their sense of belonging — common among urban migrants |
| **Acculturation Stress** | Psychological pressure faced by migrants as they adapt to a new cultural environment, often losing their old identity in the process |
| **Urban Commons** | Shared public spaces — parks, lakes, footpaths, wetlands — that belong to everyone; rapidly shrinking in Indian cities |
| **Urban Heat Island** | Cities being hotter than surrounding rural areas because concrete and asphalt trap heat and there is little vegetation |
| **Smart City** | A city using technology and data-driven governance to improve services, sustainability, and quality of life for citizens |
| **Transit-Oriented Development (TOD)** | Building dense, mixed-use neighbourhoods around transport hubs (metro, bus) to reduce car dependency and improve equity |
| **Decentralisation** | Transferring power and funds to local urban bodies; India's 74th Constitutional Amendment (1992) was a major step, though implementation remains weak |
| **Right to the City** | The principle that all urban residents — especially the marginalised — have a right to housing, services, public spaces, and a say in governance |

## Quick Revision Summary

### The 4 Uniquenesses of Indian Urbanisation
1. **Urbanisation without Urbanism** — lifestyle change without adequate infrastructure
2. **Informality of the urban economy** — 75%+ informal employment in Delhi
3. **Over-urbanisation without industrialisation** — Dharavi as symbol
4. **Urban primacy and regional imbalance** — Delhi, Mumbai dominate

### The 7 Major Challenges
1. Housing crisis and slums (10 million unit shortage)
2. Segregation and marginalisation (ghettos, enclaves, gated communities)
3. Urban infrastructure stress (water, waste, congestion, pollution, flooding, groundwater)
4. Health challenges (dual burden: communicable + NCDs)
5. Vulnerability of urban migrants (45.36 crore; invisible in policy)
6. Hidden urbanisation (31% official vs. 63% satellite estimate)
7. Weak decentralisation (74th Amendment not fully implemented)

### The 4 Key Recent Trends
1. Growth of IT hubs and their challenges
2. Rise of Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities (50% startups now outside metros)
3. Urban sprawl → TOD as solution
4. Conspicuous consumption as new urban identity

### The 10 Key Government Schemes
SCM (2015) → AMRUT/AMRUT 2.0 (2015/2021) → PMAY-U (2015) → SBM-U (2014) → DAY-NULM (2013) → PM SVANidhi (2020) → PM e-Bus Sewa (2023) → HRIDAY (2015) → RURBAN Mission (2016) → National Urban Transport Policy (2006)

### Critical Quote for Answers
> "The real challenge is to make urbanisation **inclusive, humane, and sustainable** — so that the benefits of city life reach not just the privileged few, but every Indian who calls the city home, however recently they arrived."
