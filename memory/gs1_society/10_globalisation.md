## 1. Definition of Globalisation

**Globalisation** refers to the growing interconnection of the world's economies, societies, and cultures through the cross-border movement of goods, services, capital, people, ideas, and technology.

- It is not a recent development — human civilisations have always traded across distances.
- What is unprecedented is the **pace, scale, and depth** of integration in the modern era.

### Three Core Dimensions

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| **Economic** | Trade, foreign investment, global supply chains, and financial markets |
| **Technological** | The internet, mobile communication, digital platforms, and satellite networks that reduce cost and time of global interaction |
| **Socio-cultural** | The spread of ideas, languages, values, and lifestyles across societies, producing new cultural forms and sometimes eroding old ones |

### Key Concept: Time-Space Compression
The idea that modern technology has effectively "shrunk" the world. A financial crisis in New York, a pandemic in Wuhan, or a war in Ukraine is felt in Indian homes within hours. This compression of distance and time is what makes modern globalisation qualitatively different from anything before it.

## 2. India and Globalisation — The 1991 Turning Point

For India, globalisation became a lived reality after the **LPG Reforms of 1991** — Liberalisation, Privatisation, and Globalisation — which opened India's economy to global trade and investment after decades of a relatively closed, state-controlled system.

### Scale of India's Integration Since 1991

- India's **trade-to-GDP ratio** rose from just over 10% in the 1990s to around 45% in recent years.
- **FDI inflows** reached a record $84 billion in 2021–22.
- India is the **world's largest recipient of remittances** — $125 billion in 2023 (World Bank).
- India accounts for over **55% of the global IT-BPM** (Information Technology–Business Process Management) outsourcing market.
- **Internet users** crossed 900 million by 2025 (IAMAI–Kantar projection).

These numbers show globalisation has restructured the economy, reshaped social institutions, and transformed everyday life for hundreds of millions.

## 3. Economic Impact — Opportunities and Structural Failures

Globalisation has acted as a **double-edged sword** for Indian society — catapulting India as a "services powerhouse" while creating a fractured economic landscape where high-tech growth coexists with deep-seated financial insecurity.

### A. The Service Sector Revolution and the New Middle Class

- By 2025–26, the IT and BPM sector's contribution to India's GDP is projected to reach **10%**, with industry market size hitting **$350 billion**.
- **Employment Boom**: The sector now directly employs over **5.4 million professionals**.
- **The GCC Evolution**: India has transitioned from a "back-office" to an "innovation hub." There are now over **1,800 Global Capability Centres (GCCs)** in India, accounting for 50% of the global total. Brands like Vanguard, Walmart, and McDonald's now run global R&D and AI operations out of Hyderabad and Bengaluru.
- **Cultural Shift**: A new urban middle class that is globally connected and consumption-driven. Their demand has transformed urban landscapes — rise of "Millennial Cities" with premium real estate, global retail chains, and a booming experience economy.

### B. The Gig Economy: Flexibility vs. Structural Vulnerability

- India's gig workforce is projected to reach **23.5 million workers by 2029–30** (NITI Aayog).
- **Appeal**: Low entry barriers, schedule flexibility, supplementary income — particularly for women managing household responsibilities.
- **The Reality of Precarity**: Gig workers are classified as "independent contractors," excluded from EPF, health insurance, minimum wage guarantees, and paid leave.
- **Algorithmic Management**: Workers controlled by opaque algorithmic systems with little room for human grievance redressal.
- As of 2024 data, **90% of gig workers had no emergency savings**, highlighting extreme financial vulnerability.

### C. The K-Shaped Paradox: Deepening Inequality

The most accurate analytical framework for globalisation's uneven effect on India:

| Arm | Who | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Upper Arm (Rising)** | Globally-connected, formal sectors (IT, finance, pharma); skilled, English-speaking, urban professionals | The top 1% of Indians now control nearly **40% of the nation's total wealth** (World Inequality Report 2026) |
| **Lower Arm (Stagnating)** | Informal sector, traditional agriculture, small-scale manufacturers — the majority of India's population | The bottom 50% earns only **15% of national income** but contributes **64% of total GST revenue** |

> Globalisation in India has not acted as a tide that lifts all boats equally. Instead, it has tended to deepen pre-existing structural inequalities, disproportionately rewarding those already advantaged by access to quality education, English language proficiency, and urban geography.

## 4. Technological Impact: Rewiring Indian Society

Globalisation has facilitated the rapid spread of technologies such as the internet, smartphones, social media, digital classrooms, e-commerce platforms, dating apps, biotechnology, artificial intelligence, and robotics in India. These have transformed communication, education, healthcare, work, entertainment, and social relationships — making life more connected and efficient.

**Challenges introduced**: digital inequality, privacy concerns, job displacement, and cultural homogenisation.

### Social Media — Identity Assertion and Polarisation

- Social media gave historically invisible communities — Dalits, Adivasis, linguistic minorities, LGBTQ+ individuals — platforms to assert identity, organise collectively, and challenge dominant narratives.
- But the same platforms are **engineered to maximise engagement, and outrage travels faster than reason**.
- **WhatsApp**, with over **500 million users in India**, has become the primary vehicle for communal misinformation.
- IT Cell culture across political parties has professionalised the manufacture of polarising content.
- IT (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021 are a step toward accountability, but enforcement remains inconsistent.

### Deepfakes and Digital Harassment

- Deepfake technology — using AI to create fabricated audio, video, or images — has become an everyday reality.
- Globally, the overwhelming majority of deepfake content online is **non-consensual synthetic pornography**, with women as the primary targets.
- In India: ordinary women, students, journalists, and activists face morphed images used to harass and silence.
- For women in public life, digital harassment has become a **tool of professional suppression**.
- India's legal framework remains inadequate — no dedicated legislation targeting deepfakes specifically.
- Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 addresses data rights but does not directly tackle synthetic media misuse.

### Cognitive Outsourcing and the Erosion of Independent Thinking

- **Cognitive outsourcing**: the growing tendency to delegate mental functions to devices and algorithms rather than exercising them independently.
- When GPS replaces spatial reasoning, search engines replace recall, and AI tools replace writing — the brain's engagement gradually diminishes.
- India-specific concerns:
  - YouTube Shorts and Instagram Reels are training audiences to expect simplicity and distrust nuance. Complex issues compressed into 60-second formats.
  - Growing use of AI writing tools in academic work raises questions about development of original analytical capacity.
- NEP 2020 emphasises critical thinking, but classrooms have not meaningfully adapted to address cognitive consequences of digital dependency.

### Algorithmic Bias in Governance

- Indian governance is increasingly deploying algorithmic and AI-based systems for welfare delivery, public services, and law enforcement.
- Algorithms learn from historical data — when that data reflects existing social exclusions, the algorithm **reproduces and amplifies those patterns while appearing neutral and objective**.

## 5. Globalisation 4.0 — The Digital Frontier

The next wave of globalisation is driven not by physical goods or services but by **flows of data and knowledge** — powered by:
- Artificial Intelligence (AI)
- Internet of Things (IoT)
- Big Data
- Biotechnology

It is harder to resist or regulate with traditional tools like tariffs or border controls.

### Cryptocurrency and Digital Finance

**What is Cryptocurrency?**
- A digital or virtual currency that uses cryptography (advanced encryption) for security.
- Operates on **blockchain technology** — a decentralised digital ledger maintained by a network of computers rather than a central bank.
- Examples: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Ripple.
- Features: Decentralised, borderless, peer-to-peer transactions.

| Positive Effects | Security Concerns |
|---|---|
| Faster and cheaper cross-border transactions | Used for money laundering, tax evasion, and illicit transactions due to anonymity |
| Promotes financial innovation and digital payments | Can facilitate cybercrime, ransomware attacks, and darknet markets |
| Can improve financial inclusion for the unbanked | Challenges governments' ability to regulate financial flows |
| | Creates risks to financial stability due to high price volatility |
| | Raises concerns about terrorism financing and transnational crime |

**Impact on Indian society**:
- Growing popularity among urban youth and retail investors.
- Increased use of digital finance and emergence of crypto-related startups.
- Concerns regarding speculation, financial fraud, investor protection.
- Reflects India's increasing integration into the global digital and financial economy.

### Data Colonialism

- In the new global economy, **data is the new oil**.
- Global tech giants — Google, Meta, Amazon — harvest vast amounts of data from Indian citizens and monetise it without fair benefit-sharing.
- India generates enormous data but captures little of its economic value.
- **India's response**: Personal Data Protection framework and push for **data localisation** — asserting digital sovereignty against global platforms.

### Disinformation and the Infodemic

- Global social media platforms enable rapid spread of disinformation and hate speech.
- In a diverse country like India, algorithmically amplified divisive content can threaten social cohesion and trigger communal tensions.
- India's **IT Rules, 2021** — requiring local grievance officers and message traceability — represent an assertion of regulatory sovereignty over global tech.

## 6. Impact Across Social Institutions

### A. Family

**Changes driven by globalisation:**
- **Accelerated nuclearisation** — migration for global jobs separates families physically. Nuclear households now dominate urban India.
- **Transnational families** — members living across continents, connected by remittances and video calls but separated in daily life.
- **"Skype Parents" phenomenon** — parents working abroad while children are raised by grandparents in India, creating new socialisation challenges and emotional strain.
- **Kerala's Empty Nest crisis** — an estimated **6% of elderly parents in Kerala live alone**, financially supported by remittances but emotionally isolated. This illustrates the central paradox of globalisation: **economic integration generating prosperity at the cost of social disintegration**.
- **Growth of individualism** — personal career and lifestyle choices increasingly prioritised over collective family obligations.
- **New family forms** — DINK couples, single-parent households, live-in relationships, and child-free couples are becoming more visible in urban India.

**What persists:**
- **Functional jointness (A.M. Shah)** — families living separately but maintaining mutual financial, emotional, and ritual support through phone calls, remittances, and periodic gatherings.
- Kinship networks continue to be crucial for migrants arriving in new cities.
- Festivals and life-cycle rituals still bring dispersed families together, reaffirming bonds across distance.

---

### B. Marriage

**Changes:**
- **Rising age of marriage** — urban women marry at **22.1 years** versus **19.8 years** for rural women (NFHS-5), driven by career aspirations and global norms.
- **Greater individual agency** — love marriages and self-arranged marriages are increasing.
- **Rising divorce rates** — urban divorce petitions have **tripled over two decades**; exposure to global ideas of individual happiness and women's economic independence are key drivers.
- **Marriage as spectacle** — destination weddings, pre-wedding photoshoots, choreographed ceremonies — marriage is increasingly a social performance for social media.
- **Live-in relationships** — legally protected by the Supreme Court under Article 21; socially contested but growing.

**What persists:**
- Bulk of the marriages remain arranged — endogamy is resilient.
- Inter-caste marriages stand at a miniscule fraction — though rising slowly.
- Inter-religious marriages remain even rarer — social resistance and legal barriers are significant.
- Even matrimonial platforms like **Shaadi.com maintain caste and religion filters** — showing that digital technology is being used to **reinforce traditional endogamy, not dismantle it**.

---

### C. Religion

**Positive impacts:**
- Greater religious pluralism — exposure to diverse faiths has in many cases promoted tolerance and interfaith dialogue.
- Global platforms have given Indian spiritual traditions — yoga, Vedanta, Sufi music — international audiences and new relevance.
- Digital tools have made religious texts, discourses, and ceremonies accessible to wider communities.

**Negative impacts:**
- **Commodification of religion** — Christmas, Diwali, and Eid are increasingly reduced to consumer occasions, losing spiritual depth.
- **Decline of indigenous tribal religions** — absorption into mainstream religions or replacement by evangelical Christianity in some Northeast tribal areas.
- **Cyber-radicalisation** — the internet has become a vehicle for extremist religious ideologies to reach young people globally.
- **Reactive revivalism** — exposure to global cultural homogenisation has triggered conservative religious identity assertion in some communities, as a defensive response to perceived cultural erosion.

---

### D. Education

Globalisation has transformed Indian education in two directions simultaneously — expanding opportunity for some while deepening inequality for others.

**Positive shifts:**
- Digital platforms like **Coursera, edX**, and India's own **SWAYAM** have made global knowledge accessible to millions.
- **NEP 2020** reflects global trends — multidisciplinary learning, skill development, flexibility, and research orientation.
- India's IITs and IIMs have signed academic partnerships with MIT, Harvard, and other global universities.

**Persistent challenges:**
- **Digital divide** — during COVID-19 school closures, crores of rural children had no internet access, falling irreversibly behind.
- **Brain drain** — top graduates from IITs and AIIMS migrate to the US and Europe for better research funding and pay. India loses the human capital it invested in.
- **Commercialisation** — private universities have multiplied under globalisation, but quality is uneven and fees are exclusionary.
- **Cultural alienation** — overemphasis on English and Western curricula is slowly marginalising regional literature, indigenous knowledge systems, and traditional learning institutions.

---

### E. Health

Globalisation has simultaneously modernised Indian healthcare and introduced new burdens.

**Gains:**
- India has emerged as the **"Pharmacy of the World"** — exporting generic medicines to 200+ countries.
- **Medical tourism** is growing — cardiac, orthopaedic, and Ayurvedic treatments attract foreign patients seeking affordable quality care.
- Digital health tools like **eSanjeevani** telemedicine have extended healthcare access to remote areas.

**New burdens — the NCD crisis:**
- Rapid urbanisation and the spread of global fast-food culture have driven a sharp rise in **Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)**.
- India now has over **100 million diabetics** — the world's highest.
- **Obesity stands at ~24%** and hypertension affects an even greater proportion of adults.
- Adolescents consuming fast food 3–4 times weekly show significantly higher obesity rates.
- Healthcare is increasingly commercialised — private hospitals charge prohibitive fees, making quality care inaccessible to the poor.

---

### F. Governance and Polity

**E-Governance and the Neoliberal State:**
- Globalisation has pushed the Indian state toward a **facilitator role** rather than a direct provider of services.
- Digital governance platforms — **Aadhaar, Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT), DigiLocker** — have improved transparency and reduced leakage in welfare delivery.
- However, privatisation and deregulation have weakened universal public services in health, education, and transport — the benefits reaching the already-connected, excluding the digitally marginalised.

**Globalisation and the Sovereignty Debate in India:**

Globalisation demands openness. Sovereignty demands control. For India, managing this tension has been one of the defining challenges of the post-1991 era.

*Where Sovereignty Comes Under Pressure:*
- **Economic sovereignty** faces strain when global institutions like the WTO, IMF, and World Bank attach conditions to trade agreements that constrain domestic policy choices. India's resistance to certain WTO agricultural subsidy norms reflects this tension.
- **Data colonialism** is the defining sovereignty challenge of Globalisation 4.0. Tech giants like Google and Meta harvest data from hundreds of millions of Indian citizens, monetising it abroad without fair benefit-sharing.
- **Cultural sovereignty** faces quiet erosion as globalisation promotes homogenised consumption patterns, displacing local languages, crafts, and traditions.

*India's middle path:*
- **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** and push for data localisation assert digital sovereignty.
- **IT Rules 2021**, mandating local grievance officers for global platforms, mark India's effort to regulate rather than merely consume global technology.
- **Atmanirbhar Bharat** reflects a broader assertion of policy autonomy within an open economy.

---

### G. Lifestyle

Globalisation has transformed lifestyles in India by introducing new patterns of consumption, communication, and cultural expression:
- Fast-food chains, international cuisines, and online food delivery have influenced dietary habits.
- Global fashion trends have reshaped clothing preferences, especially among urban youth.
- Growing use of English, Hinglish, and global popular culture through social media and streaming platforms has affected language and entertainment choices.
- Globalisation has encouraged greater consumerism, individualism, and aspiration for modern lifestyles, particularly among India's expanding middle class.

**McDonaldization and the Rise of Consumer Culture (George Ritzer):**

Sociologist **George Ritzer** coined the term **McDonaldization** to describe how the principles of fast-food restaurants — efficiency, calculability, predictability, and control — are spreading into all areas of social life. In urban India:
- Multiplexes are replacing single-screen theatres, standardising the movie-going experience.
- Corporate hospital chains apply efficiency logic to healthcare.
- Online food delivery platforms are standardising urban food culture.

**Culture of consumption** in India:
- Rising urban incomes, easy credit, and global advertising have made consumption a primary marker of social identity and status.
- Shopping malls, branded goods, destination weddings, and premium smartphones signal who a person is.
- The "Big Fat Indian Wedding" has transformed from a sacred social occasion into a choreographed spectacle of consumption — driven by social media, global aesthetics, and competitive display.
- Consumption is increasingly **displacing caste as the primary axis of social differentiation in urban India**.

> Alongside consumerism, globalisation also produces rights consciousness, environmental awareness, cultural creativity, and digital empowerment. It is a multidimensional process, not reducible to any single outcome.

## 7. Impact on Different Social Groups

### A. Women — A Double-Edged Sword

**Opportunities:**
- New employment in BPO, IT, and export-oriented manufacturing has provided economic independence to a generation of women.
- Urban migration of skilled young women has increased personal freedom — delaying marriage, enabling independent housing, expanding social networks.
- Global feminist movements and human rights discourse have strengthened rights consciousness — the **MeToo movement** had global roots and significant Indian branches.
- Women are entering new fields — the Indian Air Force, policing, business, and entrepreneurship — and global corporate culture increasingly promotes gender diversity.

**Challenges:**
- The **double burden** has intensified — women manage paid professional work alongside the primary responsibility for domestic work and caregiving.
- **Feminisation of labour** — women are concentrated in low-wage, insecure, informal jobs in global supply chains.
- **Tech-facilitated gender-based violence (TFGBV)** — cyberstalking, online harassment, and morphing are new forms of patriarchal control in digital spaces.
- **Moral policing** — women who adopt global lifestyles face social backlash in conservative communities.

---

### B. Children — Screen vs. Social

- Grandparents' storytelling — the traditional mechanism for transmitting values, history, and cultural identity — is being **replaced by YouTube and digital entertainment**.
- Physical play and cooperative games are giving way to solo screen time, reducing the development of empathy, patience, and social skills.
- Children have become primary targets of global advertising — premature consumerism and unhealthy food choices follow.
- **Cyber-safety risks** — cyberbullying, inappropriate content, and data misuse are growing concerns.
- However, family-based festivals, religious rituals, and extended kin networks — even across distances — continue to transmit cultural identity to children.

---

### C. Elderly — Abandoned by Progress

- The traditional joint family support system has been weakened by migration — children working abroad or in distant cities.
- **Loneliness epidemic** — Kerala's empty nest crisis is replicated across urban India; financial security from remittances does not replace physical presence and emotional care.
- **Rise of old-age homes and paid care services** — reflecting the commercialisation of care that was previously provided by family as a social obligation.
- **New risks**: elder financial abuse, difficulty navigating digital services, exclusion from online healthcare systems.
- Some silver lining: advanced medical care, digital connectivity tools like video calls, and government schemes like **Atal Pension Yojana** provide partial support.

---

### D. Youth — Aspiration and Anxiety

**Opportunities:**
- Access to global education through **MOOCs (Massive Open Online Courses)**, study-abroad programmes, and international collaborations.
- New careers in IT, digital marketing, startups, and global services.
- A cosmopolitan outlook, cultural exposure, and digital empowerment.
- Youth-led activism on climate, gender, and civil rights, networked globally.

**Challenges:**
- **Aspiration-opportunity mismatch** — high aspirations but insufficient jobs; over **80% of unemployed Indians are youth** (NITI Aayog).
- **Identity crisis** — caught between the traditional values of parents and the individualistic consumer culture of globalisation, creating inter-generational conflict.
- **Mental health crisis** — social media comparison, competitive career anxiety, and burnout; the **Kota Factory phenomenon** of student suicides in coaching hubs reflects the pressure of globalisation-fuelled career competition.
- **Digital addiction** — reduced face-to-face social interaction; cyberbullying and online radicalization as emerging risks.

---

### E. Tribal Communities — Globalisation's Most Unequal Impact

- **Resource exploitation and displacement** — global demand for minerals has driven large-scale land acquisition in tribal areas, displacing communities from jal, jangal, zameen.
  - The **Dongria Kondh vs. Vedanta case in the Niyamgiri Hills** is the defining example of a tribal community resisting corporate globalisation and asserting its right to land and identity.
- **Cultural erosion** — tribal languages, customs, and ecological knowledge are threatened; UNESCO lists many Indian tribal languages (Sora, Mundari) as endangered.
- **Cultural commodification** — tribal art and dance are reduced to tourist spectacles and exotic products for the global market, divorced from their original social meaning.
- **Positive counterpoint** — Warli and Gond paintings are finding global digital markets; tribal entrepreneurship is gaining some institutional support.
- **Implementation gap** — PESA and the **Forest Rights Act (FRA)** exist as constitutional protections but are routinely bypassed when global capital and state interests converge.

---

### F. Farmers — The Sector Left Behind

- Integration with global markets encouraged a shift from food crops like millets and pulses to **cash crops like cotton and tobacco for export**, threatening food security and nutritional diversity.
- Small farmers are exposed to **global price volatility** — a crash in global cotton or coffee prices translates directly into Indian farm distress and debt.
- Dependence on MNCs for patented seeds (Bt Cotton), fertilisers, and pesticides has driven up input costs.
- WTO trade rules and **subsidised agricultural imports from developed countries** make it difficult for small Indian farmers to compete.
- **Agrarian distress and farmer suicides in Maharashtra and Telangana** are directly linked to globalisation-induced market integration without adequate safety nets.

## 8. Criticisms of Globalisation

### Neo-Colonialism

Critics argue that globalisation is a **new form of imperialism** — powerful Western nations dominating global markets, financial institutions, and decision-making:
- Technology transfer is controlled by developed nations and their corporations, creating structural dependency.
- IMF and World Bank programmes have historically prioritised global capital interests over local developmental needs.
- **Bio-piracy** — indigenous knowledge like turmeric and neem has been patented in the US and Europe without consent or benefit-sharing with the communities that developed it.

### Economic Inequality

- **Oxfam (2024)**: India's richest 1% own more than **40.5% of national wealth**.
- Globalisation rewards the skilled, the urban, and the capital-owning — not the majority.
- A **"race to the bottom"** in labour and environmental standards occurs as countries compete to attract investment by weakening protections for workers and nature.

### Environmental Degradation

- Globalised supply chains and consumerism increase transportation emissions and resource extraction.
- Global demand for minerals pressures tribal and forest areas — driving deforestation, mining, and water depletion.
- India faces increasing climate stress through air pollution, rising temperatures, and extreme weather — partly driven by globalisation-induced industrialisation.

### Erosion of Sovereignty

- WTO trade rules, IMF conditionalities, and international climate agreements constrain domestic policy autonomy.
- Supranational institutions limit what national governments can do on tariffs, subsidies, and industrial policy — raising fundamental questions about democratic self-determination.

### New Transnational Problems

- Globalisation has facilitated the spread of transnational threats — **terrorism, human trafficking, cybercrime, and pandemics** — that transcend national boundaries and overwhelm traditional governance structures.
- COVID-19 demonstrated how rapidly risks spread in a hyper-connected world, and also how vaccine nationalism emerged as a response.

## 9. Cultural Homogenisation

One of the most common criticisms of globalisation is that it leads to **cultural homogenisation** — the erosion of local cultures and their replacement by a uniform, usually Western, global culture.

**Evidence of homogenisation in India:**
- English is increasingly preferred over regional languages in urban education, employment, and corporate settings.
- Global fast food chains — McDonald's, KFC, Domino's — have standardised food cultures in urban areas, displacing local culinary traditions.
- Western fashion — jeans, t-shirts, branded clothing — has become everyday wear, especially among urban youth.
- International occasions like Valentine's Day and Friendship Day have entered the Indian cultural calendar, partially displacing traditional festivals.
- Hollywood dominates global entertainment and influences Indian tastes, storytelling styles, and aspirations.
- Indigenous crafts, folk music, and traditional performance arts face decline.
- Individualism and materialism are partially replacing community and collective values.
- Traditional institutions — the joint family, folk arts, oral traditions — are weakening.

### The Indian Reality — Glocalization and Cultural Hybridity

However, reducing India's cultural experience of globalisation to simple homogenisation would be inaccurate. The more precise term is **glocalization** — the adaptation of global products and ideas to fit local contexts, producing something new that is neither purely global nor purely local.

**Examples of glocalization in India:**
- McDonald's offers the **McAloo Tikki burger** and goes vegetarian during Navratri.
- Starbucks sources local ingredients and offers chai-based drinks.
- OTT platforms like Netflix produce Indian-language original content in Malayalam, Tamil, and Marathi that now reaches global audiences.
- Matrimonial platforms like Shaadi.com blend digital technology with traditional caste-based matchmaking.
- **Hinglish** — a fusion of Hindi and English — has become the dominant language of urban advertising, social media, and popular culture.
- Navratri celebrations blend traditional Garba and Dandiya with Bollywood songs and electronic music without losing their religious character.
- Bollywood has absorbed Hollywood narrative styles and action sequences while retaining distinctly Indian themes of family, community, and morality.
- **Coke Studio India** fuses classical Indian ragas with global genres — a living example of cultural hybridity.

**India as a cultural exporter:**
- **Yoga**, once a niche spiritual practice, is now a multi-billion dollar global wellness industry. The UN's declaration of International Yoga Day confirms this.
- **Ayurveda** and Indian wellness traditions are gaining international recognition and commercial value.
- **Indian cuisine** is among the most widely consumed globally.
- The Indian diaspora celebrates Diwali in New York and London — carrying Indian culture into global spaces.

> Globalisation is not just a one-way flow from the West to the East. India both absorbs and spreads cultural influences, challenging the idea of cultural imperialism. At the same time, it can lead to a revival of local identity as people feel their culture, language, and traditions may be overshadowed.

**Layered identities**: A young software engineer in Bengaluru can be a proud Kannadiga, a practising Hindu, an Indian citizen, and a global professional at the same time. These identities do not conflict — they coexist and reinforce each other. Globalisation has created **multi-dimensional identities**, not replaced local ones.

## 10. Deglobalisation

**Deglobalisation** refers to the decline or reversal of global economic integration — marked by rising protectionism, reshoring of industries, and emphasis on national self-reliance.

### Drivers of Deglobalisation

- **Economic inequality** — Oxfam (2024): the world's richest 1% own more than twice the wealth of 6.9 billion people; this fuels political backlash against openness.
- **COVID-19** — exposed the fragility of global supply chains; shortage of PPE, pharmaceuticals, and vaccines revealed dangerous dependencies.
- **US-China trade war** — geopolitical competition is disrupting global value chains and forcing countries to choose sides.
- **Rise of populism and nationalism** — Brexit, Trump tariffs, and "America First" politics signal a shift in domestic political mood in major economies.
- **Environmental concerns** — calls for localised, sustainable production challenge the logic of global supply chains built on cheap labour and high transportation.

### India's Response — Strategic Balance

India is navigating carefully between two imperatives:
- **Embracing Globalisation 4.0** — participating in the digital economy, developing AI capabilities, and leveraging the GCC ecosystem.
- **Strategic deglobalisation (Atmanirbhar Bharat)** — domestic manufacturing in defence, semiconductors, and pharmaceuticals through PLI schemes, reducing strategic vulnerability.

> There is no binary choice between globalisation and self-reliance. The most effective approach is **selective integration** — engaging deeply in areas where India has a competitive advantage, while maintaining **strategic autonomy** in critical sectors.

## 11. Continuity and Change

There is a tension between globalisation's transformative power and the resilience of Indian social structures. The honest answer is that globalisation has changed India enormously — but **selectively and unevenly**.

### What Has Genuinely Changed

- The structure of the economy — from agrarian to services-dominated.
- Family forms — nuclear families and new household types are now the statistical norm.
- Women's roles — significantly expanded workforce participation and rights consciousness.
- Youth aspirations — cosmopolitan, career-oriented, and globally networked.
- Cultural forms — hybrid, glocalized, and two-directional.

### What Has Proven Remarkably Resilient

- **Caste endogamy in marriage** — still the majority of marriages are within the same caste.
- **Patriarchal norms** — the double burden on women persists despite greater workforce participation.
- **Religious identity** — in many cases, globalisation has strengthened rather than weakened religious identity as a defensive assertion.
- **Kinship networks** — functional jointness persists even when structural jointness declines.

### The Concept of Selective Adaptation

Arranged marriages continue but now use the language of "compatibility" and "consent." Matrimonial platforms use global digital technology but retain caste and religious filters. Dowry is illegal but widely practised, reframed as "gifts." These are not examples of deep transformation — they are examples of **surface adaptation that preserves underlying social structures**.

> Real transformation requires shifts in consciousness, not just in law or form.

## 12. Way Forward — Managing Globalisation for Inclusive Outcomes

Globalisation's impact is not predetermined — it is shaped by policy choices, state regulation, and social movements. India has both the scale and the democratic tradition to manage globalisation on its own terms.

### Protect and Empower Vulnerable Groups
- Extend social security — health insurance, pension, paid leave — to gig and informal workers. **Karnataka's Platform-Based Gig Workers (Social Security and Welfare) Bill, 2024** is a model worth replicating nationally.
- Implement the **Forest Rights Act** fully to protect tribal communities from displacement driven by global resource extraction.

### Preserve Cultural Diversity
- Invest in regional languages, folk arts, and indigenous knowledge — include them meaningfully in school curricula.
- Support creative industries so local culture becomes economically viable in the global marketplace, not just a museum exhibit.
- Protect tribal and community intellectual property from bio-piracy through genuine benefit-sharing frameworks.

### Regulate Digital Globalisation
- Implement robust data protection and localisation frameworks to assert digital sovereignty against extractive global tech platforms.
- Regulate social media to curb disinformation and hate speech — while preserving legitimate expression.
- Bridge the **digital gender divide** — only **31% of women own mobile phones** compared to 61% of men (GSMA 2023).

### Agricultural Justice
- Legally guarantee a **Minimum Support Price (MSP)** before further integration into global commodity markets.
- Provide genuine land rights and market access to small and marginal farmers.

### Pursue Strategic Autonomy
- Atmanirbhar Bharat in critical sectors — defence, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals — while remaining open to beneficial global integration in sectors where India has genuine comparative advantage.
- Diversify trade partnerships to reduce overdependence on any single country or bloc.

### Elder Care Policy
- Move beyond the assumption that families will automatically provide elder care — invest in state-supported geriatric services.
- Expand digital literacy programmes for the elderly to prevent exclusion from healthcare, banking, and government services.

## 15. Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Atmanirbhar Bharat** | India's strategic self-reliance initiative aimed at promoting domestic manufacturing in critical sectors through PLI (Production-Linked Incentive) schemes |
| **Bio-piracy** | The appropriation of traditional indigenous knowledge or biological resources by corporations, often through patents, without consent or fair benefit-sharing |
| **Cognitive Outsourcing** | The growing tendency to delegate mental functions — memory, reasoning, writing — to devices and algorithms rather than exercising them independently |
| **Consumerism** | A cultural system in which personal identity and social status are primarily expressed through the acquisition and consumption of goods |
| **Cultural Homogenisation** | The process by which local cultures are absorbed, diluted, or replaced by a dominant global culture (often Western) |
| **Cultural Hybridity** | The emergence of new cultural forms through the blending of elements from different cultures (e.g., Hinglish, fusion music) |
| **Cultural Imperialism** | The dominance and spread of one culture (usually Western culture) over others through the influence of global media, corporations, and institutions |
| **Data Colonialism** | The extraction and monetisation of data from developing societies by global technology corporations, often without fair consent or benefit-sharing |
| **Deglobalisation** | The decline or reversal of global economic integration, characterised by protectionism, reshoring, and strategic self-reliance |
| **Deterritorialisation** | The process by which culture becomes less tied to a specific geographic location due to globalisation (e.g., Diwali celebrations in London) |
| **DINK** | Dual Income No Kids — a new urban family form where both partners work and choose not to have children |
| **Functional Jointness (A.M. Shah)** | Families living separately but maintaining mutual financial, emotional, and ritual support — a modified form of joint family in globalised India |
| **Gig Economy** | A labour market based on short-term, platform-mediated, and flexible work arrangements, often providing income flexibility but limited employment security |
| **GCC (Global Capability Centre)** | Advanced corporate units in India that handle global R&D, AI, and strategic operations — moving India from back-office to innovation hub |
| **Globalisation** | The growing integration of the world's economies, cultures, and societies through cross-border flows of goods, capital, people, information, and ideas |
| **Globalisation 4.0** | The current phase of globalisation driven by Artificial Intelligence (AI), the Internet of Things (IoT), Big Data, and biotechnology, where data flows are often more important than physical goods |
| **Glocalization** | The adaptation of global products, services, and ideas to suit local cultural contexts; a fusion of the global and the local |
| **Infodemic** | The rapid spread of disinformation and misinformation through digital platforms, especially during crises |
| **K-Shaped Impact** | An economic metaphor in which the upper arm (skilled and urban groups) progresses rapidly, while the lower arm (informal and rural groups) stagnates, highlighting unequal gains from globalisation |
| **LPG Reforms** | India's 1991 economic reforms based on Liberalisation, Privatisation, and Globalisation, which opened the economy to global markets |
| **McDonaldization** | The spread of the principles of the fast-food model — efficiency, calculability, predictability, and standardisation — into various spheres of social life (concept developed by George Ritzer) |
| **Platform Capitalism** | An economic model in which digital platforms (e.g., Uber, Amazon) generate value by connecting users, often without owning the underlying assets or services |
| **Race to the Bottom** | Competition among countries to attract investment by weakening labour and environmental protections, resulting in lower standards for workers and nature |
| **Selective Adaptation** | A process of social change in which the external form of a practice is modernised while its underlying social structure or values remain largely unchanged |
| **TFGBV (Tech-Facilitated Gender-Based Violence)** | Digital forms of gender-based violence, including cyberstalking, image morphing, doxxing, and online harassment, that reproduce existing patriarchal power structures |
| **Time-Space Compression** | The phenomenon whereby advances in communication and transportation technologies reduce the effective distance between places, making distant events immediately relevant locally |
| **Transnational Family** | A family whose members live across multiple countries while maintaining social, emotional, and economic ties through remittances and digital communication |
