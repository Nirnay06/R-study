## 2. What is a Society?

- A **society** is a group of people living together in a **shared territory** who interact regularly, follow common rules, share a culture, and depend on one another for survival and progress.
- It is an **organised and self-sustaining human community**.
- A society is characterised by both **similarities** (language, food, religion → 'we-feeling') and **differences** (skills, occupations, gender → complementarity and cooperation).
- Society is the **web of interactions** we create to make life predictable, meaningful, and sustainable. It is constantly shaped by technology, globalisation, and migration.

---

## 3. What Kind of Society is India?

- One of the **oldest civilisations** in the world — continuous cultural history of over **5,000 years**.
- Hundreds of languages, dozens of religions, thousands of communities with different customs.
- India's uniqueness: ability to **hold all diversity together**. Empires fell, colonial powers restructured the economy — yet India did not lose its cultural identity. It **absorbed every influence and made it its own**.

## 4. Salient Features of Indian Society

### 4.1 Unity in Diversity

**Key Statistics:**
- **19,500+** languages and dialects spoken
- **22** officially recognised in the **Eighth Schedule** of the Constitution
- **1,652** mother tongues recorded in Census **1961**
- **Six major religions**: Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, Jainism — plus Jews, Parsis, Baháʼís, tribal faiths
- **705 Scheduled Tribes**, over **3,000** caste-based communities
- **Racial diversity**: Caucasoid (North), Dravidian (South), Mongoloid (Northeast)
- **Geographical diversity**: coastal plains, river valleys, mountains, deserts, forests

**Philosophical foundations of unity:**
| Concept | Source | Meaning |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam** | Maha Upanishad | "The world is one family" |
| **Anekantavada** | Jainism | Many-sidedness of truth; no single correct world-view |
| **Sarvadharma Samabhava** | Gandhi | Equal respect for all religions |

**Unity in practice:**
- Bihari labourer works in Kerala; Tamil engineer builds career in Hyderabad; Bengali professor teaches in Pune.
- **Metropolitan cities** like Mumbai and Delhi function as "mini-Indias."
- **IPL** and **Bollywood** work as pan-national unifiers.

**Cultural Pockets of "Mini India":**
- Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru: people from all states; South Indian food, North Indian festivals, Northeast communities coexist.
- Kolkata: strong Marwari and Gujarati business communities.
- Hyderabad and Pune: attract students and workers from all over India.
- Industrial and IT hubs: people celebrate Diwali, Eid, Christmas, and Pongal together.

**How India Manages Its Diversity — The Mosaic Model:**
- Sociologists describe three models: Melting Pot (assimilation), Salad Bowl (separation), and **Mosaic Model** (diversity woven into composite national identity).
- India follows the **Mosaic Model** — the most sophisticated of the three.

**Diversity and Marginalisation — A Critical View (for 2024 PYQ):**

*Evidence supporting the link:*
- Adivasi communities face poverty, poor health, displacement, low literacy.
- Speakers of smaller languages face disadvantages in education and administration.
- SCs and STs face higher levels of poverty and exclusion.
- Social practices like early marriage restrict women's opportunities.

*Evidence against a direct link:*
- **Parsis and Jains** are minorities but are relatively advanced in education and income — minority status does not always mean marginalisation.
- Reservation and welfare schemes have helped create a growing Dalit and tribal middle class.
- In cities, education, skills, and mobility often matter more than cultural identity.
- Groups like Dalits and OBCs have gained political influence through democratic mobilisation.

**Key conclusion:** Diversity itself is not the cause of marginalisation. Marginalisation arises from **historical exclusion, social hierarchy, and discrimination**. India must move from mere diversity (coexistence) to genuine **pluralism** (empowered coexistence).

---

### 4.2 Tradition and Modernity Coexist

In the West, modernity replaced tradition. In India, **tradition and modernity live side by side**, often within the same household:

| Traditional | Modern |
|-------------|--------|
| QR-code payments at kirana shops and temple vendors | Modern fintech infrastructure |
| Sarees, kurtas, turbans at offices and colleges | Contemporary fashion |
| Ayurveda and yoga | Allopathic medicine, wellness apps, hospitals |
| Joint-family traditions and arranged marriages | Global careers, love marriages, independent lifestyles |

This coexistence reflects India's **civilisational capacity to absorb the new without abandoning the old**.

---

### 4.3 The Caste System — Persistent and Evolving

- Caste is determined **by birth** — one is born into a **Jati** (local, close-knit community with its own customs, traditional occupations, social rules).
- Thousands of Jatis across India, varying by region.
- Today, caste has **not disappeared — but it has changed**:
  - Economic reforms since 1991 created class-based stratification alongside caste.
  - Social mobility across castes — especially in marriage — remains very difficult.

---

### 4.4 Hierarchical Gender Relations

- Indian society has been, and largely remains, **patriarchal**.

*How patriarchy appears in daily life:*
- Son preference is common, especially in northern and central India → skewed sex ratios at birth.
- Women face a **double burden**: work outside the home + handle most domestic responsibilities.
- In rural areas, women have limited control over property, mobility, and family decisions.

*Change is happening but uneven:* faster in cities and among educated groups; traditional norms still dominate in rural areas.

---

### 4.5 Balance Between Individualism and Collectivism

- Indian society sits at neither extreme of **individualism** (like the West) nor **collectivism** (like traditional tribal societies).
- Career decisions are increasingly individual, but made with family consultation.
- Personal finances managed independently, yet people contribute to joint family property and community festivals.
- **COVID-19 example**: community kitchens, neighbourhood welfare groups, and Gurudwara **Langar** service fed millions — the collective instinct reasserting itself in crisis.
- This balance is gradually tilting toward individualism: nuclear families, rising divorce rates, delayed marriages, **DINK trend** (Dual Income, No Kids).

---

### 4.6 Spirituality and Materialism — Two Sides of the Same Society

- India is known as a **spiritual centre** of the world but is also a **fast-growing consumer market**.
- **Charvaka**: focused on material life and enjoyment.
- **Vedanta**: emphasised spiritual liberation.
- Both developed within the same civilisation.
- **Kumbh Mela**: world's largest spiritual gathering AND creates enormous economic activity through tourism, transport, trade, and media.

---

### 4.7 Tribal Society — India's Oldest Communities

- **705 Scheduled Tribes** = **8.6%** of the population.
- India's oldest inhabitants — social systems, languages, spiritual traditions predate recorded history.

**Key features:**
- **Communal landholding**: land owned collectively by the community.
- **Animistic religion**: worship of nature, rivers, forests, spirits.
- **Greater gender equality**: tribal sex ratio = **990 females per 1,000 males** (vs. national average of 943) — reflects lower patriarchy, absence of dowry, presence of bride price customs.

**Challenge:** Tribes are only 8.6% of population but constitute **nearly 40% of all people displaced** by development projects (dams, mines, highways). Cultural erosion, forest displacement, inadequate education remain serious concerns.

---

### 4.8 Family System — From Joint to Nuclear

- The **family** is the basic unit of Indian society.
- Traditionally: **joint families** — multi-generational households sharing income and decisions collectively.

*This is changing:*
- **Nuclear families** now make up approximately **53% of households** (Census 2011).
- **New family forms**: DINK families (Dual Income, No Kids), single-parent households (4.5% of all households), "weekend families."

*Yet the joint family has not simply died:*
- Sociologists introduced the concept of **"functional jointness"** — even when families live separately (structurally nuclear), they maintain emotional ties, financial support, and shared responsibilities with the wider kin network. The **form has changed, but the spirit often persists**.

---

### 4.9 Rural-Urban Divide

- **65%** of India's population still lives in rural areas (Economic Survey 2022–23).
- **Rural life**: community-centred, agrarian, tradition-bound. Caste hierarchies more visible and enforced.
- **Urban life**: more individualistic, mobile, and class-based. People are judged more by what they earn than by which community they were born into.
- Migration creates **hybrid identities** — neither fully rural nor fully urban. This rural-urban interaction is one of the most important forces shaping contemporary Indian society.

---

### 4.10 Syncretic and Dynamic Character

- **Syncretism**: the blending of different cultural elements into something new. This is arguably India's most defining quality.

*Everyday examples:*
- **Urdu language**: born from a blend of Arabic, Persian, and local Hindavi dialects — grew organically from cultural contact.
- **Rashtrapati Bhavan**: blends European, Rajput, and Mughal architectural styles in a single building.
- **Bollywood**: uses Western cinematography techniques but tells deeply Indian stories.
- **Yoga**: originated in ancient Indian philosophy, now practised by millions worldwide.
- People wear a kurta with jeans, eat Chinese noodles at a roadside stall, and celebrate Christmas alongside Diwali without seeing any contradiction.

This syncretism was built through trade, migration, the **Bhakti-Sufi movements**, and colonial contact. The result: a society always evolving, but never uprooted.

## 5. Continuity and Change in Indian Society

Indian society is characterised by the **simultaneous presence of continuity and change**. Ancient institutions, beliefs, and practices persist, yet are constantly being reshaped by democracy, education, urbanisation, technology, and globalisation.

**Nehru's metaphor**: In *The Discovery of India*, he described Indian society as a **palimpsest** — an ancient manuscript written on, erased, and written on again, yet the old writing never fully disappears.

### Sources of Continuity

**A. Cultural Practices:**
- Festivals (Onam, Pongal, Durga Puja, Diwali, Kumbh Mela) reinforce shared heritage and collective memory.
- Life-cycle rituals — **Mundan** (first haircut), **Saptapadi** (marriage ritual), **Antyesti** (funeral rites) — continue across communities.
- **Sanskritisation** — lower castes adopting upper-caste rituals — as evidence of continuity in ritual life.

**B. Philosophical Traditions:**
- Concepts of **Dharma** (righteous duty) and **Karma** (moral causation) continue to shape ethical decisions.
- Practices like pilgrimage, meditation, fasting, and yoga remain widely practised.

**C. Social Institutions:**
- **Caste endogamy persists**: Around 5–6% of marriages in India are inter-caste (IHDS 2011–12; Census 2011) → **over 90–95% of marriages remain within caste**.
- **Arranged marriages** remain dominant, accounting for over **90%** of Indian marriages (BBC, 2018 sample survey).
- Hierarchical family roles, kinship obligations, and instructions from elders continue to influence everyday behaviour.

**D. Technology as a Carrier of Continuity:**
- Temples livestream rituals and festivals.
- Classical arts like Bharatanatyam, Kathak, and Carnatic music have found new audiences on YouTube.
- WhatsApp groups help maintain intergenerational family bonds across distances.

### Forces of Change

**A. Constitutional and Democratic Forces:**
- Universal Adult Franchise empowered marginalised groups politically.
- **Articles 14, 15, 17, and 21** challenged hierarchy, discrimination, and untouchability.
- Affirmative action (reservations) created pathways for upward mobility in education, employment, and politics.
- The Constitution can be understood as a **project of social transformation** — replacing hereditary hierarchy with equality, and custom with rights.

**B. Economic Transformation:**
- Shift from agrarian to service-based economy → jobs outside caste-linked occupations.
- A new middle class has emerged, cutting across traditional caste divisions.
- Income, education, and occupation increasingly matter more than ritual status.

**C. Urbanisation and Migration:**
- City life reduces direct enforcement of caste-based purity-pollution norms.
- New associations form around occupation, neighbourhood, and shared interests.
- Large-scale rural-to-urban migration has increased nuclear families.

**D. Education:**
- Modern education promotes scientific temper, rationality, constitutional values, and individual choice.
- For Dalits, Adivasis, and women, education has been the **most important pathway to social mobility**.
- Higher female literacy → declining fertility, greater workforce participation, shifting gender norms.

**E. Technology and Globalisation:**
- Internet and smartphones transformed communication, work, learning, and political participation.
- Social media enabled new forms of mobilisation: MeToo, #DalitLivesMatter, farmers' protests.
- **Glocalization**: adaptations like McAloo Tikki, Hinglish, fusion music.
- The **gig economy** (Swiggy, Uber, Zomato) created new livelihoods and reshaped work culture.

**F. Social Movements:**
- Collective action by marginalised groups has reshaped social norms and institutions.
- Dalit movement, women's movement, and environmental struggles have expanded rights and challenged entrenched hierarchies.

## 6. Selective Adaptation — Real Transformation or Surface Change?

A key sociological debate: Is India genuinely transforming, or merely **adapting tradition to look modern** while preserving its core inequalities?

Evidence suggests **selective adaptation** rather than deep transformation:
- Arranged marriages continue, but now with the language of "compatibility" and "consent" — the form changes, the structure remains.
- Caste persists in matrimonial ads and political mobilisation, even as people publicly reject caste discrimination.
- **Dowry** is illegal but widely practised, often reframed as "gifts."
- Technology is used to livestream religious rituals — reinforcing traditional practices through modern means.

**Key insight:** Real transformation requires **shifts in consciousness**, not just in law.

## 7. Customs and Traditions — A Double-Edged Sword

Every society is held together partly by its customs and traditions. In India, these are extraordinarily rich and diverse — but also **contradictory**: the same customs that bind communities together can also exclude, oppress, and resist progress.

### When Tradition Strengthens Society:
- **Festivals as social glue**: Diwali, Durga Puja, Onam, Pongal bring communities together across class and caste.
- **Family and kinship**: Joint family traditions promote collective responsibility, emotional support, and intergenerational care.
- **Cultural continuity**: Marriage ceremonies and funeral rites transmit values from one generation to the next.
- **Living heritage through arts**: Classical and folk arts like Bharatanatyam, Kathak, Madhubani, and Kalamkari continue to evolve, carrying civilisational memory forward.
- **Scholar André Béteille** observed: community customs, even caste-based ones, can provide a framework of social order and predictability — particularly where formal institutions are weak.

### When Tradition Becomes an Obstacle:
- **Caste endogamy and occupational inheritance** limit social mobility. When a person's profession and marriage partner are decided by birth, merit has little room to operate.
- **Gender oppression**: Practices like dowry, child marriage, and purdah restrict women's freedom and directly violate **Articles 14, 15, and 21** of the Constitution.
- **Superstition over science**: Witch-hunting, faith healing, rejection of modern medicine persist in some communities, especially affecting women and marginalised groups.
- **Honour killings**: NCRB 2020 recorded **77 honour killing cases** — instances where tradition is weaponised to punish individual choice in matters of marriage.
- **LGBTQ+ exclusion**: Many traditional norms stigmatise non-heteronormative identities, even after the Supreme Court's decriminalisation of homosexuality in **2018**.

## 8. The Core Tension: Constitutional Morality vs. Social Morality

**Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** warned of this conflict.

| Constitutional Morality | Social Morality |
|-------------------------|-----------------|
| Equal right to choose one's partner | Caste-based marriage restrictions |
| Dignity of every individual | Caste discrimination in villages |
| Gender equality | Dowry, purdah, child marriage |
| LGBTQ+ rights | Traditional exclusion and stigma |

- **Social morality**: norms upheld by community consensus — often rooted in caste, patriarchy, and religious hierarchy.
- **Constitutional morality**: values enshrined in the Constitution — equality, dignity, individual liberty, non-discrimination.

**Key insight:** Legal reform often fixes the **form** of inequality, but not its **root causes**. Untouchability was abolished in 1950; manual scavenging was banned in 1993 — yet both still exist because they are sustained by **social attitudes**, not just law.

The conflict is **structural**: between the state and community, the individual and collective, written law and social customs. Bridging this gap requires not just laws, but **education, political will, and a change in public mindset**.

As **Ambedkar said**: political democracy without social democracy is a **contradiction** — and resolving this remains India's **unfinished task**.

## 13. Exam-Ready Frameworks

### For "What Makes Indian Society Unique?" (2019 PYQ):
1. Unity in diversity (Mosaic model, 19,500+ languages yet one nation)
2. Tradition and modernity coexist (civilisational capacity)
3. Syncretic character (Bhakti-Sufi-colonial synthesis)
4. Palimpsest quality (Nehru) — absorbs without erasing
5. Balance between individualism and collectivism
6. Philosophical pluralism (Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam, Anekantavada, Sarvadharma Samabhava)

### For "Customs and Traditions Suppress Reason" (2020 PYQ):
- **AGREE**: dowry (illegal but practised), honour killings (NCRB 2020: 77 cases), witch-hunting, child marriage, LGBTQ+ exclusion
- **DISAGREE**: festivals as social glue, family systems as welfare, classical arts as living heritage, Béteille's point on order and predictability
- **SYNTHESIS**: Ambedkar's distinction — constitutional morality must prevail over social morality; tradition must be reformed, not abandoned

### For "Cultural Pockets of Mini India" (2019 PYQ):
- Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru: metropolitan mini-Indias
- Kolkata: Marwari and Gujarati business presence
- Hyderabad and Pune: education and work migration
- IT hubs: inter-religious festival celebrations
- Conclusion: people keep own identity while becoming part of a larger Indian identity → unity in diversity
