# SECTION I: THE INDIAN FAMILY SYSTEM

## 1. Introduction — What is Family?

### Kinship
- **Kinship** = social recognition of relationships based on blood ties (consanguinity — parent-child, siblings) or marriage ties (affinity — husband-wife, in-laws)
- Western societies limit kin to close relatives; non-Western societies (including India) recognize a much wider network

### Family (Definition)
- A small group bound by kinship ties, living together, and sharing economic and emotional life
- Most **primary and universal** social institution — older than the state, religion, or economy
- Functions: primary socialization (language, values, roles, identity), economic unit, welfare system, cultural repository

### Family in India
- Not merely a personal arrangement — it is the **basic unit of social organisation**
- Decisions about marriage, career, property shaped by family and kinship networks
- Marked by: strong bonds of reciprocity, sense of collective obligation, integration of individual into wider web of relatives
- Traditional ideal: **joint family** (multiple generations under one roof)
- Modern trend: nuclear families dominant in urban areas, but underlying ethos of kinship continues

---

## 2. Types and Classification of Families

### By Size and Structure
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Nuclear family** | Parents + unmarried children; increasingly dominant in urban, mobile, middle-class settings |
| **Joint/Extended family** | Multiple related nuclear units sharing residence, kitchen, property, and responsibilities; traditional Indian ideal |

### By Post-Marital Residence
| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Patrilocal** | Wife moves to husband's household | Historically dominant in India |
| **Matrilocal** | Husband lives with wife's family | Common in matrilineal societies (Khasi) |
| **Neolocal** | Couple establishes independent home | Increasingly common among urban salaried professionals |

### By Descent
| Type | Description | Communities |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **Patrilineal** | Lineage, inheritance, family name follow male line | Widespread across India |
| **Matrilineal** | Lineage and inheritance follow female line; youngest daughter often inherits property, though men may retain ritual authority | Khasi of Meghalaya, Nairs of Kerala, Minangkabau communities |

---

## 3. The Joint Family — Characteristics, Functions, and Merits

### Characteristics
- Co-residence of **three or more generations**
- Common ownership of property (individual members hold no separate claims)
- Collective decision-making, often under authority of eldest male (patriarch)
- Shared responsibilities for child-rearing, elder care, and ritual observance
- Strong sense of **"we-feeling"** and collective identity
- Common kitchen (*chulha*), common property, common religious obligations

### Functions of the Joint Family
| Function | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Social security** | Acts as India's original welfare state — provides for sick, elderly, unemployed, widowed without institutional support; irreplaceable in a country with historically low formal social security coverage |
| **Economic pooling** | Shared resources reduce individual financial burden; agricultural families pool land/labour/produce; business families (Marwari trading, Parsi industrial) built enterprises on joint family capacity to pool capital across generations |
| **Cultural continuity** | Grandparents transmit festivals, stories, rituals, and values to grandchildren through daily interaction; primary vehicle for intergenerational cultural transmission |
| **Division of labour** | Responsibilities (cooking, childcare, elderly care, agricultural work, ritual) distributed across members — reduces burden on any single person |
| **Emotional support** | Dense network of relationships cushions individuals against personal loss, illness, unemployment, social pressure |

### Merits vs. Demerits

| Merits | Demerits |
|--------|----------|
| Economic security through shared resources | Financial dependency of non-earning members |
| Built-in social security for elderly and sick | Limited personal privacy and individual space |
| Division of labour reduces individual burden | Conflict of interests across generations |
| Cultural and religious continuity | Suppression of individual choice (career, marriage) |
| Collective decision-making and crisis resilience | Unequal burden on women — especially daughters-in-law |
| Emotional depth and kin-based support | Resistance to social change and progressive values |

## 4. The Shift Toward Nuclearity — Trends and Drivers

### The Structural Shift
- Most visible change in Indian family over the last century: shift from joint to nuclear households
- Nuclear families rose significantly over past few decades, trend strongest in **southern and western states**
- **Census 2011:** nuclear families make up approximately **53% of households**
- This can be misleading: what looks like breakdown of joint family is often **structural separation without functional separation**

### Key Insight: "The address changes, but the obligations don't"
Even when a young couple moves to a different city and lives in a nuclear household, they continue to:
- Send money home regularly
- Return for festivals and family rituals
- Consult elders on major decisions
- Pool resources during family crises

### Drivers of Nuclearity
- Industrialisation and urbanisation
- Occupational mobility and migration for education/work
- Salaried employment (not family enterprise)
- Rising individualism and privacy norms
- Changing gender roles and women's employment
- Urban housing constraints (smaller apartments)

### Sociological Position
- Sociologists argue that the nuclear household is not the death of the joint family — it is merely a **stage in its lifecycle**
- **Functional Jointness (A.M. Shah):** nuclear households maintaining the emotional, ritual, and financial bonds of the joint family system — nuclear families that still maintain emotional and financial ties with the wider family network

---

## 5. Life Cycle of a Joint Family

The life cycle refers to the process through which a family evolves over time. A joint family expands through marriage and childbirth, remains stable, and eventually divides into smaller units. Both **economic factors** AND **social values** shape this cycle.

### 5.1 Economic Factors Influencing the Life Cycle

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Occupational Mobility** | Migration for education/employment/business encourages separate households |
| **Industrialisation and Urbanisation** | Urban living, limited housing, modern occupations favour nuclear families |
| **Income and Economic Independence** | Self-sufficient members may prefer independent living |
| **Inheritance disputes** | Division of ancestral property can lead to disputes and family breakup |
| **Changing Economic Functions** | Decline of family-based occupations reduces economic necessity of staying together |

### 5.2 Social Values Influencing the Life Cycle

| Value | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| **Respect for Elders** | Authority of elders encourages family cohesion |
| **Collectivist Values** | Cooperation, mutual support, shared responsibility promotes joint living |
| **Family Solidarity and Kinship Bonds** | Strong emotional ties and kinship obligations sustain joint families |
| **Marriage and Ritual Obligations** | Joint families facilitate performance of social, religious, ceremonial functions |
| **Care and Security Norms** | Values of caring for children, women, elderly strengthen the institution |

### Assessment (UPSC Angle — 2014 PYQ)
- Economic factors often determine **practical viability** of a joint family in modern urban-industrial society
- Social values influence whether members **choose** to remain together despite economic pressures
- **Conclusion:** Life cycle is shaped by **interaction of both** — neither factor alone determines it
- While economic factors have grown increasingly important, social values continue to play a significant role

## 6. Emerging Family Forms — Beyond Joint and Nuclear

Modern India is witnessing diversification of family forms that don't fit neatly into either category:

### DINK (Dual Income, No Kids) Families
- Urban, educated, career-oriented couples deliberately choosing not to have children
- Shows a clear shift away from the traditional **pronatalist norm** — parenthood becoming a **personal choice** rather than social duty
- Drivers: high cost of raising children, career priorities, work-life balance, shift towards individual fulfilment
- These families have higher per-capita consumption and contribute to **premium FMCG markets**
- Growth rate: **~30% per annum** (Gitnux Market Data, 2024)

### Single-Parent Households
- Causes: divorce, separation, abandonment, widowhood
- Increasing number of Indian households headed by **single mothers**
- Challenges: economic insecurity, childcare burden, social stigma, weak support systems not fully addressed by current policies

### Weekend Families
- Driven by **job-related migration** — family members live in different cities during the week and meet mostly on weekends
- Challenges the traditional idea of a shared household

### Transnational Families
- Separated due to **international migration** but stay connected through remittances, digital communication, and occasional visits
- Key issue: **"Emotional orphanhood"** — children grow up without regular physical presence of parents

### Community-Based Living
- Response to nuclear family isolation
- Families choose to live close to each other to recreate support systems similar to a joint family
- Shows how **functional jointness** continues even in a modern, nuclear setup

## 7. Contemporary Pressures on the Family

### 7.1 Work From Home — Blurring Boundaries (COVID-19 Impact)

**Positive Impacts:**
- Greater physical presence at home strengthened family bonds; increased **paternal involvement** in childcare
- Flexible schedules improved work-life balance (especially for those with long commutes)
- Some women able to continue employment alongside household responsibilities, supporting **female labour participation**

**Negative Impacts:**
- Blurring of work and home boundaries led to longer working hours, **mental fatigue**, reduced quality of family time
- Women faced an increased **double burden** (paid work + unpaid domestic work)
- Cramped urban housing without proper workspace created conflict and stress
- Lack of clear boundaries increased domestic tension and, in some cases, **domestic violence**

**Overall:** Impact is uneven — depends on class, gender roles, and living conditions — making it either an opportunity or a burden.

### 7.2 The Care Crisis — Elder Care and the Silver Economy

**Challenges for Elderly:**
- Isolation and loneliness in cities as children migrate for work
- **Empty Nest Syndrome** — emotional distress when children move out
- Greater vulnerability to crime (theft, fraud) due to living alone
- Financial dependence with limited pension or social security

**Demographic Ageing:**
- India experiencing demographic ageing due to lower fertility and longer life expectancy
- Rise of **Sandwich Generation** — middle-aged adults who must care for both children and ageing parents, often without enough support

**The Silver Economy:**
- Growing sector including old-age homes, caregiving services, senior living spaces, digital tools for elder support
- Singapore has laws making children responsible for parents
- Sweden provides state-supported elder care
- India has **Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act (2007)**, but implementation remains weak

### 7.3 Children, Socialization, and the Digital Shift

**The Transformation:**
- Primary socialization function of family (teaching language, values, social norms, emotional skills) being deeply affected by digital technology
- The issue is not just screen time, but a bigger change in how children build **identity, relationships, and social skills**
- In nuclear families with working parents, role of grandparents in storytelling, value transmission, emotional bonding has reduced
- Gap increasingly filled by **digital devices** — children socialised more by screens than people

**Negative Consequences:**
- Less face-to-face interaction weakens empathy, emotional intelligence, ability to understand social cues
- Digital interaction focuses on **quick reactions**, not deep relationships
- Physical issues: eye strain, myopia, inactive lifestyle
- **Attention fragmentation** due to constant notifications affects focus, learning, deep thinking

**Positive Dimensions:**
- Digital tools help maintain family connections across distance through video calls
- Cooperative digital games can build teamwork and interaction among peers
- Internet provides access to educational and creative resources

**The Challenge:** Ensure digital socialization supports, not replaces, human interaction. Families that set screen boundaries, maintain shared activities, and engage in co-creative digital use can balance both.

### Helicopter Parenting
- Parents are **over-involved** in a child's life — constantly monitor, control, and make decisions for the child
- Consequences:
  - Children develop more **anxiety** (not used to handling situations on their own)
  - Weaker **self-regulation** (cannot manage emotions or behaviour independently)
  - Lower **self-confidence (self-efficacy)** as they depend on parents for decisions
  - Can even affect **academic performance** over time
- The paradox: parents spend more time and effort, but instead of building independence, children become less able to make own decisions, take responsibility, or deal with challenges

# SECTION II: THE INSTITUTION OF MARRIAGE

## 1. Introduction — Marriage as Social Institution

- Marriage is a **universal social institution** — exists in every society, though form and meaning differ across cultures and time
- In India, marriage is more than a union of two individuals: it is an **alliance between families**, creation of kinship ties, way to ensure legitimate children, fulfilment of dharma, and sign of social adulthood
- Very low number of unmarried women (about **0.5%**) shows marriage is deeply embedded — not just individual choice
- Marriage in India is at a **turning point**: education, women's economic independence, legal changes, urbanisation, and changing values are reshaping its age, form, and meaning

---

## 2. Functions of Marriage

| Function | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Regulation of sexual behaviour** | Provides a socially approved, culturally recognised framework for sexual relations and reproduction |
| **Legitimation of offspring** | Establishes legal and social basis for children — determining lineage, inheritance rights, and social identity |
| **Formation of kinship networks** | Creates families and broad kin relationships, integrating individuals into larger social structures |
| **Conferring social status** | Defines legal rights, obligations, inheritance norms, and social standing for partners and children |
| **Emotional support and companionship** | Provides mutual care, stability, and psychological support across the life course |
| **Economic cooperation** | Creates an economic partnership — pooling resources, dividing labour, providing mutual material support |

---

## 3. Marriage as Sacrament vs. Contract

### Hindu View — Marriage as Sacrament (Sanskar)
- **Sacred and permanent union** — not a choice but a social and religious obligation
- Three main aims: **dharma** (duty), **praja** (children), **rati** (pleasure)
- Sons valued for: continuing family line, performing rituals (funeral rites), supporting parents in old age
- Marriage (vivah) is an indissoluble union fulfilling religious duty

### Islamic View — Marriage as Contract (Nikah)
- Marriage is a **socio-legal contract (nikah)**, but also considered **sunnah** (recommended duty)
- Features making it formal and contractual: **mehr** (dower), consent, witnesses
- Still holds strong religious importance

---

## 4. Types and Forms of Marriage

### By Number of Partners

| Type | Description | Legal Status in India |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Monogamy** | One man, one woman | Legally mandated for Hindus, Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists, Christians, Parsis |
| **Polygyny** | One man, multiple wives | Still permitted under Muslim Personal Law (up to 4, with equal treatment) |
| **Polyandry** | One woman, multiple men | Practiced by Todas, Kota, Khasa, and some Ladakhi communities |

### By Spouse Selection

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Endogamy** | Marriage within one's caste, sub-caste, or tribe; dominant norm in India |
| **Exogamy** | Marriage outside one's kin group; **gotra exogamy** among Hindus prohibits same-gotra marriage |
| **Levirate** | Man marries the widow of his deceased brother; practiced in some communities |

### Contemporary Forms

| Form | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Live-in relationships** | Cohabitation without formal marriage; growing in urban India |
| **Same-sex marriage** | Between two individuals of the same sex; **legally unrecognised in India** (SC judgment, 2023) |

### Social Reform and Monogamy
- **Hindu Marriage Act (1955)** legally enforced strict monogamy among Hindus
- Reform championed by: **Raja Rammohun Roy**, **Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar**, **Dayananda Saraswati** — all recognised polygyny as a source of oppression and social degradation

---

## 5. Changing Patterns of Marriage in India

### 5.1 Factors Driving Change

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Women's education and economic independence** | Most important factor — delays marriage, allows women to leave unhappy marriages, changes partner selection, leads to educational hypogamy |
| **Urbanisation** | City life brings privacy, distance from family, exposure to new ideas — weakens traditional norms, encourages new relationship forms |
| **Globalisation** | Media, travel, social interaction spread ideas of love marriage, individual choice, personal freedom (e.g., films like *Dilwale Dulhania Le Jayenge*) |
| **Legal reforms** | Laws now support change — allow divorce, civil marriage, live-in relationships, and same-sex relationships |
| **Digital technology** | Online platforms changed partner-finding — matrimonial sites reinforce endogamy while dating apps sometimes help cross social boundaries |

---

## 6. Changes in Marriage

### 6.1 Rising Age at Marriage
- Most consistent trend in Indian marriage demography: rising age at first marriage, particularly for women

**Drivers:**
- Women's rising education levels and career aspirations (completing professional degrees before marriage)
- Economic considerations (financial stability as precondition for household formation)
- Demographic transition (as TFR falls and life expectancy rises, urgency of early marriage diminishes)

**Legal Reform:**
- **Prohibition of Child Marriage (Amendment) Bill, 2021** — seeks to raise minimum age of marriage for women from 18 to **21 years** (equal to men)
- Arguments in favour: gender equality, better health outcomes (reduced anaemia and maternal mortality), improved educational and career opportunities, reduced birth rates
- Critics argue: legal age change without addressing poverty and social norms will merely drive child marriage underground

**Key Data:** Kerala (higher average marriage age) → TFR 1.8; Bihar (lower average age) → TFR 3.4. Correlation between marriage age and fertility is strong and consistent.

### 6.2 Inter-Caste Marriages — Slow Growth
- Inter-caste marriages = unions between individuals from **different jatis within the same religion** — challenge the foundational norm of caste endogamy
- Approximately **5% to 6%** of marriages are inter-caste (very slow growth from historical baselines)

**Why they have increased:**
- Education, professional employment, and urban life bring individuals from different castes into sustained equal-status contact — reducing social distance
- Shared class identity reduces hierarchical anxiety: when two individuals from comparable socio-economic backgrounds marry, the **status-loss fear** diminishes
- Nuclear family structures reduce power of caste elders over marriage decisions
- Government schemes and **Special Marriage Act (1954)** provide some legal legitimacy

**Why they remain rare:**
- The family (not just the individual) is the unit of marriage — families retain strong caste preferences
- **Honour violence** against inter-caste couples, particularly in North India
- Caste identity is not merely social but ritualistic, alimentary, and residential
- Growth concentrated among urban, educated, middle-class populations — in rural and traditional communities, caste endogamy remains near-absolute

### 6.3 Inter-Religious Marriages — Significantly Rarer
- Far less common than inter-caste marriages and face greater social resistance

**Why they are rarer:**
- Religious identity is a comprehensive cultural identity (festivals, food practices, life-cycle rituals, naming conventions, community belonging) — families fear erasure of cultural continuity
- Inter-caste marriage within the same religion preserves shared ritual identity — it is **socially negotiable** because the larger cultural framework is shared; inter-religious crossing is a deeper, more emotionally charged boundary
- Residential and educational segregation along religious lines limits opportunities for sustained interaction
- **Anti-conversion laws** in several states create obstacles, particularly for Hindu-Muslim unions (the "Love Jihad" narrative)
- Procedural barriers under **Special Marriage Act (1954)** — including mandatory **30-day public notice** — expose couples to harassment, moral policing, family interference

**Key Insight:** Caste is becoming more negotiable, but religion remains a stronger social boundary in India.

### 6.4 Educational Hypogamy — An Emerging Trend

**Earlier pattern: Educational hypergamy** — women usually married more educated men (women had less education, men were main earners)

**New pattern: Educational hypogamy rising** — more women marrying men who are less educated than them

**Reasons for the rise:**
- **Persistent gender scripts:** Marriage market still values women primarily for appearance and family background; educated women end up matched with economically well-off (not equally educated) men
- **Shrinking partner pool:** Women's education rises faster than men's in some communities — number of equally/more educated men decreases
- **Consanguineous marriages:** In communities where cross-cousin marriages are preferred, restricted partner pool pushes women toward hypogamy

**Consequence:** Highly educated women in such marriages may face constraints on career choices and reduced decision-making power at home. Education alone, without changing gender norms, cannot fully empower women.

### 6.5 Algorithmic Endogamy — Digital Matrimony
- Websites like **Shaadi.com, BharatMatrimony, Jeevansathi** allow filtering by caste, sub-caste, religion, region, language, socio-economic background
- Similar to traditional arranged marriages, but on a much larger scale
- **Algorithmic endogamy**: technology reinforces social boundaries instead of breaking them — algorithms ensure people mostly see matches within their own caste/community
- Result: technology is **scaling up social conservatism** — millions of users matched but still within fixed social limits
- The online matrimony market in India is growing fast, making this trend increasingly important

### 6.6 Rising Divorce Rates in India
- India traditionally has one of the lowest divorce rates in the world — but gradually changing
- **NFHS-5 (2019-21):** separation and divorce rates, though still low, rising steadily — particularly in urban areas and southern states
- Divorce rate: **~1.1%** (doubled in 20 years) — NCRB estimates

**Key Causes:**
- Economic independence of women (rising female literacy and workforce participation — confidence to exit unhappy/abusive marriages)
- Urbanisation and nuclear families (weakening of joint family removes traditional social pressure to "adjust and stay")
- Changing aspirations (younger generations prioritise personal happiness and compatibility over social obligation)
- Awareness of legal rights (Hindu Marriage Act, Domestic Violence Act make divorce more accessible)
- Social media and exposure (changing cultural values reshaping expectations within marriage)

**Sociological Significance:** Rising divorce rates reflect not just broken families but also **greater individual agency**, especially for women. The challenge for society is to reduce stigma around divorce while strengthening support systems for separated individuals and children.

### 6.7 Decline in Sacramental Character of Marriage

**Traditional view:** Sacred lifelong bond, governed by religious duties and social obligations

**Modern transformation:** Marriage becoming more secular, contractual, and companionate — based on personal choice, mutual compatibility, individual fulfilment

**Reasons for weakening sacramental view:**
- **Rising individualism:** Partnership based on love, compatibility, respect rather than just duty (especially urban areas)
- **Legal recognition of divorce:** Hindu Marriage Act (1955) made marriage legally dissolvable; mutual consent divorce treats it as agreement between equals
- **Rising incidents of divorce:** Less stigma, women's independence, changing expectations
- **Women's exit options:** Financial independence allows women to leave unhappy/unequal marriages

**Evidence that sacramental values still continue:**
- Low divorce rates overall, especially in rural areas
- Indian weddings remain large, elaborate, culturally important
- Marriage still linked to adulthood, family honour, and social status — pressure to marry persists
- **Judicial conservatism:** Courts often stress need to protect marriage

**Conclusion:** Marriage in modern India is not declining but **changing in meaning** — moving from sacred duty to partnership based on equality and choice, where traditional and modern values coexist.

## 7. Alternative and Emerging Relational Forms

### 7.1 Live-In Relationships

**Arguments in favour:**
- **Right to personal choice** (Article 21): Adults have freedom to choose partner and how they live
- Better compatibility: couples can understand emotional and financial compatibility before marriage
- Changing values: modern relationships based on choice and emotional connection, not social pressure
- Legal protection for women: **Domestic Violence Act (2005)** gives protection in long-term live-in relationships

**Legal Status:**
- Indian courts have recognised live-in relationships and given them a degree of legal validity
- Long-term live-in relationships can, in certain cases, be treated as **marriage-like relationships** (especially for maintenance)
- Children born from such relationships have **inheritance rights** and should not be treated as illegitimate

**Arguments against:**
- Seen as going against traditional values and the idea of marriage
- Lack of clear laws on maintenance, property, and custody
- Children may face social stigma and legal confusion
- Risk of exploitation or abandonment (especially for women)

**Recent Development:** Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code requires **registration** of live-in relationships — raises concerns about privacy, state surveillance, and fear of social pressure. Shows conflict between individual freedom and social norms, and between legal protection and right to privacy (Article 21).

### 7.2 Same-Sex Marriage — The Current Legal Position

**Landmark Case: Supriyo v. Union of India (SC, 2023)**
- Supreme Court declined to recognise same-sex marriage as a constitutional right
- Held that redefining marriage requires **parliamentary action** rather than judicial intervention
- Court acknowledged rights and dignity of LGBTQIA+ individuals but determined that creating an institution of marriage requires **democratic deliberation**

**Arguments in favour of recognition:**
- Constitutional rights: **Articles 14, 15, and 21** prohibit discrimination and protect liberty and dignity — advocates argue this encompasses right to marry regardless of gender
- Privacy and dignity: **Puttaswamy judgment (2017)** recognised privacy as fundamental right, includes sanctity of intimate relationships and sexual orientation
- Legal protection: Recognition would extend inheritance, adoption, medical consent, and property rights to same-sex couples
- **Global precedent:** Same-sex marriages are legally recognised in at least **30 countries** including USA, Canada, Australia, France, Germany

**Arguments against:**
- Legislative domain: redefining a fundamental social institution requires democratic mandate, not judicial fiat
- Conflict with personal laws: India's personal laws (Hindu Marriage Act, Muslim Personal Law) are not designed to accommodate same-sex unions — would require comprehensive legislative revision
- Social concerns: children adopted by same-sex couples may face stigma

**Way Forward:**
- Discussion with LGBTQIA+ groups, legal experts, and religious leaders
- Recognising **civil unions** — giving legal rights without calling it marriage (as done in Germany earlier)
- Reform adoption and surrogacy laws
- Strong anti-discrimination laws in jobs, housing, healthcare, and education

---

## 8. Critical Analysis — Continuity and Change in Marriage

India's marriage system is not simply moving toward Western-style individualism. The reality is far more **complex and layered:**

- **Endogamy persists, only its form has changed.** Caste and religious boundaries remain powerful in mate selection — just expressed through matrimonial app filters and parental vetting rather than village elders. Inter-caste and inter-religious marriages remain a small minority.

- **The sacred and the practical coexist.** The same family may spend lavishly on traditional wedding rituals while also accepting that an unhappy marriage can — and should — end in divorce. Tradition and pragmatism are no longer opposites.

- **Women's agency has grown, but unevenly.** Urban, educated women exercise real autonomy in marriage decisions. For rural and less-educated women — where dowry violence, child marriage, and honour killings remain real risks — that freedom is still very limited.

- **New forms are not automatically liberating.** Live-in relationships without legal protection can be just as coercive as formal marriages. Choosing a partner through an app with caste filters is still **arranged endogamy** — just with a modern interface.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Affinity** | Kinship created through marriage — the relationship between a person and their spouse's relatives |
