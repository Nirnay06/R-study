## 8.1 — IMPACT OF HINDUISM ON TRIBAL SOCIETIES

### Key Statistics
- More than **90%** of the tribal population in India are directed towards Hinduism
- **95%** among the total tribal population are Hinduised

### Historical Background
- During British rule, cultural contact between tribal societies and urban areas was rapid
- Significant increase in cultural contact in the form of **"Peasantization"** (N. K. Bose)

### Hindu Accommodation to Tribal Societies
- Hinduism is **smooth and more compatible** — tribes can continue with many original values and also adopt new ones
- **Hinduised tribal groups:** Bhils, Bhumij, Gonds
- Tribes tried to adopt Hinduism without leaving their original values → **unification of religion and tribe**
- Forms of gotras are more **totemistic than Vedic** → basis of the **Tribe-Caste Continuum**

### Tribe-Rajput Continuum
- Bhumij tribes studied → **"Tribe Rajput Continuum"** — tribes follow the Rajput Model
- Tribes like **Tharu** and **Khasa** got themselves accepted as Kshatriya
- Process called **"Kshatriyaization"** — a popular form of **Sanskritization**

### Case Study — Bhil Tribe
- Bhils practiced theft and dacoity
- Believe Goddess Parvati was a Bhil; when they demanded bride price from Shiva, he refused
- They killed Nandi (Shiva's bullock), were cursed to poverty but given a Vardaan: theft for livelihood invites no sin
- Some sub-tribes consider themselves **"thieves of Mahadev"**

---

### POSITIVE IMPACTS OF HINDUISM

| Impact | Details |
|---|---|
| **Cultural Integration & Social Mobility** | Through Sanskritisation (Srinivas 1952), Gonds, Bhils, Oraons adopted Hindu customs to claim higher status |
| **Incorporation into Civilizational Ethos** | Tribal deities absorbed into Hindu pantheon — Gond deity Pharsa Pen → Shiva; Santhal Marang Buru → Mahadeo |
| **Spread of Agriculture & Settled Life** | Peasantization (N. K. Bose) — Bhumij, Bhils, Oraons moved from jhum to settled agriculture |
| **Introduction of Literacy** | Raj Gonds used Sanskrit titles (Raja/Thakur) to legitimize political authority |
| **Religious Tolerance** | Polytheistic worldview allowed coexistence — Navakhani (Oraons) and Holi (Bhils) celebrated jointly |

---

### NEGATIVE IMPACTS OF HINDUISM

| Impact | Details |
|---|---|
| **Replacement of Simple Rituals** | Lambadi (Banjara) now employ Brahmin priests for life-cycle rituals — economic dependence |
| **Decline of Traditional Institutions** | Youth dormitories Ghotul (Muria Gonds) and Dhumkuria (Oraons) declined under Hindu moral codes |
| **Emergence of Dowry** | Bride price → dowry among Ho, Munda, Gond — reversed egalitarian gender norms |
| **Child Marriage & Widow Restrictions** | Bhil and Gond tribes: child marriage encouraged, widow remarriage prohibited |
| **Caste-like Hierarchies** | Raj Gonds developed internal stratification resembling caste; similar among Oraons and Bhils |
| **Loss of Cultural Autonomy** | Tribal deities subordinated as local Hindu manifestations; tribal myths reinterpreted through Hindu cosmological lens |

### Conclusion
Hinduism has had a significant influence leading to **Tribe-Caste Continuum**. Interaction resulted in assimilation of novel customs and practices.

## 8.1 — IMPACT OF CHRISTIANITY ON TRIBAL SOCIETIES

### Overview
- Spread of Christianity began during the **colonial period**
- Christianity is the **second largest religion among tribal communities**, next only to Hinduism
- **Northeastern region** has the highest concentration of Christian tribal populations

---

### POSITIVE IMPACTS OF CHRISTIANITY

**1. Christianity as Early Agency of Tribal Welfare**
- First organized agency for tribal welfare before state schemes
- Missionaries brought literacy, healthcare, and social awareness to remote forest areas

**2. Introduction of Education and Tribal Scripts**
- **Mizoram:** Rev. J. H. Lorrain and F. W. Savidge (1894) — schools, books in Mizo language
- **Nagaland:** American Baptist Mission — first village school at Molungyimsen (1872)
- **Jharkhand/Chhattisgarh:** Rev. John Baptist Hoffmann (Munda grammar), Paul Olaf Bodding (Santal dictionary)
- **Arunachal Pradesh:** Among Adi and Nyishi, mission schools were first sources of literacy

**3. Westernization of Tribals**
- Western lifestyle: shirts, trousers, church, Western music
- **Nagaland:** Headhunting and fertility rituals disappeared
- **Arunachal Pradesh:** Nyokum (Nyishi) and Mopin (Adi) became cultural events

**4. Elimination of Harmful Practices**
- If mother died during childbirth, baby was buried with her → missionaries established orphanages and infant homes

**5. Improved Health and Hygiene**
- Dispensaries, hospitals, health camps, vaccination, maternal care
- Mission hospitals in Arunachal Pradesh and Jharkhand became only reliable medical centers

**6. Prohibition of Alcohol**
- Young Mizo Association (YMA) campaigns against rice beer (Zu)
- Reduced alcoholism and family disputes

**7. Syncretism and Religious Coexistence**
- Jharkhand: Christian Santals and Oraons celebrate Karma and Sohrai festivals with drums and hymns
- Described as **"religious layering"** — new beliefs added without erasing old ones

---

### NEGATIVE IMPACTS OF CHRISTIANITY

**1. Ethnocentric Approach**
- Missionaries saw tribal faiths as "primitive" → erosion of customs, songs, and institutions

**2. Sense of Superiority among Converts**
- Christian Oraons of Chotanagpur developed feeling of superiority over non-Christians → fractured tribal unity

**3. Demographic Change**
- **Arunachal Pradesh:** Conversion among Wancho, Nocte, Tangsa, Nyishi rose from <5% (1970s) to ~80% (2011)
- **Nagaland:** ~90% of Nagas (Ao, Angami, Lotha, Sema) became Christian via Miles Bronson and E. W. Clark's missions
- **Mizoram:** Mizo population converted almost entirely by early 20th century

**4. Loss of Traditional Institutions**
- Morung (Nagas) and Ghotul (Gonds) declined — disrupted transmission of folklore and moral instruction
- Mundas and Oraons stopped celebrating Karma and Sarhul

**5. Resistance and Revivalist Movements**
- **Donyi-Polo movement (Arunachal Pradesh):** Institutionalized Sun-Moon worship through temples (Khumkos) and schools
- **Sarna Dharma revival (Central India):** Reaffirmed nature worship (Singbonga) among Munda and Oraon; All India Sarna Samiti demands separate Census code
- **Gond Mahasabha:** Promotes Koya Punem, worshipping Baradeo as supreme god

**6. Political Identity Implications**
- **IFCSAP (Arunachal Pradesh):** Demands delisting of converted Christians from ST benefits
- **Nagaland/Mizoram:** Christianity became base for ethnic nationalism
- **Jaipal Singh Munda:** Blended Christian and tribal ideals to mobilize Adivasi political movements

### Conclusion
Christianity played transformative yet complex role — education, literacy, healthcare, social reforms, yet also cultural loss and religious polarization.

## 8.1 — IMPACT OF BUDDHISM ON TRIBAL SOCIETIES

### Overview
- Buddhism followed by around **0.5% of Indian tribal population**
- Spread linked to **Mauryan and Kushan emperors**; **Ashoka** played key role
- Buddhism is a **way of life** — tribes could follow it alongside their own beliefs
- Impact primarily in **North-west and North-east Himalayan regions**

### Key Tribal Groups Influenced
- **Bhutias** (Sikkim)
- **Lepchas** (Meghalaya, Arunachal Pradesh, Bhutan, Sikkim, Darjeeling)
- **Chakmas** (Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh)
- **Tribes of Arunachal Pradesh** (Monpa, Sherdukpen, Khowa)
- **Tribes of Ladakh** (Brokpa, Changpa, Balti)

### Establishment of Gompas
- Monasteries like **Bapuchang** in Khampti and Singpho villages established
- Sacred specialists: **Lamas**

### Symbiosis of Buddhism and Tribal Rituals
- Unlike Christian converts, tribes embracing Buddhism maintained **symbiotic relationship** with traditional rituals
- **C. von Furer-Haimendorf (1983):** Buddhist converts in Arunachal did not renounce tribal rituals
- **Sherdukpen:** Worship tribal deities alongside Lamas of Gompas
- **Monpas:** Elements of ancient **Bon religion** coexist with dominant Buddhist faith

---

### CASE STUDIES: BUDDHISM AND TRIBES

| Tribe | Region | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| **Bhutia** | Sikkim, Darjeeling | Follow Vajrayana Buddhism (17th–18th c.); Losoong (Sikkimese New Year) and animistic dances coexist → cultural continuity within conversion |
| **Lepcha** | Sikkim, NE Himalayas | Original inhabitants; Bon-like healing rituals and ancestor worship alongside Buddhist ceremonies — syncretism |
| **Chakma** | Arunachal, Mizoram | Theravada Buddhists; still worship Lakshmi and forest spirits; village councils blend Buddhist codes with customary law — accommodation, not replacement |
| **Monpa/Sherdukpen/Khowa** | Arunachal Pradesh | Mahayana Buddhism; Sherdukpen worship tribal deities alongside Lamas; Bon elements among Monpas — symbiotic |
| **Brokpa/Changpa/Balti** | Ladakh | Mahayana dominant; pre-Buddhist mountain-spirit worship (Lha/Yul-lha), animal-sacrifice rituals continue — religious coexistence |

### Conclusion
Buddhism integrated with indigenous cosmologies **rather than replacing them**. Non-dogmatic nature, emphasis on ethics over exclusivity, acceptance of local gods as Dharma protectors → **syncretic evolution**. Even the adoption of Buddhism could not cut through traditional beliefs. **Limited but significant impact.**

## 8.1 — IMPACT OF ISLAM ON TRIBAL SOCIETIES

### Overview
- Islam's penetration faced challenges due to **dogmatic nature** and limited support from Muslim rulers
- Dissemination fell largely upon **Sufi saints and preachers**

### Muslim Tribal Groups
- **Siddis of Gujarat**
- **Gujjars** of North-western Himalayas
- **Bakarwals** of Jammu and Kashmir
- **Bhils, Kotis, Dhankasi** of Rajasthan
- **Entire population of Lakshadweep** — seven Muslim tribal communities: Koya, Malmi, Malacheri, Manikfan, Thakrufan, Thakru, and Reveri

### Key Tribal Group Profiles

| Group | Region | Islamic Identity + Tribal Retention |
|---|---|---|
| **Bakarwal & Gujjar Bakarwal** | J&K, Ladakh | Nomadic, Sunni Muslim, ST since 1991; transhumance intact; Gujari language retained |
| **Siddi** | Gujarat, Karnataka, Goa | African origin; Muslim but retain African music/dance (Goma/Dhamaal) — Islam overlaid on older cultural base |
| **Lakshadweep Islanders** | Lakshadweep | Sunni Muslim but still matrilineal, still have Taravad, still practise duolocal residence |
| **Van Gujjars** | NW Himalaya | Pastoral, Sunni Muslim; marriage and seasonal movement follow traditional pastoral rules |

### General Impacts of Islam

| Impact | Details |
|---|---|
| **Shift to Patrilineal Norms** | Transition from matrilineal to patrilineal norms |
| **Islamisation of Life Cycle Rituals** | Progressive Islamisation of traditional practices |
| **Observance of Islamic Festivals** | Prophet Mohammed's birthday adopted |
| **Decline of Indigenous Practices** | Folk dances and indigenous practices declined sharply |
| **Replacement of Tribal Social Order** | Islamic personal laws replaced traditional social mechanisms |
| **Preference for Cousin Marriages** | Became preferential among converted tribes |

### Islam and Matriliny — Lakshadweep (Detailed)

**The Taravad (Matrilineal Unit)**
- Group of individuals tracing descent through common genealogy from an **ancestress** in unbroken female line
- Depth: **3 to 6 or more generations**
- Every Taravad has a **name** used as prefix to personal names
- Birth in Taravad gives right to share in **Taravad property** (land, trees, boats, buildings) — passes through female members
- Male members have only **usufructuary rights**
- Taravad is a **matrilineal exogamous unit**
- Father has **no legal responsibility** for children's maintenance — children belong to mother's descent group
- Man can give away individual property to his children

**Islam's Influence on Matriliny**
- Islamic marriage procedure adopted BUT **duolocal residence pattern not given up**
- **Duolocal residential arrangement:** Men and women including spouses live in separate houses
- Most religious activities (festivals, Quran chanting, fulfillment of vows) carried out **within the context of the matrilineal group**
- If Taravad has an ancestor-saint, anniversary celebrated by entire matrilineage
- Islam provides easy divorce → **high frequency of divorce** in Lakshadweep

**Current Changes (Islamisation)**
- Matrilineal practices (inheritance through mother, duolocal residence) gradually eroding
- Growing shift toward **patriliny, nuclear family residence, and male headship**
- Background of Lakshadweep matriliny: brought by nearest coastal people of **Kerala**

### Negative Impacts of Islam

1. **Erosion of Traditional Kinship:** Shift from matrilineal/bilateral to patrilineal under Sharia inheritance — Lakshadweep Taravad weakened
2. **Decline of Indigenous Rituals:** Ancestor worship, spirit appeasement, sacred dances replaced by namaz, fasting, Islamic festivals
3. **Dual Marginalisation:** Gujjars and Bakarwals perceived as socially inferior to Ashraf Muslims (Syeds, Pathans) — outside both Islamic upper layers and mainstream economy

### Conclusion
Islam influenced tribal societies through new religious practices, laws, and social norms. Yet tribes like those in Lakshadweep **adapted Islam within their own cultural frameworks**, creating a blend of faith and indigenous identity.

## 8.2 — TRIBE AND NATION STATE

### Key Definitions

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Tribe** | Social group with territorial affiliation, endogamous, no specialization of functions, ruled by hereditary/other tribal officers, united in language/dialect |
| **Nation** | Community based on shared language, history, ethnicity, culture, society — collective identity of a group |
| **State** | Centralized political organization imposing rules over a population within a territory; maintains **monopoly on legitimate use of violence** (Max Weber) |
| **Nation State** | Political unit where state and nation are **congruent**; people share common identity, culture, history |

### Nation vs. Nation State
- **Kurds** = nation without a state
- **France, Germany, Japan** = nation-states
- **Korea** = one nation divided into two states (North and South)

### Who are Indigenous and Tribal Peoples?
- More than **370 million people** in more than **70 countries** worldwide
- No universal definition; **ILO Convention No. 169** takes a practical approach

### ILO Convention No. 169 — Criteria

| Category | Subjective Criteria | Objective Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **Indigenous peoples** | Self-identification | Descent from populations present at time of conquest/colonization; retain own social, economic, cultural, political institutions |
| **Tribal peoples** | Self-identification | Social, cultural, economic conditions distinguish them from national community; status regulated by customs, traditions, or special laws |

- Other terms: native peoples, aboriginal peoples, first nations, adivasi, janajati, hunter-gatherers, hill tribes
- In **Latin America:** "tribal" applied to some afro-descendent communities
- **India's Special Case:** Division into indigenous vs settlers is **NOT valid** — India cannot be divided into natives and non-natives

### Challenges of Tribes vis-à-vis Nation States
1. **Tribal identity & culture:** Distinct cultures, languages, traditions → conflicts over land rights and cultural preservation
2. **Self-governance & self-determination:** Clashes with centralized authority; self-determination is a fundamental human right
3. **Colonization & forced assimilation:** History of resentment and mistrust; nation-state must acknowledge historical injustices

---

## VARIOUS APPROACHES TOWARDS TRIBAL DEVELOPMENT

### 1. Isolationist Approach — "Keep them apart"
- Put forward by **Verrier Elwin** in **'The Baiga', 1939**
- Tribes left alone, minimum outside contact
- Also proposed **"National Park Theory"** — huge tract of land given to tribes to live in isolation
- **Criticism:** Accused of keeping tribes as "labs" for field studies; suspected of keeping them underdeveloped; tribes should contribute to India
- **Example:** USA — **Indian reservation system** → life was difficult; poverty, malnutrition, low standards of living
  - In 1868, President Ulysses S. Grant tried assimilation through Christian clergy → violent resistance → abandoned
- **Image:** Two separate bowls

### 2. Assimilationist Approach — "Melting Pot"
- Tribes absorbed into mainstream; lose customs, language, traditions
- **Ghurye** called them **"backward Hindus"** — tribes would catch up by mingling with rest of society
- **Criticism:** Ethnocentric; forced assimilation is unfair
- **Example:** Australia — **"Stolen Generations"** (1910–1970): ~100,000 Indigenous children removed from families, placed with white families
  - **Aboriginal Protection Act of 1869** (Victoria): restricted movement
  - Policies abolished in 1970s–80s; impacts still felt
- **Image:** Melting pot

### 3. Integrationist Approach — "Salad Bowl" ✅ (India's approach)
- Put forward by **post-independent Verrier Elwin** in **'A Philosophy for NEFA', 1957** (foreword by **Jawaharlal Nehru**)
- Integrating tribes into mainstream while they **retain their identity**
- How, when, and to what extent to integrate — **choice left to tribals themselves**
- Adopted officially by **Nehru** as India's tribal policy
- **Image:** Salad bowl — each ingredient keeps its flavor

### Tribal Panchsheel — 5 Principles (Nehru, 1957)

| Principle | Explanation |
|---|---|
| 1. Non-imposition | Tribals develop along their own genius; nothing imposed |
| 2. Respect for tribal rights | Rights to land and forests must be respected |
| 3. Development of tribal leadership | Train tribal youth to lead their own administration |
| 4. Simplicity of administration | Avoid over-bureaucratisation; schemes should be people-centric |
| 5. Emphasis on human growth | Evaluate success by quality of life, not statistics |

### UNDRIP 2007
- **Against forced assimilation (Article 8):** Right not to be subjected to forced assimilation or destruction of culture
- **For integration (Article 5):** Right to maintain distinct political, legal, economic, social, cultural institutions while retaining right to participate in national life

### Affirmative Action — USA (Comparative)
- Policies to address historical discrimination of African Americans, Hispanic Americans, Native Americans, Women
- **Fisher v. University of Texas (2016):** Court upheld race as factor in admissions; stricter standard requiring no workable race-neutral alternative

## 9.1 — HISTORY OF TRIBAL ADMINISTRATION IN INDIA

### British Era — Isolationist and Exploitative

**Rationale behind Isolationist Policy**
1. **Security & Political Control:** Tribal groups seen as "explosive" — e.g., Santhal Uprising; isolation cut them from national movements
2. **Economic Interests:** Tribal belts rich in forests/minerals; Forest laws to secure timber and land for British revenue and railways
3. **Cultural Justification:** British anthropologists portrayed tribes as "noble savages" needing protection
4. **Missionary Access:** Allowed missionaries and select officials; cultural penetration with political dominance

**Case Study: The Malers (Paharias) of Rajmahal Hills**
- Among first tribes under separate administrative model
- **Cleveland** established ring-fence of sepoys around hill tracts to isolate Paharias
- **1782:** Rajmahal Hill Tract withdrawn from ordinary judicial system; special Hill Assembly with tribal reps + British officers, could impose capital punishment
- Codified as **Regulation I of 1796**, later repealed by **Regulation I of 1827**
- Area known as **Daman-e-Koh** — indirect rule through limited tribal autonomy
- Prototype for later "special administrative areas"

**Tribal Uprisings**
- **Ho Mutiny of Singhbhum (1831)**
- **Khond Uprising (1846)**
- **Santhal Rebellion (1855–56)**
- **Angami Naga Resistance (1879–80)**
- **Abor and Lushai Expeditions (1880–1910)**

**Causes:** Loss of land/forest rights (Permanent Settlement, Forest Acts), economic exploitation (zamindars, moneylenders), cultural displacement, political marginalization

**Major Legislative Measures**
- **Scheduled District Act 1874 (Act XIV):** Exempt specified districts from conventional laws; protect from exploitation
- **Government of India Act 1919:** Two types of tribal areas:
  - **Wholly excluded areas:** Completely outside British legislation; Governor has legislative authority; neither federal nor provincial legislatures can pass laws
  - **Partially excluded areas:** Partially exempted from British legislation
- **Government of India Act 1935** (Simon Commission recommendations): Continued designation as excluded and partially excluded zones

---

### Post-Independence Tribal Administration

**Constitutional Provisions**
- **Article 342:** Defines Scheduled Tribes; President can declare tribes as STs
- **DPSP:** State shall promote with special care educational and economic interests of STs
- **Article 366(25):** ST means tribes deemed under Article 342 of the Constitution
- **Article 244 (Part IV):** President can declare any area as **Scheduled Area** (Fifth Schedule) — substantial tribal population
- **Sixth Schedule:** Administration of northeastern states
- **Fifth Five-Year Plan:** Introduced **Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP)** for integrated development

**British Era vs. Post-Independence Comparison**

| Dimension | British Era | Post-Independence |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | Isolation and control | Integrationist and empowerment |
| Objective | Maintain colonial order, extract resources | Social justice, inclusion, national integration |
| Laws | Criminal Tribes Act 1871, Scheduled District Act 1874, GoI Act 1935 | Constitutional safeguards, PESA 1996, FRA 2006 |
| Administration | Centralised, autocratic, exploitative | Decentralised, participatory (Panchayati Raj) |
| Outcome | Widespread alienation, land dispossession, uprisings | Uneven progress; persistent challenges in displacement, inequality |

## 9.1 — PARTICULARLY VULNERABLE TRIBAL GROUPS (PVTGs)

### General Tribal Features
- Primitive traits, distinctive culture, geographical isolation, shyness of contact, backwardness

### Additional Features of PVTGs (over and above general tribal features)
- Dependency on **hunting and gathering** for food
- **Pre-agriculture level of technology**
- **Zero or Negative growth of population**
- Extremely **low level of literacy**

### Criteria for Identifying PVTGs
1. Forest dependent livelihood
2. Pre-agriculture level of technology
3. Stagnant or declining population
4. Low literacy rate
5. Subsistence-based economy

### Historical Development of PVTG Category
- **1973:** Dhebar Commission created **Primitive Tribal Groups (PTGs)** as a separate category
- **1975:** Government of India identified **52 groups** as PVTGs
- **1993:** Another **23 groups** added → total **75 PVTGs** out of **705 Scheduled Tribes**
- Spread across **18 States and 1 Union Territory** (2011 Census)
- **2006:** Government renamed PTGs as **PVTGs (Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups)**

### Population of PVTGs
- 2001 Census: approximately **27,68,322**
- **12 PVTGs** have population of 50,000; remaining have 1,000 or less
- **Huge variation:** Great Andamanese, Onge, Sentinelese (very few individuals) to Toda of Nilgiris (~1,000+)
- **Declining PVTGs:** Onge, Andamanese — biggest reason: **inbreeding**
- **Stagnant:** Birhor in central India
- **Largest PVTG:** **Saharia people of Madhya Pradesh and Rajasthan** — population more than 4 lakhs

### Literacy Rate
- Increased from **single digit to 30–40%** in many PVTGs
- **Female literacy** still considerably lower than male

**Factors for low literacy:** Linguistic barriers, social exclusion, lack of cultural sensitivity, discrimination, poor infrastructure

### Livelihood
- Depend on: **food gathering, NTFP, hunting, livestock rearing, shifting cultivation, artisan works**
- Forests are their life and livelihood

**Forest Rights (FRA 2006)**
- **Section 3(1)(e) FRA:** Recognizes "rights including community tenures of habitat and habitation for primitive tribal groups and pre-agricultural communities"
- **Section 2(h) FRA:** Defines 'habitat' as customary habitat in Reserved and Protected Forests of PTGs and pre-agricultural communities

**Challenges to Livelihood**
- Shrinking forests, environmental change, new forest conservation policies
- **2009:** Baiga families moved out of Achanakmar Reserve — loss of livelihood, no proper compensation
- **Kattunayakans (Tamil Nadu):** Prevented from collecting honey in Mudumalai Wildlife Sanctuary — harassment from forest guards
- Exploitation by middlemen — PVTGs unaware of value of their products (honey, tendu leaves)

### Health Issues
- Poverty, lack of safe drinking water, bad sanitary conditions, difficult terrain, malnutrition, poor maternal health
- Examples of diseases: **Anaemia, Malaria, Gastro-intestinal disorders**

### Bonded Labour
- **Sahariya (Rajasthan):** Work as bonded labour for rich landlords for generations; tricked into loans with exorbitant interest
- Banned under **Bonded Labour (Abolition) Act 1976**
- PVTGs in **Odisha:** Debt bondage locally called **goti** — despite Orissa Debt Bondage (Abolition and Regulation) Act, 1948

### Schemes for PVTGs
- Ministry of Tribal Affairs implements **"Development of Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)"**
- **Conservation-Cum-Development (CCD):** Annual plans prepared by each state/UT based on need assessment, approved by Project Appraisal Committee

## 7.1 — DENOTIFIED TRIBES (DNTs)

### Historical Background
- Traced to **Criminal Tribes Acts (CTA) of 1871** (later 1911)
- **150–200 communities** deemed **"Hereditary Criminals"** → surveillance, confinement, gross discrimination
- Part of colonial model: communities that **resisted British rule** were targeted
- **Criteria:** Only "reason to believe" needed (not factual evidence) that community was "addicted" to crime
- Notion that **crime was a genetic trait** passed down generations

**Legal Definition (CTA):** "A Criminal Tribe is a gang, tribe or class of people addicted to the systematic commission of Non-Bailable Offenses and with reason to believe, a local Government could notify using the local gazette that an entire tribe, gang or class of people were criminals."

### Repeal
- CTA repealed in **1952** on recommendation of **All India Criminal Tribes Inquiry Committee (1949)**
- 'Criminal Tribes' became **'Denotified Tribes'**
- CTA replaced by **Habitual Offenders Act, 1951**
- Communities celebrate **August 31 as Independence Day** (denotification date), but stigma remains

### Classification
- Some listed as **Scheduled Tribes**, others as **Scheduled Castes**, some as **OBCs**
- Example of same community across states:
  - **ST** in Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Bihar
  - **SC** in Karnataka, Delhi, Rajasthan
  - **OBC** in Uttar Pradesh
  - **Denotified Tribe** in Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu

### Challenges Faced by DNTs

1. **Social Stigma:** Labelled "born criminals"; stigma not completely abolished by 1952 Act
2. **Economic Hardship:** Traditional occupations (snake charming, street acrobatics, performing with animals) notified as criminal activity
3. **Large-scale Exclusions:** Spread among SC/ST/OBC but many not classified anywhere; lack of data
4. **Poor Grievance Redressal:** No permanent commission unlike NCSC/NCST
5. **Lack of Uniform Approach:** Anomalies in identification from state to state
6. **Declining Population:** Result of accumulated disadvantages

### Recommendations
- **Repeal of Habitual Offenders Act, 1952**
- Repeal must be accompanied by slew of legal reforms
- **Socio-economic survey** through reputed social science institutes (basic census data missing)
- **Permanent commission** constituted independently
- Centre should carve **sub-categories DNT-SC, DNT-ST, and DNT-OBC** with dedicated sub-quota

### Government Schemes for DNTs
1. **Dr. Ambedkar Pre-matric and Post-Matric Scholarship** for DNT Boys and Girls
2. **Nanaji Deshmukh Scheme of Construction of Hostels** for DNT Boys and Girls
3. **Assistance for Skill Development** of OBCs/DNTs/EBCs

### Key Committees on DNTs
1. **Idate Commission / Renke Commission:** Data on **809 Nomadic** and **334 Denotified Tribes**; recommended:
   - Permanent commission
   - Repeal of Habitual Offenders Act, 1952
   - Sub-quotas within SC, ST, OBC categories

### Conclusion
DNTs continue to face stigma, exclusion, and economic hardship despite repeal of Criminal Tribes Act. True rehabilitation demands de-stigmatisation, accurate identification, targeted welfare measures.

## 9.1 — ROLE OF NGOs / VOLUNTARY AGENCIES IN TRIBAL DEVELOPMENT

### Historical Evolution
- **Ancient times:** Rishis and sannyasins — medicine, spirituality, ethical living
- **British period:** Christian missionaries — education, healthcare, social reform in tribal areas
- **Post-independence:** Secular social organisations and NGOs

### Definition of NGO
- World Bank: "private organizations that pursue activities to relieve suffering, promote the interests of the poor, protect the environment, provide basic social services, or undertake community development"
- Non-profit group functioning independently of government; also called **civil societies**

### 1. Christianity as Early Agency
- M.N. Srinivas observed: developmental activities by missionaries viewed by Hindus as "bait in the trap of conversion"

## 9.2 — ROLE OF ANTHROPOLOGY IN TRIBAL AND RURAL DEVELOPMENT

### Introduction
- Anthropology: study of human societies and cultures
- **M.N. Srinivas (1972)** at Simla seminar: "I find today sharply increased awareness of the importance and urgency of associating sociologists and anthropologists in the solution of the many complex and baffling problems which confront the country today."

### Why Anthropologists' Studies Help

| Method | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Cultural Relativism** | Neutral perspective without preconceived notions; objective work |
| **Field Work / Participant Observation** | Insider perspective; become part of the society |
| **Holistic Studies** | Every aspect analyzed from multiple dimensions |
| **Integrated Studies** | Understand how elements are interconnected |

### Historical Background
- Anthropologists' importance first recognised by **Court of Directors in 1807**
- **Hutton, S.C. Roy, D.N. Majumdar, Elwin** highlighted negative impacts of contact — labelled "no changers," "revivalists," "isolationists"
- Post-independence: policies did not include anthropologists → benefits did not reach tribals → led to **Applied Anthropology**

### Applied Anthropology
- "Anthropology put to use"
- Sometimes called the **"fifth sub-field"**
- Application of methods and theory to analysis and solution of **practical problems**

---

### Key Anthropological Contributions to Tribal Development

**1. Nature-Man-Spirit Complex (L.P. Vidyarthi)**
- Helps understand equilibrium between ecology, social organization, and superhuman component
- Helps plan tribal rehabilitation **with least resistance**

**2. Tribe-Caste Continuum**
- Shows why it is difficult to define tribes with specific criteria
- Talks about influence of tribal and non-tribal communities over centuries

**3. Integrationist Approach (Verrier Elwin)**
- 'A Philosophy for NEFA', 1957 (foreword by Nehru)
- Tribes not forced to give up tribal identity; choice left to tribals themselves

**4. Development of PVTG Category**
- **1973:** Committee created Primitive Tribal Groups (PTGs)
- **2006:** Renamed PVTGs
- Anthropologists contributed by: in-depth understanding of socio-economic marginalization, highlighting vulnerabilities, advocating for policies

**5. Tribal Sub Plan (TSP)**
- Expert Committee under Ministry of Education and Social Welfare, **1972**
- Adopted for first time in **Fifth Five-Year Plan**
- Anthropologists highlighted socio-economic and cultural distinctiveness

**6. Criminal Tribes Act Repeal & DNTs**
- CTA 1924 repealed by Criminal Tribes Laws (Repeal) Act, 1952
- Anthropologists' role: provided understanding of history, culture, social dynamics; advocated DNTs as citizens with equal rights

**7. Forest Rights: B.K. Roy Burman Committee on Forests and Tribals (1982)**
- Recognised traditional forest-dwelling communities had deep understanding of forests
- Advocated **community-based forest management** and tribal rights over forest resources
- Helped shape **Forest Rights Act of 2006**

**8. Xaxa Committee Report (2014)**
- High-level committee headed by **Virginius Xaxa**, Professor at TISS
- Submitted to PMO in **May 2014**
- Six thematic issues:
  1. Livelihoods and employment status
  2. Education
  3. Health
  4. Land alienation
  5. Displacement and enforced migration
  6. Legal/constitutional issues; delivery of public goods and services
- Recognized: rights of tribals to refuse land acquisition; empowerment of gram sabha and tribal advisory council; rights of tribes in Scheduled Areas to mine minerals
- Emphasized tribal groups have fallen behind over last 60 years despite India's rapid expansion

**9. Development of Tribal Health**
- Expert Committee on Tribal Health (head: name not specified in notes)
- Report: **"Tribal Health in India — Bridging the Gap and a Roadmap for the Future"** (first of its kind)
- Recommendations:
  - Annual budget equal to **2.5% GDP per capita** allocated for tribal healthcare (~INR 2500 per tribal in 2015–16)
  - New entities: **Tribal Health Council** and **Directorate for Tribal Health** at state and union levels
  - Government focus **70% of resources** on **primary care**
  - Adequate human resources and infrastructure

**10. Safeguarding Endangered Languages**
- Conducting linguistic research and documentation
- Raising awareness about language endangerment
- Advocating for language revitalization programs

## 9.3 — COMMUNALISM

### Definition
- Communalism: "an ideology based on the belief that society is divided into religious communities whose interests differ and are opposed to each other and hence antagonistic in nature" (1979)
- Antagonism practiced by people of one community against people of another community and religion

### Factors Responsible for Communalism

**1. Divisive Politics**
- Religion or cultural identity used to mobilize support; "majority-minority divide" consolidated for votes
- Weakens secular democracy

**2. Politics of Appeasement**
- State concessions to religious groups for electoral gain
- **Shah Bano Case (1985–86):**
  - Supreme Court upheld Shah Bano's right to post-divorce maintenance under secular law
  - **Rajiv Gandhi government** reversed verdict through **Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986**
  - Textbook case of **vote-bank politics and state surrender to religious orthodoxy**

**3. Shift to Religious Mobilisation**
- **Rath Yatra (1990):** Large religious procession from Gujarat to Ayodhya
- By **1992:** Demolition of Babri Masjid — violence erupted from both sides
- Reflected fragility of communal balance

**4. Economic Causes**
- **UN MPI (2023):** One in three Muslims in India is multidimensionally poor
- Economic competition between Hindu and Muslim traders adds to distrust
- Continued reliance on outdated madrasa education

**5. Historical and Structural Factors**
- Communal tensions reappear in places with past history of conflict
- Delays in justice or administrative gaps allow small disputes to grow

**6. Psychological and Social Dimensions**
- Sustained by **social distance and fear**; arises from misunderstanding, lack of communication
- **Karnataka Anti-Conversion Bill (2021):** Christian community protests reflected perception/communication gaps

**7. Role of Media and Social Media**
- Sensationalist coverage inflames tensions
- **Delhi Riots (2020):** Viral videos used to spread anger and misinformation
- Social media **nationalises local conflicts** into pan-India communal narratives

**8. Communal Violence and Civic Ties**
- **Saumitra Jha:** Gujarat's medieval port towns with historic Hindu-Muslim trade ties were **25% less likely to experience riots in 2002**
- **Ashutosh Varshney (2002):** Shared civic associations and neighbourhood networks reduce communal violence

### Instances of Communal Violence

| Event | Year | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Partition Riots** | 1947 | ~15 million displaced; close to 1 million lives lost; trauma of divided homeland |
| **Anti-Sikh Riots** | 1984 | After assassination of PM Indira Gandhi; thousands of Sikhs killed; gurdwaras destroyed |
| **Gujarat Riots** | 2002 | Sabarmati Express attacked at Godhra (59 Hindu pilgrims killed) → retaliatory violence across state |
| **Kandhamal Violence** | 2008, Odisha | After killing of Swami Laxmanananda Saraswati; Maoists claimed responsibility but Christian communities attacked |

### Features of Communal Riots (Probe of Major Riots, Last 5 Decades)
- More **politically motivated than fuelled by religion**
- Besides political interests, **economic interests** play vigorous part
- More common in **North India** than South and East
- **Possibility of recurrence is stronger** in towns where riots have already occurred
- Most take place during **religious festivals**
- Use of **deadly weapons** is on the ascendancy

### Overall Assessment
- India: 1.4 billion people of diverse faiths; **99% coexist harmoniously** every day
- India's civilisational philosophy of tolerance reflected in Vedas, Upanishads, Buddha, Mahavira, Sufis, Bhakti saints
- These incidents are **exceptions rather than the rule**

### Concluding Philosophy
- India: land of rich diversities; "We're all minorities in India"
- **"Acceptance of difference rather than mere tolerance"** — the great Indian secret
- **Rig Veda:** "Ekam Sat Vipra bahudha vadanti" — *"There is only one truth and learned persons call it by many names"*
