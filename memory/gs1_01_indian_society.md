## 1. What is Society?

**Definition:** A society is a group of people living together in a shared territory who interact regularly, follow a common set of rules, share a culture, and depend on one another for survival and progress. It is an **organised and self-sustaining human community**.

**Key idea:** Society is the **web of interactions** created to make life predictable, meaningful, and sustainable.

### Characteristics of Society

- **Similarities** — language, food habits, religious beliefs → ensure smooth interaction, social agreement, harmony, and a **'we-feeling'**
- **Differences** — in skills, occupations, genders → ensure **complementarity and cooperation**
- It is **constantly changing** — shaped by technology, globalisation, migration
- Its core purpose: organise human life into a cohesive, manageable unit

> *Example:* People in a village who know each other, follow the same customs, help each other during floods, and raise children with similar values — that is a society.

## 2. What Kind of Society is India?

- One of the **oldest civilisations** in the world — continuous cultural history of over **5,000 years**
- Home to hundreds of languages, dozens of religions, and thousands of communities with different customs
- **Remarkable complexity**: not just diverse, but capable of holding diversity together
- Empires rose and fell, foreign rulers came and went, colonial powers restructured the economy — yet **India did not lose its cultural identity**
- It **absorbed every influence and made it its own**

## 3. Salient Features of Indian Society

### 3.1 Unity in Diversity

India exemplifies cultural, linguistic, religious, and geographical diversity while remaining a unified nation.

**Statistical Diversity:**

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Languages/Dialects | 19,500+ spoken across India |
| Officially recognised languages | 22 (Eighth Schedule of the Constitution) |
| Mother Tongues (1961 Census) | 1,652 |
| Religions | 6 major: Hinduism, Islam, Christianity, Sikhism, Buddhism, Jainism + Jews, Parsis, Baháʼís, tribal faiths |
| Scheduled Tribes | 705 |
| Caste-based communities | 3,000+ |
| Racial Groups | Caucasoid (North), Dravidian (South), Mongoloid (Northeast) |
| Geographical habitats | Coastal plains, river valleys, mountains, deserts, forests |

**Three Philosophical Ideas Underpinning Unity:**

1. **Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam** (from the *Maha Upanishad*) — "The world is one family." India created space for people to be different rather than forcing uniformity.
2. **Anekantavada** (Jainism) — The many-sidedness of truth; there is no single correct way to see the world.
3. **Sarvadharma Samabhava** (Gandhi) — Equal respect for all religions, brought into modern political life.

**Unity in Practice:**
- A Bihari labourer works in Kerala; a Tamil engineer builds a career in Hyderabad; a Bengali professor teaches in Pune.
- Metropolitan cities like **Mumbai and Delhi** function like "mini-Indias."
- **IPL and Bollywood** work as pan-national unifiers.

#### Cultural Pockets of "Mini India"

Spaces where people from different states, languages, and traditions live and share everyday life:

- **Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru** — South Indian food, North Indian festivals, and Northeast communities in one locality
- **Kolkata** — strong presence of Marwari and Gujarati business communities (migration and cultural mixing)
- **Hyderabad and Pune** — attract students/workers from all over India
- **IT hubs** — people celebrate Diwali, Eid, Christmas, and Pongal together

> People keep their own identity while also becoming part of a larger Indian identity — **unity in diversity**.

#### How Does India Manage Its Diversity? — The Mosaic Model

Sociologists describe three models for how societies handle diversity:
1. **Melting Pot** — assimilation into a single uniform culture
2. **Salad Bowl** — cultures kept completely separate
3. **Mosaic Model** — diversity woven together into a composite national identity

**India follows the Mosaic model** — the most sophisticated of the three — where diversity is neither suppressed nor kept completely separate, but **woven together into a composite national identity**.

#### Diversity and Marginalisation — A Critical View (UPSC 2024)

**Evidence supporting a link between diversity and marginalisation:**
- Many **Adivasi communities** face poverty, poor health, displacement, and low literacy
- Speakers of smaller languages face disadvantages in education and administration
- SCs and STs continue to face higher levels of poverty and exclusion
- Social practices such as early marriage restrict women's opportunities

**Evidence against a direct link:**
- **Parsis and Jains** are minorities but relatively advanced in education and income — minority status does not always mean marginalisation
- Reservation and welfare schemes have helped create a growing **Dalit and tribal middle class**
- In cities, **education, skills, and mobility** often matter more than cultural identity
- Groups like Dalits and OBCs have gained **political influence through democratic mobilisation**

**Key Conclusion:** Diversity itself is *not* the cause of marginalisation. Marginalisation arises from **historical exclusion, social hierarchy, and discrimination**. India must move from mere **diversity** (coexistence) to genuine **pluralism** (empowered coexistence).

### 3.2 Tradition and Modernity Coexist

In most Western societies, modernity was seen as a *replacement* for tradition. **India does not work this way** — tradition and modernity live side by side, often within the same household.

| Traditional | Modern |
|-------------|--------|
| QR-code payments at kirana shops and temple vendors | Modern fintech infrastructure |
| Sarees, kurtas, turbans at offices and colleges | Contemporary fashion |
| Ayurveda and yoga | Allopathic medicine, wellness apps, hospitals |
| Joint-family traditions and arranged marriages | Global careers, love marriages, independent lifestyles |

> This coexistence reflects India's **civilisational capacity to absorb the new without abandoning the old**.

---

### 3.3 The Caste System — Persistent and Evolving

Caste is arguably the **most distinctive feature** of Indian social organisation.

- **Unlike class** (based on economic status), caste is determined by **birth** — one cannot leave it
- Each caste group (**Jati**) is a local, close-knit community with its own customs, traditional occupations, and social rules
- Thousands of Jatis exist across India, varying region to region

**Today:**
- Caste has not disappeared — but it has **changed**
- Economic reforms since **1991** have created class-based stratification alongside caste
- A lower-caste person can, with education and effort, enter the middle class
- Social mobility across castes — especially in **marriage** — remains very difficult

---

### 3.4 Hierarchical Gender Relations (Patriarchy)

Indian society has been, and largely remains, **patriarchal** — where men hold more power in family, society, and public life.

**How patriarchy appears in daily life:**
- **Preference for sons** — still common especially in northern and central India → skewed sex ratios at birth
- **Double burden** — women increasingly work outside the home but still handle most domestic responsibilities (cooking, childcare, elder care)
- In many rural areas, women have limited control over property, mobility, and family decisions

**Change is happening, but uneven** — faster in cities and among educated groups; traditional norms still dominate in rural areas.

---

### 3.5 Balance Between Individualism and Collectivism

Indian society sits at **neither extreme of individualism** (like the West) **nor collectivism** (like traditional tribal societies). It maintains a careful balance.

**Examples of the balance:**
- Career decisions are increasingly individual, but made with family consultation
- Personal finances managed independently, yet people contribute to joint family property and community festivals
- During **COVID-19**, community kitchens, neighbourhood welfare groups, and **Langar service of Gurudwaras** fed millions — the collective instinct reasserting itself in crisis

**Trend:** The balance is **gradually tilting toward individualism** — nuclear families, rising divorce rates, delayed marriages, and the **DINK trend** signal this shift, especially in cities.

---

### 3.6 Spirituality and Materialism — Two Sides of the Same Society

India is known as a **spiritual centre** but is also a **fast-growing consumer market**. These two realities coexist comfortably.

- **Charvaka** focused on material life and enjoyment
- **Vedanta** emphasised spiritual liberation
- Both developed within the same civilisation

**Example:** The **Kumbh Mela** — one of the world's largest spiritual gatherings *and* simultaneously creates enormous economic activity through tourism, transport, trade, and media.

---

### 3.7 Tribal Society — India's Oldest Communities

- **705 Scheduled Tribes** constitute **8.6% of the population**
- Oldest inhabitants of India, with social systems, languages, and spiritual traditions that predate recorded history

**Key Features:**
- **Communal landholding** — land owned collectively by the community
- **Animistic religion** — most tribal groups worship nature, rivers, forests, and spirits
- **Greater gender equality** — Tribal sex ratio is **990 females per 1,000 males** (significantly better than the national average of **943**), reflecting lower patriarchy, absence of dowry, and presence of **bride price customs**

**Challenges:**
- Though only 8.6% of the population, tribals constitute **nearly 40% of all people displaced** by development projects (dams, mines, highways)
- Cultural erosion, forest displacement, and inadequate education remain serious concerns

---

### 3.8 Family System — From Joint to Nuclear

The family is the **basic unit** of Indian society. Traditionally a country of joint families — multi-generational households sharing income and collective decision-making.

**This is changing:**
- **Nuclear families** now make up approximately **53% of households** (Census 2011)
- New family forms: **DINK families** (Dual Income, No Kids), **single-parent households** (~4.5% of all households), **"weekend families"** (couples meet mainly on weekends due to work obligations)

**Yet the joint family has not simply died:**
- Sociologists have introduced the concept of **"Functional Jointness"** — even when families live separately (structurally nuclear), they maintain emotional ties, financial support, and shared responsibilities with the wider kin network
- *The form has changed, but the spirit often persists.*

---

### 3.9 Rural-Urban Divide

India is simultaneously a **rural civilisation** and a **rapidly urbanising society**.

| Rural | Urban |
|-------|-------|
| 65% of India's population (Economic Survey 2022–23) | Growing rapidly |
| Community-centred, agrarian, tradition-bound | More individualistic, mobile, class-based |
| Caste hierarchies more visible and enforced | People judged more by income than community |

**Migration** between villages and cities creates **hybrid identities** — people who are neither fully rural nor fully urban, carrying both worlds within them. This rural-urban interaction is one of the most important forces shaping contemporary Indian society.

---

### 3.10 Syncretic and Dynamic Character

**Syncretism** = the blending of different cultural elements into something new. This is arguably India's **most defining quality**.

Indian culture has never been rigid or closed — over centuries, it has absorbed ideas, languages, art forms, and practices from different civilisations and **transformed them into something uniquely Indian**.

**Everyday examples:**
- **Urdu language** — born from a blend of Arabic, Persian, and local Hindavi dialects; grew organically from cultural contact
- **Rashtrapati Bhavan** — blends European, Rajput, and Mughal architectural styles in a single building
- **Bollywood** — uses Western cinematography/storytelling but tells deeply Indian stories about family, love, and social conflict
- **Yoga** — originated in ancient Indian philosophy, now practised by millions worldwide
- **Daily life** — people wear a kurta with jeans, eat Chinese noodles at a roadside stall, and celebrate Christmas alongside Diwali without contradiction

**Built through:** Trade, migration, the **Bhakti-Sufi movements**, and colonial contact.

> Result: A society that is **always evolving, but never uprooted**.

## 4. Continuity and Change in Indian Society

Indian society is characterised by the **simultaneous presence of continuity and change**. Ancient institutions, beliefs, and practices persist, yet they are constantly being reshaped by democracy, education, urbanisation, technology, and globalisation.

**Nehru's metaphor:** In *The Discovery of India*, Nehru described Indian society as a **palimpsest** — an ancient manuscript that has been written on, erased, and written on again, and yet the old writing never fully disappears. New influences (Islamic, colonial, modern) were layered on top, but the older ones remained visible underneath.

---

### Sources of Continuity

**A. Cultural Practices**
- Festivals such as **Onam, Pongal, Durga Puja, Diwali, and Kumbh Mela** reinforce shared heritage and collective memory
- Life-cycle rituals — **Mundan** (ceremonial first removal of a child's hair), **Saptapadi** (marriage ritual), **Antyesti** (funeral rites) — continue to follow ancient traditions across communities
- **Sanskritisation** — where lower castes adopt upper-caste rituals — as evidence of continuity in ritual life

**B. Philosophical Traditions**
- Concepts of **Dharma** (righteous duty) and **Karma** (moral causation) continue to shape ethical decisions and worldviews
- Practices like pilgrimage, meditation, fasting, and yoga remain widely practised

**C. Social Institutions**
- **Caste endogamy persists**: Around 5–6% of marriages are inter-caste (IHDS 2011–12; Census 2011), implying **over 90–95% of marriages remain within caste**
- **Arranged marriages remain dominant** — over 90% of Indian marriages (BBC, 2018 sample survey)
- Hierarchical family roles, kinship obligations, and instructions from elders continue to influence everyday behaviour

**D. Technology as a Carrier of Continuity**
Rather than erasing tradition, technology often helps preserve and transmit it:
- Temples **livestream** rituals and festivals
- Classical arts like Bharatanatyam, Kathak, and Carnatic music have found new audiences on **YouTube and social media**
- **WhatsApp groups** help maintain intergenerational family bonds across distances

---

### Forces of Change

**A. Constitutional and Democratic Forces**
- **Universal Adult Franchise** empowered marginalised groups politically
- **Articles 14, 15, 17, and 21** challenged hierarchy, discrimination, and untouchability
- **Affirmative action (reservations)** created pathways for upward mobility in education, employment, and politics
- The Constitution can be understood as a **project of social transformation** — replacing hereditary hierarchy with equality, and custom with rights

**B. Economic Transformation**
- Shift from an agrarian to a **service-based economy** created jobs outside caste-linked occupations
- A **new middle class** has emerged, cutting across traditional caste divisions
- In urban and market settings, **income, education, and occupation** increasingly matter more than ritual status

**C. Urbanisation and Migration**
- City life reduces the direct enforcement of **caste-based purity-pollution norms**
- New associations form around occupation, neighbourhood, and shared interests rather than caste or kinship
- Large-scale rural-to-urban migration has increased nuclear families and diversified urban cultures

**D. Education**
- Modern education promotes **scientific temper, rationality, constitutional values, and individual choice**
- For Dalits, Adivasis, and women, education has been the **most important pathway to social mobility**
- Higher female literacy has contributed to declining fertility, greater workforce participation, and shifting gender norms

**E. Technology and Globalisation**
- Internet and smartphones have transformed communication, work, learning, and political participation
- Social media has enabled new forms of mobilisation — **MeToo, #DalitLivesMatter, farmers' protests**
- **Glocalization** is visible in adaptations like McAloo Tikki, Hinglish, and fusion music
- The **gig economy** (Swiggy, Uber, Zomato) has created new livelihoods and reshaped work culture

**F. Social Movements**
- Collective action by marginalised groups has reshaped social norms and institutions
- The **Dalit movement, women's movement, and environmental struggles** have expanded rights and challenged entrenched hierarchies

---

### Selective Adaptation — Real Transformation or Surface Change?

A key debate: Is India **genuinely transforming**, or merely adapting tradition to look modern while preserving its core inequalities?

Evidence suggests **selective adaptation** rather than deep transformation:
- Arranged marriages continue, but now with the language of "compatibility" and "consent" — the form changes, the structure remains
- Caste persists in **matrimonial ads and political mobilisation**, even as people publicly reject caste discrimination
- **Dowry** is illegal but widely practised, often reframed as "gifts"
- Technology is used to **livestream religious rituals** — reinforcing traditional practices through modern means

> Selective adaptation reflects how societies *actually* change: slowly, unevenly, and through internal negotiation. But it also suggests that **formal legal change alone is insufficient**. Real transformation requires shifts in **consciousness**, not just in law.

## 5. Customs and Traditions — A Double-Edged Sword

Every society is held together partly by its customs and traditions. In India, these traditions are extraordinarily rich and diverse — but also **contradictory**. The same customs that bind communities together can also exclude, oppress, and resist progress.

### When Tradition Strengthens Society

| Positive Role | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| **Festivals as social glue** | Diwali, Durga Puja, Onam, Pongal bring communities together across class and caste |
| **Family and kinship** | Joint family traditions promote collective responsibility, emotional support, and intergenerational care |
| **Cultural continuity through ritual** | Marriage ceremonies and funeral rites transmit values from one generation to the next |
| **Living heritage through arts** | Classical and folk arts like Bharatanatyam, Kathak, Madhubani, and Kalamkari continue to evolve, carrying civilisational memory forward |
| **André Béteille's observation** | Community customs, even caste-based ones, can provide a framework of social order and predictability — particularly where formal institutions are weak |

### When Tradition Becomes an Obstacle

| Negative Role | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| **Caste endogamy and occupational inheritance** | Limit social mobility — when profession and marriage partner are decided by birth, merit has little room to operate |
| **Gender oppression** | Practices like dowry, child marriage, and purdah restrict women's freedom and directly violate Articles 14, 15, and 21 of the Constitution |
| **Superstition over science** | Witch-hunting, faith healing, and rejection of modern medicine persist in some communities, especially affecting women and marginalised groups |
| **Honour killings** | NCRB 2020 recorded **77 honour killing cases** — instances where tradition is weaponised to punish individual choice in marriage |
| **LGBTQ+ exclusion** | Many traditional norms stigmatise non-heteronormative identities, even after the Supreme Court's decriminalisation of homosexuality in **2018** |

---

### The Core Tension: Constitutional Morality vs. Social Morality

This is the deeper conflict that tradition's double-edged nature produces. **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar warned of it long ago.**

- **Social morality** — norms upheld by community consensus, often rooted in caste, patriarchy, and religious hierarchy
- **Constitutional morality** — values enshrined in the Constitution: equality, dignity, individual liberty, and non-discrimination

| Constitutional Morality | Social Morality |
|-------------------------|-----------------|
| Equal right to choose one's partner | Caste-based marriage restrictions |
| Dignity of every individual | Caste discrimination in villages |
| Gender equality | Dowry, purdah, child marriage |
| LGBTQ+ rights | Traditional exclusion and stigma |

**Key Insight:**
- Legal reform often fixes the **form** of inequality, but not its **root causes**
- Untouchability was abolished in **1950**; manual scavenging was banned in **1993** — yet both still exist because they are sustained by **social attitudes**, not just law
- The conflict is structural: between the **state and community**, the **individual and collective**, and **written law and social customs**
- Bridging this gap requires not just laws, but **education, political will, and a change in public mindset**

> **Ambedkar:** "Political democracy without social democracy is a contradiction" — and resolving this remains India's **unfinished task**.

### What is Caste?

A social group in India into which a person is born. Members share common customs, follow similar traditions, and marry within their own group. Caste is linked to traditional occupations and forms part of a larger system of social hierarchy, including **varna and jati**.

#### Key Features of the Caste System

1. **Hereditary Identity** — Caste is determined entirely by birth; an **ascriptive identity** fixed for life regardless of personal achievement.
2. **Hierarchy** — Castes are arranged in a fixed ladder from pure to impure through notions of **ritual purity and pollution** linked to occupation, food, and behaviour. This hierarchy, not wealth or power, governed social status.
3. **Endogamy** — Marriage within one's own Jati was a strict social rule; inter-caste marriages were prohibited and often punished. Endogamy remains dominant across much of India.
4. **Occupational Specialisation** — Every Jati was tied to a hereditary occupation. The **Jajmani system** institutionalised this economically within villages. Industrialisation has loosened this in cities, but the caste-occupation link persists in rural India.
5. **Restrictions and Discrimination** — The purity-pollution logic governed everyday interactions — food sharing, temple entry, use of public spaces. **Untouchability was abolished under Article 17**, but its social reality has not fully disappeared.
6. **Internal Governance** — **Caste Panchayats** served as community courts — settling disputes over marriage, conduct, and inter-caste tensions — further entrenching the system's hold over social life.

#### Varna and Jati — Two Distinct Realities

| | Varna | Jati |
|---|-------|------|
| **Nature** | Ancient, textual model | What people actually live by |
| **Scope** | Pan-Indian, ideological framework | Thousands of local communities |
| **Categories** | Four: Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra | Region-specific, varying across India |
| **Reality** | Theoretical blueprint, never ground-level reality | **Real and effective unit** of the caste system |

#### Caste as a Closed System vs. Class

| Caste (Closed System) | Class (Open System) |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Birth determines everything | Individuals can move up/down based on education, income, effort |
| Same occupation across generations | Starting point need not be ending point |
| Marriage within the same group | Merit-based social mobility |
| No amount of individual effort changes caste | Achievement-based identity |

#### Challenges to Caste in Contemporary India

Caste has been significantly challenged by colonial modernity, the independence movement, constitutional law, and urbanisation.

**Articles 14, 15, 16, and 17** abolished untouchability, prohibited caste-based discrimination, and mandated affirmative action through reservations for SCs and STs.

**Forces That Have Weakened Caste:**
1. **Constitutional and Legal Reform** — Article 17 (untouchability abolished), Articles 15 & 16 (discrimination banned), Reservations under 15(4) and 16(4), Universal Adult Franchise giving lower-caste communities real political power
2. **Urbanisation** — Cities offer anonymity; shared spaces (metros, offices, restaurants) make strict caste rules difficult; new social bonds form around profession, neighbourhood, and interest
3. **Education and Economic Mobility** — Post-1991 liberalisation created occupational opportunities not tied to caste; growing Dalit and OBC middle class
4. **Social Processes** — **Sanskritisation** (lower castes adopting upper-caste rituals) has weakened rigid boundaries; **Westernisation** (rational outlook, English education, urban lifestyles) has loosened caste rigidity
5. **Rising Inter-Caste Marriages** — Slow but directionally significant shift; Supreme Court (2018, *Shafin Jahan case*) reaffirmed the right to choose one's partner is fundamental under Article 21
6. **Anti-Caste Social Movements** — Dalit and backward-caste movements asserting dignity, demanding rights, and mobilising communities for social justice

### Fight Against Untouchability — Gandhi vs. Ambedkar

| Dimension | Mahatma Gandhi | Dr. B.R. Ambedkar |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Basic Approach** | Moral reform, change of heart, non-violence | Structural reform, legal safeguards, institutional change |
| **On Varna** | Accepted a reinterpreted, non-hierarchical varna; opposed untouchability | Insisted both caste AND varna must be dismantled |
| **Methods** | Social harmony, constructive work, temple entry for "Harijans" | Rights, legal protections, political power, modern education |
| **Terminology** | "Harijan" (children of God) | "Dalit" — emphasising oppression, resistance, and self-respect |
| **Political Position** | Opposed separate electorates — prioritised national unity (Poona Pact) | Demanded separate electorates for autonomous political voice |
| **Religious Position** | Worked within Hinduism — hoped to reform it from inside | Rejected Hinduism (*Annihilation of Caste*, 1936) — converted to Buddhism (1956) |

#### Historical Phases of Dalit Assertion

**I. The Colonial Foundation**
- **Jyotiba & Savitribai Phule** — Founded **Satyashodhak Samaj (1873)**; opened the first schools for untouchables and women, directly challenging Brahminical control over education
- **Dr. B.R. Ambedkar** — Led the **Mahad Satyagraha (1927)** for water rights; secured political representation through the **Poona Pact**; drafted the Indian Constitution; ended his journey with a **mass conversion to Buddhism (1956)**
- **Periyar (E.V. Ramasamy)** — Launched the **Self-Respect Movement** in South India, using rationalism and anti-caste rhetoric to challenge North Indian cultural dominance

**II. Post-Independence Radicalism**
- **Namdeo Dhasal & J.V. Pawar (Dalit Panthers)** — Inspired by the US Black Panthers, used radical poetry and street protest in the 1970s to fight police brutality and rural violence
- **Kanshi Ram** — Shifted focus from "protest" to "power"; founded **BAMCEF** (for employees) and **BSP**; taught Dalits they are the "rulers" (Bahujans), not just victims
- **Mayawati** — Head of BSP; became Chief Minister of UP four times; symbol of "Dalit Pride," proving a Dalit woman could lead India's political heartland

### For "Customs and Traditions Suppress Reason" (2020 PYQ)
- **AGREE**: dowry (illegal but practised), honour killings (NCRB 2020: 77 cases), witch-hunting, child marriage, LGBTQ+ exclusion
- **DISAGREE**: festivals as social glue, family systems as welfare, classical arts as living heritage, Béteille's point on order and predictability
- **SYNTHESIS**: Ambedkar's distinction — constitutional morality must prevail over social morality; tradition must be reformed, not abandoned

- Hyderabad and Pune: education and work migration
- IT hubs: inter-religious festival celebrations
- Conclusion: people keep own identity while becoming part of a larger Indian identity → unity in diversity

## 12. Quick Revision: Concluding Synthesis

Indian society reflects a **dynamic balance between continuity and change**. Ancient traditions, social institutions, and civilisational values continue to shape everyday life, even as democracy, education, markets, technology, and globalisation transform them.

The most appropriate way to understand Indian society is not as a conflict between past and present, but as an **ongoing negotiation between tradition and modernity**. It is:
- Ancient yet modern
- Unified yet diverse
- Hierarchical yet aspirationally egalitarian

What holds it all together is not a single religion, language, or race — but a **civilisational capacity for accommodation and assimilation**. India's diversity is not a problem to be solved. It is, as the Constitution recognised, **the very foundation of the nation**.
