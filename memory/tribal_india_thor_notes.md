## SECTION 1: Occupational (Economic) Classifications of Tribes

### Overview
- Economic life of tribes varies from **gathering to settled agriculture**
- Each tribe has a **distinctive economy** but also practices many subsidiary occupations
- Six major economic types found in Indian tribes:
  1. Hunting and gathering
  2. Pastoralism
  3. Shifting cultivation
  4. Agriculture
  5. Tribal artisans
  6. Agriculture and industrial labour

---

### 1. Hunting and Gathering

**Definition:**
- **Foraging** = searching for and collecting food available in nature (fruits, vegetables, nuts, animals, fish) by gathering, fishing, or hunting
- Oldest economic system — has existed since appearance of Homo sapiens

**Global Context:**
- Food gatherers form about **0.004% of world population**
- Found in marginal areas: frozen Arctic tundra, deserts, dense tropical forests
- In India: mainly in **south India and Andaman Islands**

**Characteristics of Hunting and Gathering Economy:**
| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Simple technology | Few tools; digging stick, collection basket, bows and arrows, spears, boomerangs |
| Nomadism | Characterized by nomadism and semi-nomadism |
| Low population density | Total tribe rarely exceeds a few thousand; bands of 25–30 individuals |
| Small, self-sufficient unit | Small nomadic band or semi-nomadic settlement |
| Division of labour | Men specialize in hunting; women specialize in food collection |
| No surplus | No surplus and no trade |

**Scholar Quote:**
- **Marshall Sahlins** termed hunting-gathering societies the "**original affluent society**" — affluence defined not by material accumulation but by **satisfaction through balance with nature**

**Sustainability of Hunting-Gathering:**
- Limited extraction and seasonal rotation of resource zones
- Ritual taboos on hunting during breeding seasons
- Collective ownership and sharing norms prevent hoarding
- Deep ecological knowledge transmitted through oral traditions, songs, and myths

#### Case Studies — Hunting and Gathering Tribes

**(a) Chenchu (Andhra Pradesh)**
- Live in **Nallamala Hills** of Andhra Pradesh and Telangana
- Traditionally dependent on **forest foods, honey, roots, and small game**
- Follow seasonal foraging cycles based on rainfall and fruiting patterns
- Deep ecological knowledge of flora and fauna
- **Customs:** Hunting restrictions during breeding seasons; collective sharing of meat and honey; belief in forest spirits

**(b) Yanadi (Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu)**
- Inhabit areas around **Nellore and Chittoor**, near lakes and estuaries
- Skilled fisherfolk and crab catchers; use **bamboo traps and hand-woven nets**
- Men fish and hunt small animals; women gather wild fruits, roots, medicinal herbs
- Remarkable knowledge of **tides, fish migration, and wetland ecology**

**(c) Onge (Little Andaman)**
- A **negrito tribe** practicing hunting, gathering, and fishing in coastal and forest zones
- Diet: fish, turtle, pig, honey, and yams
- Small band-level organisation; **egalitarian sharing norms** prevent accumulation and overexploitation
- Ritual restrictions forbid hunting pregnant animals or overharvesting yams

**(d) Jarawa (Andaman Islands)**
- Traditionally hunter-gatherers along coastal and forest belts
- Depend on **wild pigs, fish, honey, tubers, and fruits**
- Excellent navigational and bow-hunting skills
- **Custom:** Group sharing and ritual purification before hunting trips; symbolic reciprocity reinforces social cohesion

**(e) Kadar (Kerala)**
- Inhabit **Anamalai and Nelliampathy hills**
- Skilled collectors of **honey, wax, and forest produce**; known for knowledge of elephant trails and herbs
- Often acted as forest guides and elephant catchers for the state
- Subsistence combines **foraging and seasonal wage work** — reflecting adaptation to ecological and market changes

**Fishing as Subsistence Activity:**
- Fishing likely developed parallel to hunting
- Groups specialized in catching fish, crustaceans, and mollusks using: nets, traps, harpoons, canoes
- Fishing becomes the mainstay when land resources decline
- Example: **Yanadi** near Pulicat Lake and **Onge** along coastal reefs rely extensively on aquatic resources

**Conclusion (Hunting-Gathering):**
Tribes like Chenchu, Yanadi, Onge, Jarawa, and Kadar demonstrate intimate harmony with ecosystems. Their customs, taboos, and social structures ensure resource renewal and community equality. Even as modernisation and forest restrictions have forced many to abandon these livelihoods, their **ethos of respect for nature and minimal exploitation** remains an enduring lesson in sustainable living.

### 2. Pastoralism

**Definition:**
- Transitional phase between food collection and food production
- Subsistence technology by **domestication of animals**
- A type of ecological adaptation found in **areas not suitable for hunting-fishing or agricultural activity**
- Pastoralists move herds in search of new grazing land and water
- Exchange products with neighbouring agriculturalists

**Features of Pastoral Economy:**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Small population | Move from place to place in search of food for domesticated animals |
| Domestication of animals | Only occupation where rainfall is not suitable for agriculture |
| Semi-nomadism | Constant movement in search of grazing |
| Transhumance | Part of population moves seasonally with herds; others remain at home |

**Examples:**
- **Todas** rear buffaloes (Nilgiris)
- **Gujjar** rear sheep
- **Tschembago** rear pigs
- **Bhotias** — Uttarakhand

#### Case Study: The Nilgiri Hills — Traditional Interdependence

**1. Ethnographic Context:**
The Nilgiri Hills of Tamil Nadu are home to a cluster of tribes — **Toda, Kota, Kurumba, Irula** — and the non-tribal agricultural group **Badaga**, each occupying a specialised ecological and occupational niche.

**2. Economic and Social Interdependence:**

| Group | Role |
|---|---|
| Toda | Pastoralists herding the sacred buffalo; produce milk, curd, and ghee; depend on others for grains and craft goods |
| Kota | Artisans and blacksmiths; produce pottery, metal tools, carpentry items; serve as ritual musicians at Toda ceremonies |
| Irula | Hunters and gatherers; skilled in trapping small game, snake catching, pest control; supply forest produce to Badagas |
| Badaga | Agriculturalists; cultivate grains and vegetables; exchange food with Todas; hire Irulas and Kurumbas as labourers |
| Kurumba | Forest collectors and medicine-men; gather honey, wax, roots, herbs; perform shamanic healing for other groups |

**3. Mechanisms of Exchange:**
- **Barter:** Dairy products from Todas traded for Badaga grains or Kota crafts
- **Ritual Ties:** Toda ceremonies require Kota musicians and Kurumba priests
- **Reciprocity and Respect:** Interdependence governed by mutual obligations, not market pricing

**4. Cultural and Ecological Balance:**
- **Sacred dairies** managed by Toda priests — only ritually pure men may enter
- Buffalo milk used in offerings — symbolises link between humans, animals, and gods
- Kota craft and Kurumba healing performed with ritual restrictions that prevent resource overuse

**5. Changes in Nilgiri Interrelationships:**
- **Monetisation:** Tea and vegetable plantations, roads, and tourism have converted barter into cash-based transactions
- **Occupational Shifts:** Kotas to modern carpentry; Kurumbas and Irulas to plantation labour; Todas supply milk to cooperatives like Aavin instead of ritual barter
- **Cultural Revival:** NGOs and anthropologists promote revival of Toda embroidery and Kota pottery

**6. Sustainability and Anthropological Significance:**
- Nilgiri model = **self-regulating, multi-ethnic economy** where each group's livelihood complements others
- Traditional customs ensured: Resource diversity, Reciprocity, Ecological restraint
- Cultural memory of interdependence continues to inspire collective identity and environmental respect

### 3. Shifting Cultivation

**Definition:**
- Primary source of subsistence for many tribal communities
- Distinct type of agricultural practice generally practiced on **hill slopes**
- Dates back to **Neolithic times** (8,000–10,000 years ago) — **Nadeem Hasnain**
- Resulted in transition from **nomadic way of life to settled way of life**
- Also known as **Jhum cultivation** (Northeast India), **Bewar** (Baiga), **Podu**, **Kumri**, etc.
- Also prevalent in: Sumatra, North Burma, Borneo, New Guinea, Africa

**Process:**
1. Select a plot; cut plants and trees
2. Dry and burn to ashes
3. Till soil with digging stick or hoe
4. Sow seeds of different crops at onset of monsoon
5. Harvest in winter
6. Leave plot to fallow; move to new plot

**Tribes practicing shifting cultivation:**
- Gonds, Baiga, Kukis, Maler

**Ecological Significance:**
- Fallow regeneration ensures soil restoration and forest regrowth
- Mixed cropping increases resilience to climatic shocks
- Land often community-owned, reducing overexploitation
- Rituals before clearing and post-harvest ceremonies express gratitude to nature
- Beliefs in forest spirits and ancestral deities restrain overuse of land

**Changes and Challenges:**
- Government restrictions on burning and forest access shorten fallow cycles, reducing fertility
- Monocropping and cash crop introduction disturb ecological balance
- Sedentarisation schemes ignore socio-ecological wisdom of tribal shifting cultivators
- FAO (2019): traditional shifting cultivation **can be sustainable**, provided customary laws and long fallows are maintained

#### Case Studies — Shifting Cultivation

**(a) Maler Tribe (Rajmahal Hills, Jharkhand)**
- **Maler (Paharia)** practice small-scale, rotational shifting cultivation on forested hill slopes
- Crops: millets, pulses, maize, cucurbits, roots
- **Customs:** Ritual offering to local deities before cutting vegetation; community rules on fire control and timing; taboos on clearing near springs and sacred groves
- **Sustainability:** Short cultivation + long fallow maintains soil fertility, moisture, and biodiversity
- ICAR and Anthropological Survey of India show traditional Maler *jhum* is more ecologically stable than commercial monocropping — preserves carbon, prevents erosion

**(b) Baiga Tribe (Maikal Hills, Madhya Pradesh and Chhattisgarh)**
- Practice known as **Bewar cultivation** — burning dry vegetation and sowing mixed crops on hill slopes
- Governed by **customary laws** enforced by village elders and clan heads
- **Customs and Taboos:**
  - Felling of fruit-bearing trees (mahua, mango, tamarind) strictly prohibited
  - Sacred groves preserved for ritual and biodiversity purposes
  - Collective rules regulate rotation of cultivation patches
- **Ecological Significance:**
  - Maintains a **forest-fallow mosaic** supporting pollinators and wildlife
  - Ash acts as natural fertiliser; minimal tillage prevents soil compaction
  - Reflects **Nature-Man-Spirit Complex** (Vidyarthi, 1963) — economic activity, ecology, and belief form an integrated system

**Sustainability Dimensions of Shifting Cultivation:**

| Dimension | Key Point |
|---|---|
| Ecological Balance | Fallow regeneration; soil restoration; forest regrowth; mixed cropping |
| Social Regulation | Community-owned land; collective decision-making; prevents conflict |
| Cultural Integration | Rituals before clearing; post-harvest ceremonies; gratitude to nature |
| Gender and Labour | Men clear and burn; women sow and weed — balanced gender division |

### 4. Agriculture (Settled)

**Definition:**
- Involves: ploughing, bundling, draught animals, fertilisation, irrigation, weeding, land parcels, crop rotation
- Enables **permanent cultivation of fields**
- Unit of production and consumption = the **family**
- Surplus production exchanged for other goods and services

**Features of Settled Agriculture:**

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Settled life | Permanent rural and urban settlement |
| High population density | Higher than other economic types |
| Complex economic resources | Land, tools, markets |
| Complex technology | Ploughing, irrigation |
| Division of labour | Based on age and sex |
| Land ownership | Private/hereditary |
| Full-time political leadership | Village headman, councils |

**Examples:** Santhals, Mundas, Oraons, Gonds, Baiga

#### Case Studies — Agriculture

**(a) Santhals (Jharkhand, West Bengal, Odisha, Bihar)**

- One of the **largest tribal groups** in India
- Found in **Chotanagpur Plateau** — rolling uplands and forested plains suitable for rain-fed agriculture
- **Agricultural System:**
  - Practice **settled plough cultivation**, mainly depending on monsoon rains
  - Main crops: rice, millets, pulses, maize, oilseeds
  - Lower slopes: paddy using animal-drawn ploughs; upland fields: millets and pulses
  - Use manure, crop rotation, and fallow systems
  - **Family as basic unit** of production; both men and women participate
- **Land and Labour:**
  - Land ownership: hereditary and patrilineal
  - Labour: family-based; **reciprocal labour exchange (*parma*)** is common
  - **Village headman (Manjhi)** oversees agricultural arrangements and resource distribution
- **Cultural Integration:**
  - Agriculture interwoven with religious life
  - Rituals performed before sowing and after harvesting — thank their deities, especially **Marang Buru (the Great Mountain Spirit)**
  - Festivals: **Sohrai** (cattle, fertility) and **Baha** (agricultural cycle)
- **Contemporary Trends:**
  - Adopted hybrid seeds, fertilisers, and irrigation
  - **N.K. Bose** noted Santhal agriculture represents a bridge between subsistence economy and market participation

**(b) Apatani Rice-Fish Cultivation System (Arunachal Pradesh)**

- **Apatani tribe** lives in **Ziro Valley of Lower Subansiri District**, Arunachal Pradesh
- Valley is a **high-altitude wetland system** surrounded by pine-covered hills
- **Farming System:**
  - Known for unique **integrated rice-fish farming system** — one of the most sophisticated indigenous agricultural practices in Northeast India
  - Cultivate **rice and fish together in the same wet field** — sustainable and self-fertilising
- **Technique and Process:**
  - Paddy fields terraced and carefully levelled with bamboo irrigation channels
  - Water flow regulated using wooden sluices
  - Fish introduced after rice transplantation: **common carp (Cyprinus carpio)**
  - Fish feed on insects and weeds (natural pest control); their waste adds nutrients (natural fertiliser)
- **Ecological and Social Benefits:**
  - High productivity with minimal environmental damage
  - Promotes nutrient recycling, pest control, and soil conservation
  - Community collectively manages irrigation — strong **communal institutions** and social cooperation
- **Recognition:**
  - FAO recognised under **Globally Important Agricultural Heritage Systems (GIAHS)** for indigenous knowledge and ecological sustainability

**Comparison — Santhals vs. Apatanis:**
- Santhals: transition from subsistence to settled plough-based agriculture
- Apatanis: advanced, ecologically harmonious system of integrated rice-fish farming
- Both reflect tribal agricultural systems combining **traditional ecological knowledge** with community cooperation

### 5. Tribal Artisans

- Some tribal groups take up **handicrafts as a subsidiary occupation** — combining traditional skill with economic need
- These crafts provide additional income and **preserve rich tribal art forms**

**Major Crafts:**

| Craft | Tribes |
|---|---|
| Basket Making (Bamboo) | Bhils, Santhals, Nagas |
| Tool Making (Iron and Wood) | Agarias, Lohars |
| Spinning and Weaving (Cotton and Wool) | Gonds, Kotas, Apatanis |
| Pottery Making | Kumbhars, Santhals |

**TRIFED (Tribal Cooperative Marketing Development Federation of India):**
- Established in **1987** under **Ministry of Tribal Affairs**
- Promotes and markets tribal handicrafts through **TRIBES India** outlets and online platforms
- Supports artisans through training, design intervention, skill development, and fair pricing
- Implements: **Van Dhan Yojana** and **MSP for MFP (Minimum Support Price for Minor Forest Produce)**

#### Case Studies — Artisan Tribes

**(a) Asur Tribe (Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Bihar)**
- One of the **Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)**
- Found mainly in **Gumla and Latehar districts of Jharkhand**; also Chhattisgarh and Bihar
- One of the **earliest iron-smelting communities** in India — ore from local hills (Netarhat Plateau)
- **Traditional Occupation:** Iron smelting and blacksmithing using **primitive bloomery furnace technique**
  - Small clay furnaces; bellows made from animal skin; charcoal from local wood
  - Ore (mainly lateritic iron) heated for hours; produced "blooms of iron" hammered into sickles, ploughshares, knives, arrowheads
  - This represents an **indigenous metallurgical knowledge system**
- **Decline:** Post-Independence, traditional iron smelting declined due to industrial steel plants (Tata Steel, Bokaro) and Forest Department restriction on woodcutting
- **Cultural Significance:** Asurs consider iron sacred; mythical ancestor *Asura Raja*; festivals **Sarhul and Karma** include rituals invoking the *Iron God*
- Today: Most Asurs work as agricultural labourers or small artisans; **some families continue blacksmithing** as cultural and symbolic occupation

**(b) Agaria Tribe (Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand)**
- **Sub-group of the Gonds** traditionally engaged in iron smelting and tool-making
- Inhabit regions: Sidhi, Shahdol, Mandla (M.P.), Sonbhadra (U.P.), Bilaspur (Chhattisgarh)
- Name derives from *agar* meaning **furnace** in local dialects
- **Traditional Iron Smelting:**
  - Used locally available iron ore; **pit furnaces** lined with clay and stone
  - Hand-operated bamboo bellows; charcoal as fuel
  - Molten iron hammered into agricultural implements (hoes, ploughs, sickles), weapons, and ornaments
  - Smelting villages formed **industrial clusters** — early form of division of labour
- **Current Scenario:** Shifted to agriculture, daily labour, or petty trade; some continue blacksmithing and metalwork sold through TRIFED and Van Dhan outlets

---

### 6. Agriculture and Industrial Labourers

- A significant section of tribal population has shifted to **agricultural and industrial labour** due to historical and economic pressures
- Many tribes have become **landless labourers**, working on others' land for wages in cash, kind, or crop share
- **Colonial policies** during the British period led to large-scale **land alienation** and forced displacement
- **Examples:** Santhals and Oraons of eastern India lost traditional land and now depend on wage labour
- Some tribes engage in **seasonal migration** — moving to nearby regions for manual or industrial work during lean agricultural seasons

## SECTION 2: Linguistic Classification of Tribes

### Overview
India has over **700 tribal groups**, each possessing its own distinct language or dialect. Tribal languages belong to four main linguistic families:
1. Austro-Asiatic Family
2. Tibeto-Chinese (Sino-Tibetan) Family
3. Dravidian Family
4. Indo-European Family

---

### 1. Austro-Asiatic Family

**Global Scope:**
- One of the **largest language families** in the world — over **150 languages** spoken by over **100 million people** across Southeast Asia and parts of South Asia
- In India: primarily in **eastern and central regions**

**Origin Controversy:**
- **W. Schmidt** named the Munda language as Austro-Asiatic
- In 1928, **Heine Geldern** suggested Mongoloid people entered India from the north-eastern side and brought Munda languages which later spread to middle India
- **Hutton** opines that Austro-Asiatic was brought by the Kolarians through the west end of Himalayas and by Mon Khmer-speaking people from the east of the Himalayas

**Influence on Other Languages:**
- **Suniti Kumar Chatterji** observed certain nouns in Hindi, Punjabi, and Bengali have been borrowed from the Munda languages

**Two Main Branches in India:**

**(a) Mon-Khmer Branch:**
- Languages spoken in Southeast Asia, Northeast India, and Nicobar Islands
- Examples:
  - **Khasi** — primarily in Meghalaya
  - **Nicobari** — spoken in Nicobar Islands (Bay of Bengal)

**(b) Munda Branch:**
- Languages spoken primarily in eastern and central India
- Examples: **Santhali, Kherwari, Ho, Mundari, Gondi, Kharia, Savara, Khond, Gadaba**
- **Santhali** = one of the largest languages — spoken in Jharkhand, West Bengal, Odisha, and Bihar

**Unique Characteristics:**
- System of phonetic tones distinguishes it from other language families

---

### 2. Tibeto-Chinese (Sino-Tibetan) Family

- A subfamily of the larger **Sino-Tibetan language family** — includes languages of China, India, and Southeast Asia
- Divided into two subfamilies: Siamese-Chinese and Tibeto-Burman

**(a) Siamese-Chinese Sub-family:**
- Languages of Southeast Asia and southern China
- **Tai group:** Khampti, Phakial, and other languages

  - **Tai-Khampti:** Spoken by Tai-Khampti tribe — major tribe of Arunachal Pradesh; primarily inhabit district of **Namsai**; name Khampti means **"a land full of gold"**; strong believers of **Theravada (Hinayana) Buddhism**

  - **Tai Phake:** Small tribe of Assam (about 2,000 people); also known as **Phakial**; migrated to Assam from Thailand in the **late 18th century A.D.**

**(b) Tibeto-Burman Sub-family:**
- Languages of Himalayan region and northeastern India
- Further divided into four branches:

  1. **Tibeto-Himalayan Branch:** Languages like Bhotia (spoken in Darjeeling); western sub-groups: Chamba, Lahauli, Swangli, Kanauri
  2. **Non-Pronominalized Himalayan Group:** Languages like **Rong or Lepcha** and **Toto** (West Bengal)
  3. **Arunachal Branch:** Aka or Hrusso, Abor, Miri, and Dafla
  4. **Assam-Burmese Branch:** Languages of northeastern India and Myanmar:
     - **Bara or Bodo group:** Kachari, Dimasa or Hill Kachari, Garo, Tripura
     - **Naga group:** Angami, Ao, Sema, Rengma; Naga-Bodo: Kachcha Naga, Kabui Naga
     - **Kachin group:** Singpho language
     - **Kuki-Chin group:** Manipuri, Thado, Sokte, Ralte, Lushai

---

### 3. Dravidian Family

**Overview:**
- Used by **250 million individuals** — predominantly residing in **southern India, south-west Pakistan, and north-east Sri Lanka**
- In 1856, **Robert Caldwell** published *Comparative Grammar of the Dravidian or South-Indian Family of Languages* — established Dravidian as one of the major language families of the world
- Earliest evidence: **2nd century BCE** — Tamil-Brahmi script on cave walls in Tamil Nadu's Madurai and Tirunelveli districts

**Widely Spoken Dravidian Languages (by % speakers):**

| Language | % |
|---|---|
| Telugu | 34.5% |
| Tamil | 29.0% |
| Kannada | 15.4% |
| Malayalam | 14.4% |
| Gondi | 1.2% |
| Brahui | 0.9% |
| Kurukh | 0.8% |
| Tulu | 0.7% |
| Beary | 0.7% |
| Others | 2.3% |

- **Tamil numerals** influenced and shaped numerals of other languages in the family

**Dravidian-Speaking Scheduled Tribes:**
- **Kurukh** (Eastern India) and **Gondi** (Central India)

**Languages Outside India:**
- **Brahui** (Balochistan, Pakistan)
- **Dhangar** — dialect of Kurukh (Nepal, Bhutan, Bangladesh)
- Tamil in north-eastern Sri Lanka

**Scholars:**
- **Krishnamurti (2003):** Dravidian languages trace back at least **4,000 years** — predating Indo-Aryan expansion

---

### 4. Indo-European Family

**Origin:**
- Proto-Indo-European homeland: **Pontic-Caspian Steppe of Central Asia** (modern-day Ukraine, southern Russia, Kazakhstan) around **4000-3000 BCE**
- **Kurgan Hypothesis** proposed by **Marija Gimbutas**

**Expansion:**
- Westward into Europe: Celtic, Italic (Latin → Romance), Germanic, Hellenic, Balto-Slavic
- South-east into South Asia (Indo-Iranian branch):
  - **Indo-Aryan:** Sanskrit (Vedic, c. 1500 BCE) → Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati, Assamese, Odia
  - **Iranian:** Old Persian, Avestan → Persian, Pashto

**Tribal Examples within Indo-European:**

- **Hajong (Eastern Indo-Aryan branch):**
  - Found in Meghalaya, Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, and northern Bangladesh
  - Speak an Indo-Aryan dialect heavily influenced by Tibeto-Burman languages
  - Follow mixed Hindu and animistic practices; festivals: *Chorkhela* and *Pusne*

- **Bhili (Western Indo-Aryan branch):**
  - Bhils of Rajasthan, M.P., Gujarat, and Maharashtra speak Bhili dialects
  - Show **Dravidian and Munda influences** — revealing centuries of contact with neighbouring tribal and peasant societies

**Link with Glottochronology:**
- **Glottochronology**, proposed by **Morris Swadesh (1950s)** — estimates when languages diverged based on rate of loss of shared core vocabulary
- Indo-Iranian split from Proto-Indo-European: c. **2000–1800 BCE**
- Indo-Aryan migration into India: c. **1500 BCE** (inferred from shared vocabulary between Vedic Sanskrit and Avestan)
- **Colin Masica (1991):** Indo-Aryan languages eventually became the majority tongue of the subcontinent

**Linguistic Origins of Tribal Languages — Summary:**

| Language Family | Tribes | Location | Antiquity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dravidian | Gondi, Kurukh, Kui | Central and South India | At least 4,000 years (Krishnamurti, 2003) |
| Austro-Asiatic (Munda) | Santhali, Mundari, Kharia | Eastern and Central India | One of earliest in South Asia (Anderson, 2008) |
| Tibeto-Burman | Bodo, Naga, Lepcha | Northeast India | Affinities with East and Southeast Asia |
| Indo-Aryan Tribal | Hajong, Bhili | Northeast and West India | Shows layers of contact and assimilation |

**Significance:**
- **Abbi (2017):** Survival of Austro-Asiatic and Tibeto-Burman languages demonstrates **deep antiquity and diversity of tribal linguistic heritage**
- Tribal languages often preserve **older strata of India's linguistic history** compared to later Indo-Aryan dominance

### Endangered Languages

**Data:**
- India is home to over **19,500 languages or dialects**
- India has the **second-largest number of endangered languages** in the world
- **UNESCO** lists **197 languages as endangered** including **42 critically endangered**
- 1961 Census: **1,652 languages** recorded; People's Linguistic Survey of India (PLSI, 2010): only **780** documented

**Facts:**
- India has 68 living scripts; newspapers in 35 languages; All India Radio (AIR) broadcasts in 120 languages
- Hindi (40%) is most spoken; Bengali (8%), Telugu (7.1%), Marathi (6.9%), Tamil (5.9%)
- Only **4% of India's languages** are represented in Parliament

**Linguistic diversity highest in:** Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Odisha, West Bengal, Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan

**Critically Endangered Examples:**

| Language | Tribe | Location | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Majhi | — | Sikkim | Only 4 speakers — one family |
| Birhor | Birhor | Central India | Critically endangered — 2,000 speakers |
| Asur | Asur | Jharkhand | UNESCO endangered list |
| Korwa | Korwa | Jharkhand | UNESCO endangered list |
| Mahali | — | Eastern India | Endangered |
| Koro | — | Arunachal Pradesh | Endangered |
| Sidi | — | Gujarat | Endangered |
| Dimasa | — | Assam | Endangered |

**Reasons for Language Endangerment:**
- Increasing dominance of major regional languages (Hindi, Bengali, Tamil) in government and education
- Loss of traditional knowledge among speakers
- Younger generations migrate to urban areas and adopt modern ways of life
- Loss of intergenerational transmission

**Government Initiatives:**

1. **Central Institute of Indian Languages (CIIL)** — established 1969, Mysore
2. **People's Linguistic Survey of India (PLSI)** — created 2010
3. **Scheme for Protection and Preservation of Endangered Languages of India (SPPEL):**
   - CIIL, Mysore works on protection, preservation, and documentation of mother tongues spoken by less than **10,000 people**
   - Phase 1: **117 endangered languages** chosen for study and documentation on priority basis
4. **Multilingual Education (MLE) Programme (Odisha, 2006):**
   - Addresses language barriers faced by tribal children
   - Odisha: 3,385 tribal language teachers appointed
   - Dictionaries published for 20 tribal languages
5. **Mother Tongue Based Multilingual Education (MTBMLE):**
   - **KISS (Kalinga Institute of Social Sciences)** in Odisha — teaches and validates 10 tribal dialects
   - Bridges gap between home and school languages
6. **In 2018:** Odisha proposed inclusion of **Ho, Mundari, and Bhumji** in the Eighth Schedule of the Constitution

**Strategies:**
- Documentation and archiving of languages
- Promoting bilingual education and language revitalisation programmes
- Recognising rights of linguistic minorities
- **Community radios:** Asur Tribe in Jharkhand uses community radios to play music, recite poetry, and communicate in native language — helping revitalise the Asur language

## SECTION 3: Problems of Tribal Communities (Unit 6.2)

### 6.2.1 Land Alienation

**Definition:**
Land alienation = the process by which tribals lose ownership or access to their traditional land.

**Significance:**
- Land is not merely an economic resource — it is the basis of **social identity, cultural continuity, and ecological survival**
- **L.P. Vidyarthi:** Land alienation is the root cause of all forms of tribal unrest and discontent
- **Dhebar Commission:** The loss of land is the loss of livelihood, dignity, and identity

**Nature of the Problem:**
- Agriculture has historically been the primary occupation
- Land alienation leads to **economic insecurity, cultural erosion, and social disintegration**
- Forces tribals into wage labour or migration
- In Koraput district (Odisha), tribal landholdings fell by **over 50% between 1970–1990** (Xaxa, 2001)

#### Major Causes of Land Alienation

1. **Chronic Shortage of Cash:**
   - Low income forces tribals to borrow at exploitative terms
   - Example: Korwa Tribe (M.P.) — exploited by moneylenders who offer loans against land mortgage

2. **Inadequate Agricultural Yield:**
   - Traditional tools, lack of irrigation, small landholdings, infertile soils → subsistence-level productivity

3. **Chronic Indebtedness:**
   - Local moneylenders exploit illiterate tribals, taking land as mortgage
   - **Tharu and Bhoksa Tribes (U.P.):** Post-Partition, Punjab refugee moneylenders exploited local tribals by offering loans and later grabbing their lands through fraudulent contracts

4. **Ill Experience with State Credit Systems:**
   - Government credit institutions involve complex formalities and delays
   - Tribals return to informal lenders

5. **Mining and Industrial Projects:**
   - Example: **261 Baiga families** displaced by **Vedanta's BALCO bauxite mining** in Chhattisgarh
   - **Niyamgiri Hills (Odisha):** Dongria Kondhs resisted Vedanta's mining plans

   **Karanpura Coalfield (Hazaribagh, Ramgarh, Ranchi, Latehar, Chatra):**
   - 83 identified coal blocks across 203 villages; 1,420 sq km; 425 sq km forest area
   - Total land acquisition in Jharkhand exceeds **75,000 ha**
   - Activists: **Mithilesh Dangi (Azadi Bachao Andolan)** warns entire Barkagaon block will vanish

6. **Infrastructure Projects (Dams):**
   - **Narmada Valley Project:** On 12 December 1979, government decided to raise Sardar Sarovar Dam; 30 major, 135 medium, 3000 small dams; **200,000 people displaced**, majority Adivasis
   - **Narmada Bachao Andolan (1985 onwards):** Mass protests against dam displacement

7. **National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, Tiger Reserves:**
   - **Kanha Tiger Reserve (2014):** About 450 Baiga and Gond families evicted
   - **Kuno National Park:** Asmita Kabra (2009) — 80% of displaced Saharias lived below poverty line after losing forest produce access
   - **Assam (2017):** 1,000 people from Bodo, Rabha, and Mishing tribes evicted from Orang National Park
   - In contrast: **Soliga tribes of Biligirirangana Hills were allowed to stay** — resulted in increase in tiger population, proving co-existence models work

8. **Forest Policies:**
   - **Indian Forest Act, 1927** removed community ownership, criminalised traditional livelihood practices

9. **Lack of Implementation of Laws:**
   - Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 — poor implementation has nullified impact

#### Other Modes of Land Alienation
1. **Utilising the Machinery of Court:** Moneylenders drag defaulting tribals to court; legal ignorance ensures loss of land
2. **Temptation of Immediate Gains:** Tribals lured into selling land for short-term cash benefits
3. **Voluntary Surrender:** Engineered by landlords taking advantage of tribal illiteracy

**Typologies of Land Alienation Processes:**
- Loss of land through private transactions (land mortgaging, sale, illegal sale, encroachments, loss before survey and settlement)
- Loss of land through Survey and Settlements (permanent cultivation land categorised as government land; shifting cultivation land categorised as state-owned; unsurveyed areas)
- Notification of forests (encroachments eligible for regularisation but not regularised; shifting cultivation areas categorised as forests)
- Landlessness from land acquisition and displacement
- Poor distribution of government wastelands

#### Consequences of Land Alienation

1. **Loss of livelihood:** Dispossession from forest and cultivable land
2. **Erosion of traditional institutions:** Collective ownership systems like *Khuntkatti* (Mundas) and *Bhuinhari* (Oraons) collapse
3. **Migration and urban drift:** Landless tribals migrate seasonally to Raipur, Ranchi, construction sites, brick kilns
4. **Social stratification:** New landlord tribals emerge; others become dependent labourers — **B.K. Roy Burman** calls this class differentiation within the tribe
5. **Conflict and unrest:** Displacement by dams (Sardar Sarovar, Tawa, Bargi) led to mass protests; **Walter Fernandes** links displacement to development-induced impoverishment

#### Impact on Tribal Women
- Women lose access to forest produce, firewood, fodder, and shifting cultivation plots (their traditional domains)
- Example: **Baiga and Gond** areas of M.P. — women who once managed Bewar fields now depend on daily wage labour
- Displacement negotiations conducted with male household heads — women left **without ownership or compensation**
- Example: **Polavaram Dam (Andhra Pradesh)** — female-headed households least compensated and most food insecure

#### Remedial Steps and Legislative Measures

**(a) Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes Commission (1961) Recommendations:**
- Detailed scrutiny of land laws
- General prohibition on all land transfers from tribals to non-tribals without permission from the Deputy Commissioner

**(b) Protective Legislations:**

| Law | Key Provision |
|---|---|
| Chotanagpur Tenancy Act (1908) | Restricts transfer of tribal land to non-tribals in Jharkhand |
| Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act (1949) | Prohibits transfer of land even for public purposes — strong land protection |
| Forest Rights Act (FRA), 2006 | Recognises individual and community rights to land and forest resources |
| LARR Act, 2013 | Fair compensation and rehabilitation in land acquisition |

**Way Forward:**
- Ensuring effective enforcement and participatory governance
- Recognition of community land rights under FRA
- Sustainable development must integrate **tribal consent, compensation, and cultural continuity**

### 6.2.2 Poverty

**Overview:**
- Tribal poverty is a **multi-dimensional and structural problem** rooted in historical marginalisation, resource deprivation, and exclusion from development processes
- **Planning Commission:** STs below poverty line in rural areas declined from 62.3% (2004-05) to 45.3% (2011-12); urban from 35.5% to 24.1% — yet they remain **poorest among all social groups**
- **D.N. Majumdar:** Tribal poverty is not merely economic but an outcome of dislocation from the ecological niche and exploitation by the dominant society

**Historical Context:**
- Colonial forest and land policies, post-Independence industrialisation, state-led development displaced tribal communities
- India's growth trajectory (industrial expansion, Green Revolution, urbanisation) **bypassed the subsistence tribal economy**

#### B.D. Sharma's Five Levels of Deprivation

1. **Non-recognition of Rights over Resources:** Tribals denied access to forest, land, and water resources; e.g., loss of ancestral land during Narmada Dam without prior informed consent
2. **Alienation of Workers from Means of Production:** Dispossession pushes tribals into bonded labour or daily wage work; Bonded labour among tribal workers in central India
3. **Denial of Due Instalment of Labour:** Tribals frequently receive unfair wages; Adivasi tea plantation workers in Assam earn less than non-tribal workers
4. **Bartering of Liberty:** Poverty and vulnerability result in human trafficking and forced prostitution — particularly tribal women and girls in Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh
5. **Psychological State of Accepting Deprivation:** Continuous exploitation leads to loss of self-respect, fatalism, and internalised poverty

#### Additional Causes of Tribal Poverty

| Cause | Detail |
|---|---|
| Role of Moneylenders and Debt | Dependence on private moneylenders; land mortgaged and lost |
| Developmental Displacement | Dams, mining, industrial projects displace lakhs |
| Systemic Barriers to Mobility | Discrimination, remoteness, lack of political voice; exclusion from skill programmes |
| Lack of Access to Education and Healthcare | NFHS-5: high malnutrition and infant mortality; education gaps restrict employment |
| Weak Implementation of Welfare Schemes | IRDP, NREP, MGNREGA, PDS, Tribal Sub-Plan — fail to reach remote tribal regions |

**Examples:**
- **Tharu and Bhoksa Tribes (U.P.):** Exploited by post-Partition refugee moneylenders
- **Baiga and Gond Tribes (M.P.):** Evictions from Kanha Tiger Reserve (2014) deepened poverty
- **Saharias (M.P.):** Asmita Kabra (2009) documented post-displacement poverty due to loss of MFP access after Kuno relocation

#### Consequences

1. **Chronic indebtedness:** Moneylenders exploit through unfair interest rates; Santhals mortgage land or sell forest produce cheaply
2. **Dehumanising living conditions:** NFHS-5 — 54% of tribal households lack improved sanitation; 47% lack access to safe drinking water
3. **Health deterioration:** Poor nutrition, infections (malaria, diarrhoea, tuberculosis); infant mortality among STs remains **40% higher** than national average (MoTA, 2021)
4. **Loss of autonomy:** Dependence on MGNREGA and PDS increases external control, weakening traditional subsistence systems
5. **Inter-generational deprivation:** Poverty transmits through poor schooling and malnutrition — vicious cycle noted by **L.P. Vidyarthi** in his Ranchi tribal studies

#### Impact on Tribal Women
- Women engage in **multiple subsistence tasks** (firewood, water, wage labour) without rest or recognition
- Example: Santhal women — average daily work hours exceed 12, combining domestic and wage work (ICSSR study)
- Women **eat last and least** — high anaemia (over 60%) and undernutrition among tribal females (NFHS-5)

#### Policy and Programmatic Interventions

| Scheme | Key Detail |
|---|---|
| IRDP (Integrated Rural Development Programme) | Assisted 42% of tribal families up to the Eighth Plan |
| Tribal Sub-Plan (1974-75) | Mandated plan funds in proportion to tribal population; area-based integrated development |
| FRA, 2006 | Restores individual and community rights over forest land and produce |
| PESA Act, 1996 | Self-governance rights to Scheduled Areas through Gram Sabhas |
| LARR Act, 2013 | Fair compensation and rehabilitation |
| PM-JANMAN (2024) | Focuses on PVTGs |
| Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) | Quality education in tribal areas |
| TRIFED and Van Dhan Yojana | Value addition and marketing of forest produce |

**Way Forward:**
1. Strengthen implementation of FRA, PESA, and land-tenancy laws
2. Promote education in tribal languages and contextual curricula
3. Develop community-based forest management and eco-tourism
4. Expand livelihood diversification through skill development
5. Ensure participatory planning and respect for tribal consent in all development projects

**B.D. Sharma Quote:** "The tribal world is being impoverished in the name of development." Real change requires **inclusive, rights-based, and culturally sensitive governance**.

### 6.2.3 Indebtedness

**Overview:**
- One of the oldest and most persistent socio-economic problems
- Traditional tribal societies were based on **subsistence economies and reciprocity**
- Contact with money economy during and after colonial rule introduced **cash dependency, credit, and exploitation**
- **Christoph von Fürer-Haimendorf** observed: the shift from a barter-based economy to a monetised one exposed tribal to exploitation by moneylenders, contractors, and traders
- **Rangarajan:** About **36% of Scheduled Tribe farmer households are indebted**, mostly to non-institutional sources like moneylenders and traders

**Extent:**
- Indebtedness is widespread except in a few North-Eastern areas that were historically protected from middlemen during colonial times

#### Causes of Tribal Indebtedness

1. **Loss of Rights over Forests:** Forest restrictions (Indian Forest Act, 1927) displaced tribals from livelihood base — **Tripathi** noted tribes once shared a harmonious and symbiotic relationship with forests
2. **Poor Cultivation Methods:** Primitive tools, low productivity, no irrigation → no surplus to save or sell
3. **Ignorance and Market Exploitation:** Illiteracy and unfamiliarity with commercial transactions — prey to cunning merchants and moneylenders
4. **Extravagant Ceremonial Spending:** Social prestige requires lavish marriage and festival celebrations — borrowing even at high interest
5. **Role of Moneylenders:** Formal credit systems fail due to bureaucratic hurdles; moneylender remains ever-present and informal, offering immediate cash without documentation but at exorbitant interest

#### Consequences of Indebtedness

1. **Loss of Freedom:** Borrower and family become **bonded labourers**; Example: **Sahariya tribe of Baran (Rajasthan)** works as bonded labourers under the *Hali* system despite Bonded Labour Abolition Act, 1976
2. **Alienation of Land:** Land seized by creditors, leading to chronic landlessness
3. **Sale of Girls and Human Trafficking:** Debt repayment sometimes leads to sale of daughters; Example: **Bedia community** — women pushed into prostitution as debt-repayment practice
4. **Health Impacts:** V.G. Rao's study (Jabalpur, M.P.) found **36.5% prevalence of STDs** among tribal villagers — trichomoniasis in females and gonorrhea in males
5. **Bonded Labour and Hereditary Debt:** Indebtedness leads to permanent servitude; Example: **Juang PVTGs of Odisha** — system of *Goti* binds families into labour for generations despite Orissa Debt Bondage Abolition Act, 1948; **Anumeha Yadav** reports practice continues despite central and state laws

#### Impact on Tribal Women
- When families fall into debt, women are pushed into **bonded labour or sexual exploitation**
- Example: In Jharkhand and Odisha, women of Ho and Munda tribes are employed as informal domestic servants in cities through exploitative networks
- Indebtedness breaks kinship-based support systems, making women vulnerable to **trafficking and migration-related abuse**

#### Case Studies

**(a) Sahariyas of Rajasthan:**
- Sahariya in Baran, Rajasthan continue to work as bonded labour for rich landlords under the **Hali system** (form of bonded labour banned under Bonded Labour Abolition Act, 1976)
- Tricked into loans at exorbitant interest → work for landowners without wages

**(b) Juang (PVTG) of Odisha:**
- Many families forced to labour in repayment of debt — **system locally called *Goti*** — despite the Orissa Debt Bondage (Abolition and Regulation) Act, 1948

#### Legislations and Remedial Measures

| Law/Initiative | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| AP Scheduled Areas Moneylenders Regulation, 1963 | Regulates credit transactions in tribal areas |
| Mysore Pawnbrokers Act, 1961 | Controls lending practices and interest rates |
| Assam Money Lending Regulation, 1968 | Restricts exploitative lending in tribal regions |
| Fifth Schedule of the Constitution | Empowers states to regulate moneylending and land transfers in Scheduled Areas |
| M.P. (2019): *Anusuchit Jan Jaati Sahukar Viniyam, 1972* | All loans taken by STs from unlicensed moneylenders were waived |
| NSTFDC (National Scheduled Tribes Finance and Development Corporation) | PSU under Ministry of Tribal Affairs; concessional loans for income generation |
| AMSY (Adivasi Mahila Sashaktikaran Yojana) | Loan up to 90% for projects costing up to ₹2 lakh; at 4% per annum interest |
| MCF (Micro Credit Scheme for SHGs) | Loans up to ₹50,000 per member; max ₹5 lakh per SHG |

**Analysis:**
- Despite multiple protective laws, **implementation remains weak** — tribals still rely on informal moneylenders
- Indebtedness is both **a cause and a consequence of underdevelopment**
- Real progress requires: empowering tribals through **secure land rights, community banking, and education on financial management**

### 6.2.4 Tribal Education

**Overview:**
- According to **Census 2011:** literacy rate among STs = **59%** vs. **73% for total population**
- As **Verrier Elwin** remarked: education for tribals must be **of the forest and not against the forest**, respecting their ecology and culture

**Socio-Economic Indicators:**

| Indicator | Value |
|---|---|
| ST Literacy Rate (2001) | 47.1% |
| ST Literacy Rate (2011) | 59% |
| ST Male Literacy | 68.5% |
| ST Female Literacy | Below 50% |
| Highest ST Literacy State | Mizoram (91.6%) |
| Lowest ST Literacy State | Andhra Pradesh (49.2%) |
| Gap from national average | About 14 percentage points |

- States with gaps exceeding 18 points: Tamil Nadu, Odisha, M.P., West Bengal, Kerala

#### Constitutional Provisions for Tribal Education

| Article | Provision |
|---|---|
| Article 46 | Directive to promote educational and economic interests of SCs, STs, and weaker sections |
| Article 29(1) | Right to conserve distinct language, script, and culture |
| Article 15(4) | Enables special provisions for educational advancement of SCs and STs |
| Article 275(1) | Grants-in-aid for promoting tribal welfare, including education |
| Article 350A | Mandates instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage for linguistic minorities |

#### Causes of Educational Backwardness

1. **Poor Socio-Economic Conditions:** Education viewed as luxury; children engage in wage labour; girl child education suffers due to safety and domestic burden
2. **Lack of Infrastructure:** Schools are few, far, and often lack electricity, sanitation, and teachers
3. **Language Barrier:** Schools teach in regional or national language — not tribal mother tongue → high dropout rates; Article 350A seldom implemented
4. **Teacher-Related Challenges:** Few trained or willing to serve in remote areas; cultural insensitivity and absenteeism common
5. **Gender Disparity:** ST female literacy lags behind male literacy by nearly 20%; early marriage, lack of girls' hostels, poor hygiene contribute to dropouts
6. **Low Parental Motivation and Cultural Disconnect:** Schools alienate children from traditional occupations and cultural values

#### Consequences

1. **Low literacy and dropouts:** Drop-out rates high due to linguistic gaps and poor infrastructure
2. **Limited employability:** Lack of English or technical skills keeps most tribals in low-paid jobs despite reservation benefits
3. **Cultural alienation:** Curriculum alien to tribal worldview; e.g., Gonds in M.P. find Hindi-medium education difficult
4. **Gender disparity:** Female literacy (49%) vs. male (68%); **Xaxa Committee (2014)** noted early marriage and household chores reduce girls' schooling
5. **Leadership deficit:** Educated intermediaries form a tribal elite **(S.C. Dube, 1955)** — sometimes serving self-interest rather than community welfare

**Impact on Tribal Women:**
- **ST male literacy 68.5%, female less than 50% (Census 2011)**
- Girls drop out due to distance to schools, safety concerns, and early marriage
- Example: In **Andhra Pradesh's PVTGs (Chenchu, Konda Reddi)**, girls leave school after Class V due to lack of female teachers and hostels

#### Government Initiatives and Schemes

| Scheme | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| Eklavya Model Residential Schools (EMRS) | Quality education for ST students on lines of Navodaya schools |
| Ashram Schools | Residential schools in remote tribal areas |
| Pre-Matric and Post-Matric Scholarships | Financial aid for ST students |
| Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) and Article 275(1) Grants | Funding earmarked for educational infrastructure |
| NEP 2020 | Promotes bilingual education and inclusion of local knowledge systems |
| Adivasi Mahila Shikshan Kendra | Literacy centres for tribal women |

#### Suggestions and Way Forward

1. **Infrastructural Development:**
   - Expand Eklavya Model Residential Schools
   - Recruit local teachers familiar with tribal language and culture
   - Kerala's **One Family, One Job** model for local employment
   - Introduce bilingual systems as per **TSR Subramanian Committee** and NEP 2020

2. **Teacher Training and Sensitisation:**
   - Regular training programmes for cultural awareness
   - Organise Tribal History and Culture Exhibitions in schools

3. **Student Safety and Support:**
   - Protect from abuse and exploitation in hostels
   - Examples of abuse: Muzaffarpur shelter case; Maharashtra (29 tribal girls exploited, 2011-2016)

4. **Focus on Tribal Girls' Education:**
   - Build girls' schools and hostels in remote areas
   - Provide bicycles for girl students (Bihar model)
   - Ensure menstrual hygiene through National Menstrual Hygiene Scheme

5. **Awareness and Community Mobilisation:**
   - Awareness camps, street dramas, puppet shows, social campaigns
   - Community participation similar to campaigns like Darwaza Band or Hum Do Humare Do

6. **Regular Monitoring and Accountability:**
   - Periodic reviews by District Collectors/Tribal Welfare Officers

**Elwin Quote:** "Let us teach the tribe not to despise his culture, but to enrich it through knowledge."

### 6.2.5 Unemployment and Underemployment

**Overview:**
- Structural issues rooted in **loss of traditional livelihood, lack of education, displacement, and marginalisation**
- Nearly **75% of tribals are engaged in agriculture**, but the sector is largely subsistence-based
- **Richard Mahapatra:** Tribals are quitting farming fast, and every second household now survives on manual labour in an informal economy

**Occupational Profile of Tribes:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Agriculture and allied activities | 75% of tribals |
| Pastoral and herding groups | Todas (Nilgiris), Bhotias, Gaddis, Bakarwals |
| Artisans and blacksmiths | Asur and Agaria (Jharkhand, M.P., Bihar) |
| Foragers | Chenchu, Paniya, and Onge |
| Industrial and migrant labourers | Construction, domestic work, mining |

**Statistical Overview (NSSO, 2011-12):**

| Category | Male | Female |
|---|---|---|
| Urban ST Unemployment Rate | 3.4% (up from 2.9%) | 4.8% (up from 3.4%) |
| Rural ST Unemployment Rate | 1.3% (up from 1.1%) | 1.1% (up from 0.4%) |
| Rural ST Employment (Self-employed) | 56.4% (mainly agriculture) | — |
| Rural ST Employment (Casual labour) | 38.8% | — |
| Rural ST Employment (Regular/salaried) | 4.8% | — |

#### Causes of Tribal Unemployment and Underemployment

1. **Loss of Traditional Livelihood:** Forest laws and industrial expansion alienated tribals from land and forest; Tribal Health Report (MoHFW) notes sharp decline in forest-based livelihoods
2. **Displacement and Migration:** Development projects and mining led to forced migration to cities as contract labourers and domestic workers
3. **Educational Deprivation:** Lack of quality schooling and vocational training; most tribal youth lack modern marketable skills or career counselling
4. **Infrastructural Deficit:** Poor roads, electricity, internet, and industrial backwardness in tribal regions
5. **Discrimination and Marginalisation:** Stereotypes and social exclusion result in wage discrimination and fewer job opportunities
6. **Agricultural Dependence and Seasonality:** Over-reliance on rain-fed agriculture causes seasonal unemployment
7. **Vacant Reserved Posts in Public Employment:**
   - **112th Parliamentary Standing Committee Report (2023):** 1,199 of 3,097 ST-reserved posts in 45 central universities and over 3,000 posts in ministries remain vacant
   - **Saiyed Zegham Murtaza:** "These figures betray an extreme disregard of the reservation norms"
8. **Frustration and Extremism:** Lack of economic opportunity has led some tribal youth to join extremist movements

#### Consequences

1. **Seasonal underemployment:** Agriculture and forest work limited to 4-5 months per year — remaining time in casual labour (**N.C. Saxena**)
2. **Distress migration:** Large-scale migration from Jharkhand and Odisha to brick kilns in Andhra Pradesh or factories in Surat and Mumbai — often without social security
3. **Skill mismatch:** National Skill Development Report (2018) — less than **2% of tribal youth formally trained**
4. **Rise in bonded labour:** Persistent poverty and debt result in bonded labour in M.P. and Rajasthan (ILO, 2016)
5. **Psychological stress and marginalisation:** Joblessness fuels frustration, alcoholism, and susceptibility to extremism; studies in Bastar link unemployment with Naxal recruitment (**Bhatia**)

**Impact on Tribal Women:**
- Tribal women dominate manual labour, construction, and forest collection — little job security
- Example: In **Bastar**, over 70% of employed tribal women work as seasonal agricultural labourers under MGNREGA, earning less than men
- Decline in forest-based and agricultural jobs forces women to migrate as domestic workers or brick kiln labourers
- Studies from **Ranchi and Sundargarh** show rising female migration to Delhi and Mumbai, leading to loss of community support

#### Government Measures

1. **Employment and Livelihood Programmes:**
   - NREP, Rural Landless Employment Guarantee Programme → merged into **Jawahar Rozgar Yojana (JRY)**
   - **MGNREGA:** ST participation peaked at 36% in first year, dropped to 16% in 2013-14, stabilised around 17-20%; tribals form 20% of total MGNREGA workers (twice their share in population) — Adivasi Adhikar Rashtriya Manch (2023)

2. **Credit and Development Institutions:**
   - **NSTFDC:** Concessional loans and promotes entrepreneurship
   - **Vocational Training Centres (VTCs):** Skills in handicrafts, tailoring, carpentry, and trades

3. **Educational and Skill Initiatives:**
   - **Scheme for Management of Career and Higher Education Aspirations of ST Students (2018-19):** Counselling, scholarships, placement support
   - **EMRS:** Quality education and employability skills for tribal youth

4. **Innovative Model:**
   - **India's First Tribal Employment Exchange (Trivandrum, 2018):** Links tribal youth with jobs, offers career counselling, builds employability

#### Challenges in Implementation
- Weak coordination among Tribal Affairs, Labour, and Rural Development ministries
- Delays in wage payments under MGNREGA
- Poor skill matching between training programmes and local industries
- Low awareness about financial schemes and credit access

#### Way Forward

1. **Skill and Vocational Training:** Establish Tribal Skill Development Centres — local crafts, agro-processing, tourism, renewable energy
2. **Inclusive Industrialisation:** Encourage forest-based industries, bamboo, honey, herbal product cooperatives; Public-Tribal Partnerships in eco-friendly sectors
3. **Strengthening MGNREGA:** Prioritise tribal regions for asset creation; ensure timely wage payments and social audits
4. **Reservation Compliance:** Fill the backlog of ST-reserved posts; regular audits of ministries and universities
5. **Economic Diversification:** Support tribal youth start-ups under 'Start-Up India' with NSTFDC funding; develop micro-industrial clusters in Scheduled Areas
6. **Awareness and Anti-Discrimination:** Conduct workplace diversity campaigns; enforce equal pay

**B.D. Sharma Quote:** "The tribals are not poor by culture, but made poor by circumstance. True empowerment lies in creating dignified and skill-based employment that values both economic and cultural capital."

### 6.2.6 Health and Nutrition

**Overview:**
- Tribal communities continue to face a **disproportionate burden of disease, malnutrition, and mortality**
- STs = **8.6% of India's population (Census 2011)** but health indicators are consistently **10-25% lower** than non-tribal populations
- **Abhay Bang Committee on Tribal Health (2018):** Tribal India faces a **triple burden of communicable diseases, non-communicable diseases, and mental health problems**

**Key Health Statistics:**

| Health Indicator | Tribal Population | General Population | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Life Expectancy | 63.9 years | 67 years | Lancet Study |
| IMR (2005-06 → 2019-21) | 62.1% → 41.6% | — | SRS/NFHS |
| Under-Five Stunting | 44% | 38% | NFHS-4 (2015-16) |
| Underweight Children | 45% | 36% | NFHS-4 |
| Wasting (Low weight for height) | 27% | 21% | NFHS-4 |
| Adolescent Girls (BMI < 18.5) | ~50% | — | NFHS-4 |

**Disease Burden:**
- **Malaria:** Tribal areas contribute to **30% of all malaria cases**, **60% of Plasmodium falciparum cases**, and **50% of total malaria deaths** (Bang Committee, 2018)
- **Tuberculosis:** Prevalence among tribals — **703 per 100,000** vs. 256 per 100,000 nationally (ICMR, 2021)
- **NCDs:** ICMR study found 66% of deaths in tribal areas due to NCDs (mainly cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cancer, chronic respiratory illness) — comparable to non-tribal populations (63%)

#### Major Causes of Poor Health and Nutrition

1. **Lack of Awareness and Mistrust in Modern Medicine:**
   - Many tribals still attribute illness to supernatural causes — relying on *ojhas*, *baigas*, and *deotas*
   - Low vaccine acceptance and weak immunisation coverage

2. **Unhygienic Practices:**
   - Inadequate use of soap, clean water, and sanitation → high maternal and infant mortality
   - Open defecation, unclean childbirth, unsafe drinking water

3. **Poor Health Infrastructure:**
   - Shortfall of **27-40%** in PHCs, Sub-centres, and CHCs in tribal areas
   - **33% shortage of PHC doctors**
   - Tripura Tribal Health Review (2022) highlighted poor road access, power supply, and equipment shortages

4. **Financial Constraints:**
   - Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP) allocations often diverted or underutilised

5. **Lack of Micro-Level Data:**
   - Health planning ignores tribe-specific vulnerabilities (e.g., sickle-cell anaemia in central India, goitre in Himalayan tribes)

6. **Malnutrition and Food Insecurity:**
   - Poverty and loss of forest-based food resources cause **protein and micronutrient deficiency**
   - AARM warns that reduction in food subsidies could intensify hunger and malnutrition

**Triple Burden of Diseases:**

| Category | Key Diseases |
|---|---|
| Communicable Diseases | Malaria, TB, leprosy, diarrhoea, respiratory infections |
| Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs) | Diabetes, hypertension, cancer, chronic respiratory illness |
| Mental Health and Substance Abuse | Displacement, cultural erosion → stress, alcoholism, rising suicide rates |

#### Consequences

1. **High mortality:** Life expectancy among STs is **5-6 years lower** than national average; malaria, sickle cell anaemia, and fluorosis are common (ICMR, 2022)
2. **Child malnutrition:** NFHS-5 — **45% of tribal children under 5 are stunted** and 27% are wasted; highest in M.P., Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand
3. **Maternal health risk:** **59% of ST women anaemic**; low institutional deliveries due to distance and mistrust
4. **Reduced productivity:** Malnourishment weakens physical strength in agricultural and forest work
5. **Decline of traditional medicine:** Indigenous healers (*vaidus, ojhas*) sidelined by public health systems — **P.C. Joshi** stresses integration of ethnomedicine with PHCs could improve outcomes

**Case Study: Keonjhar District, Odisha — Mining Prosperity but Tribal Health Crisis**
- One of India's richest mineral belts — **26% of country's iron ore reserves**
- Revenue through DMF and OMBADC together exceeding **₹15,000 crore** — meant for welfare and health development
- Despite financial wealth: Keonjhar ranks among **worst-performing districts in child and maternal health**
- NFHS-5: **74.7% of pregnant women are anaemic** (state average 61.8%); 25.9% of women have low BMI
- District has the **highest number of stunted, wasted, and underweight children** in Odisha
- Remote tribal villages like **Emiri** inaccessible by ambulance — women travel over **20 km for delivery**
- **Anthropological relevance:** Illustrates how tribal and rural health deprivation continues despite extractive wealth — a "**paradox of prosperity without well-being**"

**Impact on Tribal Women:**
- **Maternal mortality among ST women: 167 per lakh live births** — higher than national average (NFHS-5)
- Example: In **Dahanu (Maharashtra)** tribal belt — one in three pregnancies is anaemic or underweight
- Cultural barriers and mistrust of allopathic medicine keep women from availing antenatal care or contraception
- Example: Among **Santhal and Bhil** women — home births assisted by untrained midwives persist → infection and postpartum complications

#### Government Initiatives

**Policy Measures:**
- **National Health Mission (NHM):** Tribal sub-plans and ASHA workers for last-mile service delivery
- **Abhay Bang Committee (2018):** Comprehensive roadmap for tribal health with focus on decentralised planning
- **Tribal Health and Nutrition Portal (SWASTHYA):** Digital repository for health data
- **E-newsletter ALEKH:** Disseminates awareness on health and nutrition

**Disease-Specific Programmes:**
- **Aashwasan Campaign (2022):** TB awareness and screening in 177 tribal districts — 10 million people
- **Malaria Elimination Programme (2030):** Focus on tribal districts with highest P. falciparum burden

**Nutrition Schemes:**
- **ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services):** Supplementary nutrition for children and lactating mothers
- **Poshan Abhiyaan:** Targeted reduction of stunting, underweight, and anaemia among tribal children and women
- **Mid-Day Meal Scheme:** Improves school attendance and child nutrition

**Infrastructure and Human Resource Development:**
- Establishment of **Tribal Health Centres** and **Mobile Medical Units** in remote areas
- **ASHAs and ANMs from tribal communities** — increase trust and continuity of care

#### Suggestions and Way Forward

1. **Integrate Traditional and Modern Medicine:**
   - Combine AYUSH and Allopathy for culturally acceptable healthcare
   - Recognise traditional healers (*baigas, ojhas*) as intermediaries to improve community trust

2. **Promote Health Literacy and Awareness:**
   - Use **folk media, street plays, and storytelling** to spread messages about hygiene, vaccination, and nutrition
   - School-based health education in tribal languages

3. **Infrastructure and Capacity Building:**
   - Fill doctor vacancies; incentivise medical service in tribal regions
   - Build **hostels for expectant mothers**; improve transport and ambulance facilities

4. **Strengthen Nutrition Programmes:**
   - Provide locally grown nutritious food through PDS and community kitchens
   - Restore **forest rights** to ensure access to traditional food sources like millets, tubers, and wild fruits

5. **Strengthen Policy Governance:**
   - Ensure direct and transparent utilisation of Tribal Sub-Plan funds for health
   - Create a **National Tribal Health Mission (NTHM)** with state-level monitoring units

**Case Example: Gadchiroli Model (Maharashtra)**
- **Dr. Abhay and Rani Bang's SEARCH initiative** in Gadchiroli successfully reduced neonatal mortality by training village health workers to manage home-based newborn care
- Shows the power of **community participation, local training, and traditional integration** in improving tribal health outcomes

**Abhay Bang Committee (2019) Conclusion:** "Tribal health is a test of India's commitment to equity."
