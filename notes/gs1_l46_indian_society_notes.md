## PART A: FUTURE OF CASTE SYSTEM IN INDIA (Unit 3.2)

### Introduction

- Caste = one of the oldest forms of social stratification; shaped India's social, economic, and political life
- Reformers like **Gandhi** and **Ambedkar** envisioned reform or abolition — yet caste persists in overt and subtle ways
- **M. N. Srinivas**: Caste adapts through **Sanskritisation** and **Westernisation** rather than disappearing
- Future of caste = tension between **persistence** and **transformation**

---

### 1. Present Reality of Caste

- Caste shows little sign of weakening
- Castes seek **upward mobility** by forming associations (e.g., All India Brahmin Association, All India Yadav Association) — function as pressure groups for jobs, education, and political influence
- Caste identity = key basis for **social organisation** and **political mobilisation**

**Counter-view:**
- In urban contexts and high-skill sectors like IT, hiring often values **merit** over caste
- Studies (e.g., **Banerjee**) show limited caste bias in software jobs when qualifications are equal
- **DICCI** (Dalit Indian Chamber of Commerce and Industry) = new forms of Dalit assertion through entrepreneurship

---

### 2. Reformist and Abolitionist Approaches

Two major ideological strands:

| Approach | Thinker | Argument |
|---|---|---|
| **Reformist** | Gandhi | Argued for a purified varna system where duties were based on **qualities, not birth** |
| **Abolitionist** | Ambedkar + G. S. Ghurye | Saw caste as inherently **oppressive**; demanded its destruction |

**Key quote — Ambedkar** (*Annihilation of Caste*):
> "Political democracy cannot last unless social democracy is achieved."

**Counter-view:**
- **Lingayat movement** rejected caste hierarchy outright
- **Dalit Capitalism** (Chandra Bhan Prasad) — entrepreneurship as a tool for social dignity; caste hierarchies can be challenged through economic means

---

### 3. Remedies Suggested

**Survey by Narmadeshwar Prasad (1957)** — based on 1,225 respondents:

| Remedy | % Respondents |
|---|---|
| Expanding education and economic opportunities | 39.1% |
| Promoting inter-caste marriages | 35.3% |
| Ensuring equality of treatment in everyday life | 13.4% |
| Removing untouchability in practice | 12.2% |

**Counter-view / Supporting data:**
- **Hnatkovska and Lahiri (2012)**: documented convergence in education and wages for SCs/STs from 1980s to 2010s
- Inter-caste marriages: only **5% nationally** (IHDS-2, 2011-12) but slowly rising in urban areas
- **NFHS-5 data**: inter-caste marriages linked to better child nutrition
- **Pew Survey 2021**: attitudes remain rigid, yet younger and urban Indians show more openness

---

### 4. Obstacles and Political Reinforcement

- **Mandal verdict (1992)** (Supreme Court): upheld caste as the basis for reservations — affirmed salience of caste in public policy
- **Pauline Kolenda (1997)**: a system shaping Indian society for over 1,000 years cannot vanish within decades
- Politics continues to reinforce caste identity — electoral mobilisation remains deeply caste-based

**Counter-view:**
- **EWS quota (2019)**: introduced economic criteria — loosening caste as the sole axis of affirmative action
- **Bihar Caste Survey (2023)**: provides nuanced socio-economic data, helping rethink welfare policies
- **Christophe Jaffrelot**: caste politics has also **empowered marginalised groups**, enabling new forms of bargaining power

---

### 5. Persistence of Caste Practices

**A. Endogamy**
- Despite legal freedom, only **5% of marriages are inter-caste**
- Caste remains the primary criterion in matrimonial alliances

**B. Untouchability**
- Survey by **NCAER & University of Maryland**: **27% of India still practises untouchability** (more than 1 in 4 people)
- IHDS-2 data (by caste): Brahmin 52%, Forward 24%, OBC 15%, SC 22%, ST 13%, Others 27%
- By religion: Hindu 35%, Jain 30%, Sikh 23%, Muslim 18%, Christian 5%, Tribal 5%, Buddhist 1%

**C. Hereditary Occupations**
- Certain jobs remain heavily caste-linked:
  - **Uttar Pradesh**: Out of 46,000 leather workers, 41,000 are from Chamars, Dhusia, and Jatava castes
    - SC sweepers aged 35-59: 63.4% are Balmikis; aged 15-34: 62% are Balmikis (little generational change)
    - Leather work (35-59 age group): 88.2% are Chamars, Dhusias, and Jatavas
  - **Rajasthan**: Out of 76,000 sweepers, almost 52,000 are Bhangi, Mehtar, and Chura
  - Similar pattern in Madhya Pradesh

**Counter-view:**
- Endogamy weakening slightly among educated and urban youth — inter-caste unions increasing through modern matrimonial platforms
- **Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers Act (2013)** directly challenges untouchability
- **NAMASTE scheme (2022)**: mechanises sanitation work
- Technological modernisation in occupations further erodes caste-based job divisions

---

### Conclusion on Future of Caste

- Caste will remain a **significant social reality** in India's foreseeable future
- Persistence ensured by: endogamy + caste-based politics + entrenched social attitudes
- Transformation underway: caste is less about ritual hierarchy and more about **identity, politics, and access to resources**
- Future = **persistence in new forms**, not disappearance
- Challenge: ensure caste identities do not translate into caste inequalities
- **Andre Beteille**: the tension between **caste and class** will define India's social structure

> India is home to Hinduism, Islam, Buddhism, Jainism, Christianity, and Zoroastrianism. All these religions living together influenced each other and shaped Indian society as a whole.

## SECTION 1: IMPACT OF BUDDHISM ON INDIAN SOCIETY

### Context at the Time of Buddha

- Indian society was **static, hierarchical, and dominated by rituals and sacrifices**
- Ascribed status given primacy over achieved status
- Conditions particularly harsh for women, shudras, and some vaishyas
- Buddhism emerged: rejected caste divisions, ritual sacrifices, and priestly monopoly
- Preached: **equality, ethics, and intellectual freedom**

---

### The Essence of Buddhism

**Noble Eightfold Path:**
1. Right Understanding
2. Right Thought
3. Right Speech
4. Right Action
5. Right Livelihood
6. Right Effort
7. Right Mindfulness
8. Right Concentration

---

### Positive Impacts of Buddhism

#### A. Social Life

**Egalitarianism:**
- Buddhism rejected the varna system
- **Anyone could join the Sangha**, irrespective of caste or gender
- **R. S. Sharma**: this shift encouraged recognition of **achieved status** over birth-based status, especially in early urban centres

**Status of Women:**
- Women were allowed to become nuns and pursue education and spiritual life
- **Uma Chakravarti**: works like the *Therigatha* show how early Buddhist nuns expressed **intellectual and spiritual autonomy** — a significant step in gender empowerment

**Ethics and Morality:**
- Code of conduct emphasising truth, non-violence, compassion, and restraint from theft or alcohol
- **S. Radhakrishnan**: these principles acted as **civic ethics** — a new form of social control beyond ritual sanction

**Religious life:** Buddhism provided an alternative to elaborate sacrifices and priestly domination

#### B. Intellectual Freedom

- Logic replaced superstition; encouraged thinking for oneself
- Written in **Pali** (not Sanskrit) — enabled ordinary people to question rituals and dogma
- **Richard Gombrich**: the Pali canon emphasised **reasoned inquiry**, making debate and questioning the default mode of religious learning

#### C. Literature

- **Pali Canon (Tipitaka)**: preserved the earliest teachings
- **Jataka tales**: narrated Buddha's earlier births, often with moral lessons
- ***Therigatha***: reflected women's voices
- **Apabhramsa writings** of Buddhism and Jainism enriched Indian linguistic traditions

#### D. Education

- Most ancient Indian universities are products of Buddhism: **Nalanda, Vikramshila, Taxila, Valabhi**
- **R. C. Majumdar**: at Taxila, Buddhist and non-Buddhist subjects like law, medicine, and grammar coexisted — showing the broad scope of education

#### E. Art and Architecture

- **A. L. Basham**: highlights the **Gandhara and Mathura schools of art**, which flourished due to Buddhist patronage — blending local and foreign artistic influences
- **Ashoka Pillars** in Bihar and UP
- **Stupas**: Sanchi, Sarnath, Nalanda — architectural marvels
- Figurines at Kanheri, Karle, Nasik
- Sculptures at Barabar, Ajanta — regarded as masterpieces of Buddhist religious art
- **Paintings**: Ajanta, Ellora — got universal attention
- Carvings on railings and gates of stupas: depicted events from Buddha's life

#### F. Economic Life

- Buddhism opposed animal sacrifice, encouraged agriculture, and sanctified the cow
- Monasteries became nodes of economic life
- **Upinder Singh**: monasteries were often located along trade routes — acted as hubs of commerce and exchange
- **Hermann Kulke**: monasteries provided social services — distributing food and medical care, enhancing welfare functions

#### G. Political Life

**Welfare:**
- Vaishya and Shudra (looked down upon in Hinduism) converted in large numbers
- **Romila Thapar**: Ashoka's *dhamma* was not a creed but a model of **ethical kingship**, emphasising welfare and moral governance

**Non-violence:**
- Message reverberating post-Kalinga War — carved in pillars, spread through *dharma mahamatras*

---

### Negative Impacts of Buddhism

**Decline of Vedic Ritual Economy:**
- **R. S. Sharma**: Buddhism's rejection of elaborate Vedic sacrifices reduced the importance of priests in the ritual economy — weakened the traditional Brahmanical order, created tensions with orthodox groups

**Monastic Withdrawal from Society:**
- **Romila Thapar**: growth of Buddhist monasteries sometimes led to **economic withdrawal** from productive activities — monks depended on lay donations, strained agrarian communities

**Decline in Military Strength:**
- Emphasis on non-aggression, non-violence, ascetic life, and spiritualism weakened the militancy of Indians
- India fell easy prey to foreign invaders — centuries of political subjugation

---

### Spread of Buddhism

**Outside India (via Ashoka):**
- Ashoka spread Buddhism through *dharma mahamatras*
- According to **Mahavamsa**: In the 17th year of Ashoka's reign, at end of 3rd Buddhist Council, he sent missionaries to **9 parts of the world**
- Sent daughter **Sanghamitra** and son **Mahindra** to spread Buddhism in Sri Lanka

**Decline within India:**
- Buddhism became primarily a monastic movement — lost touch with lay supporters
- Many monasteries became very wealthy — employed indentured slaves and paid labourers
- After Muslim invaders sacked Indian monasteries in the 12th and 13th centuries, no revival until the 20th century

**20th Century Revival:**
- Modern revival began in the late 19th century
  - **Maha Bodhi Society (1891)**
  - **Bengal Buddhist Association (1892)**
  - **Young Men's Buddhist Association (1898)**
- **B. R. Ambedkar (1891-1956)**: influenced by Pali sources and Indian Buddhists like **Dharmanand Kosambi** and **Lakshmi Narasu** — began promoting conversion to Buddhism for Dalits
- **Dalit Buddhist Movement** most successful in **Maharashtra** — large-scale conversions
- Ambedkar's **"Neo Buddhism"**: strong element of social and political protest against Hinduism and the Indian caste system

---

### Summary: Buddhism's Legacy

Buddhism reshaped Indian society by challenging caste hierarchies, promoting equality, intellectual freedom, and moral governance, while leaving a rich legacy in education, art, and architecture. Though it declined as an organised religion within India, its values and cultural contributions continue to influence Indian thought and social reform even today.

## SECTION 2: IMPACT OF JAINISM ON INDIAN SOCIETY

### Context

- One of the oldest sramana traditions; emerged in the **6th century BCE** alongside Buddhism
- Questioned Vedic orthodoxy
- Emphasis on **ahimsa, austerity, and spiritual discipline**

---

### Essence of Jainism

**Three Jewels (Ratnatraya):**
1. **Samyak Darshan** — "Right Faith"
2. **Samyak Jnana** — "Right Knowledge"
3. **Samyak Charitra** — "Right Conduct"

All three are required for spiritual liberation — none can exist without the others.

**Five Maha-vratas (Great Vows) of Ascetics:**
1. Non-violence — **Ahimsa**
2. Truth — **Satya**
3. Non-stealing — **Achaurya / Asteya**
4. Non-attachment/Non-possession — **Aparigraha**
5. Celibacy/Chastity — **Brahmacharya**

---

### Positive Impacts of Jainism

#### A. Social Equality

- Jainism **rejects the caste system** and advocates for equality
- Monastic communities do not discriminate
- **Romila Thapar**: such sectarian movements **challenged Brahmanical orthodoxy** and offered parallel paths of dignity

#### B. Ethical Values and Non-Violence (Ahimsa)

- Strong emphasis on **ahimsa** — influenced Indian society to promote compassion and respect for all living beings
- Example: Jains adhere to a vegetarian lifestyle, engage in charitable activities
- **A. L. Basham**: highlighted Jain ascetic ideals of **restraint and compassion** as one of the strongest moral vocabularies of ancient India

#### C. Language

- Jainism contributed to the evolution of the **Prakrit language** through its teachings — designed for accessibility to common people
- **Romila Thapar**: this widened the reading public and **democratised learning**

#### D. Social Welfare Initiatives

- Construction of **dharamshalas** (pilgrims' rest houses) — located near pilgrimage destinations
- **R. C. Majumdar**: mercantile patronage made **Jain temples and inns civic hubs**

#### E. Education and Scholarship

- Long tradition of scholarship and education
- Jain monks contributed to intellectual development
- **Tattvartha Sutra**: instrumental in shaping philosophical discussions

#### F. Art and Architecture

**Rock-cut caves:**
- Hindu & Jain rock-cut architecture at Badami, Aihole, Ajanta, Ellora, Elephanta, Aurangabad, Mamallapuram
- **Ellora complex**: unique artistic creation — sanctuaries devoted to Buddhism, Hinduism, and Jainism — illustrates the spirit of **tolerance characteristic of ancient India**

**Temple construction:**
- **Gommateshwara Bahubali statue** at Shravanabelagola — a revered Jain pilgrimage destination

**Paintings:**
- **Kalpa Sutra**: manuscript containing biographies of Tirthankaras (particularly **Parshvanatha and Mahavira**) — canvas for Jain artistic expressions and intricate painting tradition

#### G. Principles of Austerity

Jainism promoted: voluntary simplicity, fasting, humility, selfless service, pursuit of knowledge, and meditation

---

### Spread of Jainism

- Not widely embraced due to its stringent orthodoxy, affinity to Brahmanism, and non-missionary spirit
- Most converts were from **Vaishya** community
- Jainism is **India's sixth-largest religion**
- **2011 Census**: 44,51,753 Jains in India — majority in Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Gujarat, and Madhya Pradesh
- Influence far greater than numbers suggest

---

### Criticisms and Limitations

- **Romila Thapar**: Jainism's strict commitment to ahimsa and ritual purity often led to **social separation and isolation** of Jain communities
- While it promoted compassion, it sometimes weakened **social cohesion** with other groups

---

### Summary: Jainism's Legacy

Although Jainism remained numerically a minority faith, its ethical values, scholarship, and cultural contributions shaped Indian civilisation far beyond its size. The religion's legacy of non-violence, equality, and moral restraint continues to inspire social thought in modern India.

## SECTION 3: IMPACT OF ISLAM ON INDIAN SOCIETY

### Entry into India

- Islam entered India from the **7th century onwards** through trade, migration, and later political expansion
- Shaped Indian society in multiple dimensions: religion and culture to economy, administration, and everyday social practices

---

### Social and Political Life

- Many people from deprived castes were attracted to Islam
- Impact on the constitution observable through the **Hindu Marriage Act** and **Succession Act**
- Islam affected the position of Indian women — in some practices, birth of a girl considered inauspicious (this spread to some Hindu communities too)
- **Harbans Mukhia**: Mohammed preached the **doctrine of one God** in polytheistic Arabia — "the concept of one god meant the concept of social equality"
- **Dr. Tara Chand** (eminent historian-philosopher): emphasised the message of **sulhe-kul** ("peace for all and complete peace") of the Muslim Sufi Silsilah (mystic orders)

---

### Medicine

- Muslims introduced the **Unani system of medicine** in India
- The term *Yunani* means "Greek" — the Perso-Arabic system was based on teachings of Greek physicians **Hippocrates and Galen**

---

### Sufism

- Sufism = mystical Islamic belief and practice — Muslims seek truth of divine love and knowledge through **direct personal experience of God**
- **Richard Eaton**: Sufi saints often integrated local practices, producing a **syncretic culture**
- Example: **Nizamuddin Auliya** (1238-1325 CE) in Delhi — dargah visited by thousands every week; known for evening **qawwali** devotional music sessions

---

### Music and Dance

- **B. S. Upadhyaya** (*Feeders of Indian Culture*, 1973): described in detail the contribution of Islam and Muslims in reconstructing Indian society through music
- Musical instruments brought to India by Muslims: **Sitar and Tabla**
- Example: **Qawwali** in India
- **Kathak** (Indo-Persian Dance Style): unique in having both Hindu and Muslim gharanas — performances include Urdu Ghazals and Mughal-era instruments
- Great contributor: **Amir Khusro**

---

### Architecture

- Muslims introduced domes, minarets, turrets, and arches — greatly accepted by Indians
- Examples: **Taj Mahal, Buland Darwaza, Alai Darwaza**
- **Khanqahs** (monasteries): "provided refuge to the oppressed and wretched, the miserable, the down-trodden and the disinherited, irrespective of caste and creed" — **Rosherduddin Khan**

---

### Gardening

- **Charbagh style**: Persian and Indo-Persian quadrilateral garden layout based on the **four gardens of Paradise** mentioned in the Quran
- The quadrilateral garden is divided by walkways or flowing water into four smaller parts

---

### Language

- The synthesis of Islam and Hinduism gave birth to **Urdu** — became the common language of both communities
- Great contributors: **Mirza Ghalib**

---

### Literary Contribution

- Islam played a pivotal role in shaping historiography
- Notable works: **Babarnama, Akbarnama, Ain-i-Akbari**
- **Frances Pritchett**: Persian literary traditions enriched Indian languages

---

### Paintings

- **Mughal painting**: particular style of South Asian painting — confined to **miniatures** as book illustrations or as single works kept in albums (*muraqqa*)
- Emerged from **Persian miniature painting** (partly of Chinese origin) — developed in Mughal Empire, 16th to 18th centuries
- **Reign of Jahangir** saw painting at its zenith

---

### Games and Entertainment

- Many Indian sports were enriched: hunting, hawking, kite-flying, and **chess (Shatranj)** became popular
- **Shatranj**: old form of chess as played in the Sasanian Empire; origins in the Indian game of **chaturanga**; introduced to Europe through Muslim Al-Andalus (modern Spain) and Sicily in the 10th century

---

### Fashion and Jewellery

- **Mughal dress patterns** adopted by Hindu rulers — cross-cultural impact of Islamic aesthetics
- The **nose ring (Nath)** evolved into a symbol of Hindu marriage, drawing inspiration from Muslim traditions

---

### Negative Impacts of Islam

- **Romila Thapar**: certain rigid practices like **purdah** contributed to the subordination of women in Indian society
- **K. S. Lal**: argued that mass conversions sometimes caused **social tensions** between communities
- Strict enforcement of orthodoxy in certain regimes narrowed religious tolerance at times — in contrast to the syncretic Sufi stream

---

### Summary: Islam's Legacy

The influence of Islam on Indian society was profound and diverse. It reshaped art, architecture, language, music, cuisine, education, and social norms, while also introducing new forms of spirituality through Sufism. The interaction of Islamic and indigenous traditions produced a **rich cultural synthesis** that continues to shape Indian identity today.

## SECTION 4: IMPACT OF CHRISTIANITY ON INDIAN SOCIETY

### Entry into India

- Christianity entered India as early as **52 AD** with the arrival of **St. Thomas** on the Malabar Coast
- Real spread came with **European missionaries from the 16th century onwards**
- Shaped education, healthcare, social reforms, architecture, and culture
- **Victor D'Souza**: "In India, Christianity has been one of the important factors of **modernization and economic development** through the social values it has legitimized"

---

### Essence of Christianity

- Universal brotherhood
- Egalitarian outlook
- Service of the under-privileged

---

### Positive Impacts of Christianity

#### A. Health Initiatives

- Christianity established hospitals — pioneered social welfare, addressing gaps in government reach
- Notable in **North-East India**: Christian missions provided healthcare
- **William Sweet**: Christian missions institutionalised hospitals and clinics, making modern medicine accessible
- **CMC Vellore**: legacy of missionary health service

#### B. Educational Impact

- **Language Introduction**: English as a medium of instruction — became a unifying language among Western-educated individuals
- Christianity established schools where government reach was limited
- Example: **Mizoram** — 92% literacy rate attributed to Christian missions
- **Teotonio R. de Souza**: missionaries introduced modern education and literacy, shaping India's early Western-educated elite
- **Arun Shourie** (critical but useful to cite): noted that missionary education had both reformist and cultural assimilationist aims

#### C. Social Reforms and Opposition to Caste System

- Christian teachings influenced educated individuals to challenge the Hindu caste system — led to significant socio-religious reforms
- **John C. B. Webster**: emphasized missionary efforts in challenging untouchability and promoting social justice

#### D. Advocacy for Egalitarian Society

- Christianity's emphasis on equality and opposition to social stratification played a crucial role

#### E. Cultural Contributions

- Introduced Western architectural elements, musical instruments, and clothing styles
- Example: **Basilica of Bom Jesus** in Goa — iconic Christian architectural influence
- Music: Orchestra, guitar, piano introduced into Indian society
- Clothing styles: Western formals became mainstream

#### F. Heritage Rediscovery

- British efforts (often influenced by Christian perspectives) led to rediscovery of Indian heritage sites
- **Henry Taylor (1818)**: rediscovered **Sanchi Stupa**
- **John Smith (1819)**: stumbled upon **Ajanta Caves**
- **James Prinsep (1837)**: deciphered **Brahmi script**

#### G. Westernisation and Economic Development

- Christianity served as an early channel for Westernisation
- **B. B. Misra**: Christianity acted as a **channel of Western political ideas** — paving the way for liberalism and democratic institutions

#### H. Administrative System Adoption

- Influenced the adoption of administrative systems including the three branches: Legislature, Executive, and Judiciary (from European models)
- Role in the unification of India's administrative systems under British colonial rule

---

### Negative Impacts of Christianity

**Conversion Conflicts:**
- Missionary conversion efforts sometimes led to communal tensions and conflicts
- Example: **Kandhamal violence (2008)** in Odisha
- **Susan Bayly**: disputes over conversion often created localised social strife

**Cultural Assimilation and Loss:**
- **Teotonio R. de Souza**: aggressive cultural assimilation sometimes eroded indigenous practices — European customs replaced traditional Indian ways of life

---

### Summary: Christianity's Legacy

Despite being only about **2.3% of the Indian population**, Christianity's influence on education, healthcare, social reforms, art, and politics has been profound. From **Mother Teresa's humanitarian work** to **Mizoram's literacy success**, Christianity has left a legacy of social change, cultural enrichment, and spiritual inspiration that continues to shape Indian society today.
