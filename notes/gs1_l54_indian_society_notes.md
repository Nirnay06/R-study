## 1. VILLAGE STUDY IN INDIA AND ITS SIGNIFICANCE

### What is Village Study?
Village studies in anthropology involve **in-depth investigations** into the **social, cultural, economic, and political dynamics** of rural communities. Anthropologists use **fieldwork** and **participant observation** to understand intricate relationships, traditional practices, and socio-economic structures — providing a **holistic perspective** on the intersections of tradition and modernity.

---

### Different Views on Indian Villages

| Thinker | View |
|---|---|
| **Mahatma Gandhi** | "India lives in its villages." Villages = autonomous, self-sufficient, moral — representing authentic and sustainable life. |
| **Jawaharlal Nehru** | Did NOT romanticize villages. Saw them as sites of **backwardness, poverty, and stagnation**. Believed India's future lay in **industrialization and modernization**. |
| **B.R. Ambedkar** | Highly critical. Called villages **"a sink of localism, a den of ignorance, and a place of oppression"** — pointing to caste discrimination and marginalization of Dalits. |

---

### Key Scholars and Their Contributions

| Scholar | Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Chicago School** (Robert Redfield, Milton Singer, McKim Marriot) | Shaped theoretical and methodological foundations of Indian village studies in the 1950s–60s |
| **Robert Redfield** | Introduced analytical concept of village India; concept of **Little and Great Traditions** |
| **William Wiser** (1930s) | Published *Hindu Jajmani System* and *Behind the Mud Walls* — historical background of village studies |
| **D.N. Majumdar** | *Caste and Communication in an Indian Village* — illustrated impact of new transport and communication on **caste mobility** |
| **S.C. Dube** | *Indian Village* — considered a **classic** in village studies; valuable insights into social dynamics of rural India |
| **Oscar Lewis** | Explored **Rural Cosmopolitanism** — villages are NOT isolated little republics but interconnected communities |

### Significance of Village Study

#### A. THEORETICAL LEVEL

| Concept | Scholar |
|---|---|
| **Dominant Caste** | M.N. Srinivas — explores the influential caste within a village |
| **Sanskritization and Westernization** | M.N. Srinivas — cultural changes driven by emulation of higher castes and Western influences |
| **Little Republics vs. Rural Cosmopolitanism** | Charles Metcalfe vs. Oscar Lewis — contrasting views on village social structures |
| **Little and Great Traditions** | Robert Redfield — distinguishes between local village traditions and broader cultural influences |
| **Universalization and Parochialization** | McKim Marriot — tension between universal and local values |
| **Sacred Complex** | L.P. Vidyarthi — interconnectedness of religious beliefs in the village |
| **Nature-Man-Spirit Complex** | L.P. Vidyarthi — relationship between nature, humanity, and spirituality |

#### B. APPLIED LEVEL (Policy and Planning)

**Example 1: Community Development Programs (1950s onwards)**
- India launched community development blocks for rural progress.
- Village studies revealed: if ₹1 crore was given for development, it often went first to **dominant caste hamlets**.
- Roads, wells, electricity poles appeared in upper caste areas while Dalit hamlets remained neglected.
- Highlighted how **inequality can grow despite development schemes**.

**Example 2: Tribal Development Programs**
- **Tribal Sub-Plan (1974)** aimed to funnel funds to tribal communities.
- Anthropologists showed how **non-tribal intermediaries** (contractors, moneylenders) often captured benefits.
- Example: Madhya Pradesh and Odisha — contractors took over schemes meant for tribal welfare.

**Example 3: Barriers to Social Change**
- **George Foster's concept of "Traditional Culture"**: societies glorify their past and resist change.
- Public toilets under government schemes often failed because **caste prejudices** made people refuse to share bathrooms.
- Some resisted co-education because of **traditional gender norms**.

> Village studies bridged theory and practice — enriched anthropology with concepts like dominant caste and Sanskritization, while guiding planners in rural development.

## 2. INDIAN VILLAGES — CHANGE IN PATTERNS OF SETTLEMENTS

### Factors Responsible for Different Types of Rural Settlements

| Factor | Details |
|---|---|
| **Physical Features** | Altitude, climate, water availability. Himalayas → clustered in valleys; Rajasthan (arid) → scattered around oases/wells |
| **Cultural and Ethnic Factors** | Caste, religion, social structure. South India → villages develop around temples |
| **Security Considerations** | Areas prone to raids → clustered, fortified villages for protection |
| **Economic Factors** | Fertile plains → agrarian; coastal areas → fishing villages; market routes → trade settlements |

---

### Four Types of Rural Settlements in India

#### 1. Clustered / Agglomerated / Nucleated Settlements
- **Definition:** Compact or closely built area of houses; general living area distinct from surrounding farms, barns, and pastures.
- **Found in:** Fertile alluvial plains, northeastern states.
- **Regions:** Bundelkhand (central India), Nagaland; Rajasthan (water scarcity → compact settlement for resource use).
- **Reason:** Security/defense, or need to maximize use of scarce resources.

#### 2. Semi-Clustered / Fragmented Settlements
- **Definition:** Fragmentation of a large compact village — one or more sections live a little away from the main cluster.
- **Pattern:** Land-owning dominant community occupies the **central part**; people of lower strata and menial workers settle on **outer flanks**.
- **Regions:** Gujarat plain, some parts of Rajasthan.

#### 3. Hamleted Settlements
- **Definition:** Settlement fragmented into several units physically separated but bearing a **common name**.
- **Local names for units:** panna, para, palli, nagla, dhani.
- **Motivation:** Social and ethnic factors.
- **Regions:** Middle and lower Ganga plain, Chhattisgarh, lower Himalayan valleys.

#### 4. Dispersed / Isolated Settlements
- **Definition:** Isolated huts or small hamlets in remote jungles or small hills with farms/pasture on slopes.
- **Cause:** Extremely fragmented terrain and land resource base.
- **Regions:** Meghalaya, Uttarakhand, Himachal Pradesh, Kerala.

---

### Changes in Settlement Patterns — Anthropological Analysis

| Factor | How it Changed Settlement Patterns |
|---|---|
| **Caste Dynamics** | Traditional caste-specific areas breaking down due to social awareness movements and government policies reducing caste-based discrimination |
| **Class and Economic Changes** | Agricultural practice changes, land reforms, diversification of economy → new opportunities → shift in settlement locations |
| **Jajmani System and Occupational Changes** | Decline of traditional caste-based occupations, rise of new industries → erosion of jajmani system → settlement changes |
| **Social Mobility** | Education and access to resources → migration to urban/peri-urban areas; individuals moving outside caste-specific areas |
| **Modernization and Infrastructure** | Better transport, communication, amenities → movement away from isolated areas; growth of market towns |
| **Government Policies and Land Reforms** | Redistribution of land, infrastructure projects → shifts in population distribution |

## 3. LITTLE REPUBLIC AND RURAL COSMOPOLITANISM

### Earlier Perception — "Little Republics"
- Scholars **Henry Maine, Charles Metcalfe, Baden Powell** described Indian villages as **self-sufficient and autonomous**.
- **Metcalfe** famously said Indian villages were like **tiny republics, standing firm even when kingdoms rose and fell** around them.
- **Maine and Powell** also admired this local autonomy — villages as miniature states capable of independent functioning.

### Metcalfe's Idea of Little Republics — Key Points

**1. Self-Sufficiency and Autonomy**
- Villages seen as self-reliant units with own social, economic, and political structures.
- Managed agriculture, cottage industries, and local needs without heavy dependence on external authorities.

**2. Social and Political Independence**
- Villages had own local governance — **Panchayat** — which resolved disputes and managed affairs.
- Sense of self-governance; reduced dependence on central authority.

**3. Role in Social Cohesion**
- Maintained autonomy → prevented concentration of power in central authorities.
- Fostered community solidarity and balanced societal structure.

**Examples supporting Little Republic concept:**
- Panchayat System: Village councils as local self-government handling judicial and administrative matters.
- Traditional Economy: Agriculture and cottage industries made villages relatively independent; goods exchanged within community.

**Limitations of the Concept:**
- Villages were NOT completely isolated.
- They engaged in trade, communication, and cultural exchange with nearby villages and towns.
- While there was autonomy, there was also interconnectedness.

**Critiques:** Nehru and Ambedkar remind us that villages were not just sites of autonomy but also of **inequality, poverty, and caste oppression**.

---

### Rural Cosmopolitanism — Oscar Lewis

**Oscar Lewis** came up with the concept of **RURAL COSMOPOLITANISM** based on his study of **Rani Khera village near New Delhi**.

He **challenged** Charles Metcalfe's "Little Republic" concept.

**Key argument:** Villages are NOT self-sufficient and independent — they always maintain ties with other villages for **social, cultural, economic, religious, and political** activities.

#### Mexico vs. India — Oscar Lewis's Comparison (presented at University of Chicago seminar)

| Mexico | India |
|---|---|
| Villages are **"inward looking"** | Villages form a **complex network** |
| No socio-cultural ties with other villages | Socio-cultural ties — marriage, exogamy |
| Follow **village endogamy** | Economic ties — trade |
| Inter-village network only for trade | Religious ties — pilgrimage |
| | Political ties |
| | Lewis calls this **"RURAL COSMOPOLITANISM"** |

#### Indian Examples of Rural Cosmopolitanism

1. **Jallikattu (Tamil Nadu):** Traditional bull-taming sport during Pongal festival — participants from **multiple villages** come together, fostering inter-village social bonds.

2. **Rath Yatras (Chariot Processions):** Jagannath Rath Yatra in Puri, Odisha — pilgrims and participants from **various villages** participate, creating shared religious and cultural identity across rural communities.

3. **Village Fairs and Melas:** Annual fairs attract participants from neighboring villages — platforms for cultural exchange, economic activities, and social interactions.

> **Conclusion:** Indian villages are NOT isolated "little republics" but **dynamic, interconnected communities** shaped by social, economic, and cultural change. Villagers are active agents adapting and reshaping their societies.

## 4. AGRARIAN RELATIONS IN INDIAN VILLAGES

### Definition
The **agrarian social structure** in India refers to the organization of rural life around **agriculture**, marked by:
- Caste hierarchies
- Land ownership and tenancy patterns
- Gender inequalities

Land is often concentrated in the hands of a few, while most farmers are tenants or laborers.

### Two Key Components of Agrarian Social Structure
(Scholar's name is blank in the notes — likely refers to a sociologist/economist)
1. **Technological arrangements** — machinery and technology for agricultural purposes
2. **Social arrangements** — power structure, land ownership, and control

Relationship: As technological advancements increase → reliance on social arrangements decreases; and vice versa.

---

### Peasant Society — A.L. Kroeber's Concept
- Peasant society = characterized by **agricultural predominance**.
- Kroeber defined it as a **"part society"** — signifying reliance on a larger society across technological, economic, political, administrative, religious, and moral spheres.
- Social structures are intricately woven into the fabric of **caste and class relationships**.

---

### Daniel Thorner's Classification of Peasant Society (3 Parts)

| Category | Local Term | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Proprietors / Landowners** | Malik | Mostly rich upper-caste groups owning large tracts. Held dominant power. Example: Zamindars, Jagirdars pre-independence |
| **Working Peasants / Tenants** | Kisan | Largely middle castes. Small landowners or tenants leasing land from Maliks. "Backbone of cultivation" |
| **Agricultural Laborers** | Mazdoor | Mostly landless workers from lower castes. Dependent on wages, sharecropping, or casual labor |

> Post-independence: This structure has seen significant decline due to **social mobility and depeasantization** → rise of new powerful classes and castes.

---

### D.N. Dhanangre (1983) — Extended Classification
Critiqued Thorner's categories as insufficient. Suggested drawing from **Lenin and Mao's** analysis of agrarian classes in Russian and Chinese societies:

1. **Landlords** — large landowners, often not cultivating themselves
2. **Rich peasants** — owned enough land to hire laborers
3. **Middle peasants** — small landholders, usually working with family labor
4. **Poor peasants** — very small plots, often dependent on others
5. **Landless laborers** — no land at all, survived on daily wage labor

Dhanangre noted: social composition of agrarian classes in terms of **castes, religious or ethnic groups** is **far too complex** in different regions of India to reduce into any simplistic formulae.

---

### Social Stratification in Agrarian Societies — Caste-Class Links

| Agrarian Class | Caste Groups |
|---|---|
| Landlords and rich landowners (non-cultivating) | **Brahmin, Thakur, Bania** (North India); **Kamma, Reddi, Rao** (Andhra Pradesh); **Okkaliga, Lingayat** (Mysore/Karnataka); **Patidar** (Gujarat); **Kunbi Maratha, Deshmukh** (Maharashtra) |
| Middle and poor peasantry | Traditional castes of cultivators and artisans; largely debt-burdened |
| Landless laborers | Predominantly **untouchable castes / Scheduled Castes** |

---

### Changes in Agrarian Social Structure

| Change | Details |
|---|---|
| **Land Reforms** | Redistribution of land; increased number of small and marginal farmers; reduced influence of large landowners |
| **Tenancy Patterns** | Tenancy laws amended → better protection for tenants, improved access to credit |
| **Caste-based Hierarchies** | Social mobility increased through education; caste's influence in determining social status declining |
| **Gender Inequalities** | Women's participation in agriculture improved through government policies promoting gender equality |

**Reasons for changes:**
- Government policies (land reforms, gender equality schemes)
- Economic factors (growth of non-agricultural industries, service sector)
- Social factors (education, increased mobility)
- Technological advances (improved irrigation, farm machinery → changed land ownership patterns)

## 5. IMPACT OF GLOBALIZATION ON INDIAN VILLAGES

### Definition of Globalization
- **Anthony Giddens:** "The gradual integration of economies and societies across the world."
- Refers to the **interconnectedness and interdependence** of nations, cultures, and communities at a global scale.
- Accelerated since the 18th century due to advances in transportation and communication.

### Indian Villages — Pre-Globalization
- Centers of **self-sufficiency** and **collectivism**.
- Followed pre-literate and early methods of economic practices.
- **Jajmani system** was the dominant economic arrangement — contributed to well-founded social relations between different caste people.

---

### Impact of Globalization on Indian Villages

#### 1. Major Trends in Village Economy — A.R. Desai's Perspective
Four major trends:
- Transformation from **subsistence to surplus economy**
- Introduction of **modern technology**
- **Abolition of intermediaries** like Zamindars
- Emergence of **associations and institutions** linked with urban and national organizations

#### 2. Transformation of Agrarian Relations
- **Chand and Srivastava's Study (2014 — Jamgod Village, Madhya Pradesh):**
  - Traditional agrarian relations favoring local dominant caste have been disrupted.
  - Shift towards **contract-based employment** over traditional payment in kind.

#### 3. Depeasantization and Change in Status Calculations
- **Tilche's Study (Patidars of Sundarana Village, Gujarat):**
  - Globalization enabled Patidars (traditional agricultural community) to free themselves from agriculture through education, employment, and emigration to urban areas.
  - Reshaping their **status calculations** — farming no longer a marker of status.

#### 4. Emergence of New Conflicts and Changes in Traditional Practices
- **Simpson's Analysis (2016) of Various Village Studies:**
  - Market economy and education have given rise to conflicts between agricultural and non-agricultural ways of life.
  - Leading to **decline of the jajmani system** and **reduced appeal for Sanskritisation**.

#### 5. Disruption of Joint Families
- **Iravati Karve's** observation: Globalization has contributed to the **disruption of joint families** in Indian villages (weakening of ties).

#### 6. Decline in Jajmani System
- **William Wiser's Definition:** The Jajmani system — where each caste group provides standardized services to other castes.
- Greatly hit by globalization — rarely observed today in some rural areas.
- **Causes of decline:** Role of the state, technological innovations, and political mobilization.

#### 7. Impact on Social Customs and Traditions
- Westernization of culture through global media has **eroded traditional values and customs**, questioning age-old practices.

#### 8. Impact on Agriculture
- Globalization facilitated mobility and connectivity → **rapid migration** for employment outside the agricultural sector.
- Agricultural tools evolved with mechanization, improved seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides — but primarily **benefiting big, rich farmers**.

> **Conclusion:** Impact of globalization is multifaceted — transformations in agrarian relations, depeasantization, changes in status calculations, emergence of new conflicts, disruption of traditional practices, and decline of the Jajmani system.

## 6. INDIAN VILLAGE AS A SOCIAL SYSTEM

### Definitions

- **System:** An orderly arrangement; inter-relationship of parts where every part has a fixed place and definite role. Parts do not exist in isolation — bound by constant interaction.

- **Social System (General):** "The patterned series of interrelationships existing between individuals, groups, and institutions forming a coherent whole."

- **Talcott Parsons' Definition:** "Social system consists of a plurality of social actors engaged in more or less stable **interaction** according to **shared cultural norms and meaning**."
  - Interaction is **'patterned interaction'**.
  - Takes under its orbit all diverse **sub-systems** — economic, political, religious, and others.

---

### Key Features of a Social System (Parsons)

| Feature | Meaning | Indian Village Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Plurality of Individual Actors** | Multiple interacting individuals within the system | Indian village comprises diverse population — different castes, occupations, age groups |
| **Aims and Objectives** | Every social system must have specific aims; avoid aimlessness | Village pursues aims of maintaining social harmony, preserving cultural traditions, ensuring well-being of members |
| **Order and Pattern** | Organized structure with order among functional units | Economic, political, religious, and other subsystems coexist and interact in structured way |
| **Functional Unity** | Unity between constituents through functional relationships | Economic activities, religious practices, and social interactions contribute to overall stability |
| **Maintenance** | Achieved through socialization and social control | Socialization ensures cultural norm transmission to younger generations; social control enforces rules |

---

### How Indian Village Qualifies as a Social System

**Constant interaction** exists between individuals; no anonymity because of small population and high density.

#### 1. Caste and Jajmani System (Economic-Ritual Subsystem)
- **Plurality of actors:** Different castes (Brahmins, artisans, service castes) form multiple actors in constant interaction.
- **Order and pattern:** Caste hierarchy gives a fixed order; Jajmani defines who provides what service.
- **Functional unity:** Interdependence in Jajmani (e.g., barber cuts hair → farmer gives grain) keeps village running like a body with organs.
- Caste-Jajmani = **subsystem ensuring economic and ritual order**.

**Diagram — Jajmani System:**
```
Kamin (Services) ←→ Jajman ←→ Kumhaar (Pots/earthen materials)
   [Food grains]          [Food Grains]
Lohaar (Metal items) ←→ Jajman ←→ Nai (Hair Dressing)
   [Food grains]          [Food Grains]
```

#### 2. Panchayati Raj System (Political Subsystem)
- **Aims and objectives:** Ensures local governance, justice, and development.
- **Order and pattern:** Democratic rules, elections, and dispute resolution bring stability.
- **Maintenance:** Social control exercised by panchayats — reduces conflict, enforces norms.
- Panchayat = **political subsystem** aligning with Parsons' idea of maintaining order.

#### 3. Joint Family System (Socialization Subsystem)
- **Plurality of actors:** Many generations live together, interacting daily.
- **Functional unity:** Family members divide roles — elders guide, adults earn, children learn.
- **Maintenance:** Socialization of children, discipline of members, and transmission of culture ensure continuity.
- Joint family = **socialization and control subsystem** ensuring value transmission.

---

### Summary — Three Subsystems of Indian Village

| Subsystem | Role |
|---|---|
| **Caste-Jajmani** | Economic-ritual order |
| **Panchayati Raj** | Political order and control |
| **Joint Family** | Socialization and cultural maintenance |

Together, they fulfill **all five features of Parsons' social system**: plurality, aims, order, functional unity, and maintenance.

> **Conclusion:** The Indian village is a **cohesive social system**, and within the larger Indian society, it acts as a **subsystem**. The Indian village, with its constant interaction, deep-rooted social structures (caste, Jajmani), and the role of Panchayati Raj and joint family in socialization and control, exhibits all crucial features of a social system.

## 7. TRADITIONAL CULTURE

### Definition
- **George Foster** (American anthropologist) developed the concept of "Traditional Culture" from his fieldwork in **Mexico (Latin America)**.
- Defined as: "Cultural practices and beliefs **transmitted from one generation to the next**, creating continuity and stability in society."

---

### Key Features of Traditional Culture

1. **Stability and Coherence**
   - Traditions preserved carefully, often through **oral transmission** (songs, rhymes, rituals).
   - People follow the path of their **forefathers**, ensuring continuity.

2. **Identity and Continuity**
   - Provides a **sense of belonging and order** in society.
   - Festivals, fairs, and community gatherings reinforce cultural values.

3. **Adaptiveness and Change**
   - While traditions are stable, they may **adapt slowly** to environment and new challenges.
   - Change is often **resisted** if it threatens core cultural values.

---

### How Traditional Culture Creates Barriers to Social Change

- Cultural patterns may **resist modernization** or new practices.
- Traditions can become **cultural barriers** that hinder reforms.

**Global Examples:**
- **Gender roles in rural Africa** limit women's education and employment.
- **Resistance to Western medicine** among indigenous groups due to faith in traditional healing.
- **Resource use in tribal South America** (hunting, fishing traditions) conflicting with conservation laws.

---

### Indian Examples of Traditional Culture as a Barrier

#### 1. Caste System
- One of the oldest cultural practices in India.
- Enforces **rigid social segregation** — interaction, marriage, even residence shaped by caste.
- Higher castes controlled resources and rituals; lower castes marginalized.
- **Limits social mobility** and blocks reforms aimed at equality.
- Example: Dalits still face exclusion from village resources such as wells and temples.

#### 2. Sanitation Practices
- Study by **Dean Spears and Michael Geruso (2018):** Hindus were far less likely than Muslims to use toilets, even at the **same income level**.
- Traditional belief that **open defecation away from home is "purer"** than keeping waste inside = cultural barrier to modern sanitation.
- Leads to high levels of open defecation → child mortality and poor health outcomes.
- Despite government investment in sanitation (e.g., **Swachh Bharat Abhiyan**), this cultural barrier persists.

#### 3. Caste Segregation in Villages — Karnataka Case
- Research by **Naveen Bharathi et al. (2020):** **Intra-village segregation in Karnataka is greater than black-white segregation in the American South.**
- Ethnographic research by **Spate and Learmonth (1954):** Documents how residential space in rural India mirrors caste hierarchy.
- **Village example — Amminabhavi (near Dharwad, Karnataka):**
  - Dalits pushed to the **periphery** of the village.
  - Dominant landholding castes occupy the **center** of the village.
  - Other worker castes (Talwars, Shepherds, Washermen) relegated to margins.
  - Village headman and kin control the best land parcels, often living in a large "manor house."
- Ambedkar called Indian villages a **"sink of localism and a den of narrow-mindedness"** — pointing to such entrenched practices.

#### 4. Child Marriage
- Rooted in cultural values of **family honor and purity** — girls married off at a young age.
- **Restricts education and economic opportunities** for women.
- Increases risks of early pregnancies, poor health, and domestic violence.
- Despite legal prohibition, persists in many rural areas because deeply tied to traditional notions of social order.

#### 5. Female Infanticide
- Traditional preference for **male children** → female infanticide and sex-selective abortions.
- Cultural beliefs: sons ensure lineage, provide economic support, perform last rites; daughters seen as financial burden (dowry).
- Result: **Distorted sex ratio** — itself a barrier to gender equality and social reform.

---

### Criticism of Foster's Concept of Traditional Culture
- Too **simplistic** — assumes cultures are static.
- Overlooks that traditions are **dynamic, evolving, and negotiable**.
- Sometimes used to justify **cultural purity** and exclude minorities.

---

### Conclusion
Foster's concept highlights the **dual role of traditions**:
- On one hand: preserves **identity, continuity, and stability**.
- On the other: can act as **barriers to social change** (caste segregation, sanitation beliefs).

For meaningful development, reforms must **engage with cultural values** rather than work against them.

> Culture can be seen as both a **shield** that protects communities and a **wall** that obstructs progressive change unless addressed with sensitivity.
