## 1. Ethnicity — Concept and Perspectives

### Definition
- **Ethnicity** (broader sense): Self-consciousness of a group of people united, or closely related, by shared experience — such as language, religious belief, common heritage, etc.
- In simple terms: refers to a common ancestral, social and cultural experience; more often identifies with **race, region, religion, language** and other cultural elements.

### Six Connotations of Ethnicity (John Hutchinson and Anthony D. Smith)
1. Shares a common **name**
2. A myth of common **ancestry**
3. **Memories** of common past
4. Other elements of a common **culture — religion, custom & language**
5. A link with a homeland or **region**
6. A sense of **solidarity**

---

### Three Perspectives to Study Ethnicity

#### 1. Primordial Approach
- **Proposed by:** Edward Shils (1957) and Clifford Geertz (1961)
- "Primordial" means original or from the beginning.
- Ethnic identity is something we are **born with**, not something we choose.
- Based on **deep emotional ties** — blood relations, religion, language, culture.
- People feel a **natural attachment** to their group (it is part of who they are).
- Ethnic conflict happens when people feel their group identity is **threatened by others**.
- **Criticism:** Humans do not have only one identity. Even within one language group, people differ in accent, customs, and beliefs.

#### 2. Instrumentalist Approach
- **Promoted by:** Paul Brass (1991), Ted Gurr, Abner Cohen
- Ethnic identity is **not natural or fixed** — it is constructed and used by elites (politicians, religious leaders, teachers).
- Elites **manipulate ethnic identities** to gain political power, resources, or influence.
- Ethnicity becomes a **tool or instrument** for achieving particular goals.
- **Criticism:** Assumes people are easily manipulated; in reality, people hold strong cultural attachments that cannot always be shaped for political gain.

#### 3. Situated Primordial Approach
- **Proposed by:** Carsten Wieland
- **Combines both earlier views** — ethnicity is partly given (primordial) and also used strategically by elites (instrumentalist).
- People feel a **deep emotional bond** with their identity, yet that bond can be **activated or shaped by leaders** for political/social purposes.
- **Criticism:** Primordial view does not explain *how* shared identity turns into group mobilisation; instrumentalist view fails to explain *why* people respond to elite manipulation. The situated primordial view offers a more **balanced understanding**.

### Conclusion on Ethnicity
- Ethnic identity shapes how individuals connect with community and distinguish themselves from others.
- Gives people a **sense of belonging** but can become a **source of division** when politicised.
- In India, ethnicity is one of the major challenges for the **nation-state** — visible in autonomy demands, secessionist movements, insurgencies, and ethnic conflicts.
- Balancing **unity with cultural diversity** is essential for national integration.

## 2. Ethnic Conflicts and Political Developments

### Definition
An **ethnic conflict** is a conflict between two or more contending ethnic groups. The source of the conflict may be political, social, economic or religious — but individuals must expressly fight for their ethnic group's position within society.

### Colonial Context
- During colonial period, tribal land was exposed to outsiders.
- Moneylenders and local landlords encroached on tribal lands and exploited tribals.
- Exploitation pushed tribals into extreme poverty → anger and discontent → rebellion.

### Five Types of Tribal Movements (Surajit Sinha, 1972)
1. **Ethnic rebellious movements** — during British rule in the 18th and 19th centuries
   - Birsa movement among Mundas
   - Kol rebellion (1832)
   - Santhal rebellion (1857–58)
   - Naga rebellion (1880s)
2. **Reform movements** — emulating the pattern of higher Hindu castes
   - Bhagat movement among the Oraon
   - Vaishnavite movement among the Bhumij
   - Kehr movement among the Santhals
3. **Political movements** — for tribal states within the Indian Union, post-Independence
   - Jharkhand movement in Chotanagpur and Orissa
   - Hill states movement in Assam and Madhya Pradesh
4. **Secessionist movements** — Naga and Mizo movements
5. **Movements relating to agrarian unrest**
   - Naxalbari movement (1967)
   - Birsadal movement (1968–69)

> Note: Among these, **Political movements (positive sense of Regionalism)** and **Secessionist movements (negative sense of Regionalism)** are associated with Regionalism.

## 3. Santhal Rebellion

### Factors
- **Social:** Introduction of new land ownership system — interfered with traditional collective landholding.
- **Economic:** High taxes crippled tribals; moneylenders exploited them; many Santhals alienated from their lands.
- **Political:** Rich landlords with money power spread influence in tribal lands; local officials and British patrons sided with exploiters; courts of law only helped exploiters.

### Consequence
- Under leadership of **Kanu and Siddu**, Santhals started to rebel.
- Santhals attacked police, moneylenders, and looted what they could.
- Army was ordered to crush the rebellion. **Army curbed the rebellion.** Leaders were arrested.
- **After 1 year the rebellion subsided.**

---

## 4. Munda Rebellion

### Factors
- **Socio-religious:** Cultural contacts with Hindu royalty → few Mundas adopted Hinduism; others adopted Christianity with false hope.
- **Economic:** Hindu priests donated a larger portion of land; priests assumed traits of landlords; poor Munda cultivators faced high taxes and lived in poverty.
- **Political:** British rulers established dominance; landlords were given the power of police; whenever there was a clash between tribes and landlords, British always sided with exploiters.

### Consequence
- Under leadership of **Birsa Munda**, around 6,000 Mundas started to rebel; attacked all outsiders.
- Fierce fighting → Mundas lost; **Birsa was arrested and sent to prison in Ranchi.**
- After release, Birsa led another rebellion — rebels didn't spare Christian Mundas either.
- With army and modern weapons, **Munda rebellion was crushed.**
- The settlement and survey operations in Ranchi district took shape due to this Munda rebellion.

## 5. Naga Rebellion

### Situation
- Nagas are freedom-loving; do not bow to authority.
- **Charles Pawsey** (1945) planted the idea of seceding from India and forming a separate country.
- Under leadership of **Charles Pawsey**, **Zapu Phizo** declared Nagaland would not be part of independent India (1947).
- This **separatist tendency** aimed to define their socio-cultural identity and fear losing it in the ocean of Hindu nationalism.

### Consequence
- Phizo and followers started a rebellion to form a **separate country.**
- Formation of the **state of Nagaland in 1952** was a result of this rebellion.
- **M. Alemchiba Ao** comments that the main cause of the conflict is the government's lack of knowledge of tribal interests and needs.
- The 'separatist' tendency did not end — certain groups still exhibit secessionist tendency under the banner of **Greater Nagalim.**

---

## 6. Mizo Rebellion

### Situation
- Mizos did not like political interference from outside; fueled by unfriendly countries China and Pakistan.
- Mizos became a part of India after independence.

### Factors
- **Geographic:** Much alienated from rest of India; poor communication.
- **Religion:** Christian missionaries and conversion of tribals further widened the gap.

### Consequence
- 1954: Demand for **separate State — Mizoland** started.
- Movement grew; demand for **complete separation from India** with terrorist campaign.
- Response: 1970 — **new state of Meghalaya** created; **1971 — Mizoram declared Union Territory** (became a State in **1987**); Assam formed.

## 7. Reasons for Tribal Discontent

- **Harsh forest laws** and regulations (e.g., Indian Forest Act, 1927 — regulated rights of tribals over forest land and produce).
- **Inability of legislation** to protect tribal lands.
  - **Chotanagpur Tenancy Act, 1908** — restricts transfer of tribal land to non-tribals.
  - **Santhal Pargana Act, 1949** — does not allow transfer of land even for public purposes like education, medical facilities, skill development, urbanisation.
- **Ineffective measures** to rehabilitate tribal population.
- **Inadequacy of credit facilities** to combat tribal indebtedness.
  - Recent measure: MP government (2019) waived all loans taken by STs from unlicensed moneylenders through amendment to Anusuchit Jan Jaati Sahukar Viniyam 1972.
- **Delay of implementing** recommendations of high-level bodies — K.S. Singh Committee, XAXA Committee (2013), Dhebar Commission (1960), Bhuria Commission (2002).

## 8. Political Developments Post-Independence

- Post-Independence India was determined to end tribal misery.
- **Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights, Minorities and Tribal and Excluded Areas** — headed by **Sardar Patel**. Sub-committees:
  - **Minorities Sub-Committee** — headed by H.C. Mukherjee
  - **North-East Frontier Tribal Areas and Assam Excluded & Partially Excluded Areas Sub-Committee** — headed by **Gopinath Bardoloi**
  - **Excluded and Partially Excluded Areas (other than Assam) Sub-Committee** — headed by **A.V. Thakkar**
  - North-West Frontier Tribal Areas Sub-Committee
- Government made several important political developments: FRA 2006, Chotanagpur Tenancy Act 1908, Santhal Pargana Act 1949.
- Tribal rebellion is **not a sudden outburst** of anger but a **result of prolonged socio-economic-political exploitation** that pushed them beyond their tolerance limit.

## 9. Regionalism

### Definition
- Regionalism is an ideology and political movement that seeks to advance the causes of regions. Driven by loyalty to a **distinct region with a homogenous population** in terms of cultural, social, political, economic aspiration or ethnicity.
- **Sulli Mukherjee:** Regionalism is a process of self-determination on linguistic, religious or ethnic lines which **challenges the sovereignty** of a country and hinders nation-building.
- **David L. Sills (1968):** Defines a region as "a **homogenous area** with physical and cultural characteristics distinct from those of neighboring areas." The region possesses a sense of identity distinct from the rest of the country — having both territorial reference and socio-cultural and psychological consciousness.
- **Core of regionalism:** Profound sense of identity that prevails — widely shared sentiment of togetherness and a **we-feeling** that often reinforces a sense of separateness.
- Regionalism refers to an ideology primarily based on awareness of **linguistic, religious, ethnic and cultural identity** of a particular geographical region.

### Regionalism in Positive Sense
- Inspires people to **develop brotherhood and unity** to protect interests of regions and promote welfare and development.
- Plays an important role in **building the nation** if demands of regions are accommodated by the political system.
- Regional recognition through statehood or **state autonomy gives self-determination** — people feel empowered and happy.
- **Internal self-determination** (linguistic, tribal, religious, regional) has been the predominant form of regionalism in India.
- Regional identities in India have **not always** defined themselves in opposition to national identity — they bring people closer to local and regional governance.
- **Example:** The 3 Tribal Autonomous District Councils in Mizoram have served to protect an otherwise endangered tribal identity — providing a democratic platform for former separatists to become a party of governance, significantly reducing political extremism.
- Balanced regional development; socio-cultural diversity given due respect.

### Regionalism in Negative Sense
- Indicates excessive attachment to one's region — a significant threat to the unity and integrity of the country.
- When people identify more strongly with their region than their nation, nationalism is undercut by regionalism.

### Reasons for Regionalism in India

1. **Economic Backwardness and Exploitation — Jharkhand**
   - Historically part of Bihar; remained economically underdeveloped despite rich mineral resources.
   - Local tribal population faced land alienation, displacement due to mining and industrial projects, and lack of representation in development decisions.
   - Continuous neglect → demands for regional autonomy → **Jharkhand state in 2000**.

2. **Unmet Expectations from the State — Left Wing Extremism (LWE) Areas**
   - Regions affected by LWE: parts of Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Jharkhand, Andhra Pradesh.
   - Regional alienation due to governance failure and poor service delivery.
   - Tribal and landless groups felt excluded from development → regional discontent that extremist movements exploited.

3. **Neglect of Local Aspirations — Telangana**
   - Telangana movement arose from perceived injustice in water sharing, employment, resource allocation within united Andhra Pradesh.
   - Andhra-dominated leadership ignored Telangana's developmental needs.
   - After decades of agitation by student and political groups, **Telangana became a separate state in 2014**.

4. **Sense of Insecurity and Fear of Domination — Gorkhaland**
   - Gorkhaland movement in Darjeeling, West Bengal — reflects fear of cultural and linguistic marginalisation among Nepali-speaking Gorkhas.
   - Sought separate administrative recognition.
   - **Gorkhaland Territorial Administration (GTA)** formed, but discontent continues.

5. **Quest for Self-Identity and Cultural Fulfilment — Linguistic Regionalism**
   - Karnataka's **Kannada Rakshana Vedike** — to protect Kannada language, local jobs, regional culture.
   - Maharashtra: **Samyukta Maharashtra Movement (1950s–60s)** — demanded unified Marathi-speaking state with Mumbai as capital → evolved into Marathi pride movements.
   - **Dravidian movement in Tamil Nadu (1950s–60s)** — Tamil identity and opposition to Hindi imposition → gave rise to parties like the **DMK**.

## 10. Tribes and Regionalism

### Jharkhand Movement

- Jharkhand has tribal population spread over Bihar, West Bengal, Madhya Pradesh, and Orissa.
- Movement for separate Jharkhand state started in **1939**.

**Reasons:**
1. Rampant corruption by government officials → denudation of forest and exploitation.
2. Uncontrolled **land alienation** of tribals.
3. **Underdevelopment and unemployment** of tribals despite setting up many developmental projects in their lands.

**Key organisations/personalities:**
- **Jharkhand Mukti Morcha (JMM)** and **All Jharkhand Students Union (AJSU)** formed to support the movement.
- **Dr. Ram Dyal Munda** (former Vice Chancellor, Ranchi University): The government took over forests ostensibly to manage them, but gross **mismanagement led to virtual disappearance of forests** — the mainstay of Jharkhand economy. Projects acquired more land than necessary; used for private housing; **original landowners became destitutes.**

---

### Naga Movement

- Nagas spread over Nagaland.
- Post-Independence: non-tribal people came in large numbers to settle in Nagaland hills.
- Outsiders' greed → **economic exploitation, alienation and insecurity of Nagas** → separatist movement.
- Led by **Angami Zaphu Phizo**, a section of Nagas declared independence from India in 1951.
- Phizo turned down many offers of negotiated settlement.
- **Naga National Council** launched armed struggle for Naga sovereignty.
- Earlier demands: **separate country, national flag and constitution**.
- After violent insurgency, a section signed agreement with Government of India — but not acceptable to other rebels.
- Recent times: Nagas feel integral part of the nation; some agreements made between Naga people and Government of India → **Naga insurgency was reduced**.

---

### Zeliangrong Movement

- Zeliangrongs: minority tribe spread over Assam, Manipur, and Nagaland.
- United under **Zeliangrong People's Convention (ZPC)** to seek separate states.
- Movement started in the 1930s when they revolted against the British.
- State government neglected development of Zeliangrong-inhabited areas.
- Struggle led by **Jadonang** and **Rani Gaidinliu**.
- Since inception of ZPC in 1980, they have been demanding integration of Zeliangrong areas into a political unit.
- Movement is largely **free from violent activities**.

---

### Mizo Movement

- After Independence, Mizo Hills made an **autonomous district within Assam**.
- Some Mizos believed they were never part of British India and did not belong to the Indian Union.
- Movement for **secession** gained popular support after the Assam government failed to respond adequately to the **great famine of 1959 in Mizo hills**.
- Mizos' anger led to formation of **Mizo National Front (MNF)** under leadership of **Laldenga**.
- **1966:** MNF started an armed campaign for independence → two-decade-long battle between Mizo insurgents and Indian army.
- MNF fought a **guerrilla war**, got **support from Pakistan**, and secured shelter in **East Pakistan**.
- Indian security forces used repressive measures including the **Air Force** — caused more anger and alienation.

#### Laldenga (1937–1990)
- Founder and leader of the Mizo National Front.
- Turned into a rebel after the experience of the famine of 1959.
- Led an armed struggle against India for two decades.
- Reached a settlement and **signed an agreement with Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi in 1986**.
- Became the **Chief Minister** of the newly created State of Mizoram.

#### Peace Accord 1986
- **In 1986, a peace agreement was signed between Rajiv Gandhi and Laldenga.**
- Mizoram was **granted full-fledged statehood** with special powers; MNF agreed to **give up secessionist struggle**.
- Laldenga became Chief Minister.
- Today, Mizoram is one of the **most peaceful places in the region** and has taken big strides in literacy and development.

### Conclusion on Regionalism
- Regionalism is **not only a geographical-political phenomenon** but is a dynamic and multi-dimensional concept encompassing geo-physical, psychological, socio-cultural and politico-economic variables.
- A country like India must have better appreciation of regional cultures and minimise regional imbalance.
- **M.N. Srinivas:** Regionalism can never be tackled as merely a **law and order problem** — should be tackled with **care, sympathy and appreciation of long suppressed tribal aspirations.**

## 11. Unrest Among Tribal Communities — Naxalism / LWE

### Overview
- Naxalism (Left-Wing Extremism) is one of the serious issues in India.
- LWE can be observed in almost all tribal belts.
- Result of decades of **tribal oppression and negligence** from the government.
- The "**jal, jangal, zameen**" that belonged to tribals were openly exploited.
- The tribal voice in response to this exploitation is **Naxalism/LWE**.
- **Alpna Sran:** Argues that Naxals function like a **parallel government**, enjoying confidence and support of the people of lands they control. To establish themselves as primary law, Naxals start by attacking police and local elites, then 'tax' various economic activities.

### Reasons for Tribal Support to Naxals
- Adivasis prefer this **'jungle sarkar'** over the Indian state because new arrangements are to their benefit.
- **Example:** A key revenue source is the collection and **sale of Tendu leaves** (used to make beedis).
  - Earlier, this collection was controlled by **upper-caste elites** who paid low wages and controlled all profits.
  - Naxals came, **doubled wages**, and even awarded some contracts to local Adivasi and other impoverished groups.
  - Some of the 'tax' they collected was rerouted to **schools, medical camps and clinics** in the region.

### Two Perspectives on Naxalism
1. It is a **threat to national security** — solution is security-centric law and order.
2. It is a **socio-economic problem** — because of tribal exploitation for nearly 200 years.

---

### Underlying Roots (Problems) Fuelling LWE

#### 1. Unemployment and Poverty
- Post cultural contact with tribals (especially during colonial period), the situation worsened.
- Lack of developmental activities in tribal areas → less employment.
- Educated tribal boys and girls remain unemployed and poverty-struck → naturally attracted to sympathetic Naxals.
- **Example:** In Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, mining expansion displaced local youth without providing alternative jobs. Many joined Maoist ranks as daily wage opportunities dried up.
- **Walter Fernandes (1996):** Described such youth as the "first victims of displacement and the first recruits for rebellion" — showing how development without inclusion fuels alienation.

#### 2. Tribals as "Victims of Progress"
- Most developmental projects find their base in forest/tribal lands.
- Due to these projects, many tribal people are displaced.
- As per Planning Commission: **not even 20% of these are rehabilitated properly**.
- Tribals made to suffer as the "sacrificial goat for development."
- **Example:** Sardar Sarovar Dam on Narmada displaced thousands of Bhil and Tadvi tribals; only 17% fully rehabilitated (Planning Commission data).
- **Walter Fernandes and Stan (Jebaraj):** Termed this phenomenon **development-induced displacement** — where tribals become sacrificial goats of progress.

#### 3. Displacement and Forced Evictions
- Tribal communities **forcibly removed** from traditional lands in the name of development of mining, irrigation, and power projects.
- Acquisition of large tracts of land for "public purposes" without adequate compensation or rehabilitation → major cause of increased discontent.
- **Example:** In **Kalinga Nagar (Odisha, 2006)**, land acquisition for a Tata Steel project led to violent protests, leaving 13 tribal protesters dead. The incident intensified Maoist influence.

#### 4. Forest and Livelihood Related Reasons
- Inadequate access to forest endangers food security.
- Disruption of traditional livelihoods and absence of alternative employment options.
- Denial of traditional rights to common property resources.
- **Example:** The **Forest Conservation Act (1980)** restricted shifting cultivation and forest produce collection in Bastar, affecting the livelihood of **Muria and Maria Gonds**.
- **Roy Burman:** Highlighted that the loss of access to forests severs the **ecological and cultural lifeline** of tribal society, producing alienation and unrest.

#### 5. Land Related Reasons
- Failure to legally recognise traditional land rights.
- **Poor enforcement of laws** that prohibit transfer of tribal land to non-tribals in areas covered by **Fifth Schedule**.
- Lack of legal ownership documents for public land used by landless poor.
- Powerful individuals illegally occupying and claiming ownership of government and community lands, including bodies of water.
- **Example:** In **Khammam district (Telangana)**, non-tribals illegally occupied podu (hill) lands despite Fifth Schedule safeguards. FRA 2006 implementation remains below 40% in such areas.
- **B.D. Sharma:** Argued that land alienation is the **single most powerful cause of tribal discontent**, calling it "the root of their revolt."

#### 6. Failing to Implement the Provisions
- Many provisions made for betterment of tribals, but implementation is not strict.
- **Example:** Despite **PESA Act (1996)** mandating Gram Sabha consultation in Scheduled Areas, mining leases in **Dantewada and Korba** were granted without tribal consent.
- **Xaxa Committee Report (2014):** Observed that the state's neglect of constitutional protections (like PESA and FRA) perpetuates "structural violence" against tribal populations.

---

### Findings & Recommendations of the Expert Group (Planning Commission, 2006)
- **Open Approach Advocated:** Recommendation for open approach rather than security-centric one.
- **Land's Role in LWE:** Emphasis on recognising the pivotal role of land in growth of LWE.
- **Land Distribution for Landless:** Recommendation to ensure landless individuals receive land.
- **Better Implementation of FRA:** Urgent need for improved implementation of FRA 2006.
- **Curbing Misuse of Power:** Stress on checking the misuse of power by forest and police officials in tribal areas.
- **Checking Land Acquisition Act Misuse:** Emphasis on scrutinising potential misuse of Land Acquisition Act (Eminent Domain).
- **Highlighting Corporate Impact:** Recognition of negative impact of MNCs and their projects on tribal and marginalised communities.

### Ways to Deal with Naxal Menace (2006 Union Minister Recommendations)
- **Serious Actions Against Naxals** — decisive actions to address security challenge.
- **Inter-State Coordination** — coordinated efforts among states to combat Naxalism.
- **Discontinuation of Peace Dialogues** — cease peace dialogues as a strategic measure.
- **Holistic Policy Formulation** — development and implementation of better holistic policies.
- **Priority Development in Naxal Areas** — prioritised development initiatives in Naxal-affected regions.

### Case Studies on Tribal Unrest

#### Case Study 1: Essar and the Maoist-Affected Mining Belt of Chhattisgarh
- A 2010 WikiLeaks cable revealed that **Essar Group**, operating mining and steel projects in **Chhattisgarh's Dantewada region**, was compelled to pay "significant amounts" as **protection money to Maoist groups** to ensure safety of its property and staff.
- Even large corporations and MNCs are **helpless under extremist threats**, forced to negotiate for operational survival.

**Impact on Tribes:**
- Industrial-insurgent tensions deepened tribal insecurity.
- Adivasi communities — caught between Maoist coercion and state militarisation — face **displacement, loss of forest access, and erosion of traditional livelihoods**.
- Initiatives like **Salwa Judum** fractured intra-tribal relations.

#### Case Study 2: Salwa Judum and the Supreme Court Verdict (2011)
- Launched in **2005 in Dantewada and Bijapur (Chhattisgarh)**.
- *Salwa Judum* = "peace march" in Gondi.
- A **state-supported vigilante movement** aimed at mobilising tribal youth against Maoists.
- Government armed them as **Special Police Officers (SPOs)** with minimal training.
- Intended as community defense force → instead led to **widespread violence, displacement, and rights violations**.
- **In 2011, the Supreme Court (Justices Sudershan Reddy & Nijjar)** declared the use of SPOs and *Koya Commandos* **illegal and unconstitutional** under **Articles 14 and 21**.

**Impact on Tribes:**
- Thousands of Adivasis displaced; villages razed; civilians caught between Maoists and state-backed forces suffered severe trauma.
- Breakdown of trust between tribal communities and the state deepened alienation.
- Verdict reinforced that **counter-insurgency must remain within constitutional limits**.

---

### Way Forward for Addressing LWE
- Address structural roots of displacement, poverty, and alienation — as emphasised by the **Xaxa Committee (2014)**.
- **Livelihood Initiatives:** Promoting financial independence through self-help groups, access to credit and marketing opportunities.
- **Upgrading Infrastructure:** Developing infrastructure (especially road networks) through specialised government agencies rather than local contractors.
- **Forest Rights Protection:** Effective implementation of FRA 2006. In **Gadchiroli (Maharashtra)**, Gram Sabhas successfully manage bamboo and tendu collection under FRA — shows that recognition of forest rights reduces alienation and Maoist sympathy.
- **Elinor Ostrom's** concept of **community-based resource governance** supports localised management as sustainable and conflict-resolving.
- **Varad Pandey:** While LWE/Naxal/Maoist violence is definitely a security issue, it is equally a **political and a development issue**. We will not end this war by bullets alone — we will win it only if we **win over hearts and minds.** This can be done only by enabling conditions for grassroots political mobilisation, improving delivery of development schemes (like FRA), and ensuring fair-benefit sharing of resources — in other words, **bridging the trust deficit**.

## 12. Pseudo-Tribalism

### Definition
**Pseudo-tribalism** refers to the phenomenon where **members of a group attempt to create or reinforce a sense of tribal identity**, even if they do not have a **historically legitimate claim** to such an identity.

- In the Indian context: Pseudo-tribalism is the process by which certain sections of society try to **label themselves as 'tribals'** to enjoy concessions and privileges in education and employment.
- Example: Movements of various sections to get the "ST" label.

### Criteria for Specification as a Scheduled Tribe (Presently Followed)
1. Indications of primitive traits
2. Distinctive culture
3. Geographical isolation
4. Shyness of contact with the community at large
5. Backwardness

> Note: These criteria are not spelt out in the Constitution.

---

### Case Study: Gujjars of Rajasthan
- Demanding **5% reservation** under special backwards classes (SBC) category.
- Community began its fight for reservation in **2006** when it demanded inclusion in the **Scheduled Tribe (ST)** reservation.
- Gujjars have been covered under **OBC reservation since 1994**.
- After the **Chopra Committee Report** said Gujjars could not be given ST status, the community settled for 5% reservation under a new SBC category.
- But this took total reservation beyond the **50% ceiling** set by the Supreme Court → Rajasthan High Court quashed it.

**Government Actions:**
- **Vasundhara Raje-led BJP government in 2008** brought a bill for **5% SBC reservation** to Gujjars and four other communities (Banjara/Baldia, Labana, Gadia-Lohar/Gadalia, Raika/Rebari and Gadaria).
- HC stayed the law (December 22, 2010), asked for quantifiable data to support reservation under SBC.
- Government repealed the 2008 Act and introduced a new bill in Assembly in September 2015: *Rajasthan Special Backward Classes (Reservation of Seats in Educational Institutes in the State and of Appointments and Posts in Services under the State) Bill, 2015*.
- Notification issued October 16, 2015. Rajasthan HC struck it down on December 9, 2016 (crossed 50% limit).
- Rajasthan government brought a bill in 2017 to raise OBC quota from 21% to 26% for granting 5% reservation to Gujjars and other castes — also struck down by HC.

**Leadership:**
- **Colonel (Retd.) Kirori Singh Bainsla** leading community since 2006 (uprooted railway tracks in Hindaun City, Karauli district).
- Contested 2009 Lok Sabha election on BJP ticket; lost by 317 votes.
- In February 2019, his son **Vijay Singh Bainsla** became deputy.

---

### Case Study: Communities in Assam
- **Six communities demanding ST status:** Koch-Rajbongshi, Tai-Ahom, Chutia, Matak, Moran, and Tea Tribes.
- Groups like All Assam Tribal Sangha and Coordination Committee of Tribal Organisations of Assam are **opposing the move** — specifically opposing Koch-Rajbongshi and Tai-Ahom who reigned over large swathes of present-day Assam, Bangladesh, and West Bengal during the medieval period.

---

### Case Study: Manipur's Meitei Community
- Manipur's **dominant Meitei community wants Scheduled Tribe status**.
- Tribal groups in the state say if Meitei are granted ST status, 'Meitei millionaires' will encroach on their land.
- The demand was discussed in the **Manipur Assembly as far back as 1981**.
- Meitei elites (Vaishnav Hindus) saw the ST tag as an affront to their status.
- **Meiteis abandoned the traditional Sanamahi faith** and converted en masse to Hinduism in the late 17th and early 18th century.
- The demand got a new lease of life in 2012 when the Scheduled Tribe Demand Committee wrote memorandums, including to Prime Minister Manmohan Singh.

---

### Case Study: Narikkuravar Community of Tamil Nadu
**Background:**
- The Narikkuravars are a **nomadic, hunter-gatherer** community in Tamil Nadu, traditionally engaged in **bead-making and hunting**.
- Long faced **social exclusion, poverty, and low literacy**.

**Earlier Classification:**
- Earlier listed under **Most Backward Classes (MBC)** in Tamil Nadu.
- Already recognised as **Scheduled Tribe (ST)** in Karnataka and Andhra Pradesh.

**Demand and Inclusion:**
- Due to extreme vulnerability, Tamil Nadu sought ST status for the community.
- In **2022, the Union Government approved their inclusion** in the ST list.

**Significance:**
- Ensures **constitutional protection, reservation benefits**, and access to welfare schemes.
- Recognises their **distinct socio-cultural identity** and addresses long-standing deprivation.

---

### Impact of Granting ST Status to Non-Tribal Groups
1. **Dilution of Benefits** — reduces access to education, employment, and political representation for genuine STs (from Gram Sabha to Lok Sabha).
2. **Further Marginalisation** — already deprived tribal groups risk becoming further marginalised as competition increases.
3. **Distortion of Policy Purpose** — original aim of affirmative action is to uplift historically isolated and indigenous groups; extending ST recognition to non-tribals **undermines constitutional intent**.
4. **Erosion of Tribal Identity** — blurs the distinct cultural and ethnographic identity of indigenous tribes, weakening their social recognition and collective agency.
5. **Political Manipulation** — ST recognition demands often driven by **vote-bank politics**, not genuine socio-anthropological need.
6. **Administrative Complexity** — frequent additions make monitoring and implementation of welfare schemes difficult.
7. **Violation of Ethnographic Criteria** — as per the **Lokur Committee (1965)**, ST identification must rest on traits like distinct culture, geographical isolation, and social backwardness. Inclusion of non-tribals **breaches this anthropological basis**.
8. **Social Tensions** — may trigger **resentment and inter-group conflicts**, particularly in ethnically sensitive states like Assam and Tripura.

### Way Forward (Pseudo-Tribalism)
- **Strengthen ST identification criteria:** Implement clear, objective benchmarks based on social, economic, and cultural indicators (not solely historical narratives). Consider poverty levels, educational attainment, lack of land rights.
- **Invest in alternative development initiatives:** Focus on targeted programs — skill development, healthcare access, livelihood creation — so communities don't solely rely on reservation benefits (e.g., vocational training programs, microfinance schemes, rural infrastructure development).

## 13. Youth Dormitories

### Introduction
- Youth dormitories are found in all parts of the world where tribals reside.
- These dormitories have certain **functions, rules, and beliefs** associated with them.
- This is the place where eligible youth gather in the evening and have fun.
- Finest example: **Bukumatula** of Trobriand Islanders — mentioned by **Malinowski** in his book **"The Sexual Life of Savages in North-Western Melanesia (1929)."**

### Types (Majumdar)
- **Majumdar** did an extensive study on youth dormitories in India; describes both mono-sexual and bisexual youth dormitories.
- **Mono-sexual:** Konyak Nagas — Boys dormitory = **Ban**; Girls dormitory = **Yo**.
- **Bi-sexual:** Oraons call it **Dhumkuriya**.

| Tribe | Dormitory Name | Type |
|-------|---------------|------|
| Konyak Nagas (Boys) | Ban | Mono-sexual |
| Konyak Nagas (Girls) | Yo | Mono-sexual |
| Oraons | Dhumkuriya | Bi-sexual |
| Murias/Gonds of Bastar | Ghotul | Bi-sexual |
| Ho tribe | Giitora | — |
| Munda | — (Jharkhand) | — |
| Nagaland tribes | Morung | — |

### Origin (Controversial)
- The topic of origin is always a matter of debate.
- **Hodson:** Believes youth dormitories may be a "survival" of communal houses, designed to promote community living and sharing of resources.
- **Grigson:** Study of Hill Marias suggests traditional societies did not view the home as appropriate for sexual activity; dormitories were designed to **separate sexual activity from the home environment**.
- Other anthropologists: Dormitories were designed to **prevent incest** — highlighting importance of avoiding close familial relationships.
- Some believe: Dormitories intended to **protect children from witnessing sexual activity**, which could potentially traumatize them.

### Location
- The dormitories are specially built buildings usually situated in the **outskirts of the village** so members can carry out activities without any disturbance.

### Functions

#### Among Oraons (S.C. Roy — Three-fold Purpose)
1. As the **economic organisation** for the food quest.
2. For training young men in their **social and other duties**.
3. As a place for performance of **magico-religious ceremonies**.

#### Among Gonds (Singh)
1. For **joint economic pursuits**.
2. Training in **social and sexual behavior**.
3. For **accommodation**.

---

### Factors for the Decline of Youth Dormitories

1. **Cultural Contact and Religious Conversion**
   - Contact with missionaries and spread of Christianity, Hinduism, and Islam disrupted traditional tribal belief systems.
   - Missionaries often criticised dormitories as **immoral** or "heathen" institutions.
   - **Example:** Among Murias and Gonds of Bastar, missionaries actively discouraged the *ghotul*, leading to its disuse in several villages.

2. **Influence of Modern Education**
   - Introduction of formal schooling systems replaced traditional modes of youth education.
   - Western education shifted training and discipline from community to State-controlled institutions.
   - **Verrier Elwin (1947):** Noted that the spread of modern schools led to neglect of indigenous systems such as the *ghotul* and *dhumkuria*.

3. **Urbanisation and Migration**
   - Expansion of roads, towns, and markets created exposure to urban lifestyles.
   - Young people migrating for labour or education found little relevance in dormitory-based communal living.
   - **Example:** Ho and Munda youth in Jharkhand increasingly moved to nearby towns, leaving behind village dormitory traditions like *giitora*.

4. **Loss of Autonomy and Cultural Inferiority Complex**
   - Tribal values were mocked by outsiders and portrayed as "primitive."
   - Many tribals internalised this stigma, feeling ashamed of traditional practices.
   - **Example:** The Ho tribes now **deny the existence of the *giitora*** under social pressure to appear "modern."

5. **Development Projects and Displacement**
   - Construction of **dams, mines, industries, and forest projects** led to large-scale displacement of tribal populations.
   - Resettlement areas lacked the social and ecological setting needed to maintain collective institutions like youth dormitories.
   - **Example:** Displacement caused by **Hirakud Dam and Rourkela Steel Plant** fragmented tribal communities and disrupted institutions like the *ghotul* and *morung*.

6. **Governmental and Administrative Neglect**
   - State policies emphasised welfare schemes, not cultural preservation.
   - No institutional support to **revive or integrate dormitory traditions** into modern tribal education.

7. **Change in Economic Structure**
   - Transition from subsistence economy to cash economy reduced community interdependence.
   - With individuals earning wages outside the village, the **collective labour and social training functions** of the dormitory weakened.

### Conclusion on Youth Dormitories
- **Sachidananda** (who studied central Indian tribes) described the present condition of youth dormitories as: *"It seems that this institution is **breathing its last.** In several villages the only thing left in the name of Dhumkuriya is a dilapidated and neglected house standing as a memorial to a great social institution of tribal folks."*
