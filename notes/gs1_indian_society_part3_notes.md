## PART 1: THEORIES OF ORIGIN OF CASTE

> There are many theories that were put forward to explain the origin of the caste system in India, but none of them gained universal acceptance.

### Overview of Theories

| # | Theory | Key Scholar |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Traditional Theory | (Mythical + Metaphysical versions) |
| 2 | Brahmanical Theory | Abbe Dubois, Ghurye |
| 3 | Racial Theory | Risley |
| 4 | Occupational Theory | Nesfield |
| 5 | Ketkar's Theory | Ketkar |
| 6 | Senart's Theory | Senart |
| 7 | Hutton's Theory of Mana | Hutton |

---

### 1. TRADITIONAL THEORY

- Hindu society is described as a **supernatural-centered society** in which people are lured by abstract truth, searched for through mysticism, not science.
- The theory believes that **caste system has been established by divine ordinance** or at least with divine approval.

**Two Versions:**

#### (a) Mythical Theory
- Regards **four varnas as four castes** — these four 'castes' have emerged from **different parts of Brahma's body**:
  - Brahmins — from the mouth
  - Kshatriyas — from the arms
  - Vaishyas — from the thighs
  - Shudras — from the feet
- A man is born in a particular caste because of **actions performed in his previous incarnation** (karma). Birth in a caste is not an accident.

#### (b) Metaphysical Theory
- Explains that each **caste has a separate function** determined by the **nature (swabhav)** and **qualities (guna)** of caste members.
- Example: If a person is born to rule, he becomes part of Kshatriyas.

#### Criticism of Traditional Theory:
- Rejected by scholars **mainly for two reasons:**
  1. It considers caste as a natural phenomenon
  2. It considers four varnas as four castes
- **M.N. Srinivas** says the idea of caste as the fourfold division of society represents the **gross oversimplification of facts**. The **real unit** of the caste system is **jati**, not varna.

---

### 2. BRAHMANICAL THEORY

**Key Scholar: Abbe Dubois**

- Feels the caste system **originated and developed in India because of Brahmins**.
- The caste system is an **ingenious device made by Brahmins for Brahmins**.
- Brahmins imposed **restrictions on eating, drinking, marriage, and social relations** with non-Brahmins to **preserve their purity**.
- They gave themselves **high status and special privileges** in the Brahmana and other books and declared all others inferior.
- **Ghurye also agrees**: caste-society was the result of attempts by Brahminic civilization to exclude the aborigines and Sudras from religious and social communion with themselves.

#### Criticism — Hutton argues against it:
1. If this theory is accepted, it would mean caste must have originated when Brahmins got political power — but **caste did not originate so late**.

---

### 3. RACIAL THEORY

**Key Scholar: Risley**

- According to Risley, the **clash of cultures and contact of races** crystallized castes in India.
- The migrant **Aryans had their own ideas of ceremonial purity**. They considered the **original inhabitants as inferior**.
- Aryans were **essentially patrilineal** while the local conquered population was **matrilineal**. Aryans married daughters of aboriginals but **refused to give their daughters to them**.
- Children of "irregular union" had to be assigned the **lowest position** in society — called **chandalas**.
- The origin of 'half-breeds' and the **feeling of racial superiority** ultimately became **responsible for the origin of the caste system**.

#### Risley's Six Processes in Formation of Castes:

| Process | Description |
|---|---|
| (i) Change in traditional occupation | Adopting a new occupation — a caste or sub-division develops into a distinct caste |
| (ii) Migration | A caste section migrates to another region, gets cut off from parental caste and develops as a new caste |
| (iii) Change in customs | New castes form as old customs are discarded and new practices adopted |
| (iv) Preservation of old traditions | Castes that preserve old traditions take a new name to separate from those who adopted new customs — new caste comes into existence |
| (v) Enrolling into Hinduism | An entire tribe or section of a tribe becomes 'Hinduised' and taking a new caste name enters into Hinduism (e.g., Maria Gonds of Madhya Pradesh, Rajbanshis of Bengal) |
| (vi) Role of religious enthusiasts | A religious enthusiast preaches own doctrines; followers form a separate sect which ultimately develops as a new caste |

#### Criticism of Racial Theory:
- If Risley's theory is accepted, the **caste system should not be confined to India but should be found in all those societies** which have faced conquests by other racial groups.
- This raises the question of whether caste is a **unique Indian phenomenon or not**.

---

### 4. OCCUPATIONAL THEORY

**Key Scholar: Nesfield**

- Nesfield says the origin of caste has **nothing to do with racial affinity or religion** — it is **mainly due to functions or occupations**.
- The **technical skill of the occupation was passed on hereditarily from generation to generation**. Because of practicing the same occupation over a long period, **'occupational guilds'** came into existence which **later came to be known as castes**.
- The hierarchy in caste system was the result of the **feeling of superiority and inferiority of occupations**.

#### Criticism:
- **D.N. Majumdar** criticized Nesfield's idea of hierarchy of castes in terms of superiority or inferiority of occupations.
- He maintains that the **status of castes depends not on the superiority/inferiority of occupation but upon the degree of purity of blood** and the extent of isolation maintained by the groups.

---

### 5. KETKAR'S THEORY

**Key Scholar: Ketkar**

- Ketkar **traces the origin of castes from early tribes**, where each tribe was **endogamous** and **hostile to other tribes** for resources.
- He believes that **castes are 'developed tribes' or converted groups**.

#### Criticism:
- Accepting this would mean the caste system was evolved **not by Aryans but by native Dravidians or aborigines**.
- The caste system did not exist in the pre-Vedic period; it evolved only in the **post-Vedic period** — therefore, tribals of the pre-Vedic period cannot be held responsible for the creation of castes.

---

### 6. SENART'S THEORY

**Key Scholar: Senart** (*Caste in India*, 1930)

- Traces the **origin of the caste system to Aryan sources**.
- Indians, Greeks, and Romans are all Aryans and their civilizations are the oldest. Senart finds **certain similarities in the three systems**:
  - India: **family, gotra, and caste**
  - Rome: **gens, curia, and tribe**
  - Greece: **family, phratria, and phyle**
- Also: just as **caste panchayats exist in India** with the sarpanch as an all-powerful man, in Rome and Greece too there are similar councils with similar powers.
- Senart maintains that caste developed as a natural course of development of these ancient **Aryan institutions**.

#### Criticism:
- **Dahlman** argues against Senart's theory: the **origin of caste cannot be explained only in terms of certain elements**. Such a complex institution must be the result of **multiple forces**.

---

### 7. HUTTON'S THEORY OF MANA

**Key Scholar: J.H. Hutton** (1961)

- Hutton emphasized on the **"primitive" conception of mana** in the formation of castes.
- **Mana** is a **mysterious impersonal power** attached to individuals, objects, and places.
- **Taboos** were therefore imposed on commensality, inter-marriage, and interaction, to **save the members of one's tribe from the mana of other tribes**.
- The **restriction on sharing food** with others and having contacts with them is based on the belief that food and contacts may be **infected with the dangerous mana**.
- In his **study of Nagas in 1923-24**, Hutton found villagers objected to accepting presents or money from strangers **for fear of the influence of the mana attached to those objects**.

#### Criticism:
- **D.N. Majumdar (1952)** criticized Hutton's explanation: though tribes in other societies also **believe in mana but we do not find a caste system there**.
- He considers caste as an outcome of interaction of **geographical, social, political, religious, and economic factors**.

---

### CONCLUSION — MULTIFACTOR THEORY

> Scholars like **S.C. Roy** and **R.K. Mukherjee** supported the **Multifactor Theory**, arguing that **several factors working together over time** led to the emergence of the Indian caste system.

- The caste system is a **complex and multifaceted social phenomenon**, shaped by religious, cultural, economic, and political influences.
- Its origins cannot be traced to a single factor or event, but are the outcome of a **long and layered historical process**.

## PART 2: DOMINANT CASTE

### Concept and Origin

- The concept of **Dominant Caste** was given by **M.N. Srinivas** in his path-breaking research paper **"Dominant Caste in Rampura"**.
- It gave a new insight about the **process of social change** in India.
- **Definition:** 'Dominant Caste' is a term used to refer to those castes which had a **large population** and were **granted land rights** by partial land reforms that came into effect after Independence.
- Land reforms took away rights from erstwhile claimants — the upper castes who were **'absentee landlords'** (played no part in agricultural economy other than claiming rent).

---

### Original Characteristics of Dominant Caste (Srinivas)

| Characteristic | Significance |
|---|---|
| Sizable amount of cultivable land | Provides economic resources and control over agricultural production → political and social power |
| Considerable numerical strength | Large voting bloc → ability to mobilize resources and influence public opinion |
| Reasonably high place in local caste hierarchy | Social prestige and cultural capital → economic and political power |

---

### Changing Criteria for Dominant Caste

Mere ownership of land, numerical strength, and high place in ritual hierarchy are **no longer the only determining factors**.

**New/Additional Characteristics:**

1. **Political Clout** — control over decision-making process and access to resources
2. **Western Education** — skills and knowledge valued in modern society → greater economic opportunities and social mobility
3. **Jobs in Administration** — access to positions of power and influence (law enforcement, public policy, governance)

> Due to expansion in transport and communication, **village level dominance is no longer enough** to make a caste dominant. Instead, **regional level dominance** is a better criterion.

**Factors that changed criteria:**
- Introduction of adult franchise
- Democratic decentralization
- Reservation policies
- Spread of education among depressed groups

---

### Examples of Dominant Castes Across India (Srinivas)

| State/Region | Dominant Caste(s) |
|---|---|
| Mysore (Karnataka) | Lingayat and Okkaliga |
| Andhra Pradesh | Reddy and Kamma |
| Tamil Nadu | Gounder, Padayachi, Mudaliar |
| Kerala | Nair |
| Maharashtra | Maratha |
| Gujarat | Patidar |
| Northern India | Rajput, Jat, Giyar, Ahir |

---

### Dominant Caste and Sanskritization

- **Sanskritization for lower castes is not always Brahmins — it is the Dominant Caste** that lower castes try to emulate and climb up the social ladder.
- Example 1: In certain areas of eastern Uttar Pradesh, the dominant **Thakur landlords** make Brahmins cook food except their *kulguru* (family priest).
- Example 2: In many Punjab villages, **Jat landlords** view Brahmins as their servants — reflecting emulation of Jats.

---

### Role of Dominant Caste in Culture Preservation

- The Dominant Caste keeps its distance from others in the village and the whole region of dominance.
- It considers itself the **protector and watchdog of culture**.
- It **sets certain norms of behaviour**, violation of which is dealt with severely.

---

### Dominant Caste and Dispute Settlement

- Dominant Castes **settle disputes** through traditional village and caste councils.
- They settle disputes among members of different castes AND among members of their own caste.

---

### Contemporary Relevance / Significance of Dominant Caste

#### 1. Political Mobilization and Electoral Power
- With adult franchise and Panchayati Raj, **numerical strength of dominant castes has become central to politics**.
- Example: **Yadavs in Bihar and Uttar Pradesh** transformed demographic weight into political dominance through leaders like **Lalu Prasad Yadav** and **Mulayam Singh Yadav**.
- Scholar **Yogendra Yadav** shows how dominant OBC castes act as "vote banks" shaping regional parties (RJD, SP).

#### 2. Cultural Hegemony and Emulation
- Dominant castes remain cultural models for lower castes.
- Srinivas noted that Sanskritization often targets the lifestyle of **dominant regional groups**, not just Brahmins.
- Example: Jats in Punjab or Thakurs in UP being emulated by lower castes in rituals, food practices, and marriage customs.

#### 3. Erosion and Contestation
- Dominance is increasingly challenged by **SC/ST assertion, reservations, and social movements**.
- Example: Dalit movements in Maharashtra (Ambedkarite mobilization) and the **rise of BSP in UP** disrupted traditional dominance of Marathas and Thakurs.
- Still, dominant castes adapt by forming alliances or repositioning within party politics.

#### 4. Changing Basis of Dominance: Beyond Land
- **Andre Beteille (2003)** observed that dominance is no longer tied only to land. Access to **education, bureaucracy, and political networks** is now crucial.
- Example: Lingayats in Karnataka and Reddys in Andhra Pradesh dominate through educational institutions and political representation, not just agriculture.

---

### Criticism / Limitations of Dominant Caste Concept

#### 1. Dominant Individuals vs. Dominant Caste
- **S.C. Dube and Peter Gardner** argued that dominance is often held by **individuals or factions within a caste** rather than by the caste as a whole.
- Dube emphasized: calling an entire caste dominant only makes sense if power is shared widely and exercised for the benefit of most members. In reality, **inequalities of wealth and prestige exist within so-called dominant castes**.
- **Srinivas's Response:** He accepted there are dominant individuals and factions but argued their influence derives from belonging to a dominant caste. *"Dominant individuals and dominant factions owe their dominance to the fact they are part of the dominant caste."*

#### 2. Changing Basis of Dominance
- **D.N. Majumdar**'s study of **Mohana village (Uttar Pradesh, 1958)** showed Brahmins and Thakurs were dominant due to *zamindari*. After abolition of zamindari, Thakur dominance weakened though they retained some power through moneylending.
- Dominance based purely on land is **not permanent**. With land reforms, political power and new economic bases became more important.

#### 3. Numerical Strength Not Decisive
- Majumdar argued: numerical size alone does not ensure dominance.
- Example: In many villages, Scheduled Castes (like Lodha or Pasi in UP) may be numerically largest, but authority still rested with a few upper-caste landlord families.
- Counter-view: **Andre Beteille and Yogendra Singh** emphasized that under adult suffrage, **numerical strength became crucial** for political dominance, especially for SCs and STs with reserved seats.

---

### Conclusion on Dominant Caste

> The concept of dominant caste, introduced by M.N. Srinivas, has been significant in anthropology as it helps in understanding the **social hierarchies and power dynamics** in Indian society. It has been applied to study **caste-based social stratification** and the role of caste in shaping **political and economic relationships** in villages.

## PART 3: CASTE MOBILITY

### Definition

> Caste mobility refers to the process where the **members of lower caste try to raise their social status in the caste hierarchy** and also the **caste as a group attempts mobility** by gaining political power through the **politicization of castes**.

- Caste mobility is **not a new phenomenon** — it has been prevalent in society for many centuries.

### Key Scholars:
1. **Silvenberg** — talked about social mobility in India through **renunciation**, where lower castes tried to perform acts of sannyasins to increase social value and escape deprivation.
2. **M.N. Srinivas** — through his studies of **Sanskritization and Westernization**, explained caste mobility.

---

### Five Types of Caste Mobility

| # | Type | Key Scholar/Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Mobility through warfare | Pauline Kolenda; Example: Shivaji (M.N. Srinivas) |
| 2 | Mobility through serving rulers | Example: Patidars of Gujarat |
| 3 | Mobility through census commissioners | Herbert Risley; Examples: Kurmi Kshatriyas, Teli Vaishyas |
| 4 | Mobility through Sanskritization and Westernization | M.N. Srinivas (1952) |
| 5 | Mobility by use of politics | Eleanor Zelliot; Examples: Mahars of Maharashtra |

---

### DETAILED EXPLANATION OF EACH TYPE

#### 1. Mobility through Warfare

- **Pauline Kolenda** referred to caste mobility through warfare in the Mughal period.
- According to her, the most effective way to rise in caste hierarchy is by **acquisition of territory** either through conquest or by **peaceful occupancy of sparsely populated or empty land**.
- **M.N. Srinivas** gave example of **Shivaji** (Maratha King — Sudra varna) in the Mughal period who **overthrew Mughal rule** and established Maratha empire and went through **religious rites of transition into Kshatriyahood**. Along with Shivaji's rise, the Marathas as a whole became Kshatriyas.

#### 2. Mobility through Serving Rulers

- Jatis whose members served either Hindu or non-Hindu rulers **acquired higher Varna rank**.
- Example: **Patidars of Gujarat**, an efficient group of Shudra Varna, supported Maratha descendants of Shivaji (Gaekwads) who ruled central Gujarat. Gradually, claiming to be Kshatriyas, they established their own regimes.

#### 3. Mobility through Census Commissioners

- While recording jati identities in **census enumerations from 1891 to 1931**, many middle and low castes made efforts to get themselves **registered as members of twice-born castes**.
- **Herbert Risley**, the census commissioner, tried to rank all castes. Hundreds of jatis tried to get ranked high by claiming their Varna titles.
- Example: **Kurmi cultivators of Bengal** wanted to be Kurmi Kshatriyas; **Teli (oil pressers)** wanted to be called Vaishyas.

#### 4. Mobility through Sanskritization and Westernization

**M.N. Srinivas (1952)** explained caste mobility through two processes:

**Sanskritization:**
- A process by which a **low caste was able to rise in a generation or two** to a higher position in the hierarchy by adopting **Vegetarianism and Teetotalism**.
- They take over **rituals, customs, rites, and beliefs of Brahmins** and give up their own practices which might be considered impure.

**Westernization:**
- The process of emulating western cultures by adopting **ideology, values, institutions, and technology** as a result of cultural contact with western society for a long period.
- The important feature of westernization is **emphasis on technology and rationalism**.

#### 5. Mobility by Use of Politics

- Several castes have used **politics in their attempt to better their condition or to achieve their goals**.
- According to **Eleanor Zelliot**, use of politics covers securing **governmental benefits and representation in legislative and political bodies**.
- Example: **Mahars of Maharashtra**, **Reddys and Kammas of Andhra Pradesh**.
  - Mahars with the help of military service not only claimed a higher position in social hierarchy but also exposed themselves to Western culture.
- Scholars **Rajni Kothari and Rushiket Maru** gave examples of middle and lower castes and economically deprived communities in **Gujarat** who formed organizations in the **1940s and 1950s** for achieving political power. After winning elections against Congress, they were accepted into the Kshatriya fold.

---

### Factors Responsible for Caste Mobility (Upward)

| # | Factor | Scholar/Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Decline of the Jajmani System | Traditional system tied lower castes to hereditary occupations; its decline due to monetization, market integration, urban migration freed service castes from dominant caste dependency → new occupations |
| 2 | Emergence of Modern Occupations | Sharma (1974): industries, professions, services expanded after colonial rule; entry into teaching, administration, technical professions allowed many lower/middle castes to improve status |
| 3 | Decline of Untouchability and Purity-Pollution Principle | Kolenda (1986): social reform movements, urbanization, and state laws weakened caste restrictions → Dalits gained access to education, employment, public spaces |
| 4 | Education | Access to modern education → pathway to enter respected professions, government jobs, leadership; fostered social awareness to challenge caste inequalities |
| 5 | State Policy of Protective Discrimination | Constitutional provisions: **reservation in education, government jobs, political representation** → SC, ST, OBC opportunities for mobility historically denied |
| 6 | Social Movements | Leaders like **Jyotiba Phule, Periyar, B.R. Ambedkar** mobilized oppressed communities; Dalit movements, backward caste mobilizations pushed for rights, dignity, recognition |

---

### Downward Caste Mobility — Key Points

Caste mobility is a **two-way process** — it is not always upward.

| # | Cause | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Abolition of Zamindari and Jagirdari (Rajasthan case) | Land reforms forced many Rajputs, Brahmins, Jats (once Zamindars) into economic decline. Some became manual and agricultural labourers. **Sharma (1968, Rajasthan villages)** found ex-Zamindars working alongside Dalits in famine relief works. |
| 2 | Impact of Green Revolution and Agrarian Capitalism | Mechanisation led to **pauperisation of small peasants and tenants** while rich tenants rose as capitalist farmers (Saith and Tankha, 1972). Middle and lower peasants experienced downward mobility despite technological progress. |
| 3 | Domain-specific Decline among Service Castes | **Barbers** who gave up polluting tasks (cleaning plates) lost patronage from upper castes, faced economic hardships. **Balais (leather workers)** discarded leather work to gain ritual status but ended up migrating as casual labourers due to loss of livelihood. |
| 4 | Failed Upward Mobility of Scheduled Castes | **Regars (leather workers)** in Rajasthan, Haryana, Western UP attempted to renounce leather work (1971-72). Faced harassment from dominant Jats and severe livelihood insecurity — forced to revert to traditional occupation within months. |
| 5 | Withdrawal of Status Respect (Jagirdars) | Post-abolition, **Jagirdars in Rajasthan** lost land AND political clout. Some withdrew from Panchayat politics due to humiliation of competing with lower castes — decline in both social and political domains. |

---

### Conclusion on Caste Mobility

> **Caste mobility in India shows that the caste system, though rigid, has never been completely static.** Communities have moved upward through warfare, service to rulers, Sanskritisation, Westernisation, census claims, and politics, while modern factors like education, social movements, and state policies further enabled mobility. At the same time, structural changes like land reforms and the Green Revolution also pushed some castes downward.

> **Caste mobility is a two-way process shaped by history, economy, politics, and culture**, making it central to understanding Indian society.
