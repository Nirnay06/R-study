## PART I: RACIAL / ETHNIC CLASSIFICATION OF INDIA

### Overview

Many scholars have attempted racial classification of India's population. Three classifications stand out:

1. **Risley's Classification**
2. **Guha's Classification** (most widely accepted)
3. **S.S. Sarkar's Classification (1954)**

---

## 1. Risley's Classification

### Who was Risley?
- **Sir Herbert Hope Risley** — British civil servant, 19th century
- First to attempt a **scientific classification** of India's racial groups
- Used **anthropometric techniques**, especially the **nasal index** (ratio of nose breadth to height)
- Linked physical traits with **caste and community**
- Served as **Census Commissioner for 1901**
- Published **"The Peoples of India" (1915)**
- Classified Indians into **seven racial categories**

### Risley's Seven Racial Types

| Type | Location | Representatives | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Turko-Iranian** | Baluchistan, Afghanistan, NW India | Pathans | Tall, fair, long head, narrow/moderate nose, thick facial hair |
| **2. Indo-Aryan** | J&K, Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan | Rajputs, Khatris, Jats | Tall, fair, long arms, long head, narrow prominent nose, broad shoulders |
| **3. Scytho-Dravidian** | Sind, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Rajasthan, UP | Marathas | Short, long head, moderate nose, light brown to fair complexion, scanty body hair |
| **4. Aryo-Dravidian** | Rajasthan, UP, Bihar | Upper castes (Brahmins) and lower castes (SCs) | Short, long head, complexion light brown to black, medium to broad nose |
| **5. Mongolo-Dravidian** | West Bengal, Orissa (Brahmins and Kayasthas) | — | Broad head, dark complexion, flat nose, short to medium height, thick facial hair |
| **6. Mongoloid** | Himalayan & sub-Himalayan (Ladakh to NE India, Tarai belt) | Kinnets (Lahul-Spiti, Kullu), Lepchas & Bhutias (Sikkim), Tharus (UP & Uttarakhand) | Short, yellowish complexion, medium broad nose, flat face, oblique eyes with epicanthic fold |
| **7. Dravidian** | South India (AP, Karnataka, Kerala, TN), Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, S. MP, Orissa, S. Rajasthan | Santhals (Chotanagpur), Todas (Nilgiri), Juangs (Orissa), Gonds (Bastar), Bhils (Rajasthan) | Short, dark complexion, hairy body, curly hair, long head, broad nose |

### Criticisms of Risley's Classification

1. **Small and Inadequate Sample Size** — only 42 castes and tribes out of 87 diverse ones. Raised by **Malhotra and Vasulu (2019)**
2. **Methodology** — concerns about selection process and clarity; validity of approach questioned by various scholars
3. **Intra-Population Diversity** — anthropometric measurements show greater intra-population diversity than inter-group diversity (due to non-random mating) — argument made by anthropologists and geneticists
4. **Neglect of Environmental Factors** — **Malhotra and Vasulu (2019)** pointed out Risley did not adequately consider environmental factors

> **Conclusion:** Risley's classification is historically important but modern anthropology no longer accepts rigid racial types. Today focus is on **genetics, populations, and continuous variation** rather than fixed racial boxes.

## 2. Guha's Classification (Most Widely Accepted)

### Who was Guha?
- **B.S. Guha** — provided classification based on **scientific evidence**
- Used **anthropometric surveys** conducted in years preceding the **1931 Census**
- Considered the **most widely accepted and acceptable racial classification to date**
- Commonly used in any study of the Indian population

### Guha's Six Broad Racial Types

#### 1. The Negrito
- **Features:** Dark skin colour, short stature, frizzy hair (long or short spirals), small/medium/long or broad head with bulbous forehead, flat and broad nose, everted and thick lips
- **Examples:** Irula, Kadars, Andamanese (Onge, Jarawa)

#### 2. The Proto-Australoid
- **Features:** Short height, dark brown to nearly black skin colour, wavy or curly hair, prominent supraorbital ridges
- Found among **almost all tribes of Central and Southern India**
- **Examples:** Oraons, Santhals, Mundas of Chota Nagpur region

#### 3. The Mongoloid
- **Features:** Scanty growth of hair on face and body, obliquely set eyes with epicanthic fold, flat face with prominent cheekbones, straight hair

**Divided into two sub-groups:**

**a) Palaeo-Mongoloid** — further subdivided:
- **Long-headed:**
  - Long head, medium stature, medium nose
  - Prominent cheekbones, skin colour dark to light brown
  - Short and flat face
  - Inhabitants of sub-Himalayan region; concentration most remarkable in Assam and Burma Frontier
  - **Eg:** Sema Nagas of Assam

- **Broad-headed:**
  - Broad head with round face, dark skin colour, medium nose
  - Obliquely set eyes, epicanthic fold more prominent than long-headed type
  - **Eg:** Hill tribes of Chittagong — Chakmas

**b) Tibeto-Mongoloid:**
  - Broad and massive head, tall stature, long and flat face, medium to long nose
  - Oblique eyes with marked epicanthic fold
  - Hair on body and face almost absent
  - Light brown skin colour
  - **Eg:** Tibetans of Bhutan and Sikkim

#### 4. The Mediterranean
Divided into **three distinct racial types:**

**a) Paleo-Mediterranean:**
- Long head with bulbous forehead, high vault, medium stature, small and broad nose, narrow face, pointed chin
- Scanty hair on face and body, dark skin colour
- Probably **introduced megalithic culture to India**
- Dravidian speaking people of South India show main concentration of this type
- **Eg:** Tamil Brahmins of Madurai, Telugu Brahmins

**b) Mediterranean:**
- Long head with arched forehead, narrow nose, medium to tall stature, light skin colour
- Well-developed chin, dark hair, brownish to dark eye colour, plentiful hair on face and body
- Probably responsible for **building up the Indus Valley Civilization**
- **Eg:** Nambudiri Brahmins of Cochin

**c) Oriental Type:**
- Resembles Mediterranean in almost all physical features except nose, which is **long and convex**
- **Eg:** Punjabi Chattris, and the Pathans

#### 5. The Western Brachycephals
Divided into **three types:**

**a) Alpinoid:**
- Broad head, medium stature, prominent nose, rounded face
- Abundant hair on face and body, light skin colour
- **Eg:** Bania of Gujarat, the Kathi of Kathiawar

**b) Dinaric:**
- Broad head, very long and often convex nose, long face, very tall stature
- Dark skin colour, dark eye and hair colour
- **Eg:** Brahmins of Bengal

**c) Armenoid:**
- Resembles Dinarics in physical characters
- Only difference: shape of occiput is much more developed and nose is very prominent
- **Eg:** Parsis of Bombay

#### 6. The Nordic
- Long head, arched forehead, straight and high-bridged nose
- Tall stature with strong jaw and robust bodies
- Blue or grey eye colour, fair (reddish) body colour
- **Eg:** The Kho or Chitrali people in Gilgit-Baltistan of Pakistan

### Key Conclusion of Guha's Survey
> **Brachycephals (those with broad heads) dominate the Indian population.** Prior to Guha's classification, this was not widely held. Scholars in this discipline have overwhelmingly endorsed Guha's categorisation.

### Criticisms of Guha's Classification

1. **Identification of Negrito Elements and Attribution of Foreign Origin** — criticized by **Kalla (1994)**
2. **Sample Size** — criticized for using smaller sample sizes; questions about representativeness of findings
3. **Selection of Tribal Groups** — questions raised about selection of tribal groups and number of individuals used. **Malhotra and Vasulu (2019)** noted concerns
4. **Proto-Nordics and Indo-Aryan Invasion** — Guha's claim that Proto-Nordics were associated with the Indo-Aryan invasion was criticized for not considering other types like Alpino-Dinarics
5. **Brachycephalic Mongoloid Elements** — Guha's identification of brachycephalic Mongoloid elements in West Bengal, west coast, Deccan, Himalayan region faced opposition. **Sarkar criticized** this, arguing the brachycephalic form was restricted in some populations and not a major racial type

> **Conclusion:** Guha divided Indian people into six broad racial types: Negrito, Proto-Australoid, Mongoloid, Mediterranean, Western Brachycephals, and Nordic. His work gave a structured way to study racial diversity in India. But like Risley, his classification is now seen as an early stage in anthropology. Today we prefer to study **genetic variation and clines** instead of rigid racial boxes.

## 3. S.S. Sarkar's (1954) Classification

### Basis of Classification
- **S.S. Sarkar (1954)** classified Indian population mainly on the basis of **cephalic index (head shape)**
- Concluded India was predominantly **dolichocephalic (long-headed)** but also had **mesocephalic (medium-headed)** and **brachycephalic (broad-headed)** groups
- Identified **six ethnic elements** in India

### Sarkar's Six Ethnic Elements

| # | Type | Location | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Australoid** | South India — Urali, Paniyan, Kadar | Short stature, dark complexion, dolichocephalic head, broad (platyrrhine) nose, wavy hair |
| **2. Indo-Aryan** | Baltics of Hindukush mountains | Tall stature, fair skin and eyes, lighter hair than Australoids, long and massive head, high cranial capacity, robust body |
| **3. Irano-Scythian** | Eastern Bihar, Bengal, Assam, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Mysore, Deccan | Medium stature, mesocephalic head (index 77–79) |
| **4. Mundari-speaking People** | Chota Nagpur, Orissa hills, Madhya Pradesh (river valleys and plateaus of eastern central India) | Stocky, short stature, long head, lighter skin than Australoids, thick straight hair (similar to Mongoloids) |
| **5. Far Eastern (Malayan-Polynesian)** | Coastal populations of eastern India (e.g., Chittagong hill tracts) | Darker skin, short stature, broad to wavy head, tendency to obesity |
| **6. Mongolian** | North-eastern borders, Himalayan foothills | Yellowish skin, scanty body and facial hair, Mongolian eye fold, typical flat face |

**Additional notes:**
- **Australoid** also called proto-Australoid, pre-Dravidian, Veddid. Significance: Oldest substratum of Indian population, widely distributed, concentrated among lower castes
- **Irano-Scythian:** Entered India from the north-west. Presence visible in many populations due to southward migration along Indus valley
- **Mundari-speaking people:** Migrated from the east. Show affinities with Mongoloid type

### Criticisms of Sarkar's Classification

1. **Overemphasis on Cephalic Index** — relied mainly on head shape (dolichocephalic, mesocephalic, brachycephalic). Critics argue this single trait is not sufficient to capture racial diversity
2. **Rejection of Negrito Element** — Sarkar denied the presence of Negrito in India, based on his Kadar study (1959). Many anthropologists believe some Negrito traits are visible in coastal groups
3. **Limited Scope of Data** — his surveys were not as extensive as Guha's (1931 Census surveys); conclusions were seen as narrower in coverage
4. **Simplification of Complex Mixtures** — Indian population is highly mixed due to migration and intermarriage; Sarkar's six-type model oversimplifies this complexity
5. **Contradiction with Guha** — He opposed Guha's finding of brachycephals dominating India, insisting dolichocephaly was predominant. This led to debate among anthropologists

> **Conclusion:** Sarkar's model highlighted the diversity of India's population, but was criticised for overemphasis on head shape, rejection of the Negrito element, and limited data. Compared to Guha's broader surveys, his six-type scheme is seen as an **oversimplification** of India's **complex racial mixtures.**

## PART II: LINGUISTIC CLASSIFICATION OF INDIA

### Why India has Linguistic Diversity — Two Main Reasons

1. India has many different **racial groups**, each bringing their own speech patterns
2. India has **prolonged geographical isolation** in some areas — mountain ranges, deserts, and forests kept groups apart, allowing separate languages and dialects to evolve

> Language is not just about communication. It gives **regional identity**, a sense of belonging, and creates **social and emotional integration** among speakers of the same tongue.

### Four Language Families of India

| S.No | Family | Local Name | % of Population | Region | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Austric family | Nishad | 1.3% | Central India | Santali, Mundari |
| 2 | Dravidian family | Dravid | 20% | South India | Telugu, Tamil, Kannada |
| 3 | Sino-Tibetan family | Kirat | 0.85% | North-Eastern India | Assamese, Khasi, Manipuri |
| 4 | Indo-European family | Aryan | 73% | Punjab and Sindhi region | Marathi, Konkani, Rajasthani |

---

## Relevance of Linguistic Diversity for India Today

Linguistic diversity reflects **India's plural identity** and the principle of **unity in diversity**. It allows every region to preserve its heritage and literature while contributing to a shared national culture.

### Bridge Languages and Their Contributions

| Language | Period | Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **Sanskrit** | Ancient | Bridge language between Indo-European and Dravidian tongues; produced Ramayana, Mahabharata, Upanishads, Panini's Ashtadhyayi; pan-Indian influence |
| **Tamil** | Ancient | One of the **oldest living languages** in the world; Sangam literature (over 2000 years old) includes Thirukkural; spoken in Sri Lanka, Singapore, Malaysia |
| **Persian** | Medieval | Deeply influenced Marathi, Kannada, Telugu, Tamil, Bengali; enriched literary works, poems, court traditions |
| **Hindi-Urdu (Hindustani)** | Medieval & Modern | Language of the masses, especially in North India; Mahatma Gandhi proposed Hindustani as link language |
| **English** | Modern | India's global link; enabled Indians to excel in science, technology, and business internationally |

> Quote (attributed in notes but blank in original): *"Every language is a worldview."* India's many languages form countless ways of seeing the world — making civilisation rich.

## Challenges of Linguistic Diversity in India

**Paul Brass** reminds us that in multi-ethnic societies, language often becomes a **mobilising symbol of identity**. India's history reflects this tension.

### Key Case Studies / Examples of Linguistic Conflicts

| Movement | State/Context | Details | Key Scholar/Person |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anti-Hindi Agitations (1960s)** | Tamil Nadu | Opposition to Hindi imposition brought DMK to power, ending Congress dominance | **Robert Hardgrave** notes this movement reshaped Tamil politics permanently |
| **Gokak Agitation (1980s)** | Karnataka | Demanded Kannada as the first language in schools | Supported by poet **Kuvempu** and actor **Dr. Rajkumar**; milestone in asserting Kannada pride |
| **Marathi Mandatory** | Maharashtra | Made Marathi mandatory in CBSE and ICSE schools up to Class 10 | To protect Marathi identity |
| **Assamese Linguistic Pride** | Assam | Assamese pride clashed with minority language rights | **Sanjib Baruah** highlights how such movements shaped Assamese subnationalism |
| **Bhojpuri Movement** | National | Leaders like **Yogi Adityanath** argued Bhojpuri (spoken in 27 countries) deserves Eighth Schedule status | Recognition movement |
| **Meitei (Manipuri)** | Manipur | Won **Eighth Schedule recognition (1992)**; demands for **classical status** continue | — |
| **Fear of Hindi Imposition** | Tamil Nadu, West Bengal | States resist centralising language policies | **Yogendra Singh** explained that Indian modernity does not erase traditions but reshapes them |

### Overall Assessment

- Linguistic diversity is **both an asset and a challenge**
- Asset: literary treasures and civilisational continuity
- Challenge: conflicts like Anti-Hindi and Gokak agitations
- Key principle: **every language is part of India's collective identity** — ensuring no group feels excluded
- Languages remain not a cause of division but a **living symbol of unity in diversity**
- India is a **"composite culture"** shaped by many streams
