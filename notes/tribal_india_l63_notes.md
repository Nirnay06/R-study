## 6.3 — Problems of Rehabilitation and Resettlement (R&R)

### Background
- R&R has been a burning issue in India even before independence.
- At **independence in 1947**, India pursued development through large multipurpose river valley projects, thermal power projects, mining, roads, and industrial hubs — requiring large tracts of land with consequences of **involuntary land acquisition and involuntary displacement**.
- **No reliable data** on the number of Project Affected Persons (PAPs); estimates vary from **50 to 100 million persons**.
- No reliable data on how many have been resettled or rehabilitated.

---

### Principle Behind Resettlement
- **Resettlement** = moving people from original homes to another place (for dam, mine, factory, sanctuary).
- Core idea: **spatial adjustment** — shifting people from one area to another. Focus is on *where* to move, not *how* they will live afterward.
- Authorities promise **land-for-land or house-for-house** compensation, but in reality only physical relocation happens.
- People lose their **livelihoods, culture, and belonging** — rarely restored.
- Becomes a **logistical exercise** (count families, allot plots, move them) while **social and cultural losses** are ignored.
- **Example:** When tribals are moved from forests to plains, their farming, food habits, and spiritual ties to nature break down.

### Principle Behind Rehabilitation
- **Rehabilitation** goes beyond relocation — it means restoring the **economic, social, and cultural well-being** of displaced people.
- Key idea: **holistic restoration** — helping them regain livelihood, dignity, and standard of living through livelihood training, education, healthcare, and participation in planning.
- The **National Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy, 2007** emphasizes minimizing displacement and ensuring people live **better than before** after relocation.
- **Example:** In the **Hirakud Dam Project (1950s, Odisha)**, over **22,000 families** were displaced but received little rehabilitation — this later shaped better national policies.

---

### Issues Related to R&R (7 Key Issues)

**1. Identification of Land to be Acquired**
- Development projects require forest land as well as agriculture land.
- Government under the **law of 'eminent domain'** has the absolute right to acquire any private land for public purpose.
- Implies what is good for the larger section can be done at the cost of land oustees.
- Mining and hydro-electric projects cannot be shifted due to geological requirements. But a compromise on mining boundary or submergence level could **substantially reduce the number of PAPs**.

**2. Land Records and Payment of Compensation**
- Land is a **state subject**; records kept by the **Patwari** of the village, rarely updated (supposed to be updated every 30 years).
- Difficult to assess real value of land (based on last traded value).
- Leads to legal claims continuing in courts for decades.
- **Forest dwellers** who are displaced have **no land records** — making them **ineligible for compensation**. Whatever compensation is paid remains meagre.

**3. Identification of Project Affected Persons (PAPs)**
- Major hurdle when:
  - (i) Land owners are not traceable or not residing in the impacted area.
  - (ii) People do not own land but cultivate it on sharing basis or as daily wage earners.
  - (iii) Some have been cultivating forest land for generations but do not have land in their name.
  - (iv) Outsiders buy small pieces of land to claim R&R benefits and enroll as PAPs.

**4. Loss of Livelihoods**
- Loss of land = loss of livelihood. Not merely monetary — the **style of earning** that livelihood is equally important.
  - Land provides economic security even for taking loans.
  - Harvested crop used for bartering to meet other needs.
  - Cultivation provides food security.
  - Traditional wisdom and agricultural skills passed on through generations — no formal education/training required.
- Loss of land renders skills and knowledge harnessed over decades redundant in one go.

**5. Loss of Home**
- Resettlement into new area creates its own complexities.
- **Ecological incompatibility** — resettled area in stark contrast to environment they were accustomed to.
- Non-availability of opportunities for **traditional livelihood** forces alien occupations (agricultural and industrial labourers) — disrupts lifestyle.
- Resettlement in non-familiar environment alienates them from host community — leads to **hostility**.
- In many cases, resettled population was **not entitled to legal rights** over land in the resettled area.

**6. Lack of Stakeholder Engagement, Loss of Dignity and Self-Respect of PAPs**
- PAPs are often illiterate but have traditional wisdom, self-respect, and dignity.
- Since they do not speak corporate language, assumed not wise enough to take decisions about their own lives — opportunity for stakeholder engagement is denied.
- All decisions made by **project authorities and local government imposed** on them, assuming it is for the good and welfare of PAPs.

**7. The Grievance Redressal Mechanism**
- In the absence of genuine grievance redressal, PAPs get lost as to whom to approach.
- Ultimately, PAPs lose **trust in the process of land acquisition and R&R**.

---

### Case Studies in Rehabilitation

**Case Study 1: Rehabilitation of Baiga and Gond Tribes, Madhya Pradesh (2015)**
- In **January 2015**, around **450 Baiga and Gond settlements** were relocated from the **Kanha Tiger Reserve** under a forest conservation and rehabilitation program.
- Criticized as **forced eviction**, though government claimed it was voluntary relocation aimed at providing better facilities and livelihoods outside the reserve.

**Case Study 2: Delay in Tribal Rehabilitation in Mysuru District (2020)**
- In 2020, **Adivasi groups from Mysuru (Karnataka)** protested delays in rehabilitation and land title deeds after displacement from forest areas.
- Raised concerns over: non-declaration of tribal hamlets as revenue villages, lack of land allotment under Mastigudi rehabilitation, and diversion of funds by dominant groups.
- Around **210 families**, mainly **Jenu Kuruba and Soliga**, were affected.
- Appeals to the **National Commission for Scheduled Tribes** for justice.

**Case Study 3: Rehabilitation of Chenchu Tribals Rescued from Bonded Labour, Telangana (2018–2020)**
- In 2018, 45 **Chenchu tribals** (a PVTG) were rescued from **bonded labour** at a canal irrigation site in **Karimnagar, Telangana**.
- Two years later, **12 survivors** received ₹1.5 lakh each under the **Centrally Sponsored Rehabilitation Scheme for Bonded Labour (1978, revised 2016)**.
- Highlights how **Article 23** and the **Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act, 1976** protect tribal workers, though **delayed compensation** and **weak enforcement** remain persistent issues.

**Case Study 4: Child Labour and Labour Exploitation among Tribals in Kerala (2023)**
- In 2023, reports from **Nilambur region** along the Malappuram-Kozhikode border revealed that tribal children aged 8–15, mainly from **Paniya, Kattunayakan, and Adiya tribes**, were employed in areca nut and banana plantations near Kakkadampoyil.
- Earned ₹300–₹500/day while poverty, alcoholism, and lack of educational follow-up led to school dropouts and child labour — violating **Articles 23 and 24**.
- The banned **CPI (Maoist)** expressed solidarity with workers of the Plantation Corporation of Kerala (PCK), alleging forced overtime and wage neglect.

---

### Solutions to Rehabilitation and Resettlement Problems

**Solutions with respect to Rehabilitation:**
- Restoration of livelihoods needs careful planning and intense engagement with PAPs to make them psychologically prepared.

1. **Land for Land**
   - Best and obvious first choice — assures restoration of income in original lifestyle.
   - **Example:** In the **Rourkela Steel Plant Project (1956)**, 30 villages mainly inhabited by **Oraon, Kharia, and Bhumij tribes** were displaced. They were resettled within 5 km of their original villages and given land and houses in new colonies — helping them stay connected to old community networks.

2. **Self-Employment Schemes**
   - Most available and viable option due to increase in demand for goods and services with inflow of project-dependent population.
   - Common schemes: carpet weaving, masala grinding, bamboo crafting, dairy, poultry, carpentry, goat rearing, apiculture (bee keeping), sericulture (silkworm rearing), pisciculture (fish farming), handicraft, etc.
   - Lack of education, skills and entrepreneurship abilities may affect viability.

3. **Employment in the Project**
   - Addresses restoration of previous standard of living + provides significant improvement.
   - **Example:** **Ho community** in Singhbhum, Jharkhand, were employed in the Zinc Factory. The work brought different clans together, created social unity, and improved their economic condition. However, exposure to outsiders also changed some traditions — restrictions on dancing and singing during the Maghe festival.

**Solutions with respect to Resettlement:**
- PAPs have often **rejected fully developed resettlement colonies**, sometimes even with built-up houses.
- Factors impacting decision to select or reject resettlement colony:
  - Access to livelihood opportunities
  - Access to market
  - Access to health and education facilities
  - Availability of space in the house for storing fodder, grains, shelter for domestic animals
  - Availability of space for expanding the house as family expands
  - Availability of community land for pasture land, fodder, village pond, wells, trees, celebrating festivals
- PAPs should be given opportunities to settle at the place of their choice other than the resettlement colony.

**Core principle:** "Restoration of livelihood is not merely in monetary terms alone. People aspire to restore the *process* of earning that livelihood. They need much **empathy** rather than **sympathy**."

## Paper 2 — Chapter 7.1

### 7.1 Constitutional Provisions for Development of Scheduled Tribes

**Status of STs:** Socially and economically backward. Purpose of provisions is to accelerate their socio-economic development.

#### Social Indicators of STs (NFHS data)
- **Infant Mortality Rate (IMR):** Declined from 62.1 (2005–06) to 41.6 (2019–21).
- **Under Five Mortality Rate:** Declined from 95.7 (2005–06) to 50.3 (2019–21).
- **Institutional Delivery:** Increased from 17.7% (2005–06) to 82.3% (2019–21).
- **Immunization** of children aged 12–23 months: Increased from 31.3% (2005–06) to 76.8% (2019–21).
- **Literacy:** Most tribal groups (except Northeast) have low literacy rates.

#### Economic Indicators of STs
- **Below Poverty Line (BPL):** Majority of tribals are still below poverty line.
- **Access to Land:** FRA 2006 tried to give land rights but high rejection rate; tribals on average own less land than non-tribals.
- **Average Income:** Due to non-modern practices (no chemical fertilizers, no HYV seeds) and limited access to land, produce is only for subsistence, not for market sale.

### Fifth Schedule (Article 244(1))
- "Scheduled areas" = areas which the President declares as scheduled areas.
- Provides for establishment of **Tribal Advisory Council (TAC)** in any state having scheduled areas.
- The Union Government will have executive powers to give directions to states regarding administration of Scheduled Areas.

### Sixth Schedule (Article 244(2))
- Constitution makes special provisions for the administration of tribal dominated areas in **four states**.
- These areas called "Tribal Areas" under Article 244(2) and 6th Schedule.

### Other Important Articles
- **Article 338(A):** Directs states to create a National Commission for Scheduled Tribes to oversee implementation of provisions and safeguards for rights of STs in India.
- **Article 164(1):** In Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, MP, Odisha — there shall be a **minister in charge of tribal welfare**.
- **Article 371(A):** Specific provisions for Nagaland.
- **Article 371(B):** Special provisions for Assam.
- **Article 371(C):** Special provisions for Manipur.
- **Article 371(F):** Special provisions for Sikkim.

### Legislative Measures for STs
1. The Scheduled Castes and Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989.
2. The Scheduled Tribes and Other Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006.

---

### Identification of Scheduled Tribes — Lokur Committee (1965)

The Constitution did not define the criteria for recognition of STs; hence the **Lokur Committee** was set up.

**Five Lokur Criteria:**
1. Primitive traits
2. Distinctive culture
3. Geographical isolation
4. Shyness of contact with the larger community
5. General backwardness

These criteria guide the **Union Government** and the **Registrar General of India (RGI)** in determining ST status.

#### Criticisms of Lokur Criteria
- The **2014 Task Force on Scheduling of Tribes** (chaired by **Hrusikesh Panda**) found the criteria **obsolete, rigid, and condescending**.
- Terms like "primitive" and "shy" fail to reflect present realities where many tribal communities have undergone cultural change and modernization.
- Issues:
  - Lack of anthropological and sociological expertise in decision-making bodies.
  - Inconsistent historical records (from "tribal animists" in 1901 to "primitive tribes" in 1931).
  - Administrative inconsistencies: differing tribal names across states, exclusions due to state bifurcation or forced migration, delays in updating lists.

#### Proposed New Criteria — Panda Committee
1. Socio-economic and educational backwardness relative to the state's population.
2. Historical geographical isolation, whether continuing or not.
3. Distinct language or dialect.
4. Core cultural identity (life-cycle rituals, art, folklore, marriage customs).
5. Endogamy, or marriage largely within the community.
(These are to be assessed **holistically**, not individually.)

#### Procedure for Inclusion in ST List
1. Proposal from State Government/UT
2. Review by Ministry of Tribal Affairs (MoTA)
3. Registrar General of India (RGI) evaluation
4. National Commission for Scheduled Tribes (NCST) recommendation
5. Cabinet approval
6. Presidential assent to amend the Constitution (ST Order, 1950)

## Chapter 7.1 — Problems of Exploitation and Discrimination of Scheduled Castes

### Key Statistics (NHRC 2010 Report)
- A crime is committed against a Dalit **every 18 minutes**.
- Every day, on average: **three Dalit women are raped, two Dalits murdered, and two Dalit houses burnt**.
- **K.B. Saxena statistics:**
  - 37% Dalits live below the poverty line.
  - 54% are undernourished.
  - 83 per 1,000 children born in a Dalit household die before their first birthday.
  - 12% die before their fifth birthday.
  - 45% remain illiterate.
- Dalits are prevented from entering the police station in **28% of Indian villages**.
- Dalit children made to **sit separately** while eating in **39% of government schools**.
- Dalits **do not get mail delivered** to their homes in **24%** of villages.
- Denied access to water sources in **48% of villages** — untouchability remains a stark reality though abolished in 1955.

### Traditional Problems/Disabilities of Scheduled Castes

**1. No Physical Contact**
- Did not participate in village meetings and worship.
- Lived in a **separate hamlet**.
- Children did not attend school and play with children of higher castes.
- Had to play drums to pass through village streets.

**2. Ban on Use of Common Wells and Tanks**
- Not allowed to use common village wells and tanks for fetching water.
- Had separate wells and ponds or depended on courtesy of others to pour water.
- **Case Study: Denial of Water to Dalits in UP (2016):** A 13-year-old Dalit girl and her father were beaten by a temple priest in Gunnaur village, UP after she tried to drink water from a temple hand pump. Case registered under Sections 323, 324 and 3(1)(10) of the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act.

**3. Prohibited from Entering the Temple**
- Scheduled Caste persons not allowed to enter temple for offering worship.
- Not supposed to hear religious discourses, offer prayer, study religious texts.

**4. Did Not Receive Service from Other Occupational Castes**
- Priests, artisan castes, **dhobi** (washermen), and **dom** (death ritual performers) did not render their services to untouchable castes.

**5. Non-acceptance of Cooked Food**
- Food cooked by Scheduled Castes not accepted by higher castes. Water from the hands of SCs also not acceptable.
- **Case Study: Non-Acceptance of Food Cooked by a Dalit Woman in Uttarakhand (2022):** In May 2022, students from a government school in **Sukhidhang village, Champawat district (Uttarakhand)** boycotted midday meals cooked by **Sunita**, a Dalit bhojan mata. Earlier in 2021, her appointment had sparked protests. Despite reinstatement after filing case under SC/ST Act, some students continued the boycott.

**6. Status Incongruence and Dependence Proneness**
- Persistence of stigma of untouchability and the Constitutional status of SCs were not congruent.
- Dependent upon others for livelihood and survival.

---

### Factors of Exploitation of Scheduled Castes

**Social Factors:**
- **Deep-rooted caste system:** 4 Varnas by birth in Hinduism; discrimination of Shudras and outcastes depriving them of social intercourse, educational and cultural development. The stigma against "outcastes or chandalas" justified through the religious concept of **"ritual purity"**.
- **Rigid and biased religious literature:** Restrictions on who should and shouldn't read Vedas or religious scriptures. **Dalits and Women** were not permitted to read the Vedas.

**Psychological Factors:**
- Dalits consider themselves inferior and tolerate exploitation psychologically in the name of religion.

**Lack of Social Awareness:**
- **Illiteracy:** Literacy level of SCs is only **66.1%** vs all-India level of **73%**. Female literacy among SCs as low as **56.5%** vs all-India female literacy of **64.6%** (Census 2011).
- **Unclean occupations:** In occupations such as leather work and sweeping, SCs are in a majority.
  - In UP, of 46,000 leather workers, 41,000 are from castes traditionally associated with this work — **Chamars, Dhusia, Jatava, etc.**
  - In Rajasthan, of 76,000 sweepers, almost 52,000 belong to castes such as **Bhangi, Mehtar, and Chura**.

**Economic Causes:**
- **Lack of land**, dependence on dominant castes for livelihood. SCs lived in huts on the master's land and worked as agricultural labour and as good as bonded labour.

**Political Causes:**
- **Lack of representation in private and public sector:** According to a study by **Ajit Donner and Saxena (2012)**, out of 1000 firms (private and public), **93% of board members were from upper castes, 3.8% from backward castes, and 3.5% from SCs/STs**.
- **Political reservation → backlash.**

**Case Studies — Political Discrimination:**
- **Caste Discrimination Against Dalit Panchayat Chief, Tamil Nadu (2020):** In October 2020, Rajeshwari Saravana Kumar, Dalit panchayat president of Therku Thittai Panchayat, Cuddalore district, was forced to **sit on the floor** during panchayat meetings while caste Hindu members sat on chairs. She was also barred from hoisting the national flag. Case filed under Section 3(1)(r) of the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act.
- **Denial of Flag Hoisting Rights, Tamil Nadu (2023):** In August 2023, the Dalit woman panchayat president of Aathupakkam Panchayat, Thiruvallur district, was prevented from hoisting the national flag during Independence Day celebrations. The District Collector intervened and organized a special flag-hoisting ceremony.

**Other Causes:**
- Reservation, relaxation and concessions in favor of SC causing backlash.
- Demolition/forcible occupation of houses/shops of SCs.
- Charges of theft/looting/dacoity.
- Misusing official persons for harassing SC persons.
- Illicit relations between members of SC and non-SC.
- Disputes over drainage and other problems.
- Discrimination in educational institutions (e.g., Rohit Vemula Case).

---

### Dalit Politics — Two Forms
1. **Militant movements:** Bhim Sena caused more reactionary measures from caste Hindus.
2. **Electoral coalitions:** BSP in UP. Dalits are always in minority by themselves; can only benefit if they form alliance with dominant caste groups or mainstream political parties.
   - **Successful Dalit parties:** Lok Janshakti Party in Bihar (formed 2000 by Ram Vilas Paswan) and Viduthalai Puratchi Party in Tamil Nadu (founded 1989 by Thol. Thirumavalavan) representing Dalit interests based on Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's principles.

---

### Current Initiatives for Scheduled Castes

**Legislative:**
- **The Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989** — also known as SC/ST Act, POA Act, or Atrocities Act. Enacted to prevent atrocities and hate crimes against SCs and STs.
- **SC/ST Act Amendments 2016 and 2018:** Strengthen legal provisions; emphasize stricter punishment for offenses.
- Supreme Court (October 2021) observed: *"Atrocities against members of the SCs and STs are not a thing of the past. They continue to be a reality in our society even today."* — Justices Dhananjaya Y Chandrachud and BV Nagarathna.

**Economic:**
- **Startup India/MUDRA:** Aim to empower SCs through entrepreneurship by providing financial support, mentoring, and access to markets.
- **Dalit Entrepreneur Hub:** Focuses on fostering entrepreneurship within the Dalit community.

**Political:**
- **Sukhdeo Thorat Committee:** Addressing educational institutions discrimination (refer to the 6-point "Big Solutions" infographic in the original notes).
- **Bhopal Declaration 2001/2.**
- **Better Implementation of SC/ST Act:** Stricter enforcement, awareness campaigns, mechanisms for reporting and addressing grievances.

### "The Big Solutions" — Six Measures to End Untouchability
1. Banish untouchability in educational institutions (discrimination-free atmosphere).
2. Land for rural Dalit families (implementing pledge of land reforms, pattas to all landless Dalits, prevent grabbing of Dalit-owned land).
3. Provide viable assets in cities (make Dalits owners of viable assets they work on, like rickshaws).
4. Ensure food security (include all Dalit families in Antyodaya Anna Yojana/Priority households in NFSA 2013).
5. Implement the Sukhdeo Thorat Committee Report (constitutionally guaranteed safeguards to prevent hostility against 'students of reserved category').
6. Rehabilitate manual scavengers (Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers and their Rehabilitation Act, 2013 has not been enough).

### Constitutional Safeguards for Scheduled Castes
(Similar to STs with the following key differences:)

- **Article 341** (instead of 342 for STs): President may notify "castes, races or tribes or parts of or groups within castes, races or tribes" as Scheduled Castes in a state or UT. For states, President also required to **consult with the Governor** before issuing notification.
- **Article 366(24)** (instead of 366(25) for STs): Scheduled Castes means such castes, races or tribes deemed under Article 341 to be Scheduled Castes for purposes of the Constitution.
- **Add Article 17** — Abolition of untouchability and prohibition of any such practices.
- Remove Schedule V/VI and specific state features 164 and 371.

### Procedure for Inclusion in SC List
1. State Government/UT Proposal
2. Review by Ministry of Social Justice & Empowerment (MoSJE)
3. Examination by Registrar General of India (RGI)
4. Recommendation by National Commission for Scheduled Castes (NCSC)
5. Union Cabinet Approval
6. Presidential Notification

---

### How Social, Economic, and Political Safeguards Are Working for SCs and STs

#### Social Safeguards
**Successes:**
- Reservation policies have led to greater representation of SCs and STs.
- Legal prohibitions on untouchability and discrimination promote a more inclusive society.

**Challenges:**
- Persistent Social Discrimination: Social exclusion and atrocities continue despite laws.
- Inadequate Awareness and Enforcement: Low awareness and weak enforcement of anti-discrimination laws hinder social integration.

#### Economic Safeguards
**Successes:**
- Reservations in economic activities aim to improve economic status.

**Challenges:**
- Limited Economic Empowerment: Economic disparities persist, access to resources and credit remains limited.
- Implementation Gaps: Inconsistent execution of welfare and empowerment programs reduces their impact.

#### Political Safeguards
**Successes:**
- Reserved seats in Parliament, State Assemblies, and Panchayats have ensured greater political representation.

**Challenges:**
- Tokenism and Marginalization: Representation remains symbolic, with limited influence on policy decisions.
- Underrepresentation in Leadership Roles: SCs and STs rarely found in top leadership positions.

## Other Backward Classes (OBCs)

### Definition and Scope
- OBCs are the middle band of castes and communities situated **above the SCs and STs**, excluding prominently well-advantaged groups at the top.
- OBCs described as **"socially and educationally backward classes"** under the Constitution.
- Entitled to **27% reservations in public sector employment and higher education**.
- OBC list maintained by the **Indian Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment** — dynamic, with castes added or removed depending on social, educational, and economic factors.

### Population
- OBCs found to comprise **52% of the country's population** (Mandal Commission report, 1980).
- Shrunk/changed to **41% by 2006** (National Sample Survey).
- Substantial debate over exact numbers.

---

### Backward Classes Commissions

**First Backward Classes Commission — Kalekar Commission (1955)**
- Recommended **"caste as the criteria"** to determine backwardness.
- Report was **not accepted by the government**.

**Second Backward Classes Commission — Mandal Commission (1980)**
- Submitted report in 1980.
- Recommended **27% reservation** owing to the legal constraint that total quantum of reservation should not exceed 50%.
- Implemented in **1990**.

**Sub-Categorisation of OBCs — Rohini Commission (2017)**
- In 2017, President Ram Nath Kovind notified a five-member Commission headed by Delhi High Court's former **Chief Justice G. Rohini** under **Article 340** of Indian Constitution.
- **Mandate:** Investigate inequitable distribution of reservation benefits (27%) among castes/groups using the central OBC list; develop a scientific approach for **sub-categorization of OBCs**.
- **Findings:**
  - Out of 2,633 OBCs on the central list, **nearly 1900 castes have not benefited proportionately**.
  - Half of these 1900 castes have not benefited from reservation at all; the other half have received less than 3% of OBC quota.
  - Only **10 sub-castes have received 25% of the advantages** from OBC reservations.
  - Profession-based castes like **Kalaigars** (polish tins) and **Sikligars and Saranias** (sharpen knives) have received almost **no benefits from reservations**.
- **Panel Suggestions:** A fixed quota of 8 to 10% of the total OBC quota of 27% for around 1900 castes from the central list of 2,633 OBCs.

---

### Creamy Layer

**What is it?** The "creamy layer" is the generally small section of people that occupies the top of a marginalized community's socioeconomic hierarchy. The creamy layer test specifies that a candidate must be below a certain income ceiling in order to avail reservation in government jobs and educational institutions.

**Why was it introduced?**
- Originally, reservation for Dalits, Adivasis, and OBCs did not specify any income criteria — sole basis was **caste**.
- The Supreme Court brought in the concept of "creamy layer" in **1993** through the **Indira Sawhney case**. The court said it mapped to the principle of equality: *"Exclusion of such socially advanced members will make the 'class' a truly backward class."*
- The principle, however, only applied to the **Other Backward Classes**, NOT Dalits and Adivasis (acknowledged as the country's most backward communities).

**Will excluding the creamy layer benefit the poorer in their community?**
- Counter-argument: Dalits and Adivasis are so disadvantaged it is difficult even to fill the seats reserved for them.
- Delhi University (July 2018) did not manage to fill its reserved category seats.
- Placing further curbs to exclude the creamy layer will serve no purpose other than more valuable seats going unfilled.

---

### OBCs Among Non-Hindu Communities
- Since the caste system has been replicated by Muslims, Sikhs, Christians, and Buddhists, their backward sections have also been included in the list of OBCs in Mandal Commission.
- Eg: **Mappilas of Kerala** — Muslims.

**Muslim Stratification (3 main classes):**
1. **Ashraf** — Nobles; trace ancestry to inhabitants of the Arab peninsula or Central Asia or are converts from Hindu upper castes.
2. **Ajlaf** — "Commoners"; said to be converted from Hindu low castes.
3. **Arzals** — "Despicable"; said to be Dalit converts.

- The **savarna Muslims** constitute about **15% of the entire Muslim population in India**; the rest comprise the backward, Dalit, and tribal Muslims.

**Hindu-Muslim OBC Anomaly:**
- Communities such as **Darzis (tailors), Dhunias (cotton carders)**, and **Julahas (weavers)** — Muslims from these groups get reservation in central OBC list, but Hindus from the same groups have been left out.
- **Justice Rohini Commission** has compiled data on these religiously restricted entries and is likely to recommend removal of the anomaly, ensuring that **backward class status is independent of religion**.

**Social Movements to Abolish Casteism in Muslim Society (1990s):**
- All India Backward Muslim Morcha of **Dr. Ejaz Ali**
- All India Pasmanda Muslims Mahaj of **Ali Anwar** from Bihar
- All India Muslim OBC Organisation of **Shabbir Ansari** from Maharashtra

**Case Study: Persistence of Caste System among Indian Christians**
- Despite egalitarian ideals of Christianity, caste remains deeply embedded in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Goa, and North India.
- In **Kerala:** Syrian Christians (St. Thomas Christians) practiced caste-like purity rules, including ritual baths after contact with Dalits. Intermarriage between **Knanaya, Nasrani, and Dalit Christians** remains rare.
- In **Goa:** Portuguese assigned Portuguese surnames to suppress caste, but caste divisions persisted. Converts from Brahmin/merchant castes became **Bamonns and Chardos**; lower castes labelled **Sudirs**.
- In **Tamil Nadu:** Communities like **Paravar, Nadar, and Adi Dravida Christians** retain strong caste identities. Christian Nadars prefer marrying Hindu Nadars over Christians of lower caste status.
- In **North India:** Dalit Christians descended from **Chuhra and Chamar converts** face occupational segregation; referred to as **"New Christians"** (derogatorily).
- Dalit Christians report: separate seating, restricted church entry, exclusion from priesthood.

**Debate on Non-Hindu Scheduled Castes:**
- In **January 2020**, Supreme Court agreed to examine a petition seeking "religion-neutral" SC reservations.
- The plea challenges **Paragraph 3 of the Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order, 1950**, which limits SC status to Dalits belonging to Hindu, Sikh, or Buddhist faiths — thereby excluding **Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims**.
- The petition argues this violates **Articles 14, 15, and 25**.
- **Justice Ranganath Misra Commission (2007)** and **National Commission for Minorities Report by Satish Deshpande and Geetika Bapna (2008)** — both recommended extension of SC benefits irrespective of religion.
- In **2022**, Union Government constituted a three-member Commission headed by former Chief Justice **K.G. Balakrishnan** to re-examine the issue of SC status for Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims.

---

### How Safeguards Are Working for OBCs

**1. Social Safeguards**
- Successes: Recognition as a distinct category following Mandal Commission (1980) and implementation (1990); 27% reservation increased social mobility.
- Challenges: Internal Inequality (creamy layer cornering advantages; EBCs and MBCs remain underrepresented); Continuing Social Stigma despite upward mobility.

**2. Economic Safeguards**
- Successes: National Backward Classes Finance and Development Corporation (NBCFDC) and Stand-Up India Scheme supported self-employment and skill training; OBC groups have entered urban informal sector.
- Challenges: Economic Disparities persist (low-income and informal occupations); Incomplete Data (no nationwide caste census after 1931).

**3. Political Safeguards**
- Successes: Mandal recommendations led to political assertion of OBCs; rise of OBC-based parties — **Samajwadi Party (UP), RJD (Bihar), DMK (Tamil Nadu)**.
- Challenges: Elite Capture (political leadership dominated by few dominant sub-castes — Yadavs, Kurmis, Vokkaligas — leaving smaller communities marginalized); Inadequate National-Level Representation.

### Procedure for Inclusion in Central OBC List
1. State/UT Proposal to Ministry of Social Justice & Empowerment (MoSJE) for inclusion, exclusion, or modification
2. Examination by National Commission for Backward Classes (NCBC) (with anthropologist and economist inputs)
3. Union Cabinet Approval
4. Presidential Notification under **Article 342A(1)**

### Procedure for Inclusion in State OBC List
1. Community/Government Request
2. Evaluation by State Backward Classes Commission (SBCC)
3. State Cabinet & Legislature Approval (where required under state law)
4. Governor's Notification

## Paper 2 — Chapter 7.2: Social Change During Pre-Independence

### Definitions of Social Change
- **Anderson and Parker:** "Social change involves **alteration** in the **structure and functioning** of societal forms or processes themselves."
- **Surinder Jodhka:** Social Change refers to the **change in values, institutions, and practices**.

Tribal societies like other societies have been undergoing change. **Colonial transformation** of the tribal society forms an important area of study in Anthropological studies in the Indian sub-continent. The main theme of the changes is **disturbance of the traditional tribal socio-economic system**.

---

### Classification of Tribes Based on Cultural Process

**B.K. Roy Burman (1970)** classified the tribes based on the orientation of the tribal communities towards Hindu society:
1. Tribes **incorporated** in the Hindu society [Eg: Bhil, Bhumij]
2. Tribes **positively oriented** towards the Hindu society [Eg: Santhal, the Oraons]
3. Tribes **negatively oriented** towards the Hindu society [Eg: Mizo, Naga]
4. Tribes **indifferent** towards the Hindu society [Eg: Tribal communities from Arunachal]

Note: He himself disassociated with this approach later. This classification can be used to understand the socio-cultural situation in the pre-colonial or colonial setup but **not the social change**.

To understand the colonial and post-colonial situation, **L.P. Vidyarthi and S.C. Dube** classified based on different levels and stages of **acculturation** [assimilation towards the different culture, typically the dominant one] of Hindu peasantry in different regions.

---

### Pace of Social Change (Historical Trajectory)
- During ancient times — no social change
- During medieval India — less social change
- During colonial rule — more social change
- During post-independence — social change was **rapid**

---

### Causes of Social Change

1. Introduction of outsiders in tribal areas
2. Extension of state authority in tribal areas
3. Commercialisation of agriculture
4. Hinduisation and Christianity (Evangelisation)
5. **Ethnic revolts and tribal movements**

**Surajit Sinha (1972) — Five Types of Tribal Movements:**
1. **Ethnic rebellious movements** during British rule (18th and 19th centuries) — Birsa movement among the Mundas, Kol rebellion in 1832, Santhal rebellion in 1857–58 and Naga rebellion in the 1880s.
   - Santhal Rebellion led by **Sido and Kanhu** (1855–56; Bihar) — against practices of zamindars and moneylenders; later turned anti-British and was suppressed.
2. **Reform movements** — emulating pattern of higher Hindu castes: Bhagat movement among the Oraon, Vaishnavite movement among the Bhumij, and Kehr movement among the Santhals.
3. **Political movements** for tribal states within the Indian Union in the post-Independence period — Jharkhand movement in Chotanagpur and Orissa, Hill states movement in Assam and Madhya Pradesh, etc.
4. **Secessionist movements** — Naga and Mizo movements.
5. **Movements relating to agrarian unrest** — Naxalbari movement (1967) and Birsadal movement (1968–69).

---

### Social Changes in Economy (Pre-Independence)

**Non-monetised to Monetised:**
- Tribal markets declined.
- Reciprocity, redistribution, and socio-cultural exchange were the bases of the traditional tribal economy.
- Traditional form of barter exchange died away.
- Some evidence that sections of tribes took to **commodity production on a limited scale** (K.S. Singh).

**Peasantization and Depeasantization (Both processes operated simultaneously):**
- Several tribal communities who were **not peasants** were coming into the fold of peasantry (**Peasantization**).
- A number of tribal people after **losing their land** were entering a **different economic system (Depeasantisation)**.
  - Depeasantisation = small and marginal farmers are pushed out of the agricultural sector due to being unable to sustain farming practices. This process is increasing.
- Substantial sections of tribes from Middle India such as **Santhals, Oraon, and Munda** had to migrate to the **tea plantations of Assam** and the adjoining areas while others became industrial labor, share-croppers, etc.

---

### Social Changes in Polity (Pre-Independence)

**Customary Laws to Modern Laws:**
- Customary law of tribal communities was replaced by the **new law of the centralized state power** during British rule.
- The new law operated through its administrative and judicial organs and helped the process of Hinduisation to some extent.

---

### Social Changes — Social Dimension (Pre-Independence)

**Ethnic Conflicts:**
- Due to prolonged relative isolation, tribal communities had developed their own social structure and mechanism of social control — they were quite sensitive to them.
- They reacted to colonial changes more **strongly and often violently** than other populations.
- Most tribal movements could be covered under the headings: **'Anti-Colonial', 'Anti-Diku', and 'Pro-Ethnic'**.

**K.S. Singh's Two Phases of Ethnic Conflicts:**
- **First phase (1795–1860):** Coincided with rise, expansion, and establishment of the British empire.
  - Chuar rebellion (1795–1800)
  - Kol and Bhumij insurrections
  - Resistance by Gond zamindars in 1819 and 1842
  - Khond resistance of the mid-1830s
  - Santhal insurrection (1855–59)
- **Second phase (1860–1920):** Coincided with intensive phase of colonialism — deeper penetration by merchant capital, higher incidence of rent, etc.
  - Munda-Oraon Sardar Movement (1869–1895) led by Birsa Munda
  - Tana Bhagat Movement (1895–1901)
  - Bhil's movement led by Govind Giri

**Sanskritization:**
- Tribal society moving closer to the **peasant/caste stratified system** — strengthened the role of Sanskritization.
- Some tribes of central India unitedly moved towards the Varna system and laid their claim over **Kshatriya status** — the Kshatriya model of Sanskritization (Kshatriazation) was the easiest and most popular process.
- Example: **Santhals and the Gonds** followed Sanskritization model.

**Kinship Breakdown:**
- Breakdown of kinship relations due to alienation from their land and migration.
- Adivasi communities depend on kin relationships and community networks for access to information, assistance during health problems, child care, economic assistance, and other social support systems.
- Hardships of displacement exacerbate the gendered burden of morbidities and mortality on Adivasi women.

**Religion:**
- Indigenous religious beliefs such as Animism, sacredness of sacred groves underwent dilution.
- Sanskritization and conversions into Christianity were common.
- Christianity was perhaps the **earliest agency of welfare** in a number of tribal areas.
- The **modernizing role of Christian missions** is an important feature of social change in those tribal areas where it had a deep impact.
- Close link between Christianity and Colonialism — under the impact of Christianity, values and institutions of affected tribal communities wilted.
  - The fate of **'Youth Dormitories'** — a very popular institution of tribes — declined (missionaries wanted churches to be the only places of congregation).
- At the same time, Christianity gave a **new sense of self-respect** to the tribal peasants and sought to create a **separate identity** for them.

**Summary:** Colonial transformation profoundly altered tribal life. What began as limited contact turned into widespread economic dependence, displacement, and acculturation. The shift from **self-sufficient tribal systems to monetized, stratified, and state-controlled societies** marked one of the most significant phases of social change in India's tribal history.

---

## Social Changes Post-Colonial Period

In the post-colonial period, changes in tribal societies were due to **social development and welfare schemes** taken up by the independent Indian government. Due to differences in different tribal societies, they gave mixed reactions to these schemes. Overall these schemes had an impact on society, resulting in social change (can be positive as well as negative).

### Chaudhary (1990) — Key Observations
In the introduction to **'Tribal Transformation in India' (5 Vols.)**, Chaudhary highlighted the following salient facets of change:
1. Transformation from peripheral existence to the **awareness of civic rights**.
2. The **development has not been up to expectations**:
   - Census 2011: Literacy rate of STs was 59% whereas overall literacy rate was 73%.
   - NFHS-4 (2015–16): Under-5 mortality among tribal population was 57.2 per 1000 live births vs 38.5 among others; IMR 44.4 per 1000 vs others at 32.1.
3. **Elite class** has emerged through present democratic institutions and have **cornered the developmental benefits**.
   - Eg: According to **Mr. Prabhu**, dominant groups such as **Valmiki and the Naika communities** cornered all the developmental schemes. Primitive tribes like **Jenu Kuruba and Soligas** are left in the lurch.

---

### People of India Project (Anthropological Survey of India) — Findings
- Many tribes were participating in the country in various ways — became **entrepreneurs, businessmen, teachers, political leaders**.
- **Educational levels doubled** (8.53% to 16.35%) from 1961 to 1981.
- **Literacy rate for STs improved from 47.1% in 2001 to 59% in 2011** (Census).
- Tribes avail health and medical facilities. Decline in death rate and rise in birth rate.
- Coverage of **mass media** (radio and television) in tribal areas has increased.

---

### Case Studies — Post-Colonial Social Change by Community Type

**1. Hunter-Gatherers**
- Losing control over their means of subsistence from early part of colonial rule; forced to other survival strategies.
- **Jenu Kuruba** — with vast traditional knowledge of forests, are now **helping the forest department** in rearing elephants and collection of forest produce.
- A study by **Bhaviskar (1995)** traced criminality among the **Bhil and the Bhilala** of Jhabua district in MP to the growing control of the State over forest resources and degradation of cultivable lands.
- Another study highlights the **failure of the Chenchu resettlement scheme** — as a result, they have taken to crime in certain areas.

**2. Artisans**
- A study by **Gadgil and Guha (1992)** pointed out how certain artisan activities such as **Blacksmithing are declining** and the **Agarias and Asurs** took up agriculture.
- Availability of raw materials for other crafts like basketry and the **high price charged for bamboo by the forest department** contributed to the impoverishment of tribals.
- Another study of the **Dhokda crafts among the Dhurva of Madhya Pradesh** points out similar drawbacks — deficiencies in government schemes related to training, supply of tool-kits, and marketing.

**3. Shifting Cultivators**
- This group was hit hard due to **government forest policies**.
- Alternative activities like animal husbandry and plantation started to gain prominence.
- A class of **landless agricultural workers** has emerged as a result of the taking over of Jhum lands by the forest department. These people are now wage earners in the plantations.

**4. Settled Agriculturists**
- Tribals started adopting new methods like **line sowing and manure pits**.
- **Manapatra's** study on the **Santhals** points out that out of eight Santhal sub-groups, the **Murmu (priest), the Mardi, and the Tudu** — who are most influential and landed — have **gained the most** through the planning process, depriving the other groups.

**5. Modern Democratic Institutions — Panchayats**
- Positive impact: Greater representation and recognition of tribal rights.
- Negative impact: Conflict arising from modern governance structures that clash with traditional political institutions; undermining of traditional Panchayats.
- **People of India study discussing the Gond:** No clash observed between the Traditional Panchayat and the statutory panchayats.
  - Statutory panchayats are active in village politics and close to power structure.
  - Traditional panchayats have restricted their activities to deciding civil disputes like **marital disputes**.
  - So **both coexist now**.

**Summary:** Post-Independence development efforts brought both progress and disparity among tribal communities. While education, health, and political participation improved, uneven implementation and elite capture meant that **many vulnerable groups remained marginalized** — social change has been uneven and incomplete.

## Chapter 7.2 and 9.1 — Developmental Programmes and Welfare Measures

The government is continuously trying to improve the life and livelihood of tribal people. Important schemes:

---

### 1. Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP)

**Introduced:** 1974 — to provide for financial allocations in proportion to the ST population.

**Mandate:** Union government should earmark about **8.6% (as per Census 2011) of the total central plan budget as TSP**.

**Objectives:**
- To reduce poverty and unemployment of the Tribals.
- To eradicate the exploitation and develop remote areas.
- To improve life by providing adequate health and educational services.
- TSP funds can also be utilized to address bottlenecks affecting access to nutrition, health, and other civic services in tribal areas.

**Key Features:**
- No development is possible without the elimination of exploitation.
- **30 Central Ministries/Departments** and **23 States and 4 UTs** have specific fund allocation obligations for TSP.
- TSP is **not applicable** to states where tribals represent **more than 60% of the population**.
- TSP funds to be **non-divertible and non-lapsable**.

**BD Sharma's Contribution:**
- Asserted that the **Fifth Schedule** and the **Tribal Subplan** are two **co-equal pillars of tribal empowerment**.

**UN Dhebar Commission (1961):**
- TSP was first presented as the magic solution by the UN Dhebar Commission (1961) in response to serious challenges faced by the government in managing massive tribal unrest in central eastern India due to land alienation, poverty, indebtedness, and exploitation by non-tribal dominant groups.

**Case Study:**
- An allocation of ₹89,265.12 crore in the Union Budget accounts for only **2.26% of the total expenditure** — far below the 8.6% allocation needed.

---

### 2. Ekalavya Model Residential Schools

**Objective:** To provide quality middle and high-level education to scheduled tribes, enabling them to avail reservation and access the best educational opportunities.

**More Details:**
- **Centrally Sponsored Scheme** — at par with Navodaya Vidyalayas.
- Every block having more than **50% and at least 20,000 tribal people** will have a school.
- Centers of excellence with State-of-art infrastructure for skill development, sports training.
- **100% grant-in-aid** from Ministry of Tribal Affairs.

---

### 3. Vanbandhu Kalyan Yojana

**Objectives:**
- Improving the quality of life in tribal areas.
- To provide better quality education to tribals.
- Improving and providing quality infrastructure.
- To protect the rich culture and heritage of tribals.

**More Info:**
- Central scheme covering tribes across the country.
- This scheme oversees the other central and state schemes covered under the respective Tribal Sub-Plans.

---

### 4. Scheme for MFP Through MSP (Minor Forest Produce — Minimum Support Price)

**Full name:** Scheme for "Mechanism for marketing and development of minor forest produce (MFP) through minimum support price (MSP) and development of value chain for MFP"

**Objective:** To ensure that MFP gatherers receive reasonable rewards, primarily through MSP for identifiable MFP gathered by them, as well as the necessary infrastructure at the local level.

**More Info:**
- Designed to improve livelihood of MFP gatherers by providing them fair price.
- Being implemented in **21 states** across the country.

---

### 5. Pradhan Mantri Van Dhan Yojana

**Objectives:**
- Upgradation of skill and capacity building training.
- Setting up primary processing and value addition facility.

**More Info:**
- Part of the MFP-MSP Scheme.
- Establishes tribal community-owned **Van Dhan Vikas Kendras (VDVKs)** in tribal regions (primarily forests).
- A Kendra shall consist of **15 tribal SHGs**, each comprising up to **20 tribal NTFP gatherers or artisans**.
- **100% Central Government Funded.**
- SHGs would be provided with operational space by panchayats and the district administration.
- Training in: Value addition, use of equipment, enterprise management.
- Identification of local, district, state, national, global buyers for MFP Value Added Products.
- Arrangement for Logistics & Transportations.
- Branding & Marketing.

---

### 6. Integrated Tribal Development Projects (ITDP)

- Programme on ITDP under **Tribal Sub-Plan (TSP)** has been implemented since the **Fifth Five Year Plan**.
- Specific objectives: reducing poverty, improving educational status, and eliminating exploitation of tribal families.
- **Evaluated by the Programme Evaluation Organisation (PEO) in 1996** — report released July 1997.

**Findings of the Study:**
- Though administrative arrangements to execute this programme existed in all states as per Central guidelines, the system of **decentralized planning, implementation, and monitoring was not operational in most states**.
- The **delivery system of the programme was not effective** — tribal farmers found using irrigation water, HYV seeds, fertilizers, and other inputs from private sources, even though significant funds were being spent on free delivery.
- **Inadequate facilities for primary education and health** also observed.
- Suggestion: ITDP Project Officers should be made more effective in planning and implementation of Tribal Development Programmes.

**WADI Project Impact (NABARD funded Tribal Development Programme):**
- 5394 acres of land promoted under the WADI program, benefitting more than **5550 tribal families**.
- 5500 Tribal Marginal farmers saw a significant increase in their average yearly income to ₹20,000–₹30,000.
- The increased multi cropping system achieved food and nutritional security.
- Increased involvement and active participation of various stakeholders (development institutions, tribals, financial institutions).

---

### 7. "Friends of Tribes" Initiative (TRIFED)

- Under this initiative, **TRIFED** has tied up CSR funds to promote tribal livelihoods.
- To create awareness and promote tribal arts and crafts as well as help in socio-economic welfare.
- Products available under **Tribes India brand** and outlets could be procured through online retailers like Amazon, Flipkart, etc.

---

### 8. TRIFED — Tribal Cooperative Marketing Development Federation of India

**Establishment:** 1987, under the Ministry of Tribal Affairs.

**Objective:** National-level cooperative body to promote the socio-economic development of Scheduled Tribes through marketing support, value addition, and capacity building. Core objective: ensure **fair and remunerative prices** for the **Minor Forest Produce (MFP)** and **handicrafts** collected by tribal communities.

**Minimum Support Price Scheme:**
- Under the MSP for MFP Scheme, TRIFED acts as the **nodal agency** for procurement, preventing exploitation by middlemen and stabilizing tribal incomes.
- Through the **Van Dhan Vikas Yojana (VDVY)**, it promotes value addition, entrepreneurship, and skill training among tribal gatherers by forming **Van Dhan Vikas Kendras (VDVKs)** and clusters.

**Market and Promotion:**
- TRIFED manages **"Tribes India" retail outlets**, e-commerce platforms, and exhibitions such as **Aadi Mahotsav**, which showcase tribal art, textiles, and crafts nationally and internationally.
- Works closely with state-level federations, NGOs, and self-help groups to strengthen local supply chains.

**Significance:** TRIFED represents a key institutional mechanism for **tribal empowerment through trade**, aligning traditional skills with modern markets while preserving tribal culture and ecological sustainability.

---

### Implementation of Welfare Schemes — Challenges (Planning Commission 2013 Evaluation)

The Planning Commission in 2013 got a holistic evaluation study on the effectiveness of the TSP strategy. Prominent reasons for underperformance of TSP funds:

1. **Lack of Unified Planning, Implementation, and Monitoring Mechanism:**
   Multiple ministries and departments execute tribal welfare schemes independently without a single nodal agency to coordinate or monitor progress. This leads to duplication of efforts, fragmented accountability, and delays in project execution.

2. **Weak Convergence between Central and State Plan TSP Funds:**
   Absence of a synchronized framework to integrate Central and State-level TSP allocations has resulted in overlapping projects and inefficient fund utilization. Many states have treated TSP as an **accounting formality**, diverting funds to general schemes without specific tribal focus.

3. **Scattered Financial Resources and Fragmented Utilization:**
   Tribal welfare funds are often **thinly spread across numerous small projects**, reducing their transformative potential. Short-term or symbolic initiatives dominate, which fail to generate measurable improvements in income, literacy, or health outcomes.

4. **Absence of Location-Specific Perspective Planning:**
   The TSP lacks **context-sensitive micro-planning** that accounts for the ecological, cultural, and socio-economic diversity of tribal regions. Uniform program designs are often imposed on heterogeneous tribal areas, ignoring the specific needs of PVTGs, forest-dependent communities, or pastoral tribes.

5. **Lack of Systematic Gap Analysis:**
   No **comprehensive baseline assessment** is conducted to identify the development deficits between tribal and non-tribal populations. TSP allocations are not evidence-based, and priorities remain misaligned with ground realities.

6. **Weakening of Institutions** specifically meant for delivery of goods and services to tribal population: Integrated Tribal Development Agency (ITDA)/Integrated Tribal Development Projects/Tribal Research Institutes (TRI) and other Micro Projects.

7. **Misuse of TSP Funds:**
   - Several instances where TSP funds are actually used in forcing tribal displacement from government projects or sites for extraction of minerals.
   - A major share of TSP funds also allocated in building highways used by mining companies to transport minerals from tribal areas.
   - Worst cases include purchasing choppers and repairing jail buildings in **Jharkhand**.

**Evaluation conclusion:** Despite significant financial allocations, **ineffective coordination, poor convergence, and lack of localized planning** have limited the TSP's impact — calling for institutional reform, data-driven planning, and greater accountability in tribal welfare governance.

---

### General Way Forward

- Involve all stakeholders — not only administrators, but also anthropologists, and most importantly the tribals themselves — in order to adopt a **felt-needs approach**.
- As **Verrier Elwin** urged: Tribal development must let communities grow **"according to their own genius."**
- As **L.P. Vidyarthi** reminded: It should balance material progress with cultural happiness, ensuring empowerment through **culturally sensitive, participatory, and rights-based approaches**.
