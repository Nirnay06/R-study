## 6.3 DEVELOPMENTAL PROJECTS AND THEIR IMPACT ON TRIBAL DISPLACEMENT

### Introduction
- Development of infrastructure is necessary for national growth.
- Projects on **forest lands** result in **displacement of tribals** living at the construction site.
- This has a huge impact on the lives of displaced tribes.

### Scholar: Michel Cernea
- Involuntary displacement should be **minimized** and, if possible, **avoided**.
- If unavoidable, displacement should be carried out in a way that **protects the livelihood of tribes**.
- Rehabilitation/resettlement should be **"people-centric"** in approach.

### Magnitude of Displacement
- **Tribal population in India = 8.6%** of total population.
- Development projects resulted in displacement of **over 50% of tribes** — disproportionate impact.
- Study by **Nalin Negi and Sujata Ganguly** (University of Bielefeld, Germany, 2011): Around **50 million people** have been displaced due to development projects in India, of which **40–50% have been tribals**.

---

### Examples of Dam-Induced Displacement

**Example 1: Polavaram Dam (Andhra Pradesh)**
- Resulted in estimated displacement of **3 lakh people**, majority tribals.

**Example 2: Narmada Eviction / Sardar Sarovar Dam**
- On 12 December 1979, despite widespread protests, the Indian government decided to raise the height of the **Sardar Sarovar Dam** on the Narmada river.
- Plan: 30 major, 135 medium, and 3,000 small dams.
- Purpose: provide water to 40 million people + irrigation + electricity.
- **200,000 people displaced, majority Adivasis.**

---

### Examples of Mining-Induced Displacement

**Example 3: Pakri Barwadih Coal Mining Project (Hazaribagh District)**
- Operated by **NTPC (National Thermal Power Corporation)** under the **North Karanpura Coalfield**.
- Land acquisition began **2016**.
- Involves **3,319.42 hectares** (8,202 acres) — including 1,950.5 ha farmland, 643.9 ha forest land, 101.22 ha settlements.
- **19 villages** affected — Barkagaon, Sonbarsa, Pakri Barwadih, Deoria Khurd.
- Compensation: **Rs 9.85–10.85 lakh per adult**; re-employed only as **contract guards or labourers** on land they once owned.
- Promised jobs for women ended when factories shut down.
- In Jharkhand overall, mining companies acquired **over 87,000 hectares** — larger than the city of Bengaluru.
- Skilled jobs went to migrants from other states; locals left unemployed.
- Jharkhand's **2008 Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy** promised locals would benefit first — in reality, **tribals lost both land and livelihood**.

**Example 4: Chaal Coal Mine, Chhattisgarh**
- **Chaal open-cast coal mine**, Raigarh district, operated by **South Eastern Coalfields Limited (SECL)** since **2003**.
- Displaced ~**400 Adivasi (Kawar tribe)** families from **Lath village**, leaving only 56 families behind.
- Farmlands and rice fields destroyed by blasting and excavation → loss of livelihood + forced migration.
- Mine waste discharged into nearby rivers → **severe water contamination** (cut off clean water and fish).
- Promised jobs and compensation remained **only on paper**.
- **Hindalco Industries** nearby demolished Adivasi homes for mine expansion **without consent or compensation**.
- These cases show how mining projects in Chhattisgarh led to **land alienation, livelihood loss, and erosion of tribal dignity and culture**.

---

### Impacts of Displacement

#### Impact on Livelihood
- Tribes lose access to forest lands and forest produce.
- They lose the **natural habitat** they have lived in for generations.
- Forced to move to the **fringes** with no access to forest or forest produce.

#### Impact on Socio-Cultural Aspects
1. **Reduction of collectiveness to individualistic attitude** — loss of traditional community values and practices.
2. **Agriculturalists forced into industrial labour** — loss of traditional livelihoods and agricultural practices.
3. **Religious transformation** — slow fading of ritualistic cultures that were integral to tribal life.
4. **Changing marriage rules** — shift from **endogamy to exogamy** due to displacement.

#### Impact on Economic Aspects
1. **Loss of access to forest produce** — significant source of livelihood lost.
2. **Transformation into landless peasants** — traditional lands taken for development projects.
3. **Severe poverty among tribals** — extreme poverty due to loss of traditional livelihoods.
4. **Shift from traditional economic occupations to industrial labour** — tribals forced to abandon traditional work.
5. **Inadequate monetary compensation** — remuneration frequently insufficient and delayed.

---

### Measures Taken: National Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy, 2007

**Objectives:**
- To **minimize displacement** and promote non-displacing or least-displacing alternatives.
- To ensure **adequate rehabilitation package** with active participation of affected families.
- To ensure special care for **protecting rights of weaker sections** — SCs and STs — with sensitivity.
- To provide a **better standard of living** and sustainable income to affected families.
- To **integrate rehabilitation** concerns into the development planning and implementation process.
- To facilitate harmonious relationships between required bodies and affected families through **mutual cooperation**.

**Conclusion:** Displacement has always been a companion of development. The process should be as **humane and voluntary** as possible. Focus should be on **rehabilitation, not just displacement.** Cost of rehabilitation should be included in project cost.

## 6.3 FOREST POLICIES

### Tribal-Forest Relationship
- Existence of tribal communities is **deeply linked with forests** — a **symbiotic relationship**.
- Tribes depend on forests for food, shelter, fuel, livelihood, and **cultural identity**.
- As **Tripathi (2016)** notes: tribes live in a *harmonious and interdependent relationship* with their forest environment.
- Their economic life, social organisation, and magico-religious beliefs all **revolve around the forest ecosystem**.

---

### Colonial Period Forest Acts

#### Forest Act of 1865
- Marked the beginning of **formal state control** over forests.
- Traditional practices of local people were **restricted by law**.
- Forests treated as **state property** rather than community resources.

#### Forest Act of 1878
- Expanded government authority by dividing forests into **Reserved, Protected, and Village Forests**.
- Trespassing, grazing, or collecting produce — **prohibited or declared offences**.
- Consolidated power of **Forest Department** over local use.

#### First Forest Policy, 1894
- Introduced the idea of **"Eminent Domain"** — state ultimate control over forest land.
- Stated: **"Rights and privileges of individuals must be limited"** in public interest.
- Focused mainly on **revenue generation** and **timber supply** for British needs.

#### Indian Forest Act, 1927
- Codified previous forest laws into one statute.
- Strengthened powers of forest officers.
- **Section 64** authorised **arrest without warrant** of anyone suspected of violating forest laws.
- Continued the **colonial legacy** of exclusion and exploitation rather than conservation.
- Created to **serve British need for timber** — overrode customary rights and the forest management system by declaring forests as **state property**.
- A single official enquired into and "settled" land/forest rights disputes — these officials either did very little or recorded rights of powerful communities.
- Result: clearing of millions of hectares for **monoculture** plantations; destruction of community management systems; forced people to choose between **abandoning the forest** or living as **"criminals"** within it.

---

### Post-Independence Forest Policies

#### National Forest Policy, 1952
- Continued much of the colonial approach with **limited recognition of tribal rights**.
- Emphasised forests as a **national asset** but ignored livelihood dependence of local people.
- Tribals were given *concessions* rather than *rights* — maintaining their marginalisation.

#### National Committee on Development of Backward Areas (1980)
- Recommended that **tribal rights and concessions be fully protected**, and domestic needs be met through forest produce.
- Recognised the importance of **forest-based livelihoods** for tribal well-being.

#### B.K. Roy Burman Committee on Forests and Tribals (1982)
- Headed by eminent anthropologist **B.K. Roy Burman**.
- Highlighted that tribes earn nearly **one-third of their income from minor forest produce**.
- Emphasised the **positive role of forest dwellers** in forest preservation and regeneration.
- Suggested national forest policy must **integrate tribal participation** for sustainable forest management.

#### National Forest Policy, 1988 — A Paradigm Shift
- Marked a **paradigm shift** from revenue-oriented forestry to **people-oriented forest management**.
- Main objective: *Environmental stability and maintenance of ecological balance*.
- Recognised the **symbiotic relationship** between tribal people and forests.
- Directed that tribal communities should be **actively involved in forest protection, regeneration, and development** (Hiremath, 1994).
- Introduced the concept of **Joint Forest Management (JFM)** — partnership between Forest Department and local communities.
- Recognised **Minor Forest Produce (MFP)** as a **right** of forest dwellers, not a concession.
- Led to establishment of cooperatives such as **TRIFED (Tribal Cooperative Marketing Development Federation of India Ltd.)** to market forest produce and eliminate middlemen.

## 6.3b FOREST RIGHTS ACT — 2006 (FRA)

*(This is a standalone question topic — study in full detail)*

### I. Objectives of FRA
1. To undo the **historical injustice** suffered by forest-dwelling communities.
2. To ensure **land tenure, livelihood, and food security** of forest-dwelling Scheduled Tribes and other traditional forest dwellers.
3. To **strengthen conservation** by including responsibilities and authority on forests — sustainable use, conservation of biodiversity, and maintenance of ecological balance.

---

### II. Conditions in Forest Areas (Background)
- Millions of people subject to **harassment, evictions** in their own land.
- **Torture, bonded labour, extortion, sexual assault** quite common in forest areas.

---

### III. Why Were People's Rights Not Respected?

**The Indian Forest Act, 1927:**
- Created to serve British need for timber.
- Declared forests as state property; overrode customary rights.
- Single official settled disputes — mostly recorded rights of powerful communities.
- Resulted in clearing of forests for monoculture; forced people to either abandon forests or live as "criminals."

---

### IV. What Does the FRA Do?
1. **Grants legal recognition** to the rights of traditional forest-dwelling communities, partially correcting the injustice caused by forest laws.
2. Makes a beginning towards giving **communities and the public a voice** in forest and wildlife conservation.

---

### V. Who is a Forest Dweller Under FRA?

**Two-stage eligibility:**

**First** — Everyone must satisfy TWO conditions:
1. Primarily residing in forests or forest lands.
2. Depend on forests and forest land for livelihood (namely "**Bonafide livelihood needs**").

**Second** — Must prove one of the following:
- Conditions have been true for **75 years** → you are an **"Other Traditional Forest Dweller" (OTFD)**
  OR
- You are a member of Scheduled Tribe AND residing in a Scheduled Area → you are a **"Forest Dwelling Scheduled Tribe" (FDST)**

---

### VI. Key Provisions under FRA 2006

**Three Types of Rights:**
1. **Land rights**
2. **Use rights**
3. **Rights to protect and conserve** (Community Forest Rights)

#### A. Land Rights
- No rights to any land **not being cultivated prior to 13 December 2005** and not being cultivated currently.
- Those cultivating land without documents can claim **up to 4 hectares**, provided they are cultivating it themselves for livelihood.

#### B. Use Rights
- Rights over collection of **Minor Forest Produce (MFP)** — tendu, herbs, medicinal plants traditionally collected. (No timber included.)
- Rights over **grazing grounds and water bodies**.
- Traditional areas of use by **nomadic or pastoralist communities** (communities that move with herds, as opposed to practising settled agriculture).

#### C. Rights to Protect and Conserve (Community Forest Rights — CFR)
- For the first time, this law gives the **community the right to protect and manage the forest**.
- **Community Forest Rights (CFR)** fall under **Section 3(1)(i)** of the Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006.
- CFRs grant forest-dwelling communities the legal right to **protect, regenerate, conserve, and manage** the forest resources surrounding their villages.
- In several states, the **Forest Department's control-oriented approach** has slowed or obstructed CFR recognition.

#### D. Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs) — Special Provisions
- FRA has a special provision for PVTGs for **"Rights including community tenures of habitat and habitation"** under **Section 3(1)(e)**.
- Habitat-Area: comprising common habitat and habitats in reserved forests and protected forests.

---

### Positive Case Study: Chhattisgarh and Odisha — Recognising CFR Rights
- Chhattisgarh has recognised nearly **4,000 Community Forest Rights (CFRs)** — one of the most proactive states in India.
- **Kanger Ghati National Park** in Chhattisgarh is the **second national park in India** (after Simlipal in Odisha) where CFR rights have been successfully recognised.
- In both Simlipal and Kanger Ghati, indigenous communities (e.g., **Gudiyapadar**) allowed to reside within the park and create **community-led regulations** for sustainable forest use under CFR provisions.
- This model illustrates a **balance between conservation and livelihood rights**.

---

### Negative Case Study: Hasdeo Arand, Chhattisgarh — Revocation of CFR Titles
- **Hasdeo Aranya region** highlights the ongoing conflict between development projects and community rights.
- In **Ghatbarra village** (one of the first villages to receive CFR titles — 810 hectares in 2013), the **District Level Committee revoked CFR rights in 2016** to facilitate mining in the **Parsa East Kete Basen (PEKB) coal block** (joint venture involving **Adani Enterprises**).
- **Hasdeo Aranya Bachao Sangharsh Samiti (HABSS)** challenged this in court — argued it violated both **Gram Sabha consent provisions** and FRA's core principle of recognising *pre-existing rights*.
- **Chhattisgarh High Court (October 2025)** upheld the revocation on technical grounds — disregarding evidence of Gram Sabha resolutions and community affidavits.
- Activists termed the judgment a **"mockery of the Forest Rights Act"**, warning it sets a **dangerous precedent** allowing corporate interests to override legally recognised community rights.
- Case underscores the **gap between law and implementation** — procedural loopholes and bureaucratic interpretations can reverse decades of progress.

---

### VII. Implementation Issues of the Forest Rights Act (FRA), 2006

1. **High Rejection Rates:** Large number of claims rejected due to incomplete documentation, lack of proof of cultivation before 13 December 2005, or disputes between Forest and Revenue Departments. In **Odisha**, over **1.4 lakh claims were rejected**. Most states have not completed the review ordered by the **Supreme Court (2019)**. Genuine forest dwellers branded as *encroachers*.

2. **Non-Communication of Rejections:** Rejected applicants rarely informed in writing, violating **Section 6(2)** of the Act — denying the **right to appeal within 60 days**. Irregular meetings of State Level Monitoring Committees add to procedural opacity.

3. **Weak District Support:** Many district administrations fail to provide **revenue or forest maps** to Gram Sabhas, making verification difficult and creating a **gap between land claimed and land granted**.

4. **Faulty Evictions:** Thousands of families evicted from forest lands on incomplete or manipulated records. In **Hasdeo Arand (Chhattisgarh)**, the Ghatbarra village CFR title was cancelled to allow coal mining, violating due process.

5. **Resistance by Forest Departments:** Forest officials impede CFR recognition and retain control over forest management. **Section 3(1)(i)** of FRA — mandating community rights to protect and manage forests — is ignored in practice. *Eg: In Tamil Nadu, Kattunayakans (honey collectors) face harassment from forest guards.*

6. **Conflicting Laws and Policies:** FRA's intent conflicts with **Forest Conservation Act (1980)**, **Wildlife Protection Act (1972)**, and **CAMPA (2016)**. The **2023 amendment** to FCA now allows activities like **ecotourism and safaris** on forest land **without explicit Gram Sabha consent**.

7. **Institutional Turf Wars:** Poor coordination between **Ministry of Tribal Affairs (MoTA)** and **Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC)** — delays implementation and causes contradictory directions at state level.

8. **Procedural Violations:** Claims often rejected by **Forest Guards or Patwaris** who have **no legal authority** to do so. In places like **Chittorgarh**, rejections were made orally without notice, preventing any appeal.

9. **Data Manipulation and Errors:** In Chhattisgarh, reports by **ATREE** found duplicate entries and inflated data — only **243 Gram Sabhas** actually received community rights though records claimed 1,378.

10. **Lack of Awareness:** Many Gram Sabha members and OTFDs lack awareness about FRA provisions. Despite portals like **Adi Prashikshan**, awareness remains low in remote areas.

11. **Judicial Uncertainty:** The **2019 Supreme Court eviction order** created panic among **1.8 million forest dwellers**. Although stayed, most states are yet to review rejected claims properly.

12. **Protected Area Displacement:** Over **1 lakh forest dwellers** displaced from tiger reserves due to **NTCA relocations** without settling their FRA claims — a **conservation-vs-rights conflict**.

13. **Mining and Clearance Violations:** Forest clearances granted before FRA compliance. The **Hasdeo Arand case** (Adani-operated mine) showed cancellation of CFR titles without Gram Sabha consent — termed a **"mockery of FRA"**.

14. **Uneven State Progress:** Only **Maharashtra (36%)**, **Chhattisgarh (24%)**, and **Odisha (10%)** have recognised substantial CFR rights. Other major states remain at near **zero recognition**.

---

### VIII. Significance and Achievements of FRA

1. **Historical Justice:** FRA corrects the **"historical injustice"** faced by STs and forest dwellers under colonial forest laws. Recognises both **Individual Forest Rights (IFR)** and **Community Forest Rights (CFR/CFRR)**.

2. **Comprehensive Rights:** Sections **3(1)(b), 3(1)(c), and 3(1)(i)** legally empower communities to access, occupy, and manage forest lands, water bodies, grazing areas, and biodiversity resources.

3. **Empowered Gram Sabhas:** FRA makes **Gram Sabhas** the central authority for claim verification and forest governance — promoting **participatory democracy** at local level.

4. **Community Stewardship Successes:** Villages like **Mendha Lekha (Maharashtra)** and **Betakata (Odisha)** formed **CFR Management Committees (CFRMCs)** that sustainably regulate forest use, prevent illegal felling, and conserve biodiversity.

5. **Economic Empowerment:** Recognition of bamboo and NTFP rights has raised incomes. In **Pajilibandha and Kalakani (Odisha)**, women now earn **₹5,000–6,000 per month** through **Van Dhan Kendras**, showing how FRA links livelihoods with conservation.

6. **Ecological Benefits:** CFR villages like **Soura Kurlanda** have restored degraded forests and increased biodiversity — showing that **community control enhances conservation**.

7. **Gender and PVTG Inclusion:** Women and PVTGs participate in **Van Suraksha Samitis**, ensuring equitable access and decision-making power.

8. **Link with PESA:** States like **Odisha and Maharashtra** have merged FRA implementation with **PESA**, giving **legal corporate status** to Gram Sabhas under **Article 243G**, enabling self-rule.

9. **Technological Innovation:** In **Gujarat's Narmada district**, NGOs used **GPS and satellite imagery** to create digital maps for accurate claim verification — now replicated in other states.

10. **Administrative Achievements:** As per **MoTA (2024)**, about **60% of total claims** have been recognised, covering **13 million acres** of land.

11. **Model for Sustainable Governance:** When implemented properly, FRA balances **livelihood security with biodiversity conservation** — offering a **global model** of justice-based environmental management.

---

### IX. Way Forward and Suggestions

1. **Review Rejected Claims:** States must review all rejected claims transparently, issue written reasons for rejection, and ensure **appeal rights under Section 6**.

2. **Strengthen Gram Sabhas:** Recognise Gram Sabhas as **body corporates** (as in Maharashtra and Odisha) for fund management, decision-making, and legal representation.

3. **Joint Institutional Coordination:** Establish **joint MoTA-MoEFCC mechanisms** for consistent interpretation of forest and tribal policies.

4. **Digital Mapping Tools:** Adopt **GPS and GIS mapping** for claims to eliminate duplication and manipulation of data, with assistance from **BISAG and NRSC**.

5. **Awareness and Training:** Expand **Tribal Research Institutes** and **Adi Prashikshan**-like platforms for grassroots legal and procedural training of FRCs and community leaders.

6. **Livelihood Integration:** Link CFR areas with livelihood schemes such as **TRIFED, Van Dhan Yojana, and PM-JANMAN** to ensure forest-based income and self-reliance.

7. **Legal Safeguards:** Mandate **Gram Sabha consent** before approving mining or diversion projects to prevent FRA violations like in **Hasdeo Arand**.

8. **Inclusive Participation:** Encourage **women, PVTGs, and marginal groups** in decision-making and forest management.

9. **Monitoring and Transparency:** Create **state-level dashboards** to publish data on claims filed, approved, and pending. Promote **real-time accountability** among district committees.

10. **Model Replication:** Scale up successful models from **Mendha Lekha and Betakata**, and promote **peer learning** through exposure visits across states.

11. **Policy Convergence:** Integrate FRA with national programmes like **Green India Mission, CAMPA, and National Biodiversity Strategy** — so livelihood and conservation goals move together.

12. **National FRA Mission:** Launch a **dedicated National FRA Mission** with a separate budget and time-bound targets for claim settlement, as proposed in recent Parliament discussions.

---

### X. Current Issues of Importance

#### Supreme Court Order (2019)
- On **13 February 2019**, the Supreme Court ordered the **eviction** of lakhs belonging to Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (OTFDs) across **16 states**, whose claims as forest-dwellers had been rejected under the Forest Rights Act.
- A bench of judges ordered chief secretaries of these states to evict those whose claims were finally rejected.
- Court directed that evidence be carried out by **July 24, 2019**.
- On **February 28**, the court **stayed its order** — though it said "the mighty and the undeserving" who have encroached on forest land would be shown no mercy.
- The stay was due to the **role of anthropologists and activists** in raising awareness about the **real reasons for the high rejection rates**, and also due to **protests from tribes**.
- It has decided to examine whether **due processes were followed** by Gram Sabhas and states under the FRA before claims were rejected.

#### Transition: Colonial Forest Control to Community-Based Governance
The evolution of forest policy in India represents a **transition from coercive control to participatory conservation**:

1. **From Control to Custodianship:** Early forest laws (1865–1927) aimed at asserting state power and revenue extraction; post-1988 and 2006 frameworks recognise **tribal custodianship of forests**.

2. **From Bureaucratic Management to Community Governance:** Colonial and early post-independence models relied on top-down bureaucracy; modern laws like FRA (2006) empower **Gram Sabhas** as democratic forest managers.

3. **From Exploitation to Ecological Justice:** Shift from extractive forestry to **rights-based environmental justice**, acknowledging that **conservation without community participation is unsustainable**.

4. **From Economic Utility to Ecological Balance:** The **1988 policy** reframed forests not as economic resources but as **ecological assets** — paving the way for joint forest management and community forest rights.

5. **Persisting Contradictions:** Despite progressive intent, old administrative mindsets persist. Conflicts between MoTA and MoEFCC, and between conservation and livelihood priorities, continue to challenge policy implementation.

6. **The Way Ahead:** Success depends on **bridging historical contradictions** — ensuring that **tribal stewardship and ecological conservation coexist** through genuine decentralisation and capacity building.

**Conclusion on Forest Policy:** Forest policies in India reflect a journey from **colonial domination to democratic participation**. FRA (2006) embodies this transformation by recognising forest dwellers as **partners in conservation** rather than intruders. For its promise to be fulfilled, India must ensure effective implementation, community awareness, and strong Gram Sabha empowerment, so that **justice, sustainability, and livelihood security** truly reach those who live by the forest.

## Climate Pressures on India's Forest-Dwelling Communities

- India's tribal and indigenous communities are among the **strongest protectors of forests and biodiversity**, yet they face the **severest impacts of climate change**.
- Their livelihoods, food systems, and cultural traditions are being reshaped by ecological disruption.

### 1. Risks and Problems Due to Climate Change
Tribal livelihoods are highly climate-sensitive because they depend on forests, rainfall, and soil fertility.

- **Erratic monsoons and droughts** — threaten cultivation and forest produce. The **Bhil community** in Gujarat, once praying for rain, now faces destructive floods followed by prolonged dry spells.
- **Shrinking forests and biodiversity loss** — reduce access to minor forest produce such as tendu, mahua, and bamboo — undermining income and food security.
- **Soil erosion and deforestation** — worsen migration, especially in Central India, while extreme heat and forest fires endanger crops and health.

### 2. Coping Through Tribal Ecological Knowledge (TEK)
Indigenous communities apply **Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK)** to adapt to environmental stress and sustain ecosystems:

| Tribal Group | Location | TEK Practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Jarawa** | Andaman Islands | Predict cyclones by observing fish movement and sea behaviour — enabling timely evacuation |
| **Khasi** | Meghalaya | Community agroforestry and maintenance of *sacred groves* — preserving soil moisture and biodiversity in high-rainfall zones |
| **Apatani** | Arunachal Pradesh | Rice-fish integrated farming in the **Ziro Valley** — conserving water and enhancing soil fertility without chemicals |
| **Dongria Kondh** | Odisha | Organic multi-crop farming with traditional pest control and seed-saving — ensuring food and ecological security |
| **Irular** | Tamil Nadu | Organic multi-crop farming with traditional pest control and seed-saving |
| **Zabo System** | Nagaland | Combines rainwater harvesting, forestry, animal husbandry, and agriculture — maintaining fertility and reducing drought stress |

These practices show how TEK strengthens community resilience, conserves biodiversity, and supports sustainable adaptation to climate change.

### 3. Continuing Struggles and Policy Disconnect
Despite their ecological wisdom, tribal communities remain **marginalised in climate governance**:
- National programmes like **CAMPA** and the **Green India Mission** are largely top-down and have led to **"green displacement"** through afforestation without community consent.
- **Gender inequalities** are widening — tribal women bear the burden of resource collection and loss of autonomy.
- Traditional rain forecasting and shifting cultivation cycles are failing as climate shifts become unpredictable — eroding both livelihoods and ancestral knowledge.
- **Institutional fragmentation** between MoTA and MoEFCC and the neglect of Gram Sabha consent under FRA weaken adaptation from below.

### 4. Path Ahead: Integrating Rights and Resilience
A sustainable future demands that **traditional knowledge and modern science work together**:
- Integrate **FRA (2006) and PESA (1996)** into climate-adaptation frameworks and link them with schemes like **PM JANMAN**.
- Empower **Gram Sabhas** to manage eco-restoration and climate funds locally.
- Document and scale up models such as **Zabo and Apatani** systems across eco-regions.
- Strengthen the role of **tribal women and PVTGs** in decision-making and climate planning.
- Blend **community forecasting techniques** with modern satellite-based early-warning systems for better preparedness.

**Key insight:** The people who nurture and protect India's natural ecosystems are the ones most endangered by climate change. Their survival depends not only on external support but on recognising them as **equal partners in climate adaptation and forest governance**.

## 6.3 INDUSTRIALIZATION AND URBANIZATION

### Introduction
- Tribal lands are not only **rich in forest resources** but also contain **rich mineral deposits**.
- Post-independence government tried to exploit minerals for raw materials and constructed many projects in tribal lands.
- This development was supposed to uplift tribals — but instead **uprooted tribals from their dwellings**.
- They were deprived of land and paid meager cash which they used up eventually — leaving them **landless and moneyless**.

### Industrialization and Tribals
- Mineral-rich tribal lands attracted **mining projects** → forests started to disappear.
- Projects **acquired more land** than required → used for **private housing** in many places → original landowners left destitute.
- Rules provide for **90% reservation for locals in employment and training** — but government figures show the **reality has been exactly the opposite**.
- Outsiders continue to be given preferential treatment in employment; **land alienation** continues uninterrupted.
- Poor tribals forced to become **industrial labour or unskilled labour** in nearby towns and outskirt slums.

### Urbanization: Why and How?
- Due to developmental projects, tribals resettled elsewhere → **lost access to forest, its resources, agricultural land, and their whole livelihood**.
- To earn a living, tribals venture to nearest towns — poor tribals from secluded cultures must now **adapt to a cosmopolitan society** without access to education, equipment, or resources.
- **Identity issue**: Life in town is difficult; forced to live in **filthy slums on the outskirts** lacking basic amenities (tap water, drainage, garbage disposal).
- Initially, some tribals got employment in unskilled jobs during construction, but once construction ended, they were found **unsuitable for skilled and semi-skilled jobs**.
- Skilled jobs taken by outsiders who settled in tribal lands; tribals driven off to **fringes of forests as "rejects"**.

---

### Case Study 1: Pakri Barwadih Coal Mining Project (Hazaribagh District) — Detailed
*(Also covered under displacement section — key additional data here)*
- Mining companies acquired **over 87,000 hectares** of land in Jharkhand — larger than the entire city of Bengaluru.
- Most of these lands belonged to **Scheduled Tribes and Scheduled Castes**.
- Skilled jobs in mines often go to **migrants from other states**, while locals are left unemployed or forced to migrate.
- Even though Jharkhand's **2008 Rehabilitation and Resettlement Policy** promised locals would benefit first — in reality, **tribals lost both land and livelihood**.

### Case Study 2: Chaal Coal Mine, Chhattisgarh — Detailed
*(Also covered under displacement section)*
- Key point: Together with Hindalco Industries cases, demonstrates **land alienation, livelihood loss, and erosion of tribal dignity and culture** — turning once self-sufficient Adivasi villages into symbols of displacement.

### Case Study 3: Madhya Pradesh — Encroachment and Forest Loss
- **Madhya Pradesh has India's largest tribal population — about 15% of its total**.
- Divided into two belts:
  - **Western Tribal Belt** — dominated by **Bhil tribes (49%)**
  - **Eastern Tribal Belt** — dominated by **Gonds (36%)**
- MP also leads the country in **forest land encroachment** — over **5,347 square kilometres** lost, the highest in India.
- In **2022**, a shocking incident took place in **Shahdol district**: Tribal man **Dhyan Singh** committed suicide after the forest department demolished his hut, claiming he had encroached on **0.010 hectares** of forest land — barely a few meters. He had already received money under **Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY)** to build that very house. Department justified action under **Section 36(1) of the Indian Forest Act, 1927**.

### Impacts of Industrialization and Urbanization on Tribals

*(According to **Das and Banerji (1964)**, the impact can be studied under four major dimensions)*

---

### 1. Social Impacts

#### a) Loss of Home and Forests
- Forests represent both **livelihood and identity** for tribal communities.
- Provide food, fuel, medicinal plants, materials for shelter, cultural and spiritual significance.
- With expansion of mining, dam, and industrial projects → forests rapidly depleted.
- **Data:** Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change shows **Madhya Pradesh alone lost 219.7 hectares of forest land** to infrastructure projects — forming **about 24% of India's total forest diversion** over the last decade.
- Disappearance of forests deprives tribals of their economic base and sacred landscapes.
- Destruction of sacred groves and ecological systems weakens traditional beliefs and community bonds.
- Result: **large-scale displacement, land alienation, and loss of cultural stability**.

#### b) Resources Exploited but Tribals Left Behind
- Tribal regions contribute significantly to national economy through mineral extraction, but benefits **seldom reach local populations**.
- **Example: Keonjhar district of Odisha** — one of India's richest in iron ore and manganese, yet:
  - Over **13 lakh children are stunted or underweight**
  - **74.7% of pregnant women are anaemic**
  - **25.9% of women have a low BMI** (NFHS-5)
- Economic development in these regions primarily benefits corporations and outsiders, while tribal inhabitants remain impoverished and marginalized.

#### c) Breakdown of Family and Kinship
- **Das and Banerji (1964)** observed that industrialization and migration have weakened interpersonal ties within tribal families.
- Earlier, the **joint family system** was the norm — ensuring collective ownership of land and strong kinship relations.
- Migration to industrial areas → fragmentation into **nuclear units** → weakened traditional authority structures.
- Elders no longer hold the same decision-making power; younger generation adopts **individualistic values**.
- Rise in **divorces, inter-community marriages**, and decline in community-level festivals and rituals.
- Overall result: **erosion of kinship solidarity and weakening of community cohesion**.

---

### 2. Economic Impacts

#### a) Shift in Occupation
- Before industrialization, tribals depended on **subsistence agriculture, hunting, fishing, and forest collection** — family as basic unit of production.
- Industrialization introduced **individual wage labour** in factories, mines, and construction sites.
- Factory employment: **rigid hours, repetitive tasks, and impersonal relations** — contrasting with flexible and communal nature of traditional tribal work.
- Tribals, accustomed to seasonal agricultural cycles, find industrial work **monotonous and emotionally draining**.
- As **Das and Banerji** noted: labour among tribals has shifted from being a **collective social activity** to an **individual economic necessity**.

#### b) Unemployment and Casual Labour
- Despite promises of local employment, industrial projects rarely provided sustainable jobs to displaced families.
- Majority of skilled/semi-skilled positions occupied by **non-tribal workers**; tribals employed temporarily as **unskilled labourers**.
- After project completion, jobs vanish → families left without income or land.
- Migration to urban centres increased → tribals end up as **construction workers, street vendors, or domestic helpers**, living in poor urban settlements without sanitation, clean water, or healthcare.
- Shift from self-sufficient agrarian lifestyle to **economic dependency and urban poverty**.

---

### 3. Religious Impacts
- Industrialization and urbanization have led to a gradual **decline in tribal religious and ritual life**.
- Rituals connected to agriculture, harvest, and forest worship are now **shortened or discontinued**.
- According to **Das and Banerji (1964)**, simplification of traditional ceremonies occurred largely due to **financial hardship** and **lack of leisure** in urban/industrial settings.
- Community shrines and sacred groves are often **inaccessible after displacement**.
- Absence of ritual spaces and collective participation → **breakdown of communal and spiritual identity** among displaced tribals.

---

### 4. Psychological Impacts
- Psychological consequences of industrialization and urbanization are **severe and long-lasting**.
- Displacement, loss of land, and cultural alienation create a deep sense of **insecurity and helplessness**.
- Individuals once living as independent cultivators or forest gatherers are now **dependent on employers or contractors**.
- Resulting **loss of control over their own lives** often leads to **low self-esteem, frustration, and anxiety**.
- **Das and Banerji**: described this as the emergence of a sense of **"powerlessness"** among tribal populations.
- Younger generation — disconnected from traditional culture — faces **identity crises** and higher vulnerability to **substance abuse**.
- These conditions signify breakdown not only of economic security but of **psychological well-being**.

---

### 5. Positive Impacts
Despite numerous adverse effects, some positive outcomes have emerged:
- Industrialization indirectly facilitated the spread of **education, healthcare, and infrastructure** in several tribal regions.
- Exposure to new ideas led to gradual **social reform** — declining acceptance of practices like **witch-hunting, child marriage, and gender-based taboos**.
- Traditional art and craft forms gained national and international recognition:
  - **Gond paintings** from Madhya Pradesh
  - **Warli art** from Maharashtra
  - Both promoted through cultural organisations and cooperatives.
- Several **NGOs and self-help groups** assist tribal communities in marketing **handicrafts, forest produce, and traditional goods** — enabling economic empowerment and cultural preservation.

---

### Conclusion on Industrialization and Urbanization
- Industrialization and urbanization have brought **both development and destruction** to India's tribal societies.
- These processes expanded infrastructure and created new economic opportunities, but also:
  - Displaced millions
  - Dismantled traditional social systems
  - Eroded cultural values
- The need of the hour is **inclusive and participatory development** — where tribal communities are treated as **partners rather than victims**.
- Sustainable development must integrate **economic growth with cultural preservation** and guarantee that the rights and welfare of tribal populations are not compromised in the pursuit of modernisation.

## Key Scholars and Their Arguments (Quick Reference)

| Scholar | Argument / Contribution |
|---|---|
| **Michel Cernea** | Involuntary displacement should be minimised, avoided if possible; rehabilitation should be "people-centric" |
| **Nalin Negi & Sujata Ganguly** (2011, University of Bielefeld) | ~50 million displaced by development projects in India; 40–50% are tribals |
| **Tripathi (2016)** | Tribes live in a *harmonious and interdependent relationship* with their forest environment; their economic, social, and magico-religious life revolves around forests |
| **B.K. Roy Burman** (1982 Committee) | Tribes earn nearly one-third of income from minor forest produce; positive role of forest dwellers in preservation and regeneration |
| **Hiremath (1994)** | Tribal communities should be actively involved in forest protection, regeneration, and development |
| **Das and Banerji (1964)** | Impact of industrialization and urbanization on tribals studied under four dimensions: social, economic, religious, and psychological; described emergence of "powerlessness" among tribals |
