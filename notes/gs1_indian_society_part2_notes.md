|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Risley (1915)** | Anthropometry (body measurements — head, nose, etc.) | 7 types under 3 groups: Dravidian, Indo-Aryan, Mongolian | First systematic attempt in India; landmark work; gave detailed descriptions of groups like Rajputs, Pathans, Marathas; showed link between race, caste, region | Faced "considerable criticism" — esp. ## 1. PURUSHARTHA

**Literal meaning:** "Object of human pursuit"

Purushartha is the **fourfold path of life**: Dharma, Artha, Kama, Moksha. It provides the principles of the Indian ethical system. Of these four, **Dharma is central** to all human activities, and allows the right satisfaction of desires.

---

### A. DHARMA

**According to P.V. Kane**, Dharma can be explained as:
1. Rights and duties of a man as a man
2. His duties and privileges as a member of the Aryan community
3. His special duties, rights and functions as a member of society
4. The rights and obligations of man in the changing stations of life

#### Dharma versus Religion

**1. Dharma — the Wider Idea**
- Dharma is NOT identical to "religion"
- In Indian thought, it means **duty, righteousness, and the moral law** that sustains both individual and society
- It is eternal and universal (*Sanatana Dharma*), guiding actions in all stages of life
- **Radhakrishnan:** *"Dharma is not mere belief, but a principle of right living that directs human destiny"*

**2. Religion — the Narrower Idea**
- Religion refers to a **system of faith, belief, worship, and rituals** tied to a community
- It can vary by time and place, and often emphasizes external practice
- While religion may focus on God, scriptures, or rituals, **dharma is action-oriented**, asking: *"What is the right thing to do here and now?"*

**3. Dharma in Hinduism**
- Hinduism integrates dharma as one of the Purusharthas (goals of life)
- Dharma guides both **Artha (wealth)** and **Kama (desires)** so they do not become destructive
- Dharma also paves the way for **Moksha (liberation)**
- **Yudhishthira-dog story** in the Mahabharata: Even when offered heaven, Yudhishthira refuses to abandon the dog (symbol of dharma/loyalty) — dharma must be upheld above religious reward
- Hinduism sees dharma as **higher than ritual religion**

**4. Dharma in Buddhism**
- Buddhism emphasizes **Dhamma**, the universal law of righteousness
- The Buddha taught that **ethical conduct and compassion** are more important than ritual or birth-based religion
- The *Four Noble Truths* and *Eightfold Path* are a dharmic way of life, not religious dogma
- **Max Weber:** Buddhism transformed Indian spirituality by making **ethics and personal conduct the center of religious practice**

**5. Dharma in Jainism**
- Jainism equates dharma with **Ahimsa (non-violence)**, the highest virtue
- Jain texts describe the *Das-dharma* (ten virtues): forgiveness, humility, truth, renunciation
- Dharma is above external religion — a Jain may perform no rituals, but if they practice ahimsa, they are following true dharma
- Made Jainism a highly ethical and reformist movement against rigid ritualism

**6. Dharma versus Religion — Key Contrast**
- Religion can harden into ritualism, dogma, or external identity
- Dharma is the **inner compass** — insists one must always uphold righteousness, truth, and duty, even against religious formalism
- **Wilhelm Halbfass:** Dharma cannot be translated as "religion" because it is wider — combining **law, ethics, duty, and cosmic order**

**7. Essence**
- Dharma is **timeless and universal**, guiding every stage of life (student, householder, hermit, renouncer) and every pursuit (artha, kama, moksha)
- Religion may change across cultures, but dharma is the **principle that must be preserved above all else**
- As the Yudhishthira story shows: **heaven can be lost, but dharma cannot be abandoned**

---

### B. ARTHA

- Means non-greedy **acquisition of wealth and power** through **righteous manner**
- Acquisition of wealth is the means to an end, it is NOT an end
- Necessary only to the extent that the individual can fulfill other social obligations as enjoined by dharma
- **Kautilya's Arthashastra** emphasized wealth acquisition only when it served the larger social good — echoed in today's debates on inclusive growth and corporate responsibility

---

### C. KAMA

- Wrongly conceived as only referring to bodily desires
- Refers to **all desires in man** — includes all impulses, instincts
- Despite emphasis on exercising control over senses, man always craves indulgence in sexual and other desires
- Hence a **corrective balance** has to be struck between the needs of body and mind
- **Contemporary link:** Kama aligns with recognition of mental health rights, gender equality in relationships, LGBTQ+ rights

---

### D. MOKSHA

- Means the **liberation from the endless cycle of life and death**
- Also called *mukti* — liberation from the cycle of death and rebirth (*samsara*)
- Derived from Sanskrit *muc* ("to free") — literally means **freedom from samsara**
- Refers to absorption of self into eternal bliss
- **N.A. Nikam:** The proper analogy for moksha is given in the Upanishads — the simile of **a river joining the ocean**

**Paths to Moksha — Three Yogas (Trimarga) from the Bhagavad Gita:**
1. **Karma Yoga** — Path of Action (Karma Marga)
2. **Bhakti Yoga** — Path of Devotion (Bhakti Marga) to Ishvar (God)
3. **Jnana Yoga** — Path of Knowledge (Jnana Marga)
4. **Raja Yoga** — Path of Meditation (Dhyana Marga) — making "Four Yogas" or **"Four paths to realization"**

---

### Ethical and Philosophical Basis of Purushartha

**1. Integration of material and spiritual needs**
- Purushartha recognizes that human life is not just about spiritual goals, nor is it purely material
- Balances desires (Kama), wealth (Artha), duty (Dharma), and liberation (Moksha)
- **Radhakrishnan:** This framework prevents one-sided development of personality by harmonizing worldly and transcendental aims

**2. Primacy of Dharma as regulating principle**
- Without Dharma, Artha turns into greed and Kama becomes indulgence
- In today's context of climate change and corporate corruption scandals, the need for Dharma as a guiding force in economic and political life becomes evident

**3. Teleological vision of human life**
- The fourfold path shows that life has direction and purpose
- Moksha is the final release; the other three are preparatory
- Debates exist on whether Moksha should be prioritized in modern secular societies — some argue that pursuit of justice, equality, and rights are more immediate forms of liberation

### Contemporary Relevance of Purushartha

1. **Ethical governance and social justice** — Dharma seen in campaigns for gender justice, human rights, environmental activism; Greta Thunberg's climate strikes and Indian Supreme Court judgments on equality can be read as modern echoes of Dharma guiding public life
2. **Economic growth with social responsibility** — Artha aligns with ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) frameworks; Kautilya's Arthashastra emphasized wealth only when it served the larger social good
3. **Desires, well-being, and human fulfillment** — Kama is not mere sensuality but all forms of human enjoyment; links with mental health rights, gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights
4. **Quest for inner freedom** — Moksha remains relevant as many grapple with burnout, consumerism, and alienation; practices like mindfulness, yoga, and spiritual tourism; **N.A. Nikam** compared Moksha to a river merging into the ocean

**For civil servants specifically:** Purushartha is a moral compass — Dharma (justice and fairness), Artha (resource management), Kama (human welfare), Moksha (personal integrity)

**Global parallels:** SDGs (Sustainable Development Goals) echo Purushartha: ethics, prosperity, well-being, and long-term balance. Deva Rina (debt to nature) connects with climate justice.

## 2. ASHRAM SYSTEM (VARNASRAMA DHARMA)

**Ashram** = fourfold division of the total lifespan

Hindu philosophy developed two great organizing frameworks — **Varna** and **Ashrama**:
- **Varna** = social division of duties: Brahmin (knowledge), Kshatriya (protection), Vaishya (economy), Shudra (service)
- **Ashrama** = life stages: Brahmacharya (student), Grihastha (householder), Vanaprastha (retirement), Sannyasa (renunciation)

Together they form **Varnasrama Dharma** = *"what is one's duty, according to one's role in society and one's stage of life"* — both a **social code** and a **spiritual framework**

### The Four Ashramas

**1. Brahmacharya Ashram (Student Life)**
- The **student phase** of the individual
- Individual **attains knowledge** needed to **fulfill his Dharma** for the rest of his life
- Lead a life of piety, self-denial, discipline, and non-indulgence in artha and kama
- **By Varna:** Brahmin — Vedas, sciences, philosophy under guru; Kshatriya — warfare, administration, archery, leadership; Vaishya — agriculture, trade, animal-rearing; Shudra — crafts, skills, service roles
- **Example:** Nachiketa, who refused wealth and pleasures, insisting on knowledge of truth, represents the ideal of Brahmacharya

**2. Grihastha Ashram (Householder Life)**
- Initiated through **vivah (marriage ceremony)**
- One indulges in **Artha, Kama** — stage of full bliss and blessings
- Duties: reproduction, acquisition of wealth to feed the family, provide education, help members of society
- **By Varna:** Brahmin — teach, guide society ethically, perform rituals; Kshatriya — protect, rule with justice; Vaishya — earn wealth through honest trade, feed and support society; Shudra — provide service, maintain cultural arts and crafts
- **Most crucial stage** — householder sustained all other Ashramas through wealth, taxes, hospitality, and reproduction
- Artha and Kama are legitimate pursuits here, but always regulated by Dharma

**3. Vanaprastha Ashram (Retirement/Withdrawal)**
- Individual devotes time to religious pursuits
- **Expected to retire into a forest and lead a life of meditation** with minimal needs
- Not totally detached from this world
- Preparatory to ultimate retirement — **hands over household responsibilities to the next generation**, took an advisory role
- **Transition phase:** from householder's life (emphasis on Artha and Kama) to one with greater emphasis on **Moksha (spiritual liberation)**
- **By Varna:** Brahmin — withdraw to forests, meditate, write, selectively teach; Kshatriya — hand over kingdom to heirs; Vaishya — retire from business, focus on charity; Shudra — withdraw from labor, pass on skills
- **Example:** Dhritarashtra and Gandhari, after the war, retired to the forest and lived in meditation

**4. Sanyasa Ashram (Renunciation)**
- **Total detachment** from social life — links with family completely cut off
- He is no more a member of society
- **Complete renunciation of material desires** — state of disinterest and detachment from material life, without any meaningful property or home (Ascetic)
- Focused on **Moksha**, peace and simple spiritual life
- **By Varna:** Brahmin — renounce ties, wander as ascetic; Kshatriya — lay down arms and crown, embrace peace; Vaishya — leave wealth, live detached; Shudra — devote life to prayer and spirituality
- **Example:** Yudhishthira, at the end of the Mahabharata, renounced his throne and journeyed to the Himalayas. By refusing to abandon Dharma (symbolized by the dog), he attained liberation.

### Varnasrama Dharma: What it Ensured
- **Social harmony** — by assigning duties according to Varna
- **Personal discipline** — by dividing life into stages of responsibility and detachment
- **Spiritual purpose** — by keeping Moksha as the final aim
- *"Varna tells you what to do, Ashrama tells you when to do it"*

### Interrelation of Ashramas and Purusharthas
1. **Brahmacharya** — Focus on Dharma: learning duties, discipline, respect; links to NEP 2020's stress on ethics and citizenship education
2. **Grihastha** — Pursuit of Artha and Kama under Dharma; resonates with debates around work-life balance and responsible parenting
3. **Vanaprastha** — Gradual detachment, preparing for Moksha; reflects retirement planning, volunteering, community service
4. **Sannyasa** — Worldly pursuits given up for Moksha; resonates with minimalism, spiritual retreats, eco-conscious lifestyles

### Contemporary Relevance of Varnashrama
- **Life-stage wisdom:** Value education, work-life balance, retirement planning, spiritual well-being
- **Ethics in economy:** Artha under Dharma connects to sustainable business, CSR, climate disclosures; ESG investing
- **Desires with responsibility:** Kama under Dharma aligns with mental health awareness, gender equality, responsible digital use
- **Public service frame:** For civil servants — Dharma (justice), Artha (resource management), Kama (human welfare), Moksha (personal integrity)
- **Global parallels:** Deva Rina (debt to nature) connects with climate justice; SDGs echo Purushartha

## 3. RINA (DEBT) AND REBIRTH

### Rina — The Concept of Debt

- These debts are an acknowledgment and **expression of gratitude for the many gifts we receive from the world**
- They are **not meant to be a burden**
- Exceedingly unfavourable to take from the world without offering something in return — results in **additional karmic bondage**
- **Bhagavad Gita** condemns those who consume without giving back: *"Evil is his being, sensuous is his delight, in vain, O Partha, that man lives"*
- According to the concept of Rina, every Hindu is born with some debt and is obliged to repay them to achieve salvation. Failing to do so results in an endless cycle of birth and death.

### Types of Rina

**1. Deva Rina (Debt to God)**
- We are indebted for the gift and sustenance of our life
- We should honour by doing our part in the **conservation** and leaving it in a better state than we received it
- Repaid by **prayer, sacrifices, and performing yagnas**

**2. Rishi Rina (Debt to the Guru/Sages)**
- Indebtedness to the **guru**
- Indebted for having **accumulated knowledge** from them
- Pay this debt by **learning and helping propagate this wisdom**, and adding to it
- Also repaid by **guru dakshina**

**3. Pitra Rina (Debt to Parents/Ancestors)**
- Indebtedness to the **parents**
- Born indebted because they established lineage and were the reason for one's birth
- Repaid by **continuing the lineage** through procreation and **continuing existing customs, traditions like pind daan**, etc.

**4. Atithi Rina (Debt to Guests)**
- Indebtedness to guests
- Fulfilled by showing remembrance and serving them

### When a Man is Born (Hindu Concept of Debt/Rinn):
He is obliged to:
- Repay his ancestors without whom he would not have a lineage
- His parents who raise him
- His community and culture
- The gods and sages
- Nature at large

**The cycle:**
- Spends his entire life repaying debts → this is **dharma**
- If done dutifully without expectations, he liberates himself from all debts → this is **moksha**
- If done half-heartedly or conditionally, he entraps himself in the web of **karma** and is **reborn** once again carrying the burden of debt

### Western vs. Indian Ideas (Rina Comparison)

| Aspect | Western Model | Indic Model |
|---|---|---|
| Debt concept | We are **not born in debt** — owe nothing to society until successful and rich | Every human being is in **debt to family, culture, and nature** by virtue of being alive |
| Basis | Abrahamic concepts — zakat in Islam, tithe in Christianity | Rinna — born with debts to pay |
| Freedom | Freedom from obligation is the default | **No escape from the karmic cycle until all debts are repaid** |

### Deeper Link between Varnashrama and Rina
1. **Duties as both social and moral debts:** According to Kane, Dharma is not just rules of conduct but recognition of obligations — a Brahmin who teaches, a Kshatriya who protects, a Vaishya who sustains economy, a Shudra who supports through service — all fulfilling not just Varna-duty but also repaying debts to gods, sages, ancestors, and society
2. **Householder stage as hub of repayment:** Grihastha sustains all others — feeding guests (Atithi Rina), caring for parents (Pitra Rina), supporting teachers (Rishi Rina), making offerings to gods (Deva Rina)
3. **Spiritual liberation through social responsibility:** Fulfilment of Rina with sincerity is a stepping stone to Moksha; neglect binds one to rebirth. The Bhagavad Gita condemns those who consume without giving back. Parallels present debates on **climate justice** — developed nations owe a "climate debt" to Global South
4. **Gratitude and interdependence as ethical principles:** Rina reflects a philosophy of interdependence rather than individualism. **Indologist Arvind Sharma** notes: Hindu thought placed duties at the heart of ethics, unlike modern liberal frameworks where individual rights dominate
5. **Critiques and reinterpretations:** **Ambedkar** argued Varna tied duties to birth rather than choice, and repayment of debts became justification for hereditary hierarchies. Contemporary scholars reinterpret Rina as universal human responsibility, beyond caste and gender restrictions. Feminist scholars ask: if repayment of debts is central, why is unpaid labor of women not recognized as fulfilling Rina?
6. **Continuities in modern civic life:** Donating organs (repaying Pitra Rina by giving life), volunteering in education (repaying Rishi Rina), planting trees (repaying Deva Rina by honoring nature), supporting refugees (modern form of Atithi Rina). COVID-19 — frontline workers invoked Rishi Rina (knowledge and science); community kitchens reflected Atithi Rina
7. **Varnashrama as time-bound, Rina as timeless:** Even a renunciate who has given up worldly ties is still bound to Deva Rina (honoring the divine through meditation) and Rishi Rina (passing on wisdom). Rina provides an eternal ethical undertone to the temporal framework of Varnashrama.

## 4. KARMA

### What is Karma?
- Karma means **action, work, or deed**
- Every action has consequences, according to karma
- It makes a person accountable for his own actions
- One's current state is the outcome of his actions of previous and present life
- The concept of karma is **tied to the concept of rebirth**

### Interpretations of Karma

| Scholar | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **N.A. Nikam** | *Artha-kriya-karitva* — **good actions produce good effects instantaneously and bad actions produce bad effects instantaneously** |
| **S. Radhakrishnan** | "There is no doctrine that is so valuable in life and conduct as the karma theory — it implies hope for the future and resignation of the past" |
| **Wilhelm Halbfass** | Good intent and good deeds contribute to **good karma** and happier rebirths; bad intent and bad deeds contribute to **bad karma** and bad rebirths |
| **Natural Science interpretation** | Man is a product of natural evolution; karma asks man to **take conscious charge of his adventure** — whatever the past, the future is new |

### Is Karma Fatalistic?

**a) Karma as Fatalism — critics say:**
- **Max Weber (1958, *The Religion of India*):** Karma and rebirth created a "closed social system." People accepted their place as the result of past deeds, discouraging social change
- **Louis Dumont (1970, *Homo Hierarchicus*):** Karma provided the ideological basis of caste hierarchy. Birth status was justified as the result of previous karma, making inequality appear divinely ordained

**b) Counter Views — Karma as Ethical Responsibility:**
- **Radhakrishnan (1937):** Karma is not deterministic. Present choices shape the future, encouraging moral conduct and self-improvement
- **Swami Vivekananda:** Karma is empowerment. "Man is the maker of his destiny; each action plants a seed for tomorrow"
- **P.V. Kane:** Karma is tied to Dharma, implying duty and responsibility. It gives individuals agency to act rightly in the present

**Key Distinction:**
- *Fatalism* = belief that everything is fixed and inevitable, leading to **resignation**
- *Karma* = belief that actions have consequences, making individuals **responsible** for shaping their lives

### Significance of Karma
- Instills morality — people realize they are accountable for deeds
- Inspires individuals to perform good actions for a better future AND a more just present
- Unlike fatalism, karma is rational, encouraging conscious choices rather than passive acceptance

### Nishkama Karma — The Gita's Ideal
- The Bhagavad Gita refines the idea of karma through **Nishkama Karma Yoga** — the yoga of **selfless action**
- Actions should be performed **without expectation of fruits**
- Work done without selfish motive purifies the mind and prepares one for higher wisdom
- **Krishna tells Arjuna (Bhagavad Gita, Ch. 2, Verse 47):**
  > *"To action alone hast thou a right, never at all to its fruits; let not the fruits of action be thy motive, nor let there be in thee any attachment to inaction."*
- Nishkama Karma shows that the way to liberation is not escape from action, but **action without attachment**

## 5. REBIRTH

### Concept
- Rebirth describes the concept of soul or spirit after the death of the person
- In some traditions, **concept of rebirth is linked with karma** — soul returns to a new human body or animal form
- **Cycle:** Birth → Life → Death → Rebirth (or Moksha if karma fully discharged)
- **Doctrines of Rebirth:** Every human has an **everlasting and imperishable soul** — soul persists even when the body is destroyed

### Origin
- In **Satapatha Brahmana**, the concept of rebirth is first mentioned — those who do not perform sacrifices would be cursed with recurring deaths; the soul connects with the creator through moksha
- Linkage of rebirth with karma was a **post-Vedic development**
- In **Samkhya tradition**, the final goal of yoga is for the disciple to reach moksha — two stages:
  1. If the disciple wishes to remain in this world → goes into **super conscious ecstasy**
  2. If he wishes to leave the world → escapes through ***nirvikalpa samadhi***

### Karma, Rebirth, and the Four Purusharthas

**a) Dharma:** Dharma is righteous conduct. When Artha and Kama are pursued in line with Dharma, karma becomes *punya*. Dharma ensures karma supports higher rebirth or liberation. Example: earning wealth honestly, raising family with love, speaking truth.

**b) Artha (Wealth, Power):** Artha is necessary, but if gained through adharma (corruption, greed), it binds the soul with negative karma. If gained ethically and used for service (charity, supporting society), it creates positive karma. Hence, Artha is both opportunity and risk — karma decides how it affects rebirth.

**c) Kama (Desire, Love, Enjoyment):** Kama fulfilled within Dharma (marriage, companionship, family) sustains society and creates good karma. Kama without Dharma (lust, exploitation, selfishness) leads to negative karma and lower rebirths. Thus, Kama is not condemned but must be guided by Dharma.

**d) Moksha (Liberation):** The ultimate goal is to transcend the cycle of karma and rebirth. By living with Dharma, moderating Artha and Kama, and seeking knowledge of the Self, the soul breaks free. Moksha = no more rebirth, union with Brahman/Paramatma.

**As Radhakrishnan said, karma is self-determined destiny, not blind fate.**

### Debates on Rebirth

| Perspective | View |
|---|---|
| **Classical Hindu view** | Doctrine of Rina deeply tied to rebirth — if debts remain unpaid, soul returns until obligations fulfilled. Links social ethic of duty to metaphysical cycle of *samsara* |
| **Buddhist interpretation** | Rebirth without a permanent soul (*anatman*) — karmic continuity rather than a fixed self causes rebirth; negligence of Dhamma leads to further births |
| **Jain interpretation** | Rebirth linked to bondage of karma through passions and violence; *ahimsa* is the way to cut this cycle |
| **Advaita Vedanta** | Samsara as ignorance-driven illusion (maya) |
| **Nyaya-Vaisheshika** | Rebirth as a more concrete causal process of karma |
| **N.A. Nikam** | Rebirth was not just punishment but also opportunity — another chance to discharge debts and progress toward liberation |
| **Swami Vivekananda (modern reformist)** | Reframed rebirth less as literal cycles and more as symbolic of moral accountability |
| **Ambedkar (critique)** | Rejected rebirth framework as reinforcing caste oppression — making people believe they carry "debts of birth" justified social hierarchy. Emphasized social justice in this life rather than salvation in the next |
| **Ian Stevenson (scientific)** | Studies on past-life memories (though contested) kept discussion alive in academic psychology |
| **Contemporary resonance** | Symbolically — unfulfilled obligations (environmental, social, familial) do not disappear but return as crises for the next generation. Climate change is one such "rebirth" of neglected debts |

## 1. DEFINITIONS AND HISTORY OF CASTE

### Key Definitions

- **Kroeber (1930):** Caste is *"an endogamous and hereditary subdivision of an ethnic unit occupying a position of superior or inferior rank or social esteem in comparison with other such subdivisions"*
- **G.S. Ghurye (1932):** Wrote that *"any attempt at definition is bound to fail"* because of the **complexity** of the phenomenon

### Etymology
- The English word 'caste' is a borrowing from the **Portuguese "casta"**, meaning "pure breed"
- In Indian languages (beginning with ancient Sanskrit), referred to by two distinct terms: **varna** and **jati**
- **Varna**, literally 'colour' — name given to **four-fold division of society** into Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, and Shudra — excludes a significant section: 'outcastes', foreigners, slaves, conquered peoples — sometimes referred to as **Panchamas or fifth category**
- **Jati** — the word most commonly used in Indian languages to refer to the institution of caste

### Relationship between Varna and Jati

| Category | Caste (Jati) | Varna |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning | Social group based on birth | Class system based on occupation |
| Origin | Developed over time through social stratification | Described in ancient Hindu texts as a four-fold division |
| Number of Categories | Many, depending on region and religion | Four: Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra |
| Mobility | Little to no social mobility | Theoretically, people can move up or down based on occupation and behavior |
| Discrimination | A common feature of caste-based systems | While theoretically not discriminatory, used to justify discrimination based on birth |
| Relevance | Still a significant factor in Indian society, despite being officially outlawed | Largely superseded by caste in Indian society |

- The most common interpretation: **Varna = broad all-India aggregative classification; Jati = regional or local sub-classification** involving hundreds or even thousands of castes and sub-castes
- Four varna classification is common to all of India; the jati hierarchy has more local classifications that vary from region to region

## 2. VARNA AND BUDDHISM

### Emergence of Buddhism
- Buddhism arose in the **6th century BCE** during the **Second Urbanisation period**, when rigid Brahmanical rituals and stratification under Varna were being questioned
- **Romila Thapar:** Buddhism was both a product of and a reaction to these social conditions. It did not deny the existence of social groups but explicitly **rejected the moral legitimacy of the Varna hierarchy**

### Buddhist Teaching on Judgment
- The Buddha emphasized **karma** and **intention** (*cetana*) as the true basis of moral worth
- In the *Vasala Sutta* (Sutta Nipata): *"Not by birth is one an outcaste, not by birth is one a Brahmin; by action one becomes an outcaste, by action one becomes a Brahmin"* — a radical departure from Brahmanical orthodoxy
- **Gail Omvedt:** This teaching opened a new moral order where ethical conduct, not ritual status, determined human worth

### Rejection of Brahmin Authority
- Buddhism denied that Brahmins alone were custodians of sacred knowledge or mediators of divine power
- The Buddha **democratized access to spiritual liberation** (*Nirvana*), stressing enlightenment was attainable through the Eightfold Path, not ritual sacrifices
- **Johannes Bronkhorst (2011):** This weakened the ritual economy that had placed Brahmins at the top of the Varna system

### Attraction to Lower Castes
- Buddhism's inclusive message, use of local languages like **Pali**, and organization of the Sangha (monastic order) attracted marginalized castes and communities
- **Ambedkar** (in *The Buddha and His Dhamma*): Buddhism was a "religion of social equality" that gave dignity to the downtrodden
- Archaeological record — inscriptions at Sanchi and Bharhut — shows patronage of Buddhism by guilds and non-elite groups, reflecting egalitarian appeal

## 3. VARNA AND JAINISM

### Jainism as a Reform Movement
- Emerging around the same time as Buddhism, Jainism also rejected Brahmanical ritualism, the Varna hierarchy, and the authority of the Vedas
- Classified among the *sramana* traditions (heterodox schools)
- **Paul Dundas:** Jainism framed an alternative worldview where liberation (*moksha*) was possible for all, regardless of caste or gender, through rigorous self-discipline

### Equality and Non-Discrimination
- Jainism upheld the principle of **fundamental equality of all living beings** (*jiva*)
- This extended beyond humans to animals and plants, through its doctrine of *ahimsa*
- **Paradox:** While the theology was egalitarian, in practice Jain communities sometimes adopted caste-like structures (jati), showing the pull of social realities in India

### Jain Ethical Principles
- **Five vows** (*mahavratas*): non-violence (*ahimsa*), truth (*satya*), non-stealing (*asteya*), celibacy (*brahmacharya*), non-possession (*aparigraha*)
- **Padmanabh Jaini:** Jainism turned ethics into a personal discipline aimed at liberation, rather than a collective ritual obligation linked to Varna. This ethic was especially attractive in **mercantile communities**, who found a spiritual justification for restraint and honest trade

### Influence on Culture and Philosophy
- Jainism's emphasis on ahimsa profoundly shaped Indian cultural traditions — from **vegetarianism to Gandhian non-violence**
- Mahatma Gandhi acknowledged Jain influences in developing his philosophy of *satyagraha*
- Jain metaphysical doctrine: ***anekantavada*** (many-sidedness of truth) — offered an epistemological alternative to rigid hierarchies, fostering intellectual pluralism
- **Hermann Jacobi:** These ideas challenged the exclusivity of Brahmanical orthodoxy and influenced later Hindu and even modern Indian thought

## 4. AGE OF THE CASTE SYSTEM

### Historical Periods

**Vedic Period (900–500 BCE):**
- Late Vedic period: society organized into four varnas
- Divisions were relatively **flexible**, with scope for mobility across categories

**Post-Vedic Period:**
- Over time, the varna system hardened into a **rigid caste structure**
- Occupations and social status became **fixed and hereditary**, creating the complex caste system seen in later history
- Age of caste system is generally agreed to be around **three thousand years**

---

## 5. DEFINING FEATURES OF CASTE — G.S. GHURYE'S SIX CHARACTERISTICS

**G.S. Ghurye (1932):** Offered a model definition that could be applied across India, acknowledging regional variations — his **six characteristics:**

1. **Segmentation by birth:** Society divided into groups whose membership is **determined by birth**. Creates a hereditary system where one inherits their caste identity

2. **Endogamy:** Restrictions on marrying a person outside caste. In some situations **hypergamy** is allowed. Far less rigidity on inter-marriage between different sub-castes than between members of different castes in some regions

3. **Hierarchical system:** Generally Brahmins at the head of the hierarchy, but this hierarchy was disputed in some cases. In various linguistic areas, hundreds of castes had a gradation generally acknowledged by everyone

4. **Restrictions on feeding and social intercourse:** Minute rules on the kind of food and drink that upper castes could accept from lower castes. Lower castes generally accepted food from upper castes

5. **Occupation, generally inherited:** Lack of unrestricted choice of profession; caste members restricted their own members from taking up certain professions they considered degrading

6. **Segregation:** Individual castes lived together, the dominant caste living in the center and other castes living on the periphery. **Restrictions on the use of water wells or streets by one caste on another** — an upper-caste Brahmin might not be permitted to use the street of a lower-caste group

### Limitations of Ghurye's Model
- Attracted scholarly criticism for relying on census reports produced by the **colonial government** and the **"superior, inferior" racist theories of H.H. Risley** (1901 Census Commissioner)
- **Graham Chapman:** Highlights the complexity of caste, and notes differences between **theoretical constructs** of caste and the **practical reality** of caste

## 6. CASTE AND HIERARCHY — LOUIS DUMONT'S ANALYSIS

- **Louis Dumont** described ritual rankings within the Jati system as based on the concepts of **religious purity and pollution**
- Hierarchical ordering of castes is based on the **distinction between 'purity' and 'pollution'**:
  - Something closer to the sacred = **ritual purity** = high status
  - Something distant from the sacred = **ritually polluting** = low status
- Castes that are considered **ritually pure have high status**; those considered less pure or impure have low status
- Material power (economic or military) closely associated with social status: **those in power tend to be of high status, and vice versa** — historians believe those defeated in wars were often assigned low caste status
- Castes are not only unequal to each other in ritual terms but also supposed to be **complementary and non-competing groups** — each caste has its own place in the system which cannot be taken by any other caste
- Since caste is linked with **occupation**, the system functions as the **social division of labour** — in principle, allows no mobility

### Theoretical Basis of Caste System
- Based on two sets of principles: **difference and separation** on one hand, and **holism and hierarchy** on the other
- Each caste is supposed to be different from and strictly separated from every other caste
- Yet these different and separated castes can only exist in relation to a larger whole — the totality of society consisting of all castes
- This societal whole or system is **hierarchical** rather than egalitarian
- Each individual caste occupies an **ordered rank** — a particular position in a **ladder-like arrangement** going from highest to lowest

---

## 7. HOW DOES CASTE WORK?

- For centuries, caste has dictated almost every aspect of Hindu religious and social life
- Rural communities arranged on basis of castes — upper and lower castes almost always lived in **segregated colonies**; water wells not shared; Brahmins would not accept food or drink from Shudras; one could marry only within one's caste
- The system bestowed many privileges on upper castes while sanctioning **repression** of the lower castes
- Criticised for being **unjust and regressive** — remained virtually unchanged for centuries, trapping people into **fixed social orders** from which it was impossible to escape
- Despite the obstacles, some Dalits and other low-caste Indians rose to hold prestigious positions — **BR Ambedkar** (authored Indian Constitution) and **KR Narayanan** (first Dalit president)
- Historians note that until the 18th Century, formal distinctions of caste were of limited importance to Indians — social identities were much more flexible

## UPSC EXAM-READY POINTS (High-Yield Summary)

### On Purushartha
- Four-fold path = Dharma (duty), Artha (wealth), Kama (desire), Moksha (liberation)
- Dharma is central and regulates all other three
- Radhakrishnan: Framework prevents one-sided development of personality
- Contemporary relevance: SDGs echo Purushartha; ESG = Artha under Dharma; mindfulness = Moksha pursuit

### On Varnashrama
- Varna tells you WHAT to do; Ashrama tells you WHEN to do it
- Grihastha is the most crucial Ashrama — sustains all others
- Varnasrama ensured social harmony, personal discipline, spiritual purpose
- Critique (Ambedkar): Varna tied duties to birth rather than choice

### On Rina
- Four types: Deva (to gods), Rishi (to gurus), Pitra (to parents), Atithi (to guests)
- Western model — not born in debt; Indic model — born with debt
- Feminist critique: Why is women's unpaid labor not recognized as fulfilling Rina?
- Modern parallels: Climate debt, COVID-19 community response

### On Karma
- NOT fatalism — it is empowerment and ethical responsibility
- Weber/Dumont: Karma supported caste hierarchy (critics)
- Radhakrishnan/Vivekananda: Karma = self-determined destiny
- Nishkama Karma Yoga: Action without attachment to fruits (Gita Ch. 2, V. 47)

### On Caste
- Ghurye's 6 features: birth-based segmentation, endogamy, hierarchy, food restrictions, hereditary occupation, segregation
- Varna = broad all-India 4-fold framework; Jati = local/regional sub-classification (hundreds to thousands)
- Dumont: Caste hierarchy based on purity/pollution distinction
- Buddhism and Jainism: Both rejected Varna as reform movements — conduct, not birth, determines worth
- By 18th century, formal caste distinctions were more rigid; before that, social identities were more flexible
- BR Ambedkar authored Indian Constitution; KR Narayanan = first Dalit president
