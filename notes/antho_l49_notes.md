## 3.2 — JAJMANI SYSTEM

### Origin and Definition

- **First reported by:** William H. Wiser in *The Hindu Jajmani System* (1936)
- **Field site:** Karimpur village, Uttar Pradesh
- **Regional equivalents:** *Yajamani* system (Tamil Nadu), *Jenmi* system (Kerala)

**Definition (Oscar Lewis, 1958):**
> "Under this system each caste group within a village is expected to give certain standardized services to the families of other castes."

- Each person works for a particular family or group of families
- Occupation is based on **hereditary ties** — passed from father to son to grandson

---

### Key Stakeholders

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Jajman** | Higher caste person who *receives* services; often called *Rajah* (King) |
| **Kamin** | Lower caste person who *performs* services (*kam karne wala*); called *subjects* |

- Wiser first drew attention to the system but did not know it was **universal** in Indian villages

---

### Oscar Lewis's Study — Rampur Village, UP

Documented the relationship between **Jats (landowners)** and servicing castes:

| Caste | Service |
|---|---|
| Kumhaar (Potter) | Supplies earthen vessels |
| Lohar (Blacksmith) | Supplies agricultural tools |
| Kachhi (Carpenter) | Supplies wooden tools and repairs |
| Nai (Barber) | Shaving and haircutting |

- The Jajmani system = an **agriculture-based system of production and distribution** of goods and services
- Servicing castes (artisans, barbers, washermen) are **linked with land-owning dominant castes**
- Described as **a system of social, economic and cultural ties** between different caste groups

---

### Characteristics of the Jajmani System (6 features)

1. **Permanent relationships**
   - Kamin cannot leave Jajman; Jajman cannot replace Kamin
   - Ensures lifelong fixed relations; rural economy remains stable

2. **Hereditary**
   - In case of family separation, Jajmani rights are divided like property (per N.S. Reddy)
   - Rights pass to the son; if no male heir, to the son-in-law

3. **Goods against services (not purely monetary)**
   - Kamin paid in cash or in kind (food grains)
   - Oscar Lewis documented payment details:
     - **Carpenter (Khati):** One mound of foodgrain annually + 2-3 seers during harvest
     - **Sweeper (Chuhra):** Two meals/day + as much grain as he could carry during harvest + food at marriages

4. **Social (not just economic) relationships**
   - Relation is **humanitarian**, not employer-employee
   - Jajman takes full responsibility for Kamin's welfare; Kamin serves with devotion

5. **Peace and contentment** (W.H. Wiser)
   - Kamins feel security — not worried about employment
   - Jajmans are assured of services

6. **Related to ritual matters**
   - Kamins perform ritual/ceremonial duties at birth, marriage, death
   - Example — Thakur family (Lucknow, UP) served by **10 castes** for life-cycle rites:

| S.No. | Caste | Role in Ritual |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Brahmin | Nama-sanskarana (naming ceremony) |
| 2 | Sunar (Goldsmith) | Gold ornaments for newborn |
| 3 | Dhobi (Washerman) | Washes dirty clothes |
| 4 | Nai (Barber) | Carries messages |
| 5 | Khati (Carpenter) | Provides wooden stool (patta) |
| 6 | Lohar | Provides kara (bracelet) |
| 7 | Kumhar (Potter) | Provides kulhar (jugs) for water |
| 8 | Pasi | Provides patal (leaf plates) |
| 9 | Bhangi (Scavenger) | Cleans place after feast |

---

### Disadvantages of the Jajmani System

1. **Instrument of exploitation and discrimination**
   - In Rampur village study — what was once based on personal relationships became an instrument of exploitation of Kamins by Jajmans

2. **Restricts social mobility of lower castes**
   - Hereditary nature = no possibility of changing occupation

3. **Supported by caste system**
   - Suffers from all evils of caste system
   - Conditions of Kamins are miserable; upper castes subject them to harassment

---

### Different Scholarly Perspectives on the Jajmani System

| Scholar (Year) | Focus | Key Argument |
|---|---|---|
| Scholar (1963) — [female scholar, name blanked in PDF] | Occupational division of labour in Indian agricultural village | It is a **system of distribution** where Jajmans (higher caste landowners) are provided services/products by lower castes |
| Scholar (1953) | Social dynamics | It is not easy for an agriculturist to remove a family attached to his household and secure services of another |
| Scholar (1972) | Internal functioning | Jajmani relations operate at the **family level** — a Rajput family has Jajmani ties with one family each from Brahmin, barber, carpenter, etc. |

---

### Functions of the Jajmani System

- Regulates the **division of labour and economic interdependence** of castes
- Maintains the Indian village as a **self-sufficient unit**
- Distributes agricultural produce in exchange for menial and craft services
- Helps maintain **prestige of higher castes**
- Payments to Kamins are in cash or kind at fixed intervals

---

### Factors of Change in the Jajmani System (4 Major Trends)

1. **Subsistence economy → Surplus economy:** Shift in economic power from landowners to non-agricultural groups; alters traditional patronage
2. **Introduction of modern technology:** New economic opportunities; changes traditional division of labour
3. **Abolition of intermediaries (Zamindars):** Alters traditional power structures and patron-client relationships
4. **Emergence of associations/institutions** linked to urban and national organizations: New avenues for social mobility; fragmentation of traditional relationships

---

### Decline of the Jajmani System (Post-Independence)

**Three main causes:**

1. **Role of State**
   - Planned development transformed agriculture
   - State provided credit, fertilizers, irrigation, HYV seeds — breaking the Jajman's monopoly as provider

2. **Technical innovations and industrialization**
   - Machine-made goods reached rural areas — artisan castes had to compete with machines
   - **Safety razors** → practice of self-shaving became popular → role of barbers declined
   - **Steel utensils** → importance of potters declined
   - **Bore wells and hand pumps** → dependence on kahaars (water carriers) declined

3. **Political mobilization of dalits and backward classes**
   - Constitution committed to upliftment and empowerment
   - New sense of identity among dalits and OBCs
   - Democratic assertion of backward segments was a serious blow to the exploitative system
   - Monopolistic rights of landed higher castes over means of production challenged

## 3.2 — TRIBE-CASTE CONTINUUM

### Historical Background

- Concept of **'continuum'** originally coined by **R. Redfield** in his work *The Folk Culture of Yucatan*
- He adopted the **'Folk-Urban Continuum'** model — the cultural shift from rural to urban is a gradual process, not sudden
- Scholars like **Bailey** and **Surajit Sinha** studied the social situation in India and realized the **Tribe-Caste Continuum** represents social mobility occurring in Indian villages and tribal communities

**Continuum (simple definition):** A gradual progression or range without any clear boundaries or sudden changes

**Diagram of Tribe-Caste Continuum:**
```
TRIBE ←————————————————————————→ CASTE
(Totally     (Partially    (In Cultural   (Totally
isolated)    Isolated)      Contact)      Assimilated)
e.g., Onge,  Sentinelese   Chenchu       Gonds        Meeraasi/
                                                        Bhumij
```

---

### Scholars' Attempts to Distinguish Tribe and Caste

#### 1. J.H. Hutton's Classification
- Distinction primarily in terms of **religious beliefs and practices**
- If a group has **animist beliefs** → classified as tribe
- Otherwise → classified into caste
- Economic, political, and ecological factors were of **secondary importance**

#### 2. Andre Beteille's Classification
Distinctions based on three criteria:

1. **Relative Geographic Isolation**
   - Tribes: geographically isolated, in inaccessible areas; a world within itself, few external social ties
   - Caste: by nature, part of a larger whole, linked by multifarious ties with other castes

2. **Dialect they speak**
   - Tribes speak dialects that differ markedly from major Indian languages

3. **Religion**
   - Religion is a **loose criterion** — what we encounter is a continuum without sharp breaks; where we draw the line between 'tribal' and 'non-tribal' remains arbitrary

#### 3. F.G. Bailey
- Took all points raised by sociologists into account; presented a scheme to discriminate in **structural terms**
- Suggested we should **not view tribe and caste disjunctively** — view them in terms of continuum
- **Caste society = organic and hierarchical**
- **Tribal society = segmentary and egalitarian**
- Made distinction in relation to the **politico-economic system** (not total behaviour)
- Aware that both caste and tribe are getting transformed
- Declared: **"both caste and tribe are being merged into a different system which is neither one nor the other"**
- **Limitation:** As caste and tribe cease to fit classical definitions, the practical value of Bailey's scheme is limited

#### 4. Surajit Sinha
- Disciple of Redfield; studied Tribe-Caste continuum through fieldwork **among tribes of Bastar**
- Lower castes in India seem to share with tribes the **emphasis on equality** in social behaviour
- Gives considerable **freedom for women** in cultural activities
- **Supernatural beliefs** of lower castes have similarities with tribal beliefs

---

### Tribes and Hinduisation

- **Ghurye (1943):** Called tribal populations "an imperfectly integrated segment of the Hindus" — **'backward Hindus'**
- Tribes like **Khasa and Bhils** are referenced in the Ramayana and Mahabharata — showing cultural contacts with Hindu society despite general isolation

#### Mythological Example: Bhils of Central India and Parvati's Curse
- Bhils = traditionally hunter-gatherers and pastoralists
- Myth: Parvati asked Lord Shiva to gift her brothers (the Bhils) something valuable. Shiva gave them his sacred bull **Nandi** (could have been used for ploughing). Greedy brothers killed Nandi hoping to find treasure.
- **Parvati's curse:** They would never be able to farm
- Hence Bhils remained outside the farming tradition
- Myth links Bhils to Hindu deities Shiva and Parvati — shows how tribal identity was absorbed into Hindu framework
- Also passes lesson of respect for the natural world to younger generations

#### Key Scholarly Works on Tribes and Hinduisation
- **N.K. Bose** — *Hindu Methods of Tribal Absorption* (1941): Showed Jati influence on tribes of Odisha; how Hindu society gradually absorbed tribal groups — some fully, some partially
- **Martin Orans** — *The Santhal: A Tribe in Search of a Great Tradition* (1965): Shows tribes being attracted towards Hinduism; in some cases, tribes remain 'tribes' in name only due to Hinduisation

---

### Examples of Interaction between Tribes and Hindu Society

1. **Jenu-Kuruba (Karnataka)**
   - Food-gathering tribe skilled in catching and training elephants
   - Supplied elephants to temples and state armies
   - Supplied forest products in exchange for goods of necessity
   - Paid taxes and rents — shows economic integration

2. **Toda (Nilgiris, Tamil Nadu)**
   - Though primarily pastoral, they worshipped the deity at **Nanjangud (Karnataka)** — about 140 km away across dense forests
   - Shows long-distance religious interaction
   - Those practising settled cultivation developed contacts with neighbouring peasants and caste groups

3. **Munda (Jharkhand, 19th century)**
   - Socially and economically integrated with neighbouring populations
   - Participated in local markets; shared agricultural practices

---

### Mythological Narratives as Interaction

#### a) Guru Dronacharya and Ekalavya
- Ekalavya (tribal boy) tried to learn archery from Guru Drona
- Denied training due to background; practised on his own and became highly skilled
- Drona demanded Ekalavya's **right thumb as guru-dakshina** to maintain Arjuna's supremacy
- **Reflects the unequal interaction of tribes with caste society**

#### b) Bhima and Hidimbi
- After the Lakshagriha incident, Pandavas lived in forest
- Bhima encountered Hidimba (tribal woman), who fell in love with him
- Bhima killed her brother Hidimb; married Hidimba with Kunti's consent
- Their son **Ghatotkacha** later played a vital role in the Mahabharata war
- **Shows integration of a tribal lineage into the epic Hindu tradition**

**Conclusion on Tribe-Caste Continuum:** It is a complex and dynamic concept useful for understanding social mobility and diversity in Indian society. With increasing Hinduisation and other factors, traditional distinctions between tribe and caste are becoming less clear.

## 5.3.b — GREAT AND LITTLE TRADITION

### What is Tradition?
- The word tradition means **'to transmit'**
- Traditions = transmitted values and behaviour of any community that has persisted over time
- Observable through: oral behaviour, social behaviour, material behaviour

---

### Robert Redfield's Classification

**Scholar:** Robert Redfield
**Book:** *Peasant Society and Culture* (1956)

Redfield proposed two types of traditions in every society:

| Great Tradition | Little Tradition |
|---|---|
| Tradition of **elite class people** | Tradition of **non-literate peasant/common people** |
| Emerged from **urban centres** | Emerged from the **folk community** |
| Based on **religious texts** | Mostly based on **traditional beliefs** |
| Transmitted through **formal centres and religious texts** | Transmitted **orally** |
| Spread in a **wide geographical area** | More or less **localised** |
| **Organised** | **Unorganised** |
| Formal, literate, written | Informal, unwritten, oral |

**Examples:**
- Great Tradition: Gita, Ramayana, Upanishads, Bible, Quran (transmitted through formal centres)
- Little Tradition: Folklore, magical rituals, religious practices not found in religious epics
- Religious centres like **Gaya, Varanasi** embody Great Tradition

**Sacred Specialists:** Mediate between Great and Little traditions; propagate Great Tradition elements to Little Tradition by popularising myths and legends

**Key Point:** Though **separate in origin**, Great Tradition and Little Tradition are **interdependent and interact** with each other.

---

### Prominent Example: Jagannath at Puri

- By the 12th century, a local tribal deity at Puri (whose image is made of wood by local tribal specialists) was recognised as **Jagannath — a form of Vishnu**
- Vishnu was visualized differently from other parts of India
- **Jagannatha** (extreme right), **Subhadra** (centre, sister), **Balarama** (left, brother)
- **Goddess cults:** Local deities (often a stone smeared with ochre) were incorporated into Puranic framework as wives of principal male deities — equated with Lakshmi (wife of Vishnu) or Parvati (wife of Shiva)

---

### Limitation of Great and Little Tradition Framework

**S.C. Dube (1965)** — Indian cultural realities are **too complex** for a mere 'bipolar model'
- His view of traditions is **'multipolar'**
- Suggested a **sixfold classification:**

| Tradition Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Classical traditions** | Rooted in mythology, Sanskrit texts, religious lore | Ramayana and Mahabharata shaping Hindu culture |
| 2. **Regional traditions** | Shared cultural traits giving a region its identity, beyond religion | Bhangra in Punjab, Bihu in Assam |
| 3. **Local traditions** | Restricted to small groups or areas, often tribal/village-based | Bhuta worship (Karnataka), Karam festival (Jharkhand tribes) |
| 4. **Western traditions** | Came through colonial contact; shaped ideology and lifestyle | Railways and telegraph (British), Western dress codes among urban elites |
| 5. **Emergent national traditions** | Linked to revival and national identity formation | Ganesh festival popularised by Bal Gangadhar Tilak during freedom struggle |
| 6. **Sub-cultural traditions** | Practices of specific elite groups | Mughal court culture (Persian poetry, etiquette), Nawabi traditions of Lucknow (Kathak, tehzeeb) |

**Mandelbaum and S.C. Dube:** The term 'Great Tradition' cannot be a static one since there is constant interaction and interchange between the two traditions.

## 5.3.b — UNIVERSALISATION AND PAROCHIALISATION

### Scholar: McKim Marriott

**Background:** McKim Marriott and Milton Singer were students of Robert Redfield who came to India and studied village communities. They applied Redfield's concepts in the Indian context.

**Book:** *Village India: Studies in the Little Community* (1955)

---

### The Two Movements

**Marriott recognised** that Great and Little Traditions do not remain as separate compartments but **move towards each other.**

```
Great Tradition
      ↑                    ↓
 Universalisation     Parochialisation
      ↑                    ↓
Little Tradition
```

| Concept | Direction | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Universalisation** | Little Tradition → Great Tradition (upward) | Local/folk elements get elevated and become part of the pan-Indian Great Tradition |
| **Parochialisation** | Great Tradition → Little Tradition (downward) | Elements of Great Tradition come down and merge into localised Little Tradition |

---

### Example from Kerala (Contextual Introduction)

At **Andaloor Kavu** near Thalassaery, Kannur district:
- People worship **Daivathar, Angakkaran, and Boppooran**
- **Theyyam** performances are held during festivals — belief is local and orally transmitted (Little Tradition)
- But these deities are equated with Great Traditional deities: **Sreeraman, Lakshmanan, and Hanuman** respectively
- This movement = interplay of Universalisation and Parochialisation

---

### Classic Example: Parochialisation

**Gobardhan Puja at Kishangarhi:**
- **Govardhanagiri** (hill, ~40 km from Kishangarhi) is part of Great Traditional belief — Lord Krishna saved people from Indra's fury by lifting Govardhan hill as an umbrella
- This belief came down to become a local tradition
- People at Kishangarhi worship **heaps of cow dung** (Gobardhan = cow dung in Hindi; Govardhan = the hill) imitating the Govardhan belief
- Marriot calls this a **parochialised form of Govardhan puja**

### Classic Example: Universalisation

**Raksha Bandhan (Rakhi):**
- The local festival at Kishangarhi is called **Saluno**
- On this day, married daughters apply tilak of barley on foreheads of unmarried sisters and brothers and get money/gifts in return
- Brahmin priests tie Rakhi on their patrons (Jajman) and get something in return
- Marriot contends that **Raksha Bandhan came into being as a result of Universalisation of the local Saluno festival**

---

### Limitation: The Circular Process

- **Not two-directional but circular and continuous**
- Classic example — Ramayana:
  - Oral folk stories in villages → **Little Tradition**
  - Shaped into grand Sanskrit epic by **Valmiki** → **Great Tradition**
  - Translated into regional languages; Tulsidas's **Ramcharitmanas** in Hindi → treasured by ordinary people
  - Recitations in homes and **Ram Lila dramas** in villages → re-enters **Little Tradition**
  - Cycle: LT → GT → LT (circular, not merely two-directional)

## 3.3 — SACRED COMPLEX

### Concept and Origin

- **Scholar:** L.P. Vidyarthi (1961)
- **Book:** *The Sacred Complex in Hindu Gaya*
- **School of thought:** Chicago School of Anthropology
- **Student of:** Robert Redfield
- **Field site:** Hindu pilgrim centre **Gaya, Magadh, Bihar**

Vidyarthi tried to analyze the contribution and importance of **traditional centres of Indian civilization** in a systematic way.

---

### Three Components of Sacred Complex

Vidyarthi described three analytical concepts:

| Component | Definition | Example at Gaya |
|---|---|---|
| **Sacred Geography** | Places significant in pilgrim centres where rites are performed; help in cultural transmission of Great Tradition elements | Idols in temples, sacred trees, bathing ghats |
| **Sacred Performances** | Sets of rites and rituals performed by worshippers at sacred centres; vary with nature of each centre | Gaya Shraddha (ritual for ancestors) |
| **Sacred Specialists** | Maintain distinct style of life; transmit Great Traditions to rural population by popularising texts, organising pilgrimage, officiating as ritual/temple priests | Gayawal Brahmins at Gaya |

**Together = Sacred Complex**

**Vidyarthi's conclusion:** This place of pilgrimage reflects a level of **combination, continuity, and compromise** between elements of Great Tradition and Little Tradition.

---

### Significance of Sacred Complex

1. **Cultural transmission:** Unfolds channels for integration of civilization
2. **Social integration:** Provides meeting place for different castes, sects, classes, statuses
3. **Integrates GT and LT:** The mechanism of sacred complex and institution of pilgrimage are effective means of integration
4. **Promotes creative arts and literature:** Helps preserve 'ideal types' (GT elements)
5. **Binding force:** Binds people together against geographical diversities and social/linguistic heterogeneity

---

### Other Scholars Who Extended the Sacred Complex Concept

| Scholar | Year | Study Location | Key Contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S.C. Saraswati** | 1962, 1965 | Naimisharanya (U.P.), Goa, Kashi (with Jha and Vidyarthi) | Argued pilgrimage is a sacred act itself; showed sacred institutions also regulate secular life (festivals, community org., local leadership); Kashi shows textual and contextual continuity of sacred geography |
| **Jha** | 1971, 1977 | Janakpur (Nepal), Puri | Civilisation transcends political borders — Janakpur in Nepal tied to Indian religious consciousness (Sita's birthplace); Jagannath cult of Puri — analysed origin, rituals, pilgrims' networks; sacred complexes are dynamic, constantly reinterpreted |

## 3.3 — NATURE-MAN-SPIRIT (NMS) COMPLEX

### Concept and Origin

- **Scholar:** L.P. Vidyarthi
- **Based on:** Cultural Ecology — constant interaction between Man and Nature
- **Vidyarthi's addition:** A third element — the **Spirits**
- Constant interaction goes on between **social organization, ecological conditions, and religious complex** in the whole of tribal life

---

### The Maler Tribe — Vidyarthi's Field Study

**Tribe:** Maler (hill tribe)
**Location:** Upper side of Rajmahal Hills
**Economy:** Shifting cultivation (*Khallu*)

**Four types of Maler spirits:**

| Spirit Type | Nature |
|---|---|
| **Gossaiyan** | Benevolent spirit — governing all spirits; bridge between Malers and their concept of superhuman |
| **Jiwe Urrkya** | Ancestral spirits |
| **Alchi** | Evil spirits |
| **Chergani** | Spiritual power of a witch or witchcraft |

**Broadly classified into two:**
1. **Benevolent spirits** — responsible for good health, wealth, and increased agricultural productivity
2. **Malevolent spirits** — cause sickness, abortions, ill health, and death

**Key rule:** Maler tribes don't do anything without the approval of **Gosaiyyan**

**Example of NMS in action:** During government developmental activities, Maler tribes were brought down from the hills. Some became ill and some died. The Malers believed the **malevolent Gosaiyyan was angry** at them for leaving the hills.

---

### NMS Complex Diagram

```
Nature ——————— Man ——————— Spirit
  ↓               ↓              ↓
Economic life   Social         Super-human/
based on        organization   Super-natural
forest          (family,       world (Gosain
(Shifting       marriage,      and other
cultivation/    kinship etc.)  spirits)
Khallu)
            ↑ ↑ ↑ ↑
              Complex
```

Nature and Spirits exert profound influence over Maler life; three interrelated factors are in constant interaction.

---

| **P.K. Singh** | 1981 | Maler (restudy) | Rajmahal Hills |

**P.K. Singh's restudy of Maler (1981):**
- Studied same villages Vidyarthi studied two decades earlier
- Concluded: Despite many changes in Maler life, the NMS concept was **valid in time dimension as well**
- NMS complex **cannot change — it is a constant!**
- Even if the content of the NMS complex may change through time, **the complex itself remains constant**

---

### Significance of the NMS Complex

1. **Holistic worldview**
   - Highlights unity of nature, man, and spirit — humans as part of ecological and spiritual order
   - Example: Among **Santhals** — Baha festival involves worship of village deities along with sacred groves (**Jaherthan**), linking ecology, community, and spirit

2. **Explains resistance to change**
   - Explains why tribes resist relocation, mining, or large-scale dams — disruption of sacred land disturbs their entire worldview
   - Example: **Dongria Kondh** tribe of Odisha resisted **Vedanta's bauxite mining** project in **Niyamgiri Hills**, calling the mountain **'Niyam Raja'**

3. **Tool for applied anthropology**
   - Guides policymakers to frame culturally sensitive development projects
   - Example: **Verrier Elwin's** recommendations for **"Tribal Panchsheel"** (1950s) — stressed minimal interference and respecting tribal culture (recognising NMS worldview in development policy)

4. **Sustainable use of resources**
   - Tribes maintain ecological balance through sacred taboos and rituals
   - Example: **Khasi and Garo tribes** of Meghalaya conserve **sacred groves** — cutting trees or removing leaves is prohibited

5. **Integration of tradition and development**
   - Development must include spiritual and cultural dimensions, not just economic growth
   - Example: In Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, tribal communities combine traditional **Sarhul festival** (worship of sal trees and earth goddess) with community discussions about agriculture and water use

---

### Case Study: Dongria Kondh and Niyamgiri Hills

- More than **15,000 Kondh tribes** (Dongria and Kutia) live in 160 villages across Rayagada and Kalahandi districts, Odisha
- The tribes consider the hills sacred and worship it as **'Niyam Raja'**
- Till early 2000s, Dongria Kondhs lived peacefully in inaccessible hamlets on slopes of Niyamgiri range
- **2004:** Vedanta set up a pit-head alumina refinery at Lanjigarh (foothills of Niyamgiri)
- Odisha has **700 million tonnes** of known bauxite reserves; **88 million tonnes** estimated in Niyamgiri
- Environmental laws were violated; Dongria's consent was not sought
- **April 18, 2013:** Supreme Court ruled that mining clearance can only be given if gram sabhas comprising Dongrias agreed
- **Result:** All 12 villages selected by the government **voted against the project** — India's first environmental referendum

## IMPORTANT DEFINITIONS (Quick Reference)

- **Jajmani System:** "Under this system each caste group within a village is expected to give certain standardized services to the families of other castes." — Oscar Lewis (1958), relying on Wiser's work
- **Great Tradition:** Tradition of elite class people, transmitted through formal centres (schools, religious institutions, written books) in a formal, literate manner — Robert Redfield
- **Little Tradition:** Tradition of non-literate common people, transmitted orally from one generation to another — Robert Redfield
- **Universalisation:** The upward movement of elements of Little Tradition to become a part of Great Tradition — McKim Marriott
- **Parochialisation:** The downward movement of elements of Great Tradition to merge into Little Tradition — McKim Marriott
- **Sacred Complex:** An intricate and interdependent grouping of sacred geographies, sacred performances, and sacred specialists — L.P. Vidyarthi
- **Nature-Man-Spirit Complex:** The constant interaction between social organization (man), ecological conditions (nature), and religious complex (spirit) in tribal life — L.P. Vidyarthi
- **Folk-Urban Continuum:** The cultural shift from rural to urban is a gradual process — R. Redfield
- **Tribe-Caste Continuum:** The gradual range from totally isolated tribal groups to totally assimilated caste groups — Bailey, Surajit Sinha
